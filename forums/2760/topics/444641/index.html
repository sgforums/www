    by Morris Kline

    I read this book after wasting 4 years of my precious youth getting an engineering degree. This book changed the way I look at Mathematics. No, it is not just some formulas which one manipulates to solve problems. Mathematics, as Morris Kline puts it, arises from the search for an understanding of our physical world. He employed the example of the motion of the moon around the earth to show, using simple high school mathematics, how Newton arrived at his laws. Believe me, he explained it better than most Physics textbooks do. He explained how Calculus arose from the need to solve everyday maximization/minimization problems and pointed to the fact that the inventors of Calculus were after all mortals like us. 

    I lament the flippant manner in which engineering math is being taught to engineering undergraduates. Most of these engineering professors are not specifically trained in applied mathematics. They may have taken some graduate-level courses in engineering math but that alone does not qualify them to teach the subject. IF you are an engineering undergraduate and if you think engineering math seems more like a disparate set of techniques with no apparent link to your core modules, you are not alone. Why, for example, would a civil engineer be interested in complex variable theory? When I was an undergrad, I didn't know either because the lecturer failed to show any link of complex variables to physical problems. To sum up, the knowledge of mathematical methods has not been brought fully to bear on its applications to physical problems. Every Engineering professor ought to get a copy of Morris Kline's 'Mathematics and the Physical World' and start reading!
