Not forgetting that through such group interactions, we actually can guide and encourage one another during times of spiritual struggles, that each and everyone of us may encounter at different stages of our lives.

    Hi Spwn07,

    Just to share some of my opinions.

    Bohiruci had covered most of the problems that setting up a cell group would face. The primary problems are the commitment of the buddhists (whom are suppose to attend) and the age group issue. Even though I'm 25, I really have not much of an idea what is 'in' with the teenagers.

    But I will know what are the problems that young working adults are facing.

    My friends and I used to organise such cell group gathering for a centre. The above 2 are the reasons that made us unable to sustain. Because people who come to our group were like still in their secondary schools and I was in the Army, quite 'out-of-touch' already. Being young people themselves, probably they came due to their parents' recommendations, they soon find themselves more interested in going out having fun then exploring the spiritual path.

    I did not attend any cell group before but had attended church and cell groups and caught a glimspe of their manual in handling cell group.

    The purpose of the cell group is a feedback channel from the audience to the church. The sermon is too big and short (considering if the pastor has to answer ALL audience's questions and problems in 1 1/2 hours to 2 hours duration). Therefore if you had attended sermon, you will notice there is not Q and A. Attending sermon is to receive whatever information that the pastor and the church is trying to communicate across to the audience.

    The cell group then manages these crowd in smaller chunks. Effective cell group management are usually smaller in size (same as the classroom management, whereby smaller size means more attention to individual). That also means more cell group leaders need to be trained. Cell group would have probably recap what was taught in the last sermon and re-emphasize the essence of the message and clear the doubts of the audience.

    I don't know how City Harvest operates now, but previously the cell group that I went to have 20 over people. To what I know, Faith Community Baptist Church keeps their cell group no more than 13 people.

    The function of the cell group is to provide pastoral care. Pastoral care is not just about preaching about bible but give care and counselling to the members who encountered problem. This is a much of a cultural value that the organisation has to set pace.

    To my experience, christians have the value of sacrifice which is I don't find it from buddhists. When there is an issue, christians will pray and put into actions. Buddhists will just pray and just probably sit and radiate metta and do little or nothing to really alleviate the problem.

    I am currently a volunteer religious counsellor with Singapore Prison Service. It is very 'encouraging' when everytime I go to the prison, one or (at the most) two buddhist counsellors to a group; whereas christian counsellors (at least to the prison I'm visiting) never have less than six. There was once I saw eleven christian counsellors going to the same group.

    They see a problem, they pray, and they become the solution to the problem, or at least they try. I really admire and respect them for this.

    However, bohiruci may have overlooked on point. Megachurches like CHC does combine celebration with other churches in big events. I remember at least twice, they have these prayer rally that is around Christmas some years back, held together with other churches at the National Stadium. I think till present, the head pastor of CHC is still one of the members with the National Council of Churches.

    For the norm buddhist, when they pray for wisdom, go Manjusri; deliverance to Amitabha; healing to Medicine-Buddha, etc. Too scattered as compare to one almighty God. Anything just pray to this one God and it's settle.

Besides been well versed in the doctrines, cell leaders has to attend at least some kind of basic counselling skills, to counsel to members who are troubled; time management skills in small group settings (ie. moderate the dialogues in the cell group, not to exceed time etc); oral communications (one may know the Dhamma but may not necessary talk well, know of some venerables, good in speech, bad in writing and vice-versa), etc. So I think in any organisations, seeing that there is no immediate monetary return, they would have probably drop the idea.

    Hi,
    just to share, actually the idea of "cell-groups" existed, and still exist, in Japanese Buddhism from more than 600 years ago, they are called "Ko" (in chinese, "Jiang" as in "jiang hua" - speak). Currently, most of the "traditional" Buddhist denominations like Jodo, Jodo Shin, Nichiren and "new-age" like Rissho Kosei Kai and Soka Gakkai still have these "cell-groups" meetings. So it is possible for an individual temple in Singapore to have one of its own, even though it may differ doctrinally from another.
    The churches that were used in example here have their own cell-groups and they do not "mix" with other churches' cell-groups because it is affiliated with members of its parent church, ie CHC cell-groups, NCC cell-groups etc. All of them operate differently and serve different members. Also, sometimes, there are people of different ages and occupations in one, not all are of the same age group at all.
    However, i agree with Yamizi that it is quite difficult for Buddhists to have cell-groups because most of us tend to talk of very "high" doctrinal stuff like Sunyata (Emptiness) and the like without being able to "bring it down" to the masses. I think it is a reflection and advice we should bring in mind.
    Gassho
  • sanath
    16 Dec 07, 10:08
    But i think Yamizi was also smack-right about the point of too many icons. The groups in Japan so far that have been able to successfully keep the "cell-groups (Ko)" so far are those who emphasise on one icon, either "Namo Amida Butsu (Namo Amitabha)" - Jodo Shu, Jodo Shinshu, those of the Nichiren sects like Nichiren Shu, Nichiren Shoshu and Soka Gakkai, or those based on Kobo Daishi Kukai (founder of the Shingon Esoteric Buddhism in Japan).

    For Jodo Shin where i belong to, many temples were in-fact started off by having these "Ko", and from these "Ko" groups, members started assembling and they reorganized (or had to reorganize) into formal temples during the start of the Tokugawa rule in the 1700s. However, it does not mean that the "Ko" groups died down. Female "Ko" groups, called "Ama-ko" were also huge that they were later reorganized as the "Women's Buddhist Association" in the early 1900s.

    Yamizi also gave the problems that ordained clerics tend to demand "respect", thus making it difficult for Buddhist cell-groups in Singapore to work. In Japan, where most ordained clerics are now also living the same life as their lay counterparts, it is much more easier for them to lend a listening hand and ear to the lay congregation because they also understand and experience the same difficulties - raising children, cost of living, education etc etc. And in Jodo Shinshu where we've had a lay clergy since the inception, ordained and lay both play a part in studying and propagation, and even though most of time it is still the clerics who do the studying and propagation, there is no hindrance to a lay person leading the services or the study sessions. In these, the priests play a supporting role.

    Well I'm not sure had mistyblue attended cell group session before but then cell group session is not just another session of talk.

    The session would often start with simple praise and worship. Then the cell group leader would open up by reliterate the last sermon. Sometimes if the parent church has activities, these cell groups can be modified into small project group to help bits and pieces.
