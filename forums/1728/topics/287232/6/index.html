    Originally posted by AndrewPKYap:
    How can luminosity be form? What is form?
    Form here means appearances. There is ultimately no fixed form and shapes with inherent existence, this is an illusion. (using the analogy of the flower -- you see red, dog see black & white, therefore flower has no fixed attributes, form, shapes, etc... but is merely a manifestation of karmic causes and conditions)

    What there is however, is conditioned manifestation which is empty, therefore unconditioned. It is illusory, magical appearances -- because there is no 'solidity' or 'existence' within appearances, but merely a mere manifestation with no reality apart from the causes and conditions.

    It is this appearances that are self-luminous, self-shimmering. As Ken Wilber describes (see http://awakeningtoreality.blogspot.com/2007/05/some-writings-on-non-duality-by-ken.html), "The entire universe is a transparent shimmering of the Divine, of primordial Purity. But the Divine is not someplace else, it is just all of this shimmering. It is self-seen. It has One Taste. It is nowhere else."

    As my Master wrote, "Dharmakaya" means the Thus Come One (Buddha)'s true body; the reality of all dharma/phenomena/manifestations, pervading all spaces, and no where can it not be found. The great sages of the past said, "Green bamboos are completely Dharmakaya; each grass and each tree are all Prajna. Each grass and wood are amidst the ocean of the nature of Thus Come One (i.e Buddha Nature). The illusory great bamboos displays Dharmakaya's substance, it is not that green bamboos means dharmakaya. The illusory luxuriant chrysanthemum expresses the function of Prajna, it is not that chrysanthemums mean Prajna."

    It is not that Dharmakaya has fixed forms, shapes and attributes, it is formless -- but the formless is in fact, the appearances -- the mere appearances of sights, the sounds, the flowers, the trees. It is formless because it is empty of any fixed, inherent existence.

    This is also what Buddha meant when he said, in seeing, only the seen, no seer. In hearing, only the heard, no hearer... etc (the same goes to smelling, tasting, touching, thinking) There is literally no observer apart from the observed (and no thinker apart from thought, no doer apart from done, etc etc..)

    As a Zen Master put it, "When I heard the sound of the bell ringing, there was no I, and no bell, just the ringing."

    There is only conditioned manifestation -- mere appearances -- the scenary, the birds chirping, etc (words can never ultimately describe it, it is the pure experience that must be intuitively known) and this appearance is self-knowing, self shimmering. The divine is not anywhere else apart from manifestation. Manifestation is the Source.

    No watcher needed, the process itself knows and rolls as Venerable Buddhaghosa writes in the Visuddhi Magga.

    Self-luminous manifestation arises, never the same and never remain, nothing apart from the crystal clear manifestation of appearances. Completely and fully real and gone, with no reality/separate existence apart from conditions.

    ...What appears to the eyes are forms, which are made up of shapes and colors. Everything that is a shape and color is included in the source of consciousness (Sanskrit: ayatana) that is called form. The shapes and colors that appear to the eyes are found in all of the aspects of the environment in which we live, as well as in all of the sentient beings who inhabit this environment. What is the true nature of the appearances of shapes and colors of the environment and sentient beings? It is that they are dependently arisen mere appearances, which do not exist in essence. The forms that appear do not truly exist. In the abiding nature of reality, their nature is emptiness. They appear while being empty; while empty, they appear. They are appearance-emptiness like rainbows, water-moons, and reflections. All of the objects that appear to the eyes are appearance-emptiness undifferentiable...

    ...In our waking life, even though the environment and sentient beings appear to us, the supplication says "let them rest where no self's found." The environment and sentient beings appear, but let them rest without clinging to them as truly existent. Let them rest in their natural state of appearance-emptiness without fixating on them as being real. When we let the appearances rest without fixating on them as being real, all of the thoughts of there being an actual object out there to perceive and an actual distinct subject perceiving it just dissolve. The thoughts that take the duality of perceived object and perceiving subject to be real dissolve. They are purified.
