    Originally posted by Xprobe:
    i disagree, Arhat "Mie"/ cease" emptiness while a bodhisattva doesnt because since the essence is emptiness, what is there to cease in the first place?
    What you're speaking is in terms of conventional Theravada teaching... and that is true in a sense. For example Nagarjuna denied the Theravada notion of 'cessation' or dependent origination as having discrete causality relations, but rather refines Dependent Origination to refute views of inherent existence (therefore D.O=Emptiness). So the Madhyamika teaching of Emptiness is a further refinement on that understanding that deconstructs even subtler views of inherent existence on the 'element' level so to speak.

    Or you can say... there are three turnings of the wheel... first is the Theravada turning, the four noble truths, and so on. The next is on Emptiness... the third turning is on the Luminous Buddha Nature.

    However, though you may enter from the first turning, doesn't mean you cannot realise Emptiness and the luminous aspect of our Buddha Nature.. but the teachings dont emphasize second and third turning. Actually for example Theravada also teaches luminosity as well (see the chapter 'Consciousness: Invisible, Radiant, Limitless' at http://www.thebuddhadharma.com/issues/2003/winter/ajahnamaro.html) with many Theravada teachers emphasising this part (such as the radiant unborn Citta), and the teachings of luminosity can be seen in the Vipassana teachings as well. Modern Theravada teachers like Dharma Dan and many other Theravada teachers are also comfortable and familiar with Mahayana teachings on Emptiness and Luminosity.

    Anyway in terms of direct, intuitive experience and insights, the View, Emptiness, is the same thing. Theravada's teachings on the Three Dharma Seals and Dependent Origination when realised deeply also leads to the realisation of emptiness. But when comes to interpretation and teaching, the different traditions have different way of teaching.. such as in Tibetan Buddhism there is the difference of Rangtong and Shentong style of teaching.
