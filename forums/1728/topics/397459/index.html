    Meditation: purpose and method


    Have you ever looked at a statue of a buddha and wondered what is going on in his mind? I know I have. If you are like me you might think that he is sitting there in a state of unimaginable ecstasy and that his mind is completely blank, with no thoughts appearing at all.

    Well, it struck me one day that that is not what is happening at all. What that statue represents is freedom. The buddha sitting in that position knows without doubt that he is free. He couldn't care less what is arising in his body, mind or external environment. There may be a maelstrom going on in there but he knows his essential nature of aware freedom and he is at rest. That is what meditation is.

    I used to be a meditator. I sat for an average of six hours a day, and up to eight or ten, for several months at a time on and off for several years. My meditation was to try to just let everything be as it is. But at the time I did not really have a proper understanding of what that actually means. There was always a subtle belief that by letting everything be as it is I would get into a nice state, be it bliss, peace, no thoughts, silence, I did not fully realize what the true purpose of letting everything be as it is in meditation was.

    All of those states can and will happen. They may be nice but, big deal. It is not helpful for coming to know freedom. In fact, desiring a state or using any kind of control, like watching the breath, is counterproductive for that purpose. Why? Because freedom is not a state and is not dependent on states, any state—not silence, no thinking or bliss. If it was dependent on anything, it wouldn't be freedom. Freedom means free in the midst of anything at all.

    So then, what is the proper and most advantageous use of meditation? Well, if you are after an experience of bliss or peace, which will inevitably pass, then meditating for that reason is fine and will work. Focusing on the breath, or visualizing or doing a mantra are very good ways to achieve bliss or a peaceful mind. But very shortly after you get off the cushion those states will be gone, especially when something unwanted happens. Of course they will, they are states and all states pass, without exception. But if you are interested in coming to know freedom then a different approach is called for.

    I would say that the best purpose of meditation is to come to know your essential freedom. The way that can happen is by coming to know the presence of awareness no matter what is appearing. To do that you just let everything be exactly as it is and don't try to manipulate your experience at all. It is best to just sit there, let your mind do whatever it does, and recognize that you are aware as often as you do naturally. Get lost a million times, no problem. At first it may seem that there is either the presence of awareness or you are lost in thought. So it seems like you are going back and forth between them, mostly lost in thought. You may be "aware" for a few seconds and then lost in thought for ten minutes, or an hour, and back and forth.

    What will start to happen, more and more, is that while a thought is appearing, at the same time, you will experience the presence of awareness, or you notice that awareness is present, that you are aware. So, awareness is present in and through the thought, and not either or. It is fine if you experience it at first as either one or the other, awareness or thought. This takes some time to get acquainted with, for sure. But with repeated practice you will, first, start to recognize awareness more and more often, and then you will begin to recognize awareness and thoughts simultaneously. And this has enormous value. This is when you start to recognize your inherent freedom.

    Normally in life, when strong thoughts or emotions arise we, seemingly, lose our awareness and react blindly based on the appearance—we react out of ignorance of our essential aware nature. But what begins to happen more and more is that, because you recognize your essential aware nature while strong thoughts and emotions occur in meditation, you will also recognize the same in normal life. When we come to know freedom during the arising of thoughts and emotions we can respond out of the wisdom of our essential, aware, free nature. We have a choice in the moment to not react blindly. Do you see the freedom in this? It doesn't mean that there are never thoughts and emotions. It means that you are free in the midst of the arising of thought and emotion.

    This is why it is not necessary or even desirable to try to stop your thinking while meditating. If it does that is fine, but thought will always come back sooner or later, usually sooner. And as you practice like this on the cushion this will begin to happen when you are not meditating also. More and more you will start to recognize the presence of your aware free nature in the midst of whatever is appearing—thoughts, emotions, sensations and experiences, and you will come to see that you are always free.

    (I want to add a note here. What I described above points to freedom. But ultimate freedom is not even dependent on being aware during the arising of thoughts. You are always free, regardless of whether you know you are aware or not. True freedom just means that you are doubtless that you are free. You don't need anything at all to confirm it. This type of meditation will introduce you to your essential, free nature. It is a practice, which serves the very beneficial purpose of becoming doubtless that you are free. More about this later.)

    There is no seeking in who you actually are.

    Enjoy that!

    Is there a path? And to where would it lead?


    In each moment, when we recognize our essential aware nature, we realize that the road we have been traveling on our whole life—paved by many forms of mundane and spiritual seeking—has been pointing to this very spot, where we are right now, never lost or distant, always 100% available to notice whenever we choose to. We have just chosen to travel many unnecessary and meandering miles. And this is nowhere more apparent than in spiritual seeking.


    Just now -

    Don't focus on anything for a few seconds.

    Notice that you are aware.

    Awareness is present.

    When you notice that you are aware, that awareness is present, you are recognizing your essential nature, your true home.

    By continuing to do this at all times and in all circumstances, you will become doubtless that that is true.

    When doubtlessness that this is your true nature dawns on you, and when you realize that this has always been here, always been the case, you will come to know freedom. You have always been free, you just didn't know it.

    Because -

    You have been told your whole life by your religions and cultures that home is very far off, in the future, almost impossible to realize and attain,

    And that -

    You will need to go on a long journey to get there. So, you put on your seeking helmet, get your many maps, and you are on your way—in the opposite direction of where you have always been.


    Just now -

    Notice that you are aware!

    the sun has been present on your journey the whole time.
    Just take your eyes off the road and look out the window.

    Break the Spell and Be Free

    YOU HAVE PROJECTED ONTO YOURSELF a world of your own imagination, based on memories, on desires and fears, and you have imprisoned yourself in it. Break the spell and be free.

    Once you realize that the world is your own projection, you are free of it. You need not free yourself of a world that does not exist, except in your own imagination! However is the picture, beautiful or ugly, you are painting it and you are not bound by it. Realize that there is nobody to force it on you, that it is due to the habit of taking the imaginary to be real. See the imaginary as imaginary and be free of fear.

    Meditation will help you to find your bonds, loosen them, untie them and cast your moorings. When you are no longer attached to anything, you have done your share. The rest will be done for you.

    In the mirror of your mind all kinds of pictures appear and disappear. Knowing that they are entirely your own creations, watch them silently come and go. Be alert, but not perturbed. This attitude of silent observation is the very foundation of Yoga. You see the picture, but you are not the picture.

    Meditation is a deliberate attempt to pierce into the higher states of consciousness and finally go beyond it. The art of meditation is the art of shifting the focus of attention to ever subtler levels without losing one's grip on the levels left behind. The final stage of meditation is reached when the sense of identity goes beyond "I-am-so-and-so," beyond all ideas into the impersonally personal pure being. But you must be energetic when you take to meditation. Save all your energies and time for breaking the wall your mind has built around you. Believe me, you will not regret it.

    The value of regular meditation is that it takes you away from the humdrum of daily routine and reminds you that you are not what you believe yourself to be. Awareness is unattached and unshaken. It is lucid, silent, peaceful, alert and unafraid, without desire and fear. Meditate on it as your true being, and try to be it in your daily life, and you shall realize it in its fullness.

    Whatever happens, happens to you, by you, through you; you are the creator, enjoyer and destroyer of all you perceive. You are the maker of the world in which you live; you alone can change it, or unmake it. You are the infinite potentiality, the inexhaustible possibility. Because you are, all can be. The universe is but a partial manifestation of your limitless capacity to become.
