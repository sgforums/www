    Originally posted by Herzog_Zwei:
    Tell me how you experience anything without physical stimulus?
    Enlightenment has nothing to do with experiencing anything physical objects or any particular conditioned phenomena (in fact you need to detach from all of them first), but rather, it is likened to awakening from a long dream to find that all along all there is is just the ultimate reality of luminous emptiness, there is nothing to hold on to, therefore, it cannot be known as an "experience". I am tempted to call it "non experience", but "non experience" or "experience" are both concepts, enlightenment is beyond all.

    Next, even when all sense consciousness fades away, there is still a chance to glimpse our primordial Clear Light, and attain liberation. This also shows that our ground luminosity with emptiness as its nature does not cease upon death or upon the stopping of sense perceptions, therefore ultimate reality of Luminous-Emptiness is everlasting and is not dependent on any sense objects. But for most non practitioners this is impossible, and even if there is someone to talk him into it, without prior practise, it is very difficult.

    But this Clear Light is not the light we think of, it is not red light, or blue light, being formless it is beyond everything you can imagine. Therefore the Tibetan Book of the Dead say it is "colorless light", it does not literally mean light as we know, but a form of luminous brightness that can be fully known (when you awaken). Yet without the primal luminosity aspect, there is no way we can ever cognize any physical stimulus. An analogy: without the sunlight or whatever light, it will be impossible to see what is going on in the room.

    Regarding Clear Light, Padmasambhava says:

    Since the Clear Light of your own intrinsic awareness is empty, it is the Dharmakaya;

    And this is like the sun rising in a cloudless illuminated sky.

    Even though (this light cannot be said) to possess a particular shape or form, nevertheless, it can be fully known.

    The meaning of this, whether or not it is understood, is especially significant.
    Again, intuitive, direct experience cannot be imagined by our ideas and concepts especially when ultimate reality transcends words and concepts. Any attempts to try to grasp it conceptually is as futile as describing colours to a blind man. You need to practise and get enlightened yourself.

    As stated in the teachings, buddha nature is defined as the emptiness of the fundamental clear light mind. The clear light of death is still a relative level of mind. But, because of the lack of conceptual thought at this level - its clear, luminous nature - it is see as analogous to buddha nature and used in meditation to approach complete union with buddha nature.

    The mirror is an imperfect, but useful, analogy for the mind. Like a mirror mind 'reflects' what it comes in contact with. In that sense it also changes from moment to moment. The basic clear and knowing nature of the mind is sustained, just as the mirror's clear, reflective nature does not change.

    That which is clear and knowing (the definition of mind) is eternal, as opposed to permanent. Eternal because it never ceases; it continues in its momentary, ever-changing (impermanent) flow into eternity. So, it is eternal and impermanent.

    ~ Lama Zopa Rinpoche

I say it is not an experience of anything in particular, but the direct seeing into the true nature of everything which is at the same time no-thing. It is a mirror-like awareness that enables the 5 senses + the mind to be cognized.. without which, there can be no consciousness of any of sensory input. Whatever is present, it just reflects.
