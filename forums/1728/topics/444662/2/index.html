    @fugazzi: buddhism is not a theology but we cannot do away with right view. Having realized or having had glimpses of non-conceptual Presence is not enough to remove all our false view of reality causing subtle grasping, clinging and confusion. So one should not overly focus on experience and overlook the importance of right view. View, realization and experience are equally important if you read my other thread. Read my reply to sinweiy below and you'll understand. @sinweiy: there is no changing self. To say there is a changing self, you are implying reality is a substance that transforms itself as the myriad things that changes. This is also non-Buddhist substantialist view, and is Thusness Stage 4 substantialist non-dualism. In other words, one sees that there is an inherently existing One Mind that transforms itself as the myriad things. This is the view of Kashmir Shaivism (Shiva or pure nondual consciousness has both an unchanging and dynamic aspect, and since the world is not other than consciousness, the world is real as Shiva) and also the three steps of Advaita Vedanta - the world is illusory, Brahman alone is real, Brahman is the World. The insight of anatta in Buddhism however is very different. This insight is elucidated by Zen Master Dogen, firewood abides in the phenomenal expression of firewood, firewood does not turn/transform into ashes. In other words, since all phenomena lack a substantial self, there is no One Mind transforming itself into the myriad things. It is not that the scent, the sight, the sound are all the expressions of One Consciousness (substantial non-dualism). Rathere, in seeing it is just sight, in hearing just sound, in smelling just the experience of scent, there is no agent or substratum that links or produces those phenomenon. Effectively, the view of inherency with regards to consciousness is seen through by realization. Through the insight of anatta, one realizes that 1) the view that a seer is seeing the seen ceases through realization that in seeing always just the seen, no seer, so the view of an agent, perceiver, controller permanently ceases, 2) the view that a self, one mind, ground that links, underlies, gives rise or transforms into the myriad things permanently ceases. So now you see why you cannot say there is a "changing self" since to say a self changes is to imply a substance or reality that transforms or changes, no different from the more advanced phase of Hindu schools. To say that there is a self that is "neither unchanging nor changes" is equally erroneous, since it implies there is a substance that can sometimes change and sometimes not change, or that there is a substance that transforms (changes) yet remains unchanged, which is basically same as the view elucidated above. Such a substantialist view is false and seen through with the insight of anatta. In the substantialist non-dual phase, it is distinct from the I AM phase. In the I AM phase, one discovers oneself as all-pervading pure presence-existence-consciousness. One sees that as one's true self and everybody's true self. But since it only pertains to non-dual luminosity in the pure non-conceptual thought realm, one is unable to see non-dual luminosity in all realms, sensations, manifestation and perception. In other words, it is just luminosity experienced as one of the dhatus among the eighteen dhatus or six sense consciousnesses - pertaining to the mind level. Because of such realization and experience plus there is view of subject-object duality and the view of an inherent self, the Pure Presence is understood both dualistically and inherently. Therefore, one sees oneself as the pure Eternal Witness or Watcher behind thoughts and sense perceptions - thoughts come and go, only the true self or Witness remains unchanged. Then when one progresses to the substantial non-dual phase, one breaks the illusion of subject-object duality such that everything is no longer experienced as apart from oneself. Rather, there is just one seamless undivided field of consciousness expressing/transforming itself into the myriad phenomena, without observer-observed, subject-object split. Effectively the view of duality is gone but the view of inherency remains since one still sees an inherent Self or Mind transforming itself as the many. This is the phase of One Mind or Everything is Mind. This is the peak of the Hindu traditions - Brahman is the World. Even though non-dual luminosity is experienced as everything and it is blissful, it still causes great attachment because one does not understand the empty nature of awareness, so there is a referencing back to a source. In anatta, there is just breathe, scent, sight, not even "One Naked Awareness", not even a mirror-awareness that is inseparable from its reflections. Just the reflections alone IS, there is no substance serving as the substance, substratum, or reflector of all phenomena. Phenomena alone is the mirror. At this point one no longer subsumes the six streams of consciousness into One Mind but sees No Mind - just the six streams of consciousness that affirms itself, since one realizes there is no inherent self. However there is a difference between the experience of No Mind and the realization of Anatta even though they are linked - more is elaborated in my other article on view, realization, experience, etc... There is no unchanging self, no changing self, no neither unchanging nor changing self, no both unchanging and changing self. No self can be pinned down inside or apart from the five aggregates as a truth, reality, or substance. Btw mindstream is good - there is no "mind" apart from stream, just the stream alone is... The continuity happens as a causal process, not the continuity of any form of self-entity.

    @sinweiy: we cannot say that just chanting/nian fo means having right view or just because you chant means you are in no danger of falling into wrong views. This is because view is not dependent on your practice. View means a position, or a belief pertaining to self or reality. For example you can meditate and chant but still believe in an unchanging or eternal self, have a self-view, so you will not attain Buddhist awakening in this way. Or some people like Lee Kuan Yew also chant Catholic mantra, but he believes that the self annihilates after death - nihilist view. The externalists like Hindus also practice mantra, however they hold strongly to the view of eternalism. The Hindus are eternalists. Therefore, nian fo does not mean having right view. If you learn and understand dharma teachings, that is right view. But having an inferred right view does not mean enlightenment yet. Having a direct realization of the twofold emptiness is enlightenment, in which right view and others of the noble eightfold path are a requisite.

    Some thoughts on what constitute right and wrong views from the below sutta.

    MN 117 - Maha-cattarisaka Sutta: The Great Forty

    ......right view is the forerunner. And how is right view the forerunner? One discerns wrong view as wrong view, and right view as right view. This is one's right view. And what is wrong view? 'There is nothing given, nothing offered, nothing sacrificed. There is no fruit or result of good or bad actions. There is no this world, no next world, no mother, no father, no spontaneously reborn beings; no priests or contemplatives who, faring rightly & practicing rightly, proclaim this world & the next after having directly known & realized it for themselves.' This is wrong view.

     

    "And what is right view? Right view, I tell you, is of two sorts: There is right view with effluents [asava], siding with merit, resulting in the acquisitions [of becoming]; and there is noble right view, without effluents, transcendent, a factor of the path.

     

    "And what is the right view that has effluents, sides with merit, & results in acquisitions?'There is what is given, what is offered, what is sacrificed. There are fruits & results of good & bad actions. There is this world & the next world. There is mother & father. There are spontaneously reborn beings; there are priests & contemplatives who, faring rightly & practicing rightly, proclaim this world & the next after having directly known & realized it for themselves.' This is the right view that has effluents, sides with merit, & results in acquisitions.

     

    "And what is the right view that is without effluents, transcendent, a factor of the path? The discernment, the faculty of discernment, the strength of discernment, analysis of qualities as a factor for Awakening, the path factor of right view of one developing the noble path whose mind is noble, whose mind is free from effluents, who is fully possessed of the noble path. This is the right view that is without effluents, transcendent, a factor of the path.

    Sutta MN 117 The Great Forty is about the Eight Fold Noble Path, i.e. The Fourth Noble Truth, which is the way that leads to the cessation of suffering. The sutta begins by stating what constituted Wrong Views, listing not believing in the efficacy of Kamma, Rebirth, and not believing in Enlightened Beings as wrong views. We can group these people as the Atheists, Nihilists, Sceptics, Determinists and some believers of certain Theistic religions.

     

    At first glance, one may be also intrigued by the term not believing in ‘nothing sacrificed’ as wrong view, which would imply the sacrifice of living beings such as animals. One can find in DN5 Kutadanta Sutta what are considered right and wrong sacrifices. Here, right sacrifices are offerings that are bloodless and does not involved the taking of lives.  

     

    On what is Right Views, there is the Mundane Right View with effluents, i.e. kamma forming that lead to better rebirth and the Right View that is Supramundane that lead one to the cessation of Suffering, the Third Noble Truth.

     

    There are many repetitions on the phase ‘Of those, right view is the forerunner’ This goes to show how important it is to start our practice and understanding of the Blessed One teachings on the right footing from the very beginning to ensure it does not lead us the wrong way, away from the path of the realization of the cessation of suffering.
