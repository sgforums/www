    Student: There is not much writing by spiritual teachers about how they deal with loss. How did you deal with the death of your wife?

    Bernie: For me the three Zen Peacemaker tenets of not-knowing, bearing witness, and loving actions are almost the answer to everything.

    It is very important that we understand what is meant by them. From the questions you sent in this week, I sense that there is still confusion about what is meant by "not-knowing," and "bearing witness." When there is loss or fear, if one can bear witness to what is happening, the terminology becomes very different; You would no longer necessarily use words like "loss." Certainly, when loss occurs, you are not bearing witness, so you talk about it as loss. Or when the fear rises, you are not bearing witness, and you deal with fear. But as one bears witness, radical changes occur.

    First, let me go back to not knowing, Someone asked how to not-know at a place like Harvard where all you're supposed to do is know. That's true about life generally. People want you to know. Even your kids want you to know. "Why is the sky blue?" They ask all kinds of questions. Everybody wants you to know. And we want ourselves to know. So we search for knowledge and wisdom.

    What do I mean by not knowing? Not knowing has nothing to do with knowledge. My sense is that one should have as much knowledge as possible. Learn as many languages as you can. Study as many fields as you can. Penetrate as deeply as you can. Learn all the tools and the techniques. Fill up your bag with as much stuff as you can. You have to know. I studied a lot when I was young, when I was middle-aged, and now I am old. I enjoy studying and I think there is huge value in more study and knowledge.

    What do I mean by not knowing? Not knowing is simply not being attached to any particular piece of knowledge. In the same way, it is also not rejecting any piece of knowledge. You hear something and say "Ah! That's ridiculous! Forget it!" If you hear something, try responding "Well, maybe that is possible also." "Oh! That's another way of looking at it." Add that to your set of knowledge; don't exclude anything. Keep adding, and don't stick to one piece of information as if that's the correct way. The state of not knowing is not being attached to any of the packages that you have.

    Let's say here at Harvard you have to write a term paper. You figure out,"That's what they like!" Therefore you write the term paper according to what you believe is going to give you the best grade, or put in favor with the teachr, and so on. That is knowing. Not knowing is to hear the subject of the paper and then to just freshly write it based on all your own understanding and experience; you don't presuppose anything.

    Where does knowing happen mostly? It happens when you are talking to somebody. You hear them talking and before they finish you know what they are saying, You reply with your answer, your rebuttal, your ideas. You are not doing deep listening, not being totally open. We will talk about a way of council, which is to work in a group in which the first premise is to listen deeply, not presupposing anything. Can you do that? No. I can't do that. Can I attempt to? Can I train? Yes.

    I cannot point to icons that have accomplished Zen Peacemaker tenets. The tenets are ways of practice, goals, principles. They are tenets of our work. If you are going to do our kind of work, we ask that you just be open in new situations. We will try to select people who have the right tools, and enough knowledge. When they are open, they will see which tool is appropriate. We can't even say that it's correct. All we can say is, "According to my understanding and experience, as open as I can be to this situation, this is what I think should be used."

    The major error we can make is to say, "This is what should be used." We need to say, "This is what I think should be used," or "This is what I am using." Another major error is to say to somebody else "You are using the wrong thing." This implies that you know what is right. Again, have you grokked what is going on? Have you paid attention? Do you feel that, having paid attention, what you're doing is the best you can do? If so greatâ€”that is the best you can do. What more would we want?

    Many times not knowing gets confused with "I don't know what to do. That is "not knowing." This is not Zen Peacemakers' not knowing.

    One student asks about situations that push people's boundaries in order to bring them to a place of not knowing. Yes there are techniques to help us to learn how to not know, how to address things freshly We will work with them in the class. Other techniques that we have developed we call "plunges;" these are not so easy to do in a class.

    Plunges don't have to be sad or heavy. The video we just saw seemed to you to be a little heavy, huh? For me there was a lot of joy there too. The man that was practicing his martial art had tremendous joy. He had some fear about his boundaries, but there was a great delight. His friend, she is a very happy-go-lucky lady/man/whatever. To be with them, is to be full of joy. At the same time, because of our own conditioning, when you look from the outside, you could feel a little sad, too. But if you are with them, if you are bearing witness, there is nothing but delight. It is a very joyful scene especially if you saw him when he first came in weighing seventy-five pounds and in a wheelchair. Everybody said he was going to die in a month and this is two years later. Fantastic!

    Bearing witness means bearing witness to everything, and it's the joys as well as the sorrows. It's nothing to do with just one side. When we're going to do social action and help people we may think that it all has to come from the heavy side. I do a lot of clown work. That is not heavy. When you get involved and learn how to do clown work with refugee kids and others, you'll find that it's a very joyful situation. (Bernie puts on a red clown's nose.)

    Bearing witness here is essential too. This is an example of bearing witness in clowning. Pretend that you are a bunch of kids, and I'm going to clown. Now, although you're kids, I have no idea what your culture is like. I can't speak your language, and I don't know what you think is funny, what you think is sad, what you think is frightening. If I blow some bubbles that burst you may be scared. I don't know beforehand. In the refugee camps the cultures were very different. Some people had never heard a tape recorder. To hear their own voice was frightening. You don't know. That is where we start. I have no idea what's going to happen. I have to bear witness to you. What's also interesting in those refugee camps is that the kids would sit up front, then the mothers a little further back, and the fathers way behind. The kids are going to get engrossed pretty quickly; it's not that hard. The mothers also will tend to get involved. Fathers are a little trickier, in some of these cultures. And I've been in cultures where parents have never seen their kids smile. So that's a big one. Can you make the kid smile, make the mother smile? And all of a sudden, the fathers are moving up a little.

    But what's bearing witness? I have no idea what's going on. I have no idea what scares, what makes you happy. My red nose might make you scared. So I come on and I have to bear witness. I have to really feel the group. Are you afraid of me? Do I need to back away? Do I need to do this? Can I come closer? What speed? (Bernie makes a sudden movement and startling sound.) Does that make you laugh? Does it make you frightened? I need to become you, get rid of the separation, the subject-object separation. That's bearing witness. So, the way I look at life..you know a live oak tree? They always have their leaves, but the leaves are always dropping. When I say we're going to realize the oneness of life, we could look at the live oak tree as a metaphor for the universe. If I look closely, at any time, there are leaves falling.     Bearing witness has nothing to do with testimony. Technically I always say it's where there is no subject-object differentiation. I seem very technical, but it is explained by what I was trying to do as Bernie the Boobisattva. I tried to merge into the room so that I could feel everything going on there and at the same time I'm able to do things. It's not an easy state to experience, except when you experience it without quite knowing what's happened. That happens all the time. You're constantly bearing witness to yourself, and then every once and a while, you're stepping out of that, and remarking on it. Your stomach starts to hurt. You can remark on your stomach. Normally, it's functioning and acting in a way that you're just bearing witness to. If you're involved in vipassana meditations you learn techniques to observe what's going on. Zen tends to put more emphasis on just being what's happening, on trying to get into a state of just being where you don't observe. So when you observe, we call that a problem. Or, I shouldn't say a problem, it's a technique. You can have lots of observation techniques, and they're good, but the state of bearing witness is different.

    There's a technical term, a type of meditation that's used in Japan that's called "shikantaza." Literally this means"just sitting." "Shikan" means "just," "only." "Za" means "sitting." So you've probably heard of words like "zafu," a sitting pillow, "zabuton," a sitting mat. "Ta" is a grammatical term in Japanese that is not translated, but adds emphasis to the word. If you were to translate "shikantaza" as just sitting, you lose the "ta." If you translate shikantaza as "Just Sitting!" (Bernie shouts this) you gain the "ta." That's what the "ta" does if you're a Japanese person. If you're not, the word is a little flat. When I was looking at how to translate "just sitting," it means no subject-object differentiation. It's a very difficult practice, and we teach people how to do it, but it can take twenty years to really do it in a good way. But, still, you could start doing it today, and you learn, and somebody can help you to improve it along the way. So it's a practice. At one point I was trying to translate that word and I came up with the word "bearing witness" as having a certain amount of energy in our English language that I could never get by "just sitting." But I also knew that "bearing witness" has other meanings within the Christian language and a lot of it around testimony, and testifying. But, you know the root of testify, or testimony. Nobody knows the root of that word? So it's a whole different feeling than the bearing witness. It goes back to the Greek days when they were reporting on a legal matter and held their testicles as the seed of life. So that's what that's about.

    When we say "bearing witness" it's not about that, it's about getting rid of subject-object relationship. Therefore, it has nothing to do with a particular sad, joy, fearful, happy feeling.
