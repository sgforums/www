    In Buddhism, there are three traditions of Buddhism: Theravada (Thai, Sri Lanka, Burmese traditions), Mahayana (Chinese) and Vajrayana (Tibetan). Monks from the Theravada and Vajrayana traditions are allowed to eat meat, however Theravada monks only receive food by alms giving and not by buying food from coffeeshops. Mahayana monks are vegetarians due to Bodhisattva practices. Tibetan/Vajrayana monks are allowed to eat meat in their culture because Tibet is a very remote, mountainous region which is not suitable for vegetation.

    Even though Buddhists whether from Theravada, Mahayana or Vajrayana traditions are allowed to eat meat, we only eat meat under 5 conditions:

    http://www.jenchen.org.sg/vol5no4a.htm

    ...Buddha understands the ignorance and habits of sentient beings that have accumulated from aeons of their many past lives. In order to provide sentient beings with an expedient means of embracing Buddhism, those who are not able to convert to a vegetarian diet on a long term basis are permitted to consume meat provided:

    (1) That he did not slaughter the animal personally
    (2) That he did not instruct others to slaughter
    (3) That the slaughter was not committed for his sake
    (4) That he did not witness the slaughter
    (5) That he did not hear the cries associated with the slaughter...
    Under these conditions, the meat is considered 'karmically purer'... because it is not due to direct killing, no direct link with you, the karma is generally lighter. Of course vegetarianism is still recommended. However personally I am not a vegetarian yet. I am from the Mahayana tradition.

    In a sense, yes.

    The difference is that Buddhists do not believe in a Creator, and do not believe anyone including the Buddha can directly interfere with someone's karma.. and neither can the Buddha grant people enlightenment, it has to be achieved through the practitioner himself through direct insight and experience of the ultimate reality, that comes from practise. The Buddha can only point the way to the moon.. we have to go there ourselves.

    btw, since buddha is perfect, then does tat mean tat no one in the past, present n future will ever join him as a perfect being? in other words, there will be no one ever who can be exactly like buddha?
    Every single person will eventually attain Buddhahood and our Buddha Nature is already wholly complete, only that we have yet to realise it. It is like a clear bright mirror which is obscured due to a layer of dust - of mental defilements. Practising Buddhism is like using a cloth and wipe the mirror of the dust to reveal the clear shining mirror beneath... our Buddha Nature.

    Shakyamuni Buddha is not the first Buddha, and neither will he be the last. The next Buddha to appear will be Maitreya Bodhisattva, who is currently still waiting, residing in Tush1ta Heaven. However even before the next Buddha, there are uncountable other enlightened beings who have lived/are living in this planet. For example during the Buddha's period alone there are thousands of Arhants. Arhats are those who realised the ultimate truth and attained Liberation from samsara and all sufferings of life and Samsara, and the cycle of rebirth, and enters the highest bliss Nirvana. Even today, there are many many enlightened people... and they are not just the Buddhist masters, dharma teachers and so on.. they are ordinary practitioners of the Buddhadharma. Even in my forum, there are also several forummers who post here who have attained enlightenment. This is something that is definitely achievable, and the aim of every Buddhist is of course to attain Enlightenment. That is why we practise Buddhism.

    The difference between an Arhat and a Buddha is that an Arhat only strived for his own liberation, and therefore after attaining Arhatship he just rests in his Nirvanic bliss. A Bodhisattva practitioner (famous examples are like Guan Yin) who have awakened to the Ultimate Truth but does not simply rest in Nirvana, has great compassion and great vows to help and save other sentient beings and practise the Bodhisattva way that eventually leads to Buddhahood. This is of course a much longer and harder task than to simply attain self liberation as an Arhat, but is the way leading to perfect Buddhahood. There are many Bodhisattvas living today as well.
