    Text:
    The eight sufferings are: birth, old age, sickness, death, the suffering of being separated from objects of love, the suffering of encountering objects of hate, the suffering of not getting what one seeks, and the suffering of the raging blaze of the five skandhas.

    Commentary:
    The eight sufferings are:
    Birth: We are not aware of how painful the birth process is. Actually when a person is born, he feels as much pain as a cow being flayed alive, or as a live turtle getting its shell pulled off. When a baby is born, the first thing it does is cry, "Ku, ku, ku!" [In Chinese, the word ku for 'crying' ( ? ) sounds similar to the word ku for 'suffering' ( ? ).] So children experience suffering when they are first born. But after a few days, they forget about the suffering and mistake it to be happiness. So children will also smile and laugh. They think it's pretty nice, that there's no more suffering. But as they get older--passing from youth into the prime of life, and then onto old age--they experience suffering again. When they are old, they don't have the strength to chew their food or to walk, and everything becomes a hassle. That's the suffering of old age. Sickness is also a kind of suffering. When one is sick, everything one eats loses its flavor. But there is no suffering greater than that of death. The Buddha observed these four sufferings and it prompted him to leave the home-life and cultivate. There are four additional kinds of suffering: the suffering of being separated from objects of love. When you are separated from the person you love most dearly, that is this kind of suffering. When you lose your most treasured possession, you also experience this kind of suffering. If someone sets fire to your house, which you love to live in, you have this suffering. If someone steals your favorite outfit, you also have this suffering. In general, this suffering is experienced when something or someone that you are very attached to and cannot let go of is taken away from you. The suffering of encountering objects of hate. There may be someone you absolutely detest, so you decide to go somewhere else to get away from the person. But when you go to another place, you meet the same kind of person there. You hate him and loathe him, but as things turn out, you keep running into him. The suffering of not getting what one seeks. There is something you really wish to have, but you just can't obtain it. That's also a kind of suffering. The suffering of the raging blaze of the five skandhas. The five skandhas are form, feeling, thought, formations, and consciousness. They are like a roaring fire, and they also constitute a kind of suffering, although no one considers it to be suffering. Once you awaken to this kind of suffering, you'll know that it's worse than anything.

    Actually the title of the topic already gives us the perspective, when is it that we need to forgive, and when there is'nt a need to forgive. It is when we are attached to the issue at hand on a personal level, that we have attraction and aversion, what is is pleasant and what that is not to us, that arouses us us to reaction. For it is only when we or "I" is offended, it is then that there is a need to forgive, a need to let go of our attachment to the issue. The way we react strongly to an issue, is a result from, or can also form a basis to reactionary tendencies to future adversarial encounters. Thus forms a vicious cycle, often with us being unaware of it's cause, as we are often clouded by our emotions. The basis of Buddhism is the cultivation of "no self ", as it is the reaction in reference to self that arises the cloud of emotion, attraction to pleasantness and aversion to unpleasantness. We forgive because there is clinging and a need to forgive, and if you are not being personally attached, is there still a need to forgive? We practice loving kindness and compassion to allow us to empathize, to see the suffering of our fellow sentient beings so that we do not form prejudiced thoughts, behaviour and reaction from forming within us. When we are able to empathize, we no longer regard our subject of attention as something separate and opposite us, that the self in us slowly dissolves. Hell! it is not easy, if it were that easy, most of us will not be here right now, going round and round. One way, is to get ourselves ordained and leave the environment that reinforces the "self" view and work on our residual tendencies. The other is to practise bodhicitta, by taking the bodhisatta vows. As long as we are understand our wisdom and meaningful abilities(skillful means) are not fully developed, we work with and on what is in front of us now. But being compassionate does'nt means condone another wrong doings, it means seeking the most amiable solutions to the issue. This is where solutions differed due our ignorance or lack of off. The clearer our minds are, the wider our vision and probabilities of dealing are. Being right or thinking we are right and others wrong also can sometimes leads us to self righteousness, it may be pride, it may be jealousy, desire, ignorance, miserliness or anger the lead us to this line of thinking, leading to impose suffering on others. Constant vigilance is needed, both outward and inward.

    Change is inevitable, has always been. Forgiveness, in this instance, is not for benefit for the perpetuator of injustice, but for the victim. The bigger the affliction, the deeper the scarring, hence the need to let go of what's passed. For example, if you have lost someone close to you, whether the perpetuator is punished or is not punished, if you cannot accept the loss, you will still suffer the loss, it is inevitable. However, that does'nt mean the wrong doer will go scot free, he will have to be accountable and bear responsibility for his negative deeds. We often assume that the recourse of justice is always available to us, because we live in a society with relatively higher levels of awareness on equality and human dignity. This not always applicable. Buddhist are often accused of being passive, but that is not true. Doing what's right in life can be seen as courage of two kinds, the active and the passive. The active comes in doing what right and not what's getting things going for the worse and getting people hurt or killed. The passive one is about keeping peace and enduring when conditions for changes for the better are not forthcoming. Not being emotive allows us to be more objective when confronted with issues. Raising awareness in a calm, dignified, a non aggressive and non confrontational manner appeals more to a typical Buddhist, but I am just generalising. Your interest lies mainly in the perceived hypocrisy certain parties and wishes to arouse a reaction. There was story of an monk on seeing the mother with her wilful child, in which he exclaimed" punish your son, punish your son", which the mother did not. Later on the monk met the mother again, this time at the son's execution. Then the monk told the mother, that were she was able to heed his advice and disciplined the son, the son would not have met his fate.
