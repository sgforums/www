    To you and all forummers... First of all the dharma has to be spoken on the right occasion to the right people. For example what you said may not make sense to newcomer, but only to people who already had some ideas of what Buddhism is about... and your message may even be the right remedy for someone who is attached to teachings, words, concepts, and so on. Dharmas are always spoken according to conditions, as a pointer, not to be clung to as being some sort of absolute truth... which is why it is said 'there is no fixed dharmas, fixed dharma are not dharmas'. Even 'present moment' or 'This' is equally misleading it if it clung to as the absolute. The Buddha said in the Diamond Sutra, The mind of the past is ungraspable; the mind of the future is ungraspable; the mind of the present is ungraspable.

    For someone who is new to Buddhism and has an interest to learn about Buddhism, it might be better instead to talk about the fundamental views and practises of Buddhism, like the 4 noble truths, the eightfold path, the 4 seals/3 dharma seals, and to have the right understanding of Emptiness, etc... and even Emptiness teachings is just a pointer, being itself Empty, therefore the 'Viewless View is the highest View'. Emptiness is not to be grasped as an 'Absolute' but merely points out that the 4 extremes of erroneous views that sentient beings cherish are illusory -- i.e they grasp on 'entities' by grasping on the ideas that they have inherent existence, non existence, both and neither.. or other extreme erroneous concepts like eternalism or nihilism.. which is why Buddha taught about Interdependent Origination... in which everything is other-dependent, having no substance by itself, interwoven as a seamless whole.

    Learning and practising Buddhism is very important and if one practises as the Buddha taught it leads directly to insight/enlightenment. There is no need to even 'search' for truth, one can 'just follow moronically simple instructions and have it all happen' as Dharma Dan said. (more on that later)

    Of course all the Buddhist teachings needs to be experienced in the 'present moment'.. through insights.. every word that Buddha said points to the truth. But even for a beginner, it would be helpful to at least understand these teachings conceptually so that their long cherished, dualistic ideas can fall apart. Without knowing about Buddhism's Middle Way (Free from extremes and yet not even resting on a 'Middle'), which is Emptiness free from erroneous dualistic extremes/conceptual views like eternalism or nihilism, existence or non-existence, one may grasp on sensory objects and the world, thinking that they have independent, inherent existence, and think of 'themselves' as seperate from all 'others'. Furthermore having right knowledge of Buddha's teachings is important even for a beginner so that when he experiences Presence, there will not be further confusion. Thusness said,

    In Buddhism what that is most important is the no-mirror reflecting (stage 5). That is entry towards pathless and effortless. Without it, all rest is the bond at work and continual creation of wholesome actions to counter unwholesome action.

    Many non-dual fail to realize the deep latent propensities in our consciousness. Not knowing that the progressive stages that one undergone are nothing but mere working of these tendencies. This is a pity. Failure to realize this, one fails to see how consciousness works.

    Constant identification itself will create this pattern that sinks deep into consciousness. Take note. No one can escape, not even the Blessed One. There always has been no-self, there is only One Reality but the propensities over countless lifetimes will continue to take place through mere momentum. This is what we are all facing.
    Knowing this, right and firm establishment of view of the dharma seals is most crucial. Through diligent practice of mindfulness and together with the right views from start will help to prevent unnecessary pitfalls. Any transcendental experiences and insights that occurred during our journey will then be correctly understood. The experiences will in turn re-enforce our understanding instead of misleading us into the sense of 'Self' and further strengthen the bond.
    But do bear in mind that all the teachings that the Buddha taught can be experienced in this moment.. even the teachings of Emptiness and Interdependent Origination and non-locality (would be 'stage 6'), 4 Seals/3 Dharma Seals, 4 Noble Truths, etc can be seen (as an intuitive insight, direct experience).

    Next.. apart from the training in morality, mental qualities/virtues which are also very important, the 'mind training' part of Buddhism which is essential for Enlightenment/Liberation can be categorised to two types... shamatha and vipashyana/vipassana.. shamatha being able to develope certain mental qualities, calmness, jhanas, and even psychic powers... and serves as a foundation for insight practise. Insight/Vipashyana/Vipassana practise is also deeply grounded on the Buddhist teachings.. (Buddhism is the only religion that teaches non-dualistic Insight practises, other religions' practises are mostly on concentration/shamatha, and self-inquiry is practised to lead one to experience witnessing presence) as a matter of fact the Buddha himself gave a lot of various instructions on various meditational practises and these practises are extremely successful as thousands of Buddha's students attained complete liberation as Arhats during Buddha's lifetime alone. When Ananda, one of Buddha's chief disciple (at that point in time he was not yet an Arhat) went around and asked the Arhats how they attained liberation, all testified that they attained liberation through the combinations of practises of Shamatha and Vipashyana. As Thusness said.. If you can't understand and see all of the teachings taking place right Now, then it is not true knowledge. Therefore this means that in Vipashayana/Insight practise, the teachings of the Buddha can be seen moment to moment.. for instance in Vipashyana, one may focus on the characteristics of existence, i.e the three dharma characteristics. Strong mindfulness and investigation penetrates the true nature of the sensate reality and the nature of impermanence, no-self/no-duality, suffering are directly experienced.. not merely as a form of knowledge. So the main message is not just learn about the teachings, but also do the practises.

    In insight practise, reality is seen to be non dualistic (no self aspect) and self-liberating (impermanence aspect), therefore Vipashyana can be known as the effortless/choiceless non-dualistic 'practise' (hopefully that sounds neo-advaita enough )... where not even a letting go is necessary because reality 'arises' itself, 'let goes' itself. Far from the 'dualistic practises' that the neo-advaitas always criticise (that's ok since Insight practise is found solely in Buddhism and meditational practises elsewhere belongs to the Shamatha category and are indeed, dualistic practises, used as a support for gathering strength and calmness but does not lead to insights and liberation), Vipashyana practises are not ritualistic practise but Insight Practise -- where strong investigative awareness leads to insights on reality away from the ritualistic conditioned mind which J Krishnamurti always talk about. Being non-dual in nature where no dualistic relationship is assumed, Vipashyana isn't something that 'someone' does to become 'enlightened', it does not lead to union of subject and object (union can only happen when dualistic relationship is assumed in the first place), it does not lead anywhere... only insights into the illusoriness of 'self', 'duality', 'permanence' and the reality of luminous-emptiness are gained. To say that 'someone becomes enlightened' is true on the relative level, on the ultimate level it is not seen to be so.
