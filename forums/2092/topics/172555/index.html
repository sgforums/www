    Ill tell you why big markets are good.....whether u are talking about stock market, or meat market at my local wet market, or housing market, or online market like Ebay, same thing....

    Big markets are good because that means there are many buyers and sellers. If you buy, there is likely a seller willing to match your price. If you sell, there is likely a buyer willing to match the price. You can get in and out quickly.

    Small markets have less liquidity, meaning less money and fewer buyers or sellers.

    When you trade in small markets, tipically the buy/sell price difference is big. It's because when u want to buy, nobody is there willing to match your price, so you increase your buy price until you meet a willing seller. Conversely with selling too. The buy/sell price spread is therefore bigger than in more liquid markets.

    Big liquid market like US is therefore much better for tighter spreads and faster transaction.

    Secondly, small markets tend to be more volatile. Because the market is small, a big seller or a big buyer can shake the price balance suddenly. Big markets are better and more 'sturdy' and less volatile.

    Thirdly, Big markets have more information and resources. It makes your life easier.

    Hello....Im 'Li Ka Shing' above....

    When you look at an entity, such as a stock for example, and you think it's going to go up, you buy this entity with the hope when it goes up later, you sell at a higher price and get a profit.

    Just common logic, right?

    This is called 'long' . Buying low with the hope of selling higher later.

    But an entity can go down too. When you think it's going downwards, you can still get a profit by doing the opposite of long, which is short.

    Short selling means you borrow a stock (or whatever, a stock is the example here) and you sell it at the market price. When the market price drops, you buy it back, and return the stock to your lender. The difference of higher sell price and lower buy-back price is your profit.

    It's the same like the 'long' principle of buy low sell high. Except it's reversed in order, it becomes sell high buy low.

    Basically it's for participating in market downward movement.

    Dont worry there's no stupid questions in this world.

    The factors which will affect the price...supply and demand, but if we talk more specifically about the general stock market, that would be the general economic and political conditions where that stock market is in.

    That would be, for economic conditions......GDP growth, inflation rate, central bank interest rate, average earnings growth, and some more minor datas like unemployment data, housing data, consumer spending data, etc.

    Also price of oil. Important nowaways.

    For political conditions....basically whether it's politically condusive and stable. For example when a new president comes in, and during campaign he already promised some measures the market like, the stock market will go up, in anticipation of more condusive environment. That's an example of a politically motivated price movement.

    A politically motivated price movement can be caused by election results, scandals, etc.

    Another important mover is 'unexpected events' such as disasters or terrorist attacks.

    If you talk about specific stock/ company, apart from the general conditions above which also apply to them, additional factors include: news, earnings growth, new product launches, FDA approvals, mergers and acquisitions, analysts ratings (downgrade/ upgrade), etc.

    But....how to keep up with all these, right? So many of them.

    That's why people say the market is already pricing in everything that it knows currently as well as what it expects to happen in the future.

    So to know how the market price will behave, we need to study charts.

    By studying charts, we are studying what the market thinks. We are free riding on other people who study more detailed datas like above.

    It's nice to be a free rider, isn't it?

    Studying charts basically mean drawing graphs and predicting the future based on the chart. That's usually for the near and medium term.

    A stock price reflects a company's valuation. And it is affected by its specific factors as well as the overall economic, and political condition, where that stock is in.

    Therefore a stock price is affected by the price of oil, inflation rate, GDP growth, average earnings growth, central bank interest rate, etc, as these are indicative of the overall economy.

    How closely a specific stock follow the general move of the broader market as affected by these factors, depends on the nature of each company. Some companies are more sensitive, while others are less sensitive. This difference could be caused by its size, or its sector(s) where it's in.

    If you want to interpret the datas..... = means same move, >< means opposite.

    GDP growth =
    Inflation rate ><
    Central bank interest rate ><
    Average earnings growth =
    Oil price ><
    Some more minor datas:
    Unemployment ><
    Consumer spending =
    Housing data =
    Etc...

    Example, up in GDP growth, means better overall economic conditions, the stock market goes up as well. Up in inflation rate, means worse overall economic conditions, the stock market goes down (opposite).

    When all these datas come up, you are confused how to interpret them as a whole. Like if it's 3 up, 3 down, so what does that mean?

    Even the best economists miss their forecasts. We don't try to be an economist. We look at the overall market price. The overall market price has already priced in everything it knows including the overall economic and political conditions.

    So like I said, we just be a free rider. Just look at the INDEX.

    The index reflects the overall market price, which means it reflects the market digestion of overall economic and political condition as a whole.

    Since a specific stock is always affected by its specific factors as well as general economic and political factors, the index will always affect a specific stock price. How much so, look at the Beta (correlation of the 2). If you want to break it down, like oil price, also find the Beta for that component.

    Your second question. If people sell, price drop of course. If it drops from $1 to $0.10 like you said, you don't need to pay for any maintenance and it's safe, but theoretically you got a massive loss. This should worry you more than any maintenance. Such massive lost will not happen in real life though, since you have risk management like placing stop-loss.

    Your third question. You can spread rumours of course and if the stock is cheap and small, your rumour can move the stock, and you can con them all. However if the stock is big, your rumour will have very little impact.
