    Here's a summary of the changes introduced by Mah Bow Tan in Parliament on 19 July 2005:

    Housing Loans
    Banks may grant housing loans up to 90% of property value, up from the previous limit of 80%, with immediate effect. This applies to both private residential properties and HDB flats
    Cash Downpayment for Private homes halved frm 10% to 5%, with immediate effect.
    Cash downpayment for HDB flats bought with bank loans reduced to 5% from jan 1, 2006, down from the orignally slated 6%. It was supposed to rise gradually to 10% in 2008. THose buying flats with loans from HDB will still be allowed to make the full 10% downpayment with CPF savings as before.
    MAS require banks to hold more capital against housing loans which exceed 80% of property value. But it may consider mortgage insurance for banks as an alternative.

    CPF changes
    CPF Savings can be used to buy private homes with remaining leases of 30 to 59 years with immediate effect. Previously, only private homes with remining leases of at least 60 years could be bought using CPF savings.
    Non-related singles allowed to use their CPF savings to jointly buy private homes with immediate effect, in sync with prevailing rule for HDB flat.
    Available Housing Withdrawal Limit simplified, wit hthe limit now set only to the available Ordinary Account balance after leaving aside the Minium Sum cash component. Governmetn's plans to reduce CPF withdrawal limit for housing expenditure to 120% of the property valuation by 2008 remains unchanged.
    Medisave Account overflows will now be transfered into the Special Account for members aged below 55 and into the Retirement Account for those 55 and above, instead of into the Ordinary Account. The effective date is set for July 1 2006.
    Only CPF savings in the Ordinary Account in excess of the Minimum Sum cash component can be used for the purchase of second and subsequent properties.
    No new applications in the Ordinary Account in excess of the Minimum Sum cash componet can be used for the purchase of second and subsequent properties.
    No new applications to use CPF savings to fund purchases of non-residential properties will be allowed from July 1, 2006

    Foreign Property Ownership
    Under a new option to the EDB's Global Investor Programme, a foreigner can be considered for permanent resident if he invests at least $2 million in business set-ups, other investment vehicles such as venture capital funds, foundations or trusts, and/or private residential properties. Up to 50 per cent of the investment can be in private residential properties.
    Foreigners can buy apartments in non-condo developments of less than six levels without the need to obtain prior approval. The new rules will take immediate effect. for landed properties, prior approval is still needed.
    Government revokes, with immediate effect, the exemption status of 43 companies currently exempted from appling for a Qualifying Certificate when they buy residential land for development. To lower costs, the amount of a banker's guarantee that developers need to furnish will be reduced from 50% to 10% of the land price. The period allowed for completing developments is also xtended from the current 3 to 4 years to 6 years.

    source: Business Times 20/7/2005

    UOB UNVEILS '90%' HOME LOAN
    ---------------------------
    Joins OCBC, DBS in offering new packages since changes in property rules

    Val Chua
    val@newstoday.com.sg

    United Overseas Bank (UOB) has become the third bank in Singapore to come
    out with higher interest rate packages for those seeking housing loans of
    up to 90 per cent of the value of their homes.

    Its two new packages - for fixed and floating rates - follow similar moves
    by rivals OCBC Bank and DBS in the last few days, after the Government
    relaxed home financing rules on Tuesday.

    The new rates for UOB are one-third higher than those for loans of up to
    80 per cent, the previous ceiling for home buyers.

    Under its fixed rate package, UOB is charging 3.5 per cent for the first
    year, and 4.5 per cent for the second and third years, a total of 12.5 per
    cent. Under its 80-per-cent package, the interest rates for the first
    three years amount to 9.5 per cent .

    For its floating rate package, the bank is charging 3, 4 and 4.5 per cent
    for the first, second and third year, respectively. This works out to 11.5
    per cent for the first three years, compared to 8.5 per cent under the
    80-per-cent package.

    The same higher rates apply for HDB flat buyers who are seeking 90 per
    cent loans.

    The difference: The minimum loan amount is $200,000 for private property
    buyers, but $100,000 for those buying public flats.

    "The new packages are introduced to reflect the higher capital
    requirement, and are part of our strategy to provide total home solutions
    to home buyers," said UOB Loans Division first vice-president and head
    Kevin Lam.

    When contacted, foreign banks such as Maybank, Citibank, Standard
    Chartered and HSBC said that they were "reviewing" their home loan rates.

    Hong Leong Finance had said that it was maintaining all its existing loan
    rates for private and HDB properties.

    A spokeswoman for ABN Amro also said said that its existing rates apply to
    those who need 90-per-cent financing.
