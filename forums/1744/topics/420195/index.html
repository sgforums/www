    How to ask for a pay rise

     

    You’ve been in your current job for a while now, and you feel it’s going well: your performance is good; your boss seems to value your contribution to the department. But no pay rise is in sight and you’re beginning to feel undervalued and underpaid.

    This happens to all of us at some point in our careers. Management rarely comes running with substantial pay rises, so if you feel you deserve more, you’re going to have to ask for it. 
    To help you broach this difficult subject, try following our useful tips:

    1. When should you say something?
    Many companies have an annual appraisal system, but by that time management has usually fixed the budget for pay rises and promotions. If you leave negotiations until then, it will probably be too late.

    2. Find out your market value
    There’s no point asking for an unrealistic pay rise – you need to know what the going rate is for your job, preferably both within and outside your company. Do some research, which will either show that you are paid fairly anyway, or will provide useful facts for negotiations.

    3. Make some notes
    You need a good reason to get a significant rise in salary. Think about it carefully before you speak to anyone; make some notes for yourself about why you deserve more money. Consider the following points:
    • When did you last get a pay rise?
    • Have your job responsibilities changed since then?
    • Has your performance radically improved?
    • Have your personal circumstances changed?
    • What would you really do if you didn’t get a pay rise?
    • How valuable do you think you are to the company?
    • Could you take on more responsibility in your job?
    • What sort of promotion could you imagine?
    • Is the company in a position to offer pay rises at the moment?

    4. Talk to your boss
    A face-to-face meeting is the only way to conduct the discussion about your salary. Ask your boss or send an e-mail requesting a meeting, but don’t say what it’s for – simply ask if they have time to discuss a “personal matter”. Whatever you do, don’t try to have the discussion about your salary in a letter or e-mail: you don’t want your boss to adopt a defensive position before you have even presented your case.

    5. Wear something that gives you confidence
    For the meeting with your boss, wear clothes in which you feel attractive, comfortable, positive and successful. You want to show you’re someone who would be an asset to any company. Choosecolours that are smart and attractive, and which radiate confidence.

    6. If you want more, you have to do more
    Obviously, salaries are linked to performance. Pay rises are given in return for achieved objectives or increased contribution to the company. But you can also take this opportunity to ask your boss for more work or responsibility in the coming year, and link it to a pay rise to happen in the future. This is called an “unpaid promotion”. Explain that you would like to discuss how to improve your performance to enable this future promotion and pay improvement. Management will respond well to this approach: it shows your willingness to develop your role in the company – and they won’t feel threatened.

    7. Think positive 
    Don’t forget: you are asking the company for something, even if you feel you have been ignored for too long. Try to present your case without getting over-excited, and listen to what your boss has to say: if they say no, you are entitled to ask them to explain their decision. Remain objective, rational, positive and constructive throughout the discussion – and, above all, don’t threaten to resign unless you really mean it: no one is irreplaceable!
