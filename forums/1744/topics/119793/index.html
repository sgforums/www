    Ever wonder why is it easier to remember faces than names?

    When we see a face, we try to recognise it - establish whether or not it looks familiar - whereas when we see someone we do recognise and try to think of their name, we have to recall it.

    Recall is generally more difficult than recognition because in order to reach the part of the brain where names are stored, we must process other information about how we know that person, such as, "She's an actress, I saw her on TV last week."

    These extra steps are why we often find ourselves struggling to remember what someone is called, even though we do know who they are.

    What causes travel-sickness, and why doesn't everyone get it?

    Fluid in the canals of the inner ear sloshes around when you're in motion, causing the tiny hair cells to send contradictory nerve impluses to the brain.

    If you are reading or looking at the inside of the car, your eyes signal to your brain that you are not moving, whereas the inner ear tells it that you are. The disorientation can make you feel nauseous. Switching your vision between close and distant objects can help to prevent this feeling, which is why drivers are less often travel-sick than passengers.

    Young People are more susceptible because as you get older, or becom a more sesoned traveller, the inner ear becomes less sensitive.

    Why can't you tickle yourself?

    The human brain can tell which sensations are caused by our own actions, and gives them low priority. That way, it can be more receptive to stimuli from outside sources that may be more urgent, such as the pain of buring your hand, or the sound of a crying child.

    Being tickled by someone else can reduce you to a squirming jelly, but when you try to tickle yourself a message called the corollary discharge is sent around the brain, which anticipates your actions and dulls the sensation.

    A recent study using magnetic resonance imaging scanner to map brain activity, suggests that the cerebellum - the part of the brain that controls movement - is involved in processing the corollary discharge.

    How come we don't feel the pain immediately when hit by serious injury?

    Under conditions of extreme stress, such as serious injury, brain signals tell the body to produce endorphins and enkephalins, similar in chemical structure to morphine. The speed and shock of an attack can trigger production of enough of these chimicals to delay the pain.
