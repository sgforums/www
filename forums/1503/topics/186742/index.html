    Hmm how do I explain this?

    Deviations to the form usually means [b]tweaking the standard requirements     to enhance your message.

    For instance, the sonnet. Typically, it has the most rigid structure -- abab, cdcd, efef, gg (that's Shakespearean, I think) in iambic meter -- and if you read Bacon and Shakespeare, the trend is to make each verse complete on its own.

    Very seldom you see the use of enjambement (run-on lines) in sonnets. However, poets like Sophie Hannah use enjambement to great effect -- and still keeps very strictly to the rhyme scheme.

    As for the haiku question -- are you sure the writers weren't writing senryu instead?

    Because haiku is always 5,7,5 -- as it is a style originating from Japan, and the rhythm created from the 5,7,5 structure is the reason why haikus have a beautiful tone and cadence.

    Originally posted by Hyperion13:
    I got one question. If I've written a few poems, does that make me a poet?
    Well... this question is in the same vein of "I've a camera, I've taken photograhs, so I'm a photographer" as well as "I've written stuff, so that makes me a writer".

    In my experience, writing poems does not a poet make. I've written essays and stories and articles for almost ten years, and it is only in recent years that I could honestly say I'm a writer, and I write for love and for survival.

    When one claims to be a writer/photographer/poet, others will naturally assume that you have the necessary skill sets and toolkits to qualify as one. That includes a knowledge of forms, styles, print requirements, editing skills, critical review that is beyond pedestrian.

    If you think you have the above, feel free to call yourself a poet. But before you do this, ask yourself the following:

    1. Can my work withstand critical scrutiny? Is it worthy of analysis?

    2. Have you made clear your emotions, your reactions in the piece? Is it clearly communicated to the target audience? A poet can be indifferent, but clarity of expression is key.

    3. Why did I choose a particular word in the poem? Why have I chosen that voice? Where is its significance? Why are you moved to record to scene and structure the resulting work as such?

    4. Does my work have depth? Does it offer room for interpretation, however vastly different from your own? Is there an exchange between the poet and reader?

    5. Will I have the courage to revise, draft after draft, a written piece that doesn't work?

    6. Most importantly -- do you love the sound of words tumbling from your tongue, the tinkling rhythms created as a result? Can you rip apart your feelings into base factors, reconstruct them into a unique reality that no other can emulate? Could you then use the sparest words to encapsulate the crux of your experience?

    Wordsworth once said, "Poetry is the spontaneous overflow of powerful feelings: it takes its origin from emotion recollected in tranquility." I'll go on to say that a poet derives his inspiration from the anguish and exuberance of living, the examination of the human condition.

    Writing is a skill driven by motivation, and refined by critical thought. A poet's job is more excruciating by far, because he distill thoughts and emotions into an eloquent frame supported only by words so precise in their arrangement, that any substitute will be for the poorer.

    I guess, at the end of this tedious exposition, my point is -- so long as you can justify your position and stance upon your poem is written, so long as others can glean visceral value from your words, your clarity and ambuguity in tandem, feel free to call yourself a poet.

    Wait, you wouldn't have to. Others will certainly refer to you as one.
