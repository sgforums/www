    The umbrella is missing. I knew it.

    The rain hit the window like a velvet-gloved burglar trying to rattle the panes. I turned to see the grey, sleety sky greet me with the face of a thousand lazy December evenings, when the wind would howl softly and the maze of cars below would blinker in and out of existence.

    Sam said that they reminded her of candles in the wind, and chuckled as she gently yanked my shoulder back. She placed her chin on her fists as she watched me kill a thousand souls across the world. I muttered a few choice words at my teammate as he died from incompetency. I didn't think that Mexicans could understand the lazy Hokkien spoken by a Singaporean across the Pacific Ocean.

    No matter. I pulled off the headphones, and ruffled Sam's hair. I asked her if she wanted some coffee, and she nodded. While making the coffee, I gazed at her back, and saw the curves of her shoulder in the sudden sunset's shadow cast on the timber floor.

    I could say that I was deeply in love. Not just with her, but with life itself.

    The cars had stopped blinking below.

    I looked for the keys and we went down to the carpark, and to my horror, our car was blazing like a supernova. I must have forgotten to turn them off when I came back from getting the coffee beans.

    Damn!

    It is raining and I am driving when the song came on the radio. It is my fault for tuning into the wrong FM, the one that played hits of days long past.

    The rain is getting heavier and I flick the windscreen wiper a notch up. I can barely see through the slate of water that came pouring down. It looks misty outside, or is it inside?

    I hear the lyrics tell of a story of a love that had changed - inexplicably - another cliched love song, dedicated to yet another broken heart. I can hear him through my thoughts, telling me that the crack in the window frame would take longer to seal because the glue takes longer to dry up - weather like this and luck like ours doesn't go together.

    Static interrupted my reverie. He tells me sad songs are not meant for rainy days.

    It doesn't matter - I tell him - sooner or later, those sunny days will come.

    Nice! The way you wrote it makes it sound very real, like a dialogue someone really would have with himself, and I think that's really well done.

    'The umbrella is missing. I knew it.' '...after he left this afternoon, it was gone'. Maybe it should be in present tense. Or, since the speaker wasn't there when he left, it should be something like, 'he left only this afternoon, and now it's gone.'

    I think it would have more impact if you'd added more prejudice here, not in a nasty way, but in a more I-knew-it way. Like, 'It's my own fault, I knew a foreign worker would be coming, but still I didn't look after my own things.', or something like that?

    'He will surely put it back after using it.' Would it have worked better if you'd written something less sure? Because the speaker is prejudiced, after all, right? Or perhaps it might work if the speaker gave the person the benefit of the doubt, not because he had faith in the foreign worker, but because that would make him feel better about himself?

    It's quite a good ending, with a nice twist! I think it's a little too matter-of-fact here, though. The speaker is thinking to himself, isn't he? Would it work better if the speaker had said something more observational, like 'she must have took it this morning', or 'there's my wife---and the umbrella!'?

    All in all, though, I think you've managed to convey the idea very well. It's realistic and has impact, and because of the way you wrote it, it is something all readers can understand and relate too. Nice!

    "What did I miss today?" I wondered aloud much to the curious stares of the listless few who sat around me, as bus No. 666 rolled out slowly from a deep slumber with a diesel-fueled groan.

    Not much different from 666, the early morning breeze saw me unceremoniously awaken from past-rainy-day hibernation to the harsh routine of work by my boss' friendly 5 am sms.

    "Can cover todae? Alex say he sck today. Msg me once u rch. k?"

    Low in spirits made impossibly lower by the blank stares of those of similar plight, I wind down the windows to let in air eager to squeeze into the stuffy confines of 666 as it warmed up to cruise mode.

    The rushing wind slows into a lingering breeze over me. With that comes the realization of what I missed; as I looked down past my tie, my shirt trim and neat, my flannel black pants and finally resting my wide, shocked eyes upon my exposed toes peeking back at me through my slippers.
