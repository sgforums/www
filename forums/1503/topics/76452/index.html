    Arcum Leonard
    The Child

    There are many questions that were left unanswered all my life, some of which I could never have an answer to. I had came across men, rich, poor and the powerful. Many asked me the same question when they see old Cormack Pertia sitting in the tavern. They want to know about Cormack Pertia the mercenary, Cormack Pertia the legend, but few cared to ask about Cormack Pertia the scavenger, the man without his right arm. Yes, they had asked about the adventures leading to it. A frightful experience with one of the mightiest.

    It was early spring that year when I came across the village. It was shabby but it housed many villagers. There were roughly three hundred folks living in the village and these folks were a little well off, due to the regular merchant and their production of a grey garment textile called Helk which was favoured by many adventurers to common leather.

    They needed protection, and being a famous mercenary leader of the Pertia Red Wolves, I was given the assignment of protecting them from raiders. There were thirty four of us, so we were more a mercenary squad than a company. The fellows were hardened trackers, and we survived more than fifteen assignments and had scars more than cracks on a frontier fortress. They promised a hundred gold pieces if we managed to drive the raiders away, a bonus of fifty if we managed to prevent the raiders from coming in.

    The village elder then mentioned about a child that we must promise to keep in our ranks. I was not keen on the idea, but the elder said that it did not matter if the child died in our ranks during the attack. I did not understand why they put a toddler to risk on the encampment where the raiders will attack, but I never question their decisions since there was no need to assign any man to protect him, the villagers would not mind if the kid died.

    Old Harcum, may Carstina guide him in his afterlife, had a fondness for kids. He was always telling us about how the brats were full of mischief so he insisted on taking care of the child. I opposed the decision but the man would not listen so I told him to be more careful with himself, not to sacrifice himself to defend the child.

    The child sat on boulder when Old Harcum was miserably trying to relate his heroics to the kid. More than often the child of merely ten years of age, seemed to know what, where and when to do certain things. He never asked a question and never spoke a word with Harcum and the rest. Everytime I looked at him, he would return with a gaze. It always seemed that he knew he was watched.

    Old Harcum tried to pester the child for his name but he would not answer. The villagers, would shake their heads when Harcum asked them about the child. After one day, Old Harcum either grew tired of the kid or maybe it was mutual, the kid sat alone. The barricades were up and the men I came with were already lying around, waiting for the bandits.

    The night, often long and dark, was always a mercenary's friend but that night was not. It was cold and the moon was full. An old superstition amongst warriors were that anyone whose reflection that was shone on a weapon on the full moon would soon be torn asunder and brought to the Goddess Carstina's judgement hall. Retribution would be paid in kind, and all overdue punishment were meted out in there.

My heart sank and I wondered how many more of my men would be taken from me on this accursed night.

We were circled and I cannot remember how the rest were put to rest. Old Harcum was missing and so was the child when I tried to rally my men.

The raiders died but more came. There were less than twenty of us in the battle and more were dying by the moment. I did not know who was beside me, but the axe cleaved him down and my right was open.

    I rushed forward to the assailant and ran my sword into his chest. I turned and cleaved down another man. The path before was opened and I saw Old Harcum clutching to his stomach from a deep wound. I parried an attack and rushed to him, as the man whispered his final words.

    "Take good care of him," he said, pointing to the boy standing next to him.

    I said nothing but nodded, looking back at the boy, I shouted to him, "Follow me if you value your life!"

    The child replied as if he was saying it beside me softly, "No, if you value your life, follow me."

Those who went near were torched and their screams filled my skull with their voices.

    The fire ceased but that was not the end. Weapons that were originally on the ground floated around him as he stretched both hands forward with palms down. The weapons flew and struck our enemies one by one as if it was being thrown. The weapons danced and attacked the bandits. Fear spreaded into the enemies' ranks and gripped theirs hearts. The bandits were escaping.

    A powerful gale blew and tossed them back. The sounds of bone cracking was obvious when they fell against the ground after being thrown back by the gust of wind. Some were tossed up in the air as if they weighed like feathers. Still they ran, when more were drawn back by the powerful storm. Then the sandstorm came.

    The sand was raining on the men and the men were being scratched by the sand as if they were rubbing their bodies against the ground. Their skin, scrapped by the sand and the wounds became bigger and wider. Then the blizzard came. Ice, like arrows pierced and tormented the men. Some died while most had their limbs and body injured by the frozen arrow. The child saw and closed his eyes.

    Holding his hands up and the child summoned a big mist above him, forming a cloud. He clapped and lightning struck the escaping enemies. The thunder roared and the ground shook. All of us trembled as we tried to regain our footing. Everyone, including us, were on our limbs, falling to the powerful shaking of the earth.

    Cracks appeared from the ground and roots branched out dragging and tearing them into pieces. Arms and legs were peeled from the torso by the roots like paper torn by a child. The cracks that appeared swallowed the corpses of the men and buried them deeply. And soon, everything was silent, and the remaining twelve of us were praying and crying. I did not cry, not because I was brave, but I was so terrified that tears evaded me.

    I was trembling non-stop, my hands and body shaking and I was panting hard. I felt nauseous, and lied on the ground, shaking uncontrollably. The child looked at me, then looked into my eyes. It seemed like he was telling me that everything was fine. The pace of my heart beating slowed and I opened my eyes widely. My hands neatly by the side and I picked myself up, my sword, in its sheath.

    "Mystic Mesmer," I thought to myself, "the Greater God of Randomness."

    "You've already decided that I was no match for Kuri Ragewind," Arcum answered, "then what is the point of fighting me?"

    "You've already decided on that too," Arcum interrupted.

    Mystic laughed loudly, "Never was I so fond of a human like I do now!" Then he stopped laughing, "Tell me child, if you're worthy of my vote of confidence in the Greater Realm."

    "You've also decided on that," Arcum answered as if he was repeating himself again and again.

    "Then you shall go through the three trials I had in mind to win my vote in the Greater Realm," Mystic said seriously then added, "The gods all say, it is I who govern Luck. All of these come from randomness, unpredictability, and opportunities. So tell me young one, what is Randomness?"

    "Randomness is unpredictability due to insufficient rules that were in existence. Randomness is the inability to calculate all possibilities and control the eventual outcome. Randomness is the inability to deduce the eventual outcome, the inability to harness results due to environmental influence, the inability to predict an incoming result. Randomness is a flaw within all of living."

    "Amazing, even those who worshipped the God of Luck won't be able to answer that as well as you do. You've done well for the first trial." Mystic turned and looked at me, "Human, you and your lady friend must follow the child." He disappeared after his last words were whispered into the wind.
