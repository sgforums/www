In Dreams We Break (A Villanelle)

  • peebrain
    17 Apr 06, 01:24
    In dreams we break, and are torn asunder,
    shunned while velvet nights shudder and rage,
    so we wake to escape, our spirits bolder.

    Cahiers, written by a nameless author,
    though modest, defile with each stained page,
    in dreams we break, and are torn asunder.

    Exuent a fear impossible to master,
    a dark light prances and torments the stage,
    while we wake to escape, our spirits older.

    Cursed is this contract, to place an actor
    confessing her lines, even moved to persuade
    as we dream and break, and are torn asunder.

    A minstrel sings of Tristan and Isolde,
    lovers crossed and doomed by curious fate,
    unlike them we wake and escape, our spirits lighter.

    Jostled 'tween two worlds, yet courage is steered
    by checks and balances, a bittersweet adage:
    although in dreams we break, and are torn asunder,
    we shall wake to escape, our spirits bolder.

    ---------------------------------------------

    Something I wrote to explain my affection for traditional forms, and the basic writing process over at another thread, Poetry - Proper writing styles and formats....

    A dreamer that is torn apart by vivid nightmares -- in essence he/she is very much alone. Although the nights are the reasons behind her breakdown, they do not in any way take responsibility for that -- the world still goes on, and one is left feeling very insignificant, and abandoned.

    And you don't have to use archaic language at all -- the reasons why I used words such as "cahier" and "exuent" stems largely from my background, and also because I wanted to regulate the references used in the poem -- mainly that of theatre, literature and myth.

    (This is where a poet's distinct style is developed, methinks)

    If you want to read more about villanelles, check out Sylvia Plath's "Mad Girl's Love Song", and Dylan's "Do Not Go Gently Into the Night".

    More importantly, you must realize that the villanelle works by oral repetition of the couplet, more so than its admittedly archaic roots.

    Oops. I forgot to address this bit.

    Stage references -- hmm. How shall I begin? Dreaming for me, is a personal matter, of which I feel are merely stylized and dramaticised interpretations of reality. Allusions to theatre and the stage therefore are pertinent for the subject matter.

    (gosh I'm actually doing Prac Crit for my poem. -chagrined)

    So, the obvious references are:

    1. Tristan and Isolde, solely for the fact that this love story has been rehashed in all manners possible down the ages)

    2. Cahiers, or leather-bound manuscripts -- the bed where creativity basically ferments, much like Shakespeare's folios.

    3. "Prances and torments" -- derived from Macbeth's "struts and frets upon the stage", all the men "merely players".

    4. Contract -- the actor's contract, and duty to paint the script alive, to confess and to persuade. Dreams are vivid because they're so realistic.

    5. Ministrel -- another stage player, only low brow and commonplace, as oppose to theatre.

    6. "Jostled 'tween two worlds" -- to traverse the fantastical world of theater requires a healthy suspension of belief, which is relieved upon the end of the drama. But in dreaming, it is recurrent, hence the need to "steer courage" by "checks and balances", to preserve a semblence of sanity.

    I hope this helps you understand the poem a little better. I didn't think it was abstract, but thanks for letting me know.

    Do not go gentle into that good night

    Do not go gentle into that good night,
    Old age should burn and rave at close of day;
    Rage, rage against the dying of the light.

    Though wise men at their end know dark is right,
    Because their words had forked no lightning they
    Do not go gentle into that good night.

    Good men, the last wave by, crying how bright
    Their frail deeds might have danced in a green bay,
    Rage, rage against the dying of the light.

    Wild men who caught and sang the sun in flight,
    And learn, too late, they grieved it on its way,
    Do not go gentle into that good night.

    Grave men, near death, who see with blinding sight
    Blind eyes could blaze like meteors and be gay,
    Rage, rage against the dying of the light.

    And you, my father, there on the sad height,
    Curse, bless me now with your fierce tears, I pray.
    Do not go gentle into that good night.
    Rage, rage against the dying of the light.

    By Dylan Thomas (1914 - 1953)
