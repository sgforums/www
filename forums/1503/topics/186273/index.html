    Well... I've been known to write a couple of sonnets and villanelles when I want to give my poems a little structure or discipline.

    Think of forms as a base to set the tone of the poem. For instance, sonnets tend to lend an old fogey sheen to the piece, and hence gives the poem more weight. And if you write in strict iambic pentameter and rhyme schemes, you'll realize that although it's much harder to write, the payoff is infinitely more rewarding.

    I like free verse -- but I think it's very prone to abuse. And because there's no rules to it, people tend to forget to apply common literary techniques in their writing -- simple things like repetition, alliteration, allusion and metaphor are poetic tools that should be used in any form of poetry.

    Personally, for anyone to call themselves a poet, they must have a working knowledge of the most common forms -- Sonnet, tercets, haiku, villanelle -- even though they do not have to specialize in any.

    This is what differentiates a poet from someone who throws words around.

    I don't prescribe reading "how-to" books, really. Instead, just read more poetry from the various ages.

    For older poems, read Donne, Shakespeare, Bacon, Byron and Dickinson for a feel of what sonnets and tercets are like.

    For more contemporary poems, Yeats, Hughes, Sexton, Plath, cummings, Riche, Dylan are pretty good.

    Carol Joyce Oats, Sophie Hannah, Ted Kooser, Sharon Olds are poets that gives traditional forms the modern treatment.l

    To write poetry, i think one must not forget the roots.
    By this i am refering to the traditional forms of poetry : the sonnet, ballad, haiku etc. These forms are structured, they have a certain number of lines and rhyme schemes.

    Why write traditional poetry? Because it is still relevant today, it builds discipline into your writing, it will improve your craft.

    Contemporary poetry is often associated with free verse. People believe you can do anything with free verse. Which is true to an extent because it allows you to do nearly anything with the language. But there are still rules in free verse ( i like to call it open form poetry). You don't restrict the lines, or shape . You may not notice it, but if you write in free verse, you start to impose rules on your poem and yourself.

    The trick to writing good poetry is to read good poetry. Read the traditional poets and the comtemporary ones. The selection by peebrain is very good.

    Expiringpoet,

    You have raised very pertinent questions that I must try to addresss to the best of my ability.

    Creativity is not about letting loose the wild horses -- but how you rein them in to do your bidding.

    Deadpoet and Dsnake mentioned how common it is for a poet to write about a specific image in mind, and not knowing how the rest of the poem will take shape.

    I beg to differ.

    It is true that a lot of my poems have their origins in a specific idea in mind -- say, writer's block or solitude or existential angst -- and it usually starts with one verse, for instance, "In dreaming I take my waking slow"

    This verse is the start of the poet's examination of the human condition. From there, I think of how to link this insular line to a greater whole of sorts -- how to give the poem meaning using this verse as a trigger.

    I could use the line, "In dreaming I take my waking slow" to illuminate the attractions of dreaming -- how the fantastical world could be more spiritually illuminating than our harsh reality.

    As for traditional forms: they do not stifle creativity -- instead they force you to break your thoughts into coherent slices, to be assembled and appreciated by the reader.

    And as a modern poet, part of the challenge is to use these traditional devices as creatively as you can, within the boundaries of structure and reason.

    I've written a villanelle to explain what I mean.


    In Dreams We Break (A Villanelle)

    In dreams we break, and are torn asunder,
    shunned while velvet nights shudder and rage,
    so we wake to escape, our spirits bolder.

    Cahiers, written by a nameless author,
    though modest, defile with each stained page,
    in dreams we break, and are torn asunder.

    Exuent a fear impossible to master,
    a dark light prances and torments the stage,
    while we wake to escape, our spirits older.

    Cursed is this contract, to place an actor
    confessing her lines, even moved to persuade
    as we dream and break, and are torn asunder.

    A minstrel sings of Tristan and Isolde,
    lovers crossed and doomed by curious fate,
    unlike them we wake and escape, our spirits lighter.

    Jostled 'tween two worlds, yet courage is steered
    by checks and balances, a bittersweet adage:
    although in dreams we break, and are torn asunder,
    we shall wake to escape, our spirits bolder.

    When I wrote this poem, I had only one image -- of two opposing worlds, one in dreaming, and one in waking.

    That's how a villanelle is usually constructed -- you start with a couplet ("In dreams we break, and are torn asunder/ So we wake to escape, our spirits bolder") and then insert a tertiary line to form a tercet.

    Each line of the couplet is repeated in an alternating fashion, and the challenge lies in the poet's ability to use fitting rhymes and images to SUPPORT the precis of the poem.

    That's why I've stuck to imagery associated with Theater and history -- actors, Tristan and Isolde,... etc. In order to tie in with the beauty of fiction inherent in dreaming. It's tempting to cook up rubbish rhymes so as to get the stanza over and done with -- but that's hardly the point of writing poetry.

    I've also deviated from the standard villanelle -- I didn't stick strictly to the repetition of couplet -- along the way, I made slight changes to the couplet to reinforce my message.

    Which is why the poem begins with:

    In dreams we wake and are torn asunder,
    So we wake to escape our spirits bolder,
    and ends up like this:

    although in dreams we break, and are torn asunder,
    we shall wake to escape, our spirits bolder.
    You'll see that Sylvia Plath, Elizabeth Bishop, Dyland and Sophie Hannah make creative adjustments to traditional forms. Use them to your advantage -- they're your tools!

    Also, bear in mind I had no idea how I was going to finish the poem, or even come up with three other stanzas. But this is where creativity will come to save you, and discipline will force you to reexamine your writing, and make every word count.

    I've always believed that if a general remark is personally felt, then it probably isn't too far from the truth.

    Think about it.

    And think about whether the things you write contain the various elements:

    1. Structure
    2. Semantics
    3. Sound, meter and rhyme
    4. Imagery
    5. Literary allusions (metaphors, similes etc)
    6. Comparisons
    7. Rhetoric
    8. And many more.

    You don't have to purposely include these elements in your writing, but if you're an instinctive poet, these elements will be present in the poem in one form or the other.

    And someone who's adept at scansion will pick them out -- this is what differentiates a poem from a bunch of words rudely cobbled together.

    This is the reason why poetry is an art form, and not merely a pass-time.

    And please, don't write off something as "cheem" simply because the writing is structured in a way different from your natural speech. It smacks of condescension, truth be said.
