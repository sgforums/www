    This is one of the poems i wrote last night in a dash of inspiration- very raw... short too- jus thot i'll throw it for people to comment before i work further on it.(looking at my list of spices needed for cooking)

    In cooking; earthed of all arts
    dry sherry, tumeric, rice wine
    these i have, but not the art
    nor respect,
    for living food, or dead birds &
    crustaceous crabs
    painted as they ever are the beginnings
    of gluttony in dutch masters frames.
    This is what I think of fish,
    white meat.
    & beef,
    red meat.
    & fingers that paint the chicken with spice,
    mad
    magic.

    Seeing this is still a beginning stage draft and you are thinking of adding more to it, I'll just keep my comments on the short side.

    I don't think the poem needs to be long to make a point. Nobody can make poems longer or shorter than they should be. The way I see it now you are trying to say that the cook has the ability to cook but fails to see cooking as an art form, that food is just food. The way I see it,

    1) you can go on to explore the consequences of this way of thinking,
    2) you can go a different direction, redraft comparing between how a real artist of a cook cooks compared to the end-product of someone who simply cooks

    Or you can ponder and I'm sure you'll come up with more ideas.

    As it is now:

    I think the punctuation is distracting - I think you have too many unnecessary commas and full stops.

    The line breaks are also sort of strange. Each line break is like a pause and having them in the wrong place can disrupt the reading.

    To find the problem with rhythm, reading the poem back to yourself out loud helps you to spot the bumps.

    I'm pretty much oldschool, so personally I'd rather have grammar, punctuation and syntax remain intact for written pieces -- unless liberties taken with the English language have a particular purpose and intent (i.e. e.e. cummings).

    The reason's rather simple -- poetry is foremost an oral tradition. So when it is transcribed, it should also take care to preserve its readability and ensuing interpretation -- hence I find the rather liberal use of ampersands rather distracting to the eye.

    And someone's mentioned rhythm -- that's one of the problems that jump out immediately...

    That said, pretty nice imagery in certain areas -- I especially am enamoured with the line, "fingers that paint the chicken with spice".
