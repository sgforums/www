Stuck

  • Aeolius77
    18 May 06, 21:22
    Hi all,
    something from 2000, actually describes my writing life (or what little of it) most of the time. Your comments are very welcome
    --------------------------

    Another blocked sink;
    I stare morosely as I try to think
    in fluid waters, liquid thoughts.
    Yet the water refuses to move. Stuck.

    I pump the plunger
    furiously, to work up a pressure
    so that bottom vacuum pulls
    the water down. Still the path refuses to clear. Stuck.

    So tired. So out of breath.
    Yet I long for crystal images
    that once adorned my pages.
    I yearn for egg and wreath.

    As the clock ticks nonchalant to spite me,
    to tell me that which flows right through
    my hands,
    I wish its hands get stuck.

    As the sink continues its motionless rebellion,
    My mind, in stubborn parallel, gets blocked.

    I throw down my tools,
    Plunger and pen.
    In sudden hedonism

    I turn on the tap full blast and now the water flows not through, but over and out and above
    the sink to overflow and drown whatever f.ucking thing is in its path.

    So desperate. So tired.
    I want to drown and maybe hope to find the flowing watery words in my death.

    But even that, in the light of a stuck sink,
    is a futile hyperbole.

    Hi Aeolius77,

    I'll comment on the first stanza if you don't mind:

    Another blocked sink;
    I stare morosely as I try to think
    in fluid waters, liquid thoughts.
    Yet the water refuses to move. Stuck.

    In my opinion, poetic license is great because it allows a lot of creativity on our part. However, as I write more and more I always visualise and listen to my poems and try to make sure that every line is visually and aurally logical and at it's minimum grammatical. Hence, I feel some discomfort reading the line:
    "I try to think / in fluid waters"

    whereas if you omit "in fluid waters" the line sounds more logical:

    "I try to think / in liquid thoughts"

    Or alternatively I would rewrite the lines in the following way:

    Another blocked sink;
    My thoughts dissolve into stagnant
    Water, fluidity interrupted, by
    Choked debris.
    The water doesn't
    Move, my thoughts get
    Stuck.

    My other suggestion, as shown above, is to move the word "Stuck." to the next line to create emphasis.

    Also, the effect of 'being stuck' may be aptly created using enjambment (i.e. the running over of a sentence from one verse or couplet into another so that closely related words fall in different lines ref:www.m-w.com) as I have done in the suggested rewriting above.

    What do you think?

Visually yes, the word is emphasized to good effect, but when it is read, the run-on may just subsume 'stuck' into the end of the phrase and become lost. Of course, perhaps a period after 'move', and then bringing 'stuck' into the next line might achieve both effects.
