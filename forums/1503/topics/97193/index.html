Pain's Inferno

  • LazerLordz
    9 Oct 04, 11:26
    And everytime I gaze
    across this ocean which
    never seems to end
    such azure uncertainty
    like a beggar wobbling
    not sitting down,
    yet his legs do not stand
    but that's not what his brain
    tells him.

    Such is the pain
    which immobilizes me
    a patient stuck in a seedy ward
    lying in a gurney
    and infected with morosity.
    My mind's rotting away
    like a corpse in Calcutta's
    gutter.

    And where is my morphine
    my salve of a wounded soul
    which struggles but lacks
    the temerity to break down
    the walls, the monstrous walls
    that circle my heart
    and leaves
    it dry and dead

    The drug cannot keep me
    ignorant, nor dumb.
    My soul is a fire
    which even the phoenix fears
    and the Devil dare not thread
    lest it burn his bedeviled hooves.
    And your heart might just
    burn, burn forever
    if the fireman runs away.
    Only if.

    From a different angle:

    The author first describe a physical part of the body i.e. the legs and the brain in the first stanza line 7 and line 8. He then moves away from this physical aspect to something more abstract in the second stanza i.e. the mind, line 6. What is interesting here is that the author links his mind and not the brain to a corpse thus linking an abstract concept to something physical.

    In the third stanza the author brings us to the next level, the domain of spiritual world i.e. the soul. Again he gives it a physical touch by describing it as a Â‘wounded soulÂ’. Only in the last stanza does the author breaks away from the physical aspect of the pain he is feeling, a realm where soul burns and phoenix/ Devil roam.

    But to our surprise we are abruptly brought down to earth by the last two lines. (Ouch!) The introduction of the character Â‘firemanÂ’ seems very out of place. Who is this fireman? I believe he is the authorÂ’s conscious determination whose main function is to to suppress the pain. We are brought back to reality, back where we first started in the opening stanza. The journey is now complete (Though a bit rush I must say)

    There is also the risk that our friend may not be able to suppress this feeling forever. Fortunately he has a choice. Whether he chooses to contain or to be consumed by the flame is entirely up to him.
