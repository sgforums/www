    INTRODUCTION

    Dear friends, this thread will not help you score for your Literature examination or to make you fall in love with Shakespeare overnight. I will not go into details of the plays because most of you would have fallen asleep before proceeding to the next scene. In order to truly enjoy the richness of the plays it is advisable to read the full text. However such an attempt will surly bore those who have no love for Shakespeare to tears. (Maybe even to madness)

    Taking this into consideration, I decided not to post the full text in the series. If you are interested, you can easily read the full text from the Internet, or get them from the libraries. You can also grab a copy of the modern translation from any good bookshops.

    My main intention is to explore the various ways in which the plays might be relevant to our personal experiences. To do this I will need you to be creative, to be imaginative and be able to put yourself into the characters? shoes. I need you to feel the pain, the anger, the love, etc of the characters. My advice is not to judge the characters base on your first impression. Try to understand what they are going through before passing any judgements. In short, I need you to play the role of Kings, Queens, young lovers, fools, etc; I need you to be human.

    I will first summarize the scenes, follow by my own interpretations. Most importantly, I will attempt to relate the events with our own experiences in the form of question(s). For those who are familiar with Shakespeare, please feel free to criticize my interpretations for I am looking forward to a healthy debate. You can also add in your opinions to enrich the discussion, I will really appreciate your contributions.

    Also please answer the questions posted (in blue) as my intentions is to set you thinking about matters that you may have taken for granted. I will try my best to be as brief as possible. For those who have never read Shakespeare before, I will suggest you read the summary before proceeding to my interpretations. Try reading it like a story, have fun.

    Themes about friendships, marriages, justices, etc are abundant in Shakespeare's plays. I strongly encourage anyone that is doing psychology to read them. They are gems waiting to be discovered if you are willing to give it a try. Turn the stones into gems. You will realize that they have so much to offer.

    I have chosen one of his last plays, i.e. THE WINTER'S TALE for my first Interactive series. Not many people are aware about this play. We are more familiar with his famous plays like Romeo and Juliet, Hamlet, King Lear, etc. I don't think Hollywood will be interested to make a movie out of it. I am actually taking a great risk in choosing an 'unknown' play to start off my Interactive series. But I feel that we must give these 'unknown' plays an opportunity to be recognized and appreciated.

    Most importantly I need your contributions, after all the key word here is 'INTERACTIVE'.

    Well, hold on tight and let the journey begins...

    Summary Art 1, Scene 1:

    In this short scene, you are introduced to two characters i.e. Archidamus, a Bohemian courtier, and Camillo, a Sicilian courtier and trusted friend of Leontes, King of Sicilia. Through their short conversation, we learn that the Leontes, King of Sicilia and Polixenes, King of Bohemian were childhood friends. Both kings were raised together, although now their responsibilities as kings keep them apart. Both courtiers also agree that Prince Mamillius of Sicilia (son of Leontes) shows great promises.

    DP's Interpretation:

    There are a few things we can learn from this scene. Firstly, the past/present status of friendship between the two kings. Secondly, the future of Sicilia in the form of Prince Mamillius and lastly, the character of Camillo who seems to be a faithful and patriotic courtier full of love for his position, his king and queen. He is not just an advisor to Leontes, but also his trusted friend.

    A common theme in Shakespeare is the conflict between marriage and friendship. It seems that these two cannot co-exist peacefully. Polixenes tells Hermione that they were so innocence before knew women and duties of marriage. However once occupied with the responsibilities of marriage, their friendship are affected.

    Well our friend Leontes seems to be running away from his responsibilities as husband, father and king. Maybe his unfounded jealousy is a way to for him to escape from all these responsibilities. He fails to understand that a king's duties as husband and father are inseparable from his duties as head of state. Failure in any one of the roles will affect the others.

    Do you find yourself getting easily irritated or angry when you are under tremendously stress, especially when trying to run away from responsibilities?

    Put yourself in his shoes...

    You were once a care-free young lad. And gradually, you become a king, a husband and a father. Unless you are very good at coping with stress, or else at certain stage you may feel burnt out. I am not suggesting Leontes is right in taking this particular course of action, but we need to give him a chance to be heard.
