The Crying Tree

  • peebrain
    8 Mar 06, 17:33
    Dumbly, you spread your arms, a silent "why"
    as if to question your wavering franchise,
    slake the futility of wild protuberant lust.
    I carve a careful girdle upon your virgin hips

    like a lover coaxing ecstasy, with beaded intent:
    fear changes the consistency of your fount.
    Your tears, hot and moist like bleached burnt sugar
    trickle tersly into thieving palms, waiting;

    infinite patience, in the veins of commerce
    I slat the boughs of your disavowed health,
    laughing as strangled screams drip from your
    descendent heirs, the ones that turn their faith

    away from trials of fire you turn your head
    as I suck your last tear, and turn away.

    -----------------------------------------------

    Interesting, if not a somewhat incendiary response.

    Let's clear some things up, shall we?

    Firstly, this is neither a market campaign or a publicity drive. If it were -- and I have written my fair share of such copy -- the angle of the piece would have been different. You , as an audience, would have been broken down into various market segments, analyzed to a fault before I commence writing copy that engages you on your level. The word choice, presentation style and message would be specifically catered to your comprehension ability.

    However, this poem and in a larger context, this forum is neither for advertising or public relations purposes. Poetry at its fundamental level, is a personal response, a creative piece produced by the author or artist.

    It is written for the author by the author.

    Hence, the communication, a two-way one at least, exists between kindred spirits and those who share by giving constructive criticism.

    Word choice -- be it in a poem or your advertising hoopla-- is also deliberate -- it is not so much about drawing from a massive vocabulary but rather a calculated move to choose the right word that most expressively conveys the poet's intent, and it is also very much regulated by limitations on rhythm, meter, or context.

    It is hardly my fault if you cannot understand some of these words. At any rate, you are probably not my target audience any way.

    So while I appreciate you taking time to pen down your comment, and I do acknowledge your frustration stemmed from your limited vocabulary -- I have to appeal to you to view poetry as not something written for someone else's approval, but as a fundamental right to the author's expression, learning and cartharsis.

    Hey Liz, you're almost spot-on with your interpretation. Indeed, I've personified the rubber tree into a living entity that exists solely for a purpose. It gives and gives without asking for anything in return, and discarded once it has reached its maximum yield -- this thought came to me when I was engaged in heavy-duty research on the manufacture of rubber derivatives.

    As for "trial by fire". it actually refers to the process of vulcanization -- heating natural rubber with a combination of sulphur and other chemicals -- rendering it tougher and more resilient to external stresses.

    A reader from my blog once commented that duality plays a strong role in my work. I agree with him -- always, I strive to illuminate the unknown (or seldom considered) through a careful interplay of words and images. They may seem discongruent at first glance, but hopefully, together these disparate phrases would make sense. And help myself (and readers, by extension) understand my world and my experiences a little better.

    In that sense, this is very much channeled in my poetry and prose. I seldom write prose these days, due to a lack of time, so poetry has become my main vehicle for self-expression. An idea for a poem usually starts with a problem that perplexes me to no end -- and if I have the time or inspiration, I'll actually sit down and work out the problems, simplify and render it as a series of pictures or conceits, and knock it to form.

    As such, such a workflow is ingrained in the self. However, since the subject matter usually revolves around humanity -- and not so much localised (as you can tell by now, most of my work doesn't contain elements of Singapore and local slang) I find that a lot of foreign readers can identify with them.

    (This is also the reason why I don't identify myself as a wholly Singaporean poet.)

    Subjects like passion, loss, ambition, aggression, politics even, are universal and timeless, and my goal in this particular pursuit is to illuminate/articulate as much as I can, based on my level of maturity and expriences in these areas.
