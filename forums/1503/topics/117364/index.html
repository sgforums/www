    A short message

    Usually a beginner poet, started off by wanting to share his/her emotions/experiences with the reader via his/her poems. This is a good thing and I strongly encourage you to pick up your pen and draft your first poem.

    At this stage, the focus of the poems is about Â‘selfÂ’ in the highly ambiguous term Â‘self-expressionÂ’. These poems are mainly about Â‘this is how I feelÂ’, Â‘this is what I didÂ’, etc. i.e. about Â‘meÂ’, my experiences, my emotions and my feelings.

    Such writes usually received praises from friends, family members, and colleagues. I am not surprise if these poems also receive very positive comments in some poetry forums. Some of my works were highly praised in other oversea poetry forums. But I am not so sure about that now.

    It is absolutely fine to stop at this stage. Most of us do that.

    However some of you might want to take your poetry further, i.e. to explore the Â‘expressionÂ’ portion. To express something efficiently, you need to learn the necessary skills. Be it learning how to cook, dance, sing, and yes, also in the craft of writing poetry. In reality, learning the correct skills is especially important in poetry writing.

    For these small group of people the shift is towards a reader-centered writing style. I am presently working toward this direction. I am working towards writing poems that, hopefully, are able to create experiences for you, the reader. And for me to do that, I need to take many factors into considerations, i.e. linkbreaks, meter, abstract/concrete, clichÃ©s, rhythm, grammar, spelling, etc.

    I am not saying that those poems that I've written were bad poems. But the truth is they could be improved. For example, you wrote a love poem based on your experience. But remember, there are millions of love poems out here. The only thing unique about the one that you have written is because it is your experience and you don't really care what other people think about it. But wouldn't it be great to write love poems (also based on your experience) that stand out from the rest?

    For example take this poem written by me years ago. Is it special to you? No. But it is a very special poem to me. It is for my first love. But it is just another love poem to the readers.

    Five Critique Tips for Beginners By John Boddie

    There are a few things to keep in mind:

    1. You are trying to provide feedback to the author that will be useful in helping the author understand what works and what doesn't work in the poem.

    2. Try to find something specific that you like in the poem - a specific image, possibly an imaginative word choice. Tell the author what it was. This is far more helpful than saying "I really like this."

    3. If you find something you don't understand, ask the author what was meant. It's helpful to an author to know where his or her audience is confused.

    4. If you find something specific that you don't like, identify it and tell the author why you didn't like it.

    5. If you read a poem and find that it is filled with spelling and punctuation errors, don't spend time on it. The author, by not preparing the poem to be read,is showing that he or she does not value your time. Why should you think the author would value your opinion in this situation?

    As you read more, you will begin to form a richer idea of what constitutes "good poetry." This, in turn, will make your comments more valuable to the authors.

    You won't be able to improve your reviewing skills unless you actually write reviews.

    There is a famous sequence at the end of Lewis MilestoneÂ’s All Quiet on the Western Front in which the hero, Paul, a German solider, is shot by a French sniper.     Poetry and verse

    The word Â“verseÂ” refers only to the shape an expression takes, not to its content or quality. Poetry may be in verse, and often does use some kind of verselike structure. Many poets, for example, have been attracted by the shape of the sonnet, which arranges rhythms and rhymes in a definite formation. But the sonnet in itself is only a verse-form; a sonnet may be poetry or it may no.

    Poetry is not poetry because it is in verse; to the shape of verse it has to add qualities of imagination and emotion and of language itself.

    Is that really a poem? by Gilbert Koh

    "The unexamined life is not worth living." - Socrates.

    He could very well have added that the unexamined poem is also not worth writing. I think that every poet, at one time or another, has had the following experience -

    he writes, and he writes, working for hours on a new piece of writing. At the end of all that labour, he scrutinises the paper before him. Doubt creeps in, and he asks himself, "Is that really a poem?"

    Otherwise, what I've written is probably just a collection of clever words. Like a slick new ad. Or a politician's speech. But not a poem.

    If you are in the habit of writing clever words without seriously engaging yourself, well, do feel free to congratulate yourself for being clever. But don't mistake yourself for a poet. If you don't take a risk and challenge yourself, what you produce is unlikely to be a genuine poem.
