Grief and Rage

  • LazerLordz
    5 Mar 05, 11:27
    And when you're old and infirm
    and the white cars speed by
    in a hail of muddy waters
    that coat you and stick
    to you
    like the sins of children before
    and scholars who work
    on empty skulls
    because someone has taken out
    their programming.

    I weep for you
    weeping for the men on
    the stone benches
    snoring away their lives
    a quilt of life fading
    into the sunset.
    And no one seems to care
    whether you live or die.
    Inside the darkness..
    there is no such thing as
    poverty.

    So stare hard and long
    gather you wits of yore
    announce your plight
    to all and sundry.
    The newsprint your sword
    and torn mats your shield
    against
    imperial aggravation
    that seeks to wash
    you and I
    out of the picture
    for good.

    Here are some general tips.

    Five Critique Tips for Beginners By John Boddie

    There are a few things to keep in mind:

    You are trying to provide feedback to the author that will be useful in helping the author understand what works and what doesn't work in the poem.

    Try to find something specific that you like in the poem - a specific image, possibly an imaginative word choice. Tell the author what it was. This is far more helpful than saying "I really like this."

    If you find something you don't understand, ask the author what was meant. It's helpful to an author to know where his or her audience is confused.

    If you find something specific that you don't like, identify it and tell the author why you didn't like it.

    If you read a poem and find that it is filled with spelling and punctuation errors, don't spend time on it. The author, by not preparing the poem to be read,is showing that he or she does not value your time. Why should you think the author would value your opinion in this situation?
