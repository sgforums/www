    SINGAPORE : Singapore's media industry is undergoing a major shake up.

    The island's largest broadcaster MediaCorp and main-board listed Singapore Press Holdings, will merge their mass-market television and free newspaper operations - in a move to stem losses and improve shareholder value.

    Advertisement

    On Friday afternoon, top management from the two media giants signed agreements for SPH to buy a 20 percent stake in a new television company to be called MediaCorp TV Holdings.

    MediaCorp will manage this new company and own the remaining 80 percent.

    SPH will also buy a 40 percent stake in MediaCorp Press, which publishes TODAY.

    TODAY will continue to be managed by MediaCorp - and will operate independently of the stable of newspapers under SPH.

    The deals - were struck on a "willing buyer, willing seller" basis - and should receive formal regulatory approval by year's end.

    Since Singapore's media liberalisation in May 2000, both SPH and MediaCorp have incurred losses - after their respective forays into each other's businesses proved unprofitable.

    SPH will pay S$10 million for their 20 percent stake in the new TV company MediaCorp TV Holdings.

    The new company will comprise MediaCorp's Channels 5 and 8 and TV Mobile - as well as SPH MediaWorks' Channel U and I.

    The new television company will also include MediaCorp Studios - which produces the bulk of MediaCorp's local programming.

    Channel U will continue to operate but Channel I's commercial viability has to be reviewed.

    On the newspaper side, MediaCorp will sell a 40 percent stake in MediaCorp Press, which publishes TODAY, to rival SPH for more than S$19 million.

    Once the deal is completed, SPH will merge its own free newspaper Streats with TODAY.

    TODAY will continue to be managed by MediaCorp, and will operate independently of the stable of newspapers under SPH.

    The mergers will affect staff on both sides.

    A staff rationalisation exercise will be overseen by a Manpower Synergy Task Force.

    It will comprise an independent director from MediaCorp's board, and senior HR managers from the two media companies. - CNA

    SPH MediaWorks' Channel i to close on 1 Jan 2005

    SINGAPORE : Following the merger of MediaCorp's and SPH's TV and free newspaper operations, Channel i will be closed on 1 January 2005.

    Channel i is the English language channel of SPH MediaWorks.

    In a joint statement, MediaCorp and Singapore Press Holdings (SPH) said they have concluded that Channel i is not commercially viable because of the fragmented and small English language TV market.

    The merger is expected to be legally completed by the end of this year, after which MediaCorp TV Holdings Pte Ltd (MCTV), the holding company, will be 80 percent owned by MediaCorp and 20 percent by SPH.

    There will be minor interim changes to the programming schedule of Channel U in the first quarter of 2005 until MCTV introduces new complementary schedules for Channels 8 and U. These interim changes will be announced before 1 January 2005.

    As part of a staff rationalisation exercise, SPH will lay off 132 staff and MediaCorp will release 72 staff from across the group.

    MediaCorp and SPH complete staff rationalisation exercise

    SINGAPORE : The word is final - and the numbers are out.

    This in the wake of the merger of the mass-market TV and free newsapaper operations of media companies MediaCorp and Singapore Press Holdings (SPH).

    The two-month staff rationalisation exercise involved 429 staff from SPH.......the majority from SPH Mediaworks.

    Of these, 200 will be transferred to MediaCorp, 97 absorbed by SPH, and 132 will be retrenched.

    Separately, MediaCorp also carried out a rationalisation programme, retrenching 72 staff in total.

    On average, for those with more than three years of service, each retrenched person gets one month pay for each year worked, capped at 25 months.

    The total retrenchment bill? $5.3 million for SPH and $3.9 million for MediaCorp.

    The aim of the rationalisation is to stop the bleeding.

    Mr Ernest Wong, Group CEO of MediaCorp, said: "The rationalisation is that with 330 people from MediaWorks and about half of them coming over here - in that sense, the half coming over here, we don't bring full overheads to run one channel...if you bring entire overheads, no rationalisation, you will bring over $40 million losses too."

    As for the mood of the staff, SPH and MediaCorp said it was generally positive.

    And with bruising competition put aside, the focus would now be on moving into the regional market.

    Mr Man Shu Sam, COO of SPH MediaWorks, said: "This consolidation of resources and talent will help us to move even faster to compete in the region. I think we need to catch up these past four years competing locally."

    But the staff rationalistaion process was anything but easy.

    The five-man Manpower Synergy Commitee met several times a week over the past two months, communicating by phone and emails as well, and reviewing 1,200 staff to run the merged TV and studio operations - in which MediaCorp owns 80 percent and SPH 20 percent.

    Mr Soo Kok Leng, Chairman of Manpower Synergy Committee, said: "Really to determine how you compare one person with another person because when we look at the list, 1,200 of them, to make sure the job they are doing currently matches and grading on this side is the same as the other side - often they are about the same.

    "The question is who is the best...for everyone there was a debate it was not an easy thing...some are really easy - can be a matter of seconds, some took 2-3 meetings."

    Mr Alan Chan, Ceo of Singapore Press Holdings, said: "The deliberation has been very open, very objective and when there are any doubts about any individual, sometimes the supervisor is called in to give testimony or evidence, on the suitability of the individual staff and through this long process, came up with a list. From the SPH side, we are very satisfied that all our staff have been given a fair weightage."

    The changes kick in by year end.
