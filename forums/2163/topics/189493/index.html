    I'm going to go out on a limb here.

    If we're talking about skilled immigration, there really isn't a problem. There are limits to the number of people admitted, and that creates a filtering process through which people with the greatest contribution to make come through. All things being equal, if they've bothered to go through the hassle of the immigration process, they're probably motivated to make good on their lives in their new country.

    Now here's where it gets contentious. Taking in refugees is a noble idea, but it does seem to cause a lot more problems than skilled migration.

    First, their countries of origin tend to be in very poor state for them to be refugees in the first place, and this places limits on the skills that they bring along with them. While they're getting themselves upskilled (and that's not a given thing), it's a drain on the new country's coffers.

    Second, the state of the original countries would be chaotic, and moving to a new country where rule of law is taken a lot more seriously can be a big adjustment to make.

    Third, these people are not in their new countries by choice. One would think that they would appreciate having their lives saved, but more often than not they stay stuck in survival mode and try to get away with as much as they can, which doesn't gel well with civilised society.

    To some extent, the above applies to illegal immigrants as well.

    Like I said, morally I'm all for the idea of a refugee policy. The practical side of things, however, can get extremely messy.
