    Australia is currently short of engineer (especially Civil) at the moment so your chance of getting a PR is fairly good. I don't think it matters much in terms of where you do your degree though there are advantages to it if you do it here in Oz.

    One advantage is, you can apply to get your PR immediately after you completed your course (after you got your final results) and the application usually takes 6 months if everything goes well. During the duration of the application, you're allowed to stay in Oz with your student visa or a bridging visa. (if you apply from overseas with overseas degree it can take up to 12 months to process)

    The other advantage is, since you're going to work in Oz, studying in Oz means you get to learn the design etc based on Australian Standard and it will give you a better chance of getting a job here.

    Hope that answers your questions.

    There are actually two forumers here who did aerospace at RMIT, if I recall correctly.

    It's not so much a case of being disadvantaged if you do the degree at NUS or NTU, but the advantage you have with doing an Australian degree.

    If you apply for PR through the points scheme, your work/education background will contribute to your points. However, your background will have to be assessed as being compliant with Australian standards before being recognised for points awards.

    If your degree is from a non-Australian institution, you will have to jump through all kinds of hoops to get your training and experience assessed and verified. On the other hand, because Australian university courses are required to be accredited by the relevant professional bodies, getting the degree automatically means that your training/experience is recognised. Note, however, that the recognition is only valid for six months after you graduate unless you have started working in that particular field before that six months is up.

    On top of that, you get an extra ten points if your degree is Australian.

    All in all, if your intention is to migrate, go for the Australian degree.

    To some extent I suppose Fatum's got a point, but IMO it's up to the individual and it also depends on which route you take to PR.

    If you apply for PR through employment circumstances, the employer will have to demonstrate that you're bringing in skills that can't be found within the local population. This is often done by showing that the job has been advertised for some time and no local applicants have suited the role. A good part of the time it's too much of a hassle for employers to go through.

    However, if you're applying through the points scheme, you won't need an employer to vouch for you, and after getting your PR, it'll be no more hassle for the employer to hire you than to hire an Australian citizen.

    Now as for things like accents and cultural backgrounds, from 13 years in Australia, my experience is that nobody really gives a continental about that sort of thing, especially in places like Melbourne, Sydney and Perth which have an established Asian community. If you come off as a real jerk or if you can't understand simple English it would be a barrier, but that's a separate matter from your cultural background.
