    Originally posted by Fatum:

    You misinterpret.

    I have been paying taxes all this while. My employer withholds a certain amount of my salary for state and federal taxes, but the employer is under no obligation to ensure that the withholdings are sufficient to cover your entire tax bill, especially if you incur tax liabilities outside of your work (for example, interest and dividends on private investments and bank accounts).

    The amount of the withholdings can be adjusted by the employee, and I've done a good job of making sure they cover slightly more than my anticipated tax liability for a given year (the excess is returned as a tax refund). However, recent changes in tax laws require financial firms to report annual capital gains in addition to dividend payouts on private investments. In other words, prior to the changes, any increase in the principal value of an investment (i.e., a capital gain) would incur a tax liability only when that fund is liquidated. Since most people hold these funds for the long term, they don't incur a capital gains tax until they sell the investment, years down the line. However, after the tax law changes went into effect, investors are now required to pay taxes on the funds' capital gains annually.

    The capital gains on my investments weren't reported until late last year, by which time I could not adjust my tax withholdings to cover the anticipated taxes on the gains.
