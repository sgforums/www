    1. take bottle of engine flush solution, pour it into crankcase

    2. switch on the engine and let it idle for a few minutes

    3. slide underneath the car with a large basin, loosen the oil pan screw and let the oil flow ... (be careful ... oil is HOT and dirty )

    4. drip it all off ...

    6. screw in the new one .... let more oil from the oil filter drip off ....

    7. tighten back the oil pan screw ....

    8. pour in new oil .....

    A few comments:

    1. Engine flush solutions might be beneficial on older vehicles that have sludge build-up in the engine block, but it is a complete waste of money on newer vehicles. Besides, most engine oils already have detergent additives that do essentially the same thing.

    2. In addition to loosening/removing the oil pan screw to drain the oil, also remove the oil filler cap at the top of the engine block. This vents the block and allows more of the old oil to drain out.

    3. Avoid the tendency to overtighten the new oil filter. Most oil filters only need to be screwed in until they are "hand tight." There should be directions on the filter canister itself indicating how much more to turn the filter after the O-ring contacts the base.

    4. After you've replaced the oil and filter, start the vehicle and let it run a few minutes so that you can check under the engine to ensure nothing is leaking.

    And yes, as Fatum has noted, you do save a boatload of money doing it yourself, plus you ensure that the task is done correctly.

    A few comments on this too :

    On vehicles with fuel-injected engines (i.e., most modern vehicles), fuel in the fuel line is always under pressure, even with the engine off and battery disconnected. Disconnecting the fuel line from the filter will result in raw fuel spraying everywhere until the pressure is relieved. Obviously, raw fuel sprawing onto a hot engine block and an even hotter exhaust manifold can lead to Bad Things.

    Most factory service manuals (including those for my two Nissans) recommend the following method of relieving pressure in the fuel line prior to any engine maintenance work (including fuel filter replacement):

    1. Locate the fuse for the fuel pump in the fuse box, and pull it.

    2. Start the engine. It will run for a few seconds, but the inoperative fuel pump is not pressurizing the fuel injection system, and the engine will die from fuel starvation.

    3. Crank the engine a few more times to relieve any residual pressure in the fuel line.

    4. Disconnect the battery.

    The fuel line can now be disconnected from the filter without fear of raw fuel spraying out. Once the new filter is in place and everthing hooked back up, make sure to replace the fuse for the fuel pump before starting the engine.
