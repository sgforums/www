    IDE and SATA2 are very different.

    IDE cables is works on parallel communication protocol, which is the total opposite of SATA2. SATA2 works on serial communication protocol, which is pretty much like your PS/2 mouse and keyboard.

    Moreover, the size of these 2 cables differ. There's no way you can install both, unless your motherboard offer both IDE and SATA2 ports.

  • SoulDivine
    20 Dec 06, 01:05
    For your information. Almost all mainboards has PATA IDE interface. Without an PATA IDE interface, you cannot even install your internal cd-rom or dvd-rom drives.

    PATA = Parallel Advance Technology Attachment
    SATA = Serial Advance Technology Attachment
    HDD = Hard Disk Drive

    Next, you can have both PATA HDD and SATA HDD installed in your system. My current system has two SATA HDDs and one IDE HDD.

    In terms of pefromance, SATA HDDs are slightly faster than the PATA ones.

    In terms of installation, it is much easier to plug and unplug the SATA cables than PATA cables.

    Basically, there is no reason to use PATA HDD anymore unless your mainboard cannot support SATA HDD.

    It will be unfair to compare the pros and cons of this 2 generations of hard disk interface. The serial ATAs are slowly replacing the PATAs. However PATA hard disks are still around mainly because there's still the IDE connection available on new motherboards today. Its very unlike the sockets of processors which comes obsolete fast with almost every core changes. Also, there are external hard disk manufacturers using the IDE connection on PATA drives under their mod cases.

    However, a few quick comparison of the pros and cons of PATAs and SATAs are the parallel interface faces corss-talk with their ribbon cables while its greatly reduced for the slim serial ATA cable. Signal travels faster with the SATA's(I) 150 although before the SATA2 (300) came about it wasn't much of a difference between the fastest PATA 133 and SATA(I) 150. SATA hard disk's cables are almost 'hot swappable' and can be easily maintained in the PC case which in turns promotes better air flow and keeps the case cool, especially on smaller cases that tends to cramp for space. Also eATA makes communication and data transfer of like drives easier and reduces the cost of the need for data transfer interface cards and hardwares.

    The 2 drive interface exists for a purpose and until every options drive them/ it (i.e the PATAs) obsolete they will be around for quite some time.

    Originally posted by Croaking_Toad:
    So it's possible to, say, have a SATA HDD as a master and PATA HDD as a slave all on the same motherboard?
    When it comes to SATA there is nothing to worry about master or slave settings. The serial ATA connectors are numbered according to the default priority and the booting sequence of the drives connected goes with this rule unless you change it in the motherboard's BIOS. It is possible to have PATA drives and SATA drives in your computer. Even if you run short of IDE connectors i believe you can still get a controller card for it and plug it into the PCI2.3 slots.

    However when using the PATA drives always take note that the master/ slave settings have to sing in accord with the optical drive/s installed in the same system.

    Not to worry about loosing our IDE connections ever because there shall be at least one in every newer motherboards for the years to come. Optical drives (even the newer Blu-ray and HD-DVD) still relies on IDE/ATAPI connection interface. I believe only one or a few models of optical drives from Plextor supports native SATA connections on the motherboard.
