    To simplify, let's use CD ROM as example. CD ROM data can't be edited after it's been burned to a CD. That's the idea of a ROM. Yes, it means it can only be read, cannot be edited.

    EPROM, is slightly different. It is still read-only, however, it can edited, only under certain conditions. That certain condition is: It must be exposed to UV Ray. After exposing it to UV Rays, all data will be gone. To prevent accidental data loss, they are usually covered by some foil.

    How ROM works... well... exact details I don't know, I'm not an engineer.

    You can check out this article on how ROM works.

    A summary of what ROM is

    1. It stands for Read Only Memory. Data may be read, but not edited.

    2. It's a type of non-volatile memory. That means even if power is removed from it, the data will still be preserved.

    3. Even if it can be edited, certain conditions have to be met. In the example of EPROM, it has to be exposed to UV Rays for data to be erased, re-programmed and saved.

    ATX is a type of form factor. Form factor is one of the things you need to know to build a PC.

    ATX stands for Advanced Technology eXtended.

    ATX form factor is the most common type of form factor out there. Chances are, your own PC conforms to ATX form factor.

    ATX form factor is pretty easy to recognize. It uses a 20-pin (another 4-pin will plug somewhere else) or 24-pin for power supply, has quite a few expansion cards (PCI, PCI-Express, AGP), a few slots for CD/DVD drive, 2 or 3 slots for 3.5 floppy (though I doubt many use it today).

    There are also certain measurements, but off my head, I can't remember. It's around 30.5cm.

    Originally posted by Unidentified:
    so what the different between different form of ATX , difference in the number of pins ?
    There's only ONE ATX.

    The difference in the number of pins is due to technology. Old motherboards use 24 pins for power supply. I have 2 old motherboards, both are of ATX standards. They all use 24 pins.

    Custom built 3 PCs last year, they use a newer standard, 20 pins. Another 4 pins is plugged elsewhere.

    There are other form factors though.

    Before ATX, there was the AT. There's also MicroATX, BabyAT. These are the smaller versions of ATX and AT respectively. There are also rarer form factors such as NLX.
