    Freedom Fighter, Prisoner No. 46664, Statesman,
    Winner of the Nobel Peace Prize 1993]

    perhaps, forever, there’ll never be another if:
    the streets are fought with masses in desperation -
    while stopping at origination, knowing not where to go
    holding you tightly in the deepest language of love
    with flashes of images each freeing and fading off
    settling down in heart’s recess - seething profusely

    the nights were woken again to oppressor's cold rooms
    losing you - losing all courage - losing all that means
    remembering the day of freedom were rained with your departure

    foolishly without regret, without hatred -
    leaving the verbs of loneliness and helplessness

    perhaps I will choose to wait in another destiny -
    knowing that terms of freedom is never easy or fair
    and seen those laughing faces behind the scenes
    leaving only the hurting heart in parentheses

    perhaps, forever, there’ll never be another if:
    when would this pain of entanglement end?
    helplessly losing you in the chains of the seas

    Isn’t it extraordinary that our minds cannot stay still for longer than a few moments without grasping after distraction? They are so restless and preoccupied that sometimes I think that living in a city in the modern world, we are already like the tormented beings in the intermediate state after death, where the consciousness is said to be agonizingly restless.

    We are fragmented into so many different aspects. We don’t know who we really are, or what aspects of ourselves we should identify with or believe in. So many contradictory voices, dictates, and feelings fight for control over our inner lives that we find ourselves scattered everywhere, in all directions, leaving nobody at home.

    Meditation, then, is bringing the mind home.
