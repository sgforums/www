    t is one of the oldest maxims in football – lose the dressing room and you end up losing the battle.

    Rafa Benitez is likely to discover the veracity of that saying over the next few months.

    No-one is above criticism. No-one. That includes Steven Gerrard and Jamie Carragher.

    But even if a manager feels that one man who is arguably the most complete player in the world and another who has proved his physical courage beyond any debate or question are at fault, such words should be delivered in the inner sanctum of the dressing room.

    Gerrard and Carragher can take it. They are big boys.

    But once such verbal assaults are made in the public arena, it is the start of a slippery slope.

    Gerrard and Carragher this week – whose turn will it be after the next Liverpool defeat? That will be going through the minds of the players.

    Great managers keep their views private and in-house.

    I can recall Sir Alex Ferguson being asked after a Fabien Barthez howler had cost Manchester United a match what the French goalkeeper had said in the dressing room afterwards.

    That Glaswegian stare came to the fore as he answered: ”What goes on in my dressing room is absolutely none of your business. Ok ?”

    End of conversation.

    Over at The Emirates, Arsene Wenger has owned up to regular attacks of myopia when one of his players has been guilty of a misdemeanour. “I didn’t see it,” is now his famous, catch-all response.

    Over at Tottenham, Harry Redknapp made light of a glaring miss by Darren Bent by insisting his wife Sandra could have scored.

    Cue laugh.

    A dig at Bent but not in a humiliating fashion. An off-the-cuff remark.

    But Benitez doesn’t do humour. That’s a fact.

    It filters out that he is unhappy at the funds with which he is provided and yet some estimates show that his sell-buy balance is in excess of 100 million. That’s a fact.

    And like Phil Brown at Hull, who famously – or infamously – paraded his shattered players on the pitch for a half-time rollocking at Manchester City in front of the travelling fans, the line between respect and resentment is gossamer-thin.

    Hull won just one more league game after the public humiliation last Boxing Day and stayed up more through what other teams didn’t do than what they did themselves.

    They say you reap what you sow.

    Benitez has just sown the seeds of discontent and there could well be a price to pay.

    That’s a fact.
