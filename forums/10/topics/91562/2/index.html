    I can't help but smile at the sensible reply that Qitai made.

    While I sympathize with the frustrations many diploma holders have for failing to secure an interview because of the lack of sufficent paper qualifications, it is just as stereotypical to portray degree-holders as

    "idiots at work. They can conceptualize, come up with good presentations...but cannot carry out anything bcoz they cannot get anyone to work for them...their socialising skills and PR farktup lah. Double standard...cannot carry out what they preach... " (Troy437, 30 Aug 2004)

    It's simply a sweeping, ill-considered cliche to portray those who work hard in school to get to universities as incompetent, clueless and socially-inept.

    I couldn't help but carry this illogical misconception a step further: does that mean that primary school dropouts also have a case against diploma holders? ("all these diploma holders all idiot lor! industrial attachment many, but hokkien damn lao ya, think they go poly waste time only!")

    Academically, the same discipline (e.g engineering or accounting) is taught differently in the universities and polytechnics, in terms of depth, scope and focus. It doesnt take a lot of common sense to recognise this.

    While a degree may open more doors, and secure the all important first interview, ultimately, it is like what many cool-headed people here have pointed out: it's the ability and performance that counts.

    More and more people *around the world* are opting for a university education. This has stretched the operating budgets, manpower resources and facilities of many tertiary institutions including prestigious ones. These are regularly reported in international media (for example UK's Guardian Online under its Education column).

    Singaporeans are no exception in recognising that a degree gives the holder a chance for a better life (improving lifestyle, self-fulfillment, more job security, opportunities to do more things they want). This is a reality that people who complain about their lack of formal qalifications should recognise quickly. It's pure naivety if people think degree-holders have an easier life at work and do not have their share of discontentment. Perhaps the fact that Singapore is a country with 4.2 million people crowded on a small land mass amplifies these frustrations, and makes the issue appear larger and louder compared to other much bigger countries. But really, which country's populace doesnt aspire to a better life for themselves and their families, or where materialism hasn't taken a foothold?

    If diploma holders think they are being short-changed, there is no other alternative but to prove themselves (not only work performance, but in terms of training, certification and paper qualifications). Do something constructive, and stop whining.
