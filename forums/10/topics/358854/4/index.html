    Family suspects interference in David's case

    The Jakarta Post ,  Jakarta   |  Wed, 06/10/2009 1:02 PM  |  National

    The family of David Hartanto Widjaja, an Indonesian student who died at Singapore's Nanyang Technological University (NTU) on March 2, and a local verification team doubt the Singaporean court process of his death.

    The coroner court under the subordinate court in Singapore has been processing the case to decide whether David's death was a suicide. If it not, then the case will be forwarded to the criminal court.

    However, it appeared the court process was intentionally directed to a conclusion of suicide despite evidence showing a strong possibility of murder, according to David's family and the verification team.

    "We have strong evidence that he was murdered but that fact was not brought up in the court," David's father, Hartono Wijaya, told a press conference Tuesday at the National Commission on Human Rights.

    "Testimonies from witnesses presented by the NTU were fictitious and none of our witnesses was brought to the court."

    Djaja Surya Atmadja, a forensic expert from the Faculty of Medicine in the University of Indonesia, said any forensic doctor could see from the forensic report that David's body had defense wounds, and that there were no wounds of suicide.

    Bright student, dark problems

    By Chong Shin Yen and Amanda Yong

    HE WAS an outstanding student who came to Singapore to study on an Asean scholarship.

    In his first year, David Hartanto Widjaja, 21, focused on his studies at the Nanyang Technological University (NTU). But from the second year onwards, he changed.
    The electrical and electronic engineering (EEE) student would study only when exams were near.

    Instead of aiming to score well, he just wanted to pass.

    By the time he reached his final year, things had become so bad that he was scared of the supervisor of his Final Year Project (FYP), Associate Professor Chan Kap Luk, 45.

    So much so that he avoided the professor and would sneak away rather than face him because he had fallen behind in his FYP.

    His close friend, Mr Hardian Setiawan Winata, revealed this yesterday at the coroner's inquiry into Widjaja's death.

    The Indonesian fell four storeys to his death moments after he allegedly stabbed Prof Chan in his office at about 10.30am on 2 Mar.

    Mr Hardian, an Indonesian who is in the same course at NTU, told the court that he noticed that Widjaja began skipping his lessons and tutorials in August 2007.

    His grades worsened because he was spending a lot of time playing computer games in his hostel room.

    'He told me that he can play more than 10 hours a day,' said Mr Hardian.

    Then last August, Widjaja received an e-mail from the school regarding his poor grades.

    Said Mr Hardian: 'He showed (the warning letter) to me at the beginning of our final year.

    'David said it was okay. He said if his scholarship was terminated, his family can support him.'

    By then, Widjaja was close to finishing his course and would have to pay only for his last semester if he lost his scholarship.

    Avoiding professor

    Mr Hardian, who is from West Java, and Widjaja became close friends in May 2007 after they were in the same project group. Mr Hardian said Widjaja shared his problems with him while they were working on his FYP and Human Resource Management project (HRM).

    'He complained to me that the HRM project meetings took up a lot of time, disrupting his FYP,' he said.

    Mr Hardian also said that Widjaja started to struggle with his FYP and tried to avoid Prof Chan, who was pressing him for updates.

    'I could see David was scared of Prof Chan,' he said.

    Mr Hardian recounted an incident late last year when the two students wanted to go to a laboratory to work on their assignments.

    He said: 'But when David saw Prof Chan in the lab, he sneaked away. He avoided Prof Chan because he had no updates on his FYP. He was really busy (with his HRM project).'

    Still, Widjaja did not appear worried about his FYP. Hardian said this was because he had one year to work on it. It was not mentioned how much time he was given to work on his HRM project.

    The court also heard that Prof Chan would meet his FYP and post-graduate students every Wednesday evening, and the student would take turns to give updates on their projects.

    Widjaja told Mr Hardian that during one such meeting in the first semester last year, Prof Chan scolded him for failing to give an update on his FYP.

    Mr Hardian said that Widjaja played World of Warcraft and Defence of the Ancients.

    But last December, he noticed that his friend was hooked on another online game called Destiny Online. While playing this game, he got to know a girl whom he was interested in.

    'He told me he liked her. But he never showed me the girl's photograph even though I'd asked to see it,' said Mr Hardian.

    He added that Widjaja communicated with the girl online and over the phone, but he did not know if they had met in person.

    Two pathologists from the Health Science Authority also took the stand yesterday.

    Dr Marian Wang, who conducted the autopsy, told the court that Mr Widjaja had 36 external injuries, which included abrasions, lacerations, cuts and bruises on his hands, head, fingers, and legs.

    She said that there were 18 superficial cuts and one deep cut on the body.

    'They could be inflicted while David was involved in a struggle with another party to gain control of the knife,' said Dr Wang.

    She added that the other injuries sustained were consistent with those caused by a fall from height.

    'He died from multiple injuries and it's impossible to isolate the most serious injury,' she said.

    She was supervised by Dr Gilbert Lau, who was present during the autopsy.

    Criminal lawyer Shashi Nathan, who was hired by Widjaja's family to hold a watching brief, wanted to know if a faint bruise on his neck could have been caused by a punch or a karate chop.

    Dr Lau replied that he was 'rather intrigued' by the proposition. 'I have not been informed by the police of any martial artist involved (in the case),' he said.

    He then said that it was very unlikely, as a punch or karate chop would have caused more serious injuries, such as the rupture of the voice box instead of just a faint bruise.

    Widjaja's parents, his elder brother and uncle flew in from Jakarta on Tuesday and were in court yesterday.

    Also present were representatives from NTU, officials from the Indonesian Embassy here and the Indonesian media. The inquiry continues today with Prof Chan taking the stand.

    ON 2 Mar at about 10.30am, Indonesian student David Hartanto Widjaja, 21, fell four storeys to his death at the Nanyang Technological University.

    The incident happened moments after he allegedly stabbed his final year project supervisor, Associate Professor Chan Kap Luk, 45.

    The Jakarta Post, 20 Jun 2009

    Photos prove student did not commit suicide: Forensics expert

    A forensics expert insists that recently revealed photographs of the body of David Hartanto Widjaja, an Indonesian student who died at Singapore's Nanyang Technological University (NTU) on March 2, prove he died defending himself against knife attacks.

    Djaja Surya Atmadja, a noted forensic expert from the University of Indonesia who had seen the photographs, said Friday that all wounds on the body emphasized the fact David had not committed suicide as reported.

    The photographs, which The Jakarta Post also obtained, show several defense wounds that have never been revealed in or out of the Singaporean court, Djaja says.

    "I can assume, based on the photographs, David was attacked by one or two people with a knife," he told the Post in a telephone conversation.

    Early reports in the Singaporean media had accused David of stabbing his professor at NTU, Chan Kap Luk, before slitting his wrists and jumping to his death.

    One of several defense wounds found on the body is a deep diagonal 20-centimeter-long cut on the right hand near the wrist, Djaja said after observing the photographs.

    A diagonal cut, he said, showed David was more likely to have been warding off a knife attack against him.

    "If David wanted to commit suicide, he would not have cut his hand that way *diagonally*, but with a flat cut along his wrist," Djaja said.

    The pictures also showed David's right leg had been twisted, which Djaja believes was caused by somebody, or was sustained because the leg had been stuck before David fell.

    "I'm sure the twisted leg was not caused by the fall," Djaja said.

    The photographs also showed David's right palm was covered in small flat wounds.

    Such wounds could have been caused by David clenching the knife in a defensive position, Djaja said.

    Djaja said he would attend the next hearing at the Singaporean coroner's court on June 24 and 25, to explain the forensic facts, based on photographs, that wounds on David's body were defense injuries.

    Meanwhile, the lawyer for David's family, Otto Cornelius Kaligis told the Post he would undertake whatever measures were necessary to prove David was not guilty and that he had been attacked.

    "I have found many irregularities in the legal processing of David's case in Singapore," Kaligis said, adding that he suspected the Singaporean officials had been covering up many significant facts.

    David's father, Hartono Widjaja, said his family had tried to present nine of David's NTU fellow students as witnesses to the court, but only one was approved by court officials.

    "But all 27 witnesses from NTU's side were approved," he said.

    Both Kaligis and Hartono suspected the court process was intentionally directed to a conclusion of suicide, despite evidence showing a strong possibility of murder.

    Only if the coroner court finds it was not suicide will the case be forwarded to the criminal court.

    In current usage, the perception of the term has been extended beyond second identities of people who already post in a forum to include other uses of misleading online identities.
