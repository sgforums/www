    There's an article in the Straits Times (dated 9th October 2001) which described about a Malaysian Lt-Colonel who write comment on using water as a kind of strategic weapon to counter S'pore military superiority. Below is the full-content of the article described in Straits Times. Any comments?

    -----------------------------------
    An article in a Malaysian Armed Forces College journal has called on Malaysia to use water as a 'strategic weapon' to 'counter Singapore's military advantage'.

    Written by a lieutenant-colonel who used to be with the Royal Malay Regiment - the Sultan of Johor's military arm - and who is now with the Malaysian Army headquarters in Kuala Lumpur, the article argued that because water is of 'fundamental, primary interest' to the city-state, Malaysia should be 'more forceful' in controlling its supply to the Republic.

    Lt-Col Azmy Yahya wrote: 'As 'owner' of the precious resource, Malaysia must make full use of this advantage as leverage over Singapore to ensure limited room for Singapore to manoeuvre, and to reduce the incentive for Singapore to use its military superiority as leverage to threaten Malaysia.'

    Noting that the existing water agreements, signed in 1961 and 1962, had very long time frames, he said: 'Malaysia should have the will to pressure or make known to Singapore that it will not hesitate to review the agreements if Singapore is not sensitive, or if it continues patronising Malaysia in any way that would affect bilateral relations.'

    He added: 'Malaysia has to be more forceful in controlling water supply to Singapore.'

    Lt-Col Azmy's remarks were in an article analysing water as a factor in international relations. It appeared in Strategi, the Malaysian Armed Forces' annual journal, recently.

    Noting that international law offered little help in resolving water conflicts, he cited how shared water resources in the Middle East, Central Asia and South Asia had led to tension, and in some cases to violence, as in Kyrgzstan, where a dispute in 1990 cost 300 lives.

    He focused most attention on the Singapore-Malaysia situation, where, he reasoned, historical animosities were unlikely to pass even with the passing of first-generation leaders, because younger Malaysian leaders 'have learnt the utility of harnessing anti-Singapore sentiment for personal political gain'.

    He said the existing agreements were seen by many quarters in Malaysia as unfavourable to Malaysia, because while Singapore bought raw water from Johor at three sen (1.4 Singapore cents) for every 4,500 litres, it sold treated water back at 50 sen for the same amount.

    The current water review is appropriate if it results in terms more favourable to Malaysia, he said. But he also pointed out that 'it cannot be taken for granted that Malaysia will continue to accede to Singapore's appetite for more water'.

    The new price of water is among the issues Singapore and Malaysia are still haggling over. The negotiating range is between 45 and 60 sen per 4,500 litres.

    Noting a 'clear need' to resolve the water issue, Lt-Col Azmy assessed that one option open to Malaysia if Singapore decided to use its military strength to ensure its water supply was to 'pollute the supply with either chemical or biological agents'.

    This should be done only as a last resort, and it could also be 'morally wrong', but it was Malaysia's right to defend its sovereignty if Singapore took the path of military aggression, he said.

    In the meantime, he said, Singapore must introduce more confidence-building measures. The current ones, in the form of the Five Power Defence Arrangements and the joint military exercises, must be expanded to ties at the personal level.

    Singapore's leadership must also take more care with the bilateral relationship. 'There is a need for them to be less patronising and more sensitive, and to accord Malaysia the respect it deserves,' he said.
