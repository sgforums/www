    Tunku 'thought S'pore would fail'
    KUALA LUMPUR - Berita Minggu columnist Abdullah Ahmad said Malaysia's founding father Tunku Abdul Rahman was wrong to have believed that expelling Singapore from Malaysia would see the Republic flounder and become backward.

    Writing in his weekly column published in the Malay-language daily on Sunday, he said the Tunku also thought that in doing so, problems between the Chinese and Malay communities would be resolved.

    The wide-ranging column by Tan Sri Abdullah, the editor-in-chief of the New Straits Times Group, coincided with the 100th anniversary of Tunku's birth and touched, in part, on the issue of Singapore's separation in 1965.

    He said the late Umno stalwart Tan Sri Syed Jaafar Albar - father of current Foreign Minister Syed Hamid Albar - had opposed Singapore's expulsion.

    Tan Sri Syed Jaafar's stance led to him being sidelined by the Umno leadership and the government for a time.

    'In retrospect, in view of the regional and global political turmoil today, it is clear that Albar had a strong case (which the majority did not perceive or deliberately did not want to accept what was logical then),' he wrote.

    Tan Sri Abdullah said 'our territory was surrendered by Tunku Abdul Rahman Putra on a silver platter to Lee Kuan Yew 38 years ago'.

    'How wrong he was. Tunku thought that by removing Singapore from Malaysia... calm would return for as long as there were stars and the moon.'

    He ended by saying: 'Isn't Singapore Chinese? How can the Chinese become backward? Which Chinese will compromise if he feels he is strong?'
