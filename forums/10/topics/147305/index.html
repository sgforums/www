    Can the GLC companies earn money when they are always the late-comer into any profitable business ?

    We have seen the late entry of GLCs into the chip making industry - losing out in competitiveness to the Taiwanese and Koreans.

    We have also seen the $500 Million dollar bust in the late entry into the Hard Disk Drive industry, with the complete takeover of Micropolis.

    We have also seen the failed attempts to bust the profitable stranglehold of the local businesses in commodities - (rice, sugar, steel, cement, stone, sand, etc) - at a loss that remains the speculation of Singaporean's imagination.

    We have yet to see a GLC taking the lead into any 'sun rise' industry - venturing into new fields of technologies, of research and development; and even when decisions are made to invest in such technologies, it is always in an area that some other countries have also laid their eyes on.

    The Government has place emphasis on Biotech Research - in the hope of reaping the benefits of research into new medicines, and this is also an area that is targetted by the First World countries of France, Germany, Japan, Korea, UK and the USA.

    Can we succeed on our own effort ?

    The answer is 'No' - as Singapore lack men with vision, guts and political strength to push a program through, when all decisions are made from the very TOP.

    Do the talents employed by the Ruling Elites have the vision and political strength to bring Singapore in the right direction, or are their vision being tweaked and limited in the direction of the focus preferred by the Ruling Elite ?

    We have instead, taken a safer route, by entering into joint-ventures with other MNCs, or some reputable research groups, pumping our money as contribution in place of expertise.

    We should perhaps learn from Brazil, who have invested money in the search for an ALTERNATIVE fuel to oil.

    Brazil has succeeded to make usable fuel out of sugar-cane, which has been successfully tried out to fuel its domestically manufactured jet aircraft - the Embraer; and the fuel cost only US$25 per barrel, compared to the last reported current price of US$67 per barrel.

    The search for alternative is now on, with France and Germany experimenting with the stalks of wheat, and China experimenting with maize.

    Singapore alternates with Rotterdam to claim the first and second spot as the World's largest refining and oil trading center.

    Will either one suffer a demise, when alternative fuel become a roaring success, and soon the huge refinery complex may turn into ghost facilities.

    In the early 18th Century, whale oil was the common fuel that lights up homes and public places. When the new fuel - 'kerosene' - was discovered in the 19th Century, many of the whalers were skeptical to the new challenge, which finally overwhelmed the whalers to the benefit of the whales in our ocean.

    Who will benefit when our refinery complex shuts down, as the New Fuel become the STAR PERFORMER ?
