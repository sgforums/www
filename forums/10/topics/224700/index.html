    IF anyone thought the Goverment might be rethinking its strategy to develop the biomedical sector here, several big guns from A*Star were quick to dismiss it on Tuesday.
    They said the Government is fully committed to the current approach of hiring world-class researchers and letting them work on major diseases such as cancer - a policy which had come under fire recently from National Neuroscience Institute head Dr Lee Wei Ling.

    They also defended the achievements of Singapore's multibillion-dollar biomedical sciences (BMS) drive.

    'The BMS initiative has been successful and there is no rethink or change in the Government's BMS policy,' said Dr Beh Swan Gin, executive director of the Biomedical Research Council at the Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*Star).

    The statement comes in the wake of remarks made by Dr Lee last November in an article in The Straits Times and which she repeated in a Reuters interview last Friday.

    Dr Lee had said that the Government should reassess its strategy for creating a biomedical industry after spending billions of dollars without achieving significant results.

    'All I can say is that maybe they are having a rethink,' she added.

    In the interview, Dr Lee had also suggested that Singapore should invest in niche areas of research such as Asian diseases.

    Quashing suggestions of a change in policy, Dr Beh read out the Government's position on the issue at a media conference to review the performance of the BMS industry last year.

    But he added that Singapore will always continue to adjust its strategies, depending on how they perform.

    'The aim at the end of the day is to ensure we are on the right track,' he said.

    Outgoing A*Star chairman Philip Yeo and his successor Lim Chuan Poh, as well as Institute of Molecular and Cell Biology chief David Lane, also weighed in on the debate.

    On the alleged lack of results, they emphasised that the biomedical initiative is a long-haul effort that typically takes 10 to 15 years to blossom.

    'You have to give it time to show success. Some of these things cannot be done in a matter of three years or five years,' said Lieutenant-General (NS) Lim. 'We are going to stay the course.'

    But what Singapore has achieved was already attracting international attention, with many countries trying to emulate Singapore's success, said Mr Yeo.

    Lt-Gen (NS) Lim also defended the policy of attracting big names from the research world while nurturing local talent.

    'Why must it be that Singaporeans cannot be world-beaters?' he asked.

    'We have bright young Singaporeans who compete in the top league. If we don't create the kind of place for them to realise these ambitions, they will be overseas competing for other people.'

    Star researchers such as Sir David are the 'whales' needed here as role models for the pool of young 'guppies' that A*Star is grooming, explained Mr Yeo.

    On why an upstart such as Singapore should work on major diseases, Sir David noted that the major causes of death here - illnesses such as heart diseases and cancer - are the same as top killers worldwide.

    At the briefing, Mr Yeo also distributed to reporters an e-mail written by Nobel laureate Sydney Brenner of the Salk Institute for Biological Studies in the United States.

    In it, Dr Brenner reaffirmed Singapore's focus on diseases that are rising in incidence here and that have public health value, such as cancer. 'We should not accept that Singapore is a second class country only fit for cast-offs of the major countries,' he wrote.

    Characterising Dr Lee's criticism as 'a voice in the wilderness', Mr Yeo said: 'For a person who has not been here (to Biopolis) to make comments, I leave it to that person. The Singapore Government is fully committed to what we have done.'

    (Straits Times, 6 Feb)
