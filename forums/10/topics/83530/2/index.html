    Prior to her founding, Singapore was perceived as part of the Malay peninsular.

    Then the British came and Singapore (along with Penang and Malacca) grew a somewhat separate identity as part of the Straits Settlements.

    Because the Straits Settlements was the hub of business activities, many Chinese were spontaneously drawn to settle down in the area.

    When the British left, their plan was to merge Malaya and Singapore (and Sabah and Sarawak) so as to increase the viability of these newly independent entities.

    Racial politics caused the inevitable rift between the two main (but unequal) partners of the marriage.

    Today, globalisation may render the need to re-marry unnecessary. Good relations need to continue to be built, and not just between Singapore and Malaysia, but with the rest of ASEAN, Asia, and the rest of the world. We need to cultivate our sense of inter-dependence. The recent announcement on the impending links between the Singapore and Malaysian stock markets is a step in the right direction.
