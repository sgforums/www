    SINGAPORE, April 9 (Reuters) - Singapore's government ministers, already among the world's highest paid, will see their annual salaries jump by about 60 percent to an average of S$1.9 million ($1.26 million), a minister told parliament on Monday.

    Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong will see his salary rise to S$3.1 million ($2.05 million) from an estimated S$2 million, which will be five times the $400,000 that U.S. president George Bush earns.

    "For the public service to remain an attractive employer, our terms must keep pace with the private sector," Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean, who is also minister in charge of the civil service, said in parliament.

    The salary increase -- which will take effect in two steps by the end of next year -- is slightly smaller than the government had originally proposed.

    Prime Minister Lee said last month the salaries of Singapore ministers and top civil servants might have to rise to as much as S$2.2 million (US$1.45 million) because they had fallen way below benchmark top salaries in the private sector.

    The announcement sparked an outcry. Hundreds of Singaporeans signed an online petition and wrote to the state-controlled newspapers to protest against the move.

    Some Singaporeans said the salaries do not reflect the country's economy or the government's performance, and that the government was tactless to raise ministers' salaries now given Singapore's widening income gap.

    Since 1994, the salaries of Singapore ministers have been set at two-thirds the median pay of the 48 best-paid bankers, lawyers, accountants, engineers, and executives in multi-nationals and manufacturing firms.

    Expect a modest pay hike as Singapore job market heats up
    By Lee U-Wen and Lin Yanqin, TODAY | Posted: 09 April 2007 1030 hrs

    The job market may be heating up in Singapore, but don't expect a huge jump this year when the boss reviews your salary.

    Real pay increases for some groups in Singapore could range from 2.8 to 3.2 per cent, according to a study by human resource consultancy Hay Group.

    The company's Global Pay Day report revealed that salary hikes for administrative staff and senior management here could go up by 2.8 per cent, while professionals may have it slightly better Â— at 3.2 per cent. These figures are a dip from the average of 3.8 per cent predicted by it in its 2006 report.

    On the whole, this year's salary increase predictions Â— taken after adjusting for inflation Â— are modest compared to the rest of South-east Asia. Of the other five countries in the region, Indonesia is likely to see the highest increase of 4.7 per cent, followed by Vietnam (4.5 per cent), Singapore, Malaysia (2.7 to 3 per cent), the Philippines (1.5 to 2.5 per cent) and Thailand (1.9 to 2 per cent).

    When benchmarked against the rest of Asia, the expected pay increase for Singapore's professionals, for instance, lags far behind that of China (7.8 per cent), India (5.9 per cent) and South Korea (4 per cent).

    Shedding some light on this, Hay Group's reward information services managing director (Asia) Roland Ruiz said Singapore's current salary levels are already among the highest in the region, which explains the smaller percentage growth.

    Even so, the findings buck the trend set by recent surveys that said workers in Singapore could look forward to better bonuses and bigger pay hikes, in light of a healthy economy and record job creation last year.

    Employers here, said Mr Ruiz, are "cautious" about raising salaries because regional economies remain a source of risk, with "economic and political instability, potential interruption in regional production chains and financial weakness".

    "Singapore employers (have) not taken an immediate response on salaries to align with a clearly more buoyant economy in 2006, partly because they take a longer term view when it comes to increasing their fixed costs to the business," he told Today.

    On pay matters, private sector employers also usually take their cue from the civil service. The National Wages Council, meanwhile, is due to deliberate on wage guidelines for 2007/2008 later this month.

    Still, he said, things were looking up thanks to the tightening labour market. "There are many jobs chasing after a smaller pool of people, so there is now a much clearer case for employers to act on salary growth," he said.

    Job sectors likely to face pressure to increase salaries are those that saw significant growth last year, such as marine and offshore engineering, biomedical manufacturing, construction, wholesale and retail trade and financial services. "There will be continuing pressure to increase wages for critical positions in these industries since continuing growth is expected from them," said Mr Ruiz.
