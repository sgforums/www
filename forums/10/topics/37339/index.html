    Bleak outlook for Singapore
    Hardev Kaur (Mar 6 2003)

    Singapore, which once boasted the regionÂ’s best economic performance, faces unprecedented challenges including a lack of entrepreneurship among its people and rising unemployment, which has eroded consumer confidence.

    WHILE its neighbours, Malaysia, Indonesia and Thailand, were subjected to currency manipulations and speculations, Singapore's economy escaped the financial crisis in 1997-1998, registering Gross Domestic Growth (GDP) of 1.3 per cent in 1998. In fact the republic's economy was once one of the region's best performers, enjoying 10.3 per cent growth in 2000. In 2001 it fell into its worst recession since the 1960s, hit by a slump in exports due to the world economic slowdown and the collapse of the hi-tech sector.

    It registered a growth of 2.2 per cent last year and its outlook remains uncertain. Malaysia, on the other hand, recovered from the financial crisis to record a growth of 4.2 per cent.

    The impact of the sluggish world economy and the increased competition from the region and emerging China are beginning to have an impact on Singapore's economy. It faces risks of slower growth, rising structural unemployment and new uncertainties. According to the republic's Deputy Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong, "Singapore is at a turning point". Lee, who is also Finance Minister, said Singapore faces "not just greater economic volatility but also new political and security uncertainties".

    Singapore is not completely over the 2001 recession and Lee says "competition is keener and changes are coming faster..." Reports point out that the "iron bowl" of job security is cracking in Singapore as government bodies and state-linked companies alike trim their workforce in a massive restructuring exercise. Singapore's unemployment has risen from 3.3 per cent in 2001 to 4.4 per cent last year and could rise above 5.5 per cent. The outlook is uncertain and downside risks remain, according to Tan Leng Leng, director for Research and Statistics in Singapore's Ministry of Manpower.

    Layoffs have been on an unprecedented scale. "It certainly is a sign of the times for Singapore Inc," said David Cohen, economist from MMS International. Workers struggling with the current economic slowdown were disappointed that the Budget tabled in Parliament last week did not bring much monetary relief. Preliminary estimates indicate that 18,900 workers were retrenched in Singapore last year and this is in addition to the 25,800 retrenched in 2001. In the first two months of 2003, PSA Corp announced that it is cutting its force by 800 or 13.3 per cent for the first time in more than 20 years. The supremacy of PSA, which operates one of the busiest ports in the world, has been challenged with the emergence of rival ports in the region. In fact it lost two of its prominent clients Â— Danish firm Maersk Sealand and Taiwan's Evergreen Marine Corp over the last two years to Tanjung Pelepas in Malaysia.

    This prompted PSA to overhaul its strategy, which includes offering clients rebates, but that strategy has proved to be insufficient.

    Neptune Orient Lines is also cutting 1,100 jobs worldwide of its 12,000 workforce worldwide. Chartered Semiconductor will cut 500 jobs this month when its wafer fabrication facility closes. The job losses are not limited to the private sector. The Housing and Development Board, the Government's housing arm, is reducing its labour force, shedding between 2,500 and 3,000 of its 8,000 workers. The retrenchments will add to the pain Singaporeans are already enduring due to a severe downturn in 2001 when the economy produced a two per cent contraction. Singapore is losing its edge due to its rising costs. According to Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong, Singapore needs "surgery" to create new growth in the city state's trade-driven economy. The deficit budget aimed at stimulating growth and cutting business costs lacked in major policy initiatives. The Finance Minister tabled the third straight budget deficit for the republic which has boasted of budget surpluses since the 1970s.

    Analysts did not expect it to have much impact on the overall economy. "It's been a major disappointment ...much ado about nothing," Joseph Tan, economist at Standard Chartered Bank is reported as saying. The business expectations survey shows that manufacturers expect business conditions to remain lacklustre in the first half of this year. Sentiments have also worsened among firms in the commerce, real estate, transport and storage sectors. Singapore's economy has undergone a radical change in recent years, most notably in the financial sector where the Government has introduced a series of changes aimed at pushing the local players to become more competitive. Some of the world's well-known lenders such as Standard Chartered Bank and HSBC were granted licences to offer a full range of financial services and the securities industry was liberalised. But even so the financial services sector reversed sharply from a 3.7 per cent growth in 2001 to a contraction of 4.8 per cent in 2002.

    The stockbroking activity was adversely affected by poor sentiments in view of the global uncertainties. The outlook for Singapore is mixed. With an uncertain world economic scenario, a possible US-led war against Iraq, rising oil prices and contracting world trade, Singapore is facing a bleak future.

    Salomon Smith Barney points out that in terms of contagion, Singapore "should suffer greatly" due to its open economy, oil imbalance and tourism dependence, than Thailand or the Philippines.

    Undoubtedly, Singapore will not be the only economy to be affected by the war. But the impact on the republic would be much higher than others in the region as the "Singapore economy may not be in the best of shape", according to Dow Jones. At the same time the world economic scenario is not encouraging.

    The uncertain international environment will affect trade and services, including tourism. The "biggest challenge" for Singapore is deemed to be from China. Its entry into the World Trade Organisation has meant that Singapore is facing tough competition from regional rivals. The domestic scenario is also challenging. There is a lack of entrepreneurship among its people and consumer confidence in the republic has been dampened by the rise in unemployment and a decline in the stock market.

    ASIAN ECONOMIC OUTLOOK: SINGAPORE

    Elusive Recovery

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    By Trish Saywell

    Issue cover-dated February 13, 2003

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    IN HIS ANNUAL New Year message, Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong told Singaporeans that there was a "reasonable chance" 2003 would be better than 2002, but that most likely they "will have to wait till 2004 for a full recovery."

    The Monetary Authority of Singapore, the country's de facto central bank, is forecasting GDP growth of between 2% and 5% this year. In 2002, the economy grew an estimated 2.2%--a rebound from 2001 when Singapore was badly stung by its worst recession since independence in 1965.

    In its twice-yearly macroeconomic review in January the Monetary Authority predicted the economy will remain sluggish in the first half of 2003 before a more "firmly grounded" recovery ensues in the second half--assuming improved external conditions. "The frequency of shocks buffeting the Singapore economy has increased since the mid-1990s," it said in its report.

    Weakening of global electronics demand in information-technology markets has become a particular bugbear, as Singapore, like many other Asian countries, has over the past decade increased production of semiconductors--the prices of which are highly volatile and react strongly to production cycles in developed countries. Singapore's domestic electronics industry makes up almost 10% of the trade-dependent nation's GDP; and related industries such as fabricated metals and machinery together constitute another 4.6%. Singapore's key nonoil exports rose a less-than-forecast 4.4% in December--bringing total nonoil exports for 2002 to S$98.6 billion ($57 billion), up 1.9% year on year.

    Economic uncertainty and geopolitical concerns will likely impede the investment plans of both foreign and domestic companies this year, the Economic Development Board warns. The government agency responsible for attracting investments to the island says it expects fixed-asset investment in the manufacturing and services sector and total business spending to fall this year to S$9.9 billion from S$11.1 billion last year.

    Concerns over economic recovery and further terrorist attacks, meanwhile, continue to dampen domestic consumer and tourist sentiments. Unemployment remains a worry. At the end of September, the unemployment rate reached 4.8%--the highest level since December 1986. The Ministry of Manpower stated recently that a record 12,900 graduates were jobless in September, more than double the 6,300 in the same period at the height of the Asian financial crisis in 1998.
