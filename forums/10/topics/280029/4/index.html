    Generally, Pension Funds are placed with approved private investment managers, that will provide higher rates of return, but at the same time will exposed the participants to higher risks that can shrink the Fund in the event of mismanagement or political and-or economic risk factors.

    Provident Funds are subjected to less risks, which earn far less returns on this long term saving plan for final disbursement at time of retirement.

    In essence, a Pension Fund is almost similar to an 'Annuity Scheme' - in which a retiree is looked after for till his last breath through a 'Fixed Annuity'.

    While a Provident Fund is supposed to pay out a lump-sum to the stake-holder on his retirement - based on the type of Provident Fund that is set up.

    What will our Singapore CPF model morphed into after 40 odd years ?

    It started fine as a Provident Fund - with many who retired up to the mid-1980s receiving a whole lump sum of their CPF; while those who retired during the mid-1990s had received partial portion of their CPF in a lump sum - with the remaining amount paid out by instalment, and some parts being deducted for all kinds of medical and hospitalisation insurance plans.

    As we approach the end of 2007, plans are currently developed to prevent any lump sum from being paid, and with more amount being held back for later payment by instalments in some Annuity Scheme to be developed with Cash Inputs from our Provident Fund.

    Unlike other Pension Scheme, and Annuity Plans, it is now proposed that Singaporeans shall pay for our own pension through an Annuity Scheme.

    If we will critically look at the original CPF format, it is more like a Provident Fund similar to a ''Pay-as-you-go'' - where the outgoings to pay pensions to ''today's pensioners'' are paid for by the contributions of ''today's workers and employers''.

    The CPF system was ''based on a philosphy of 'intergenerational solidarity' - where today's workers support older workers''.

    The CPF is a '' national pension scheme and is essentially an 'unfunded pension scheme' - it accepts responsibility to provide retirement benefits to stakeholders, but do not set aside moneys today to meet future obligations''.

    The Singapore Government do not 'pump' any funds into the CPF despite making profits from the utilisation of the cheap CPF money, and only return a miserable 2% interest to our CPF account for the better part of one's working life contributing into the CPF.

    Is this not similar to the infamous ''Pyramid Scheme'' except that there are no products involved, and with more legitimacy as it is annointed by the Government ?

    To be successful, a ''Pyramid Scheme'' require a large base to support the narrow apex.

    Similarly, the CPF scheme based on its original plans require a continuous and ever growing number of stake holders to support the few retiring at the top, who will withdraw a larger accumulated lump sum paid out with a compounded interest of 2% per year.

    With a declining population being a known factor since the mid-1980s, changes were already afoot with the Howe Yoon Chong Committee coming out to amend the CPF pay-out rules, and this tinkering with the CPF Pay-out has continued over the last 20 years till this day.
