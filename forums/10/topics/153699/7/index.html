    No need for competition between SBS and SMRT

    I refer to the article 'It's a wish - one company running buses and trains' (ST Oct 11). I agree with Mr Gerard Ee that we should have 'one public transport company running the trains and one running the buses'.

    In the first place, Singapore Bus Service has been in charge of bus services while Singapore Mass Rapid Transit is in charge of the MRT system. This was so until the appearance of Trans Island Bus Service.

    Despite this, there is not much of a difference between TIBS and SBS besides the fact that one has bendy buses while the other has double-decker buses.

    The competition between both companies was virtually not felt from the passenger's point of view. The fact that TIBS is now known as SMRT does not seem to change anything other than the exterior design of the buses and the bus drivers' uniform.

    The apparent purpose of having two bus companies in the first place is to provide competition.

    However, the fares are fixed and both bus companies run different routes, which defeat the purpose of having two bus companies as no competition seems to be present in such a situation.

    Secondly, SBS Transit succeeded in taking control of the NorthEast train line. In response to this, SMRT merged with TIBS to get a hold on the bus services.

    There seems to be a problem here. I see no reason why a bus company should want a stake in a train line. No wonder the train company retaliated by getting a stake in bus services.

    I feel we should let SMRT and SBS have a mutual agreement to let each other get back what they rightfully should operate, the MRT system and the bus services respectively.

    Chong Woon Hian

    i extracted this from the straits times forum.

    Is there need for jail term, huge fines to deal with fare cheats?

    I refer to the numerous articles in The Straits Times on Oct 18 on the penalties - a maximum six-month jail term and $2,000 fine - for repeat offenders for public transport fare cheats.

    I am not against penalising those who cheat. I believe our society does not condone it and they should be punished. But it has to be fair with an appropriate penalty for the offence committed (ie small fines for small offences).

    Is there a need to mete out jail terms and huge fines?

    Transport Minister Yeo Cheow Tong said the maximum penalties were for 'very very drastic fare evasion' and they acted as a deterrent.

    While no specific examples of drastic fare evasions were given, one would imagine that big time cheats are those who create fake Ez-link cards and tamper with the smart chip to create fake values.

    Are the existing laws and regulations insufficient to handle such extreme cases? If they are, why duplicate work.

    In penalising the cheating commuter, perhaps we should also look at the transport providers.

    The transport companies rolled out the Ez-link system when there were still technical bugs, resulting in commuters having to pay more than they should.

    This additional income covers the losses which the transport companies suffer on account cheating, but it is not revenue rightfully earned by them. Is it considered 'cheating' to keep what is not rightfully yours? What's the official view on this?

    What is the total amount that has been collected todate as a result of Ez-link hitches? Would it be appropriate to donate the money to charities instead?

    The Ez-link glitches have persisted even though they are on a much smaller scale now, and the transport companies have yet to be penalised for them.

    Shouldn't there be penalties when transport companies do not resolve this long-outstanding problem in their system?

    If train operators are penalised for ineffective gantries which close on commuters and for the breakdown of trains, why not Ez-link glitches? How about fines and jail terms for them as well?

    Despite the fare hikes, I noticed that the frequency of trains during off-peak hours have dropped. Waiting times can be eight to 10 minutes compared to five or six minutes previously.

    Fares on feeder services, for example, have doubled from a decade ago while wages have not.

    Commuters are definitely more discerning now. The Public Transport Council only regulates bus services and public transport fares.

    It will not fight for the early opening of Buangkok MRT station or question the operators when they are slow in installing proper surveillance cameras on the platforms.

    Commuters will have to fight for their own interests.

    Greg Ting Wee Liang
