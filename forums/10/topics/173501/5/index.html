    Taiwan businesspeople working in China have criticized President Chen Shui-bian for plans to scrap the National Unification Council.

    Trade, investment and tourism have blossomed in recent years between Taiwan and China, with Taiwan investors pouring up to US$100 billion into China since detente began in the late 1980s.

    Chen announced last month that he would abolish the National Unification Council and guidelines on unification, although he had previously promised not to do so in his inauguration speeches of 2000 and 2004.

    "At present, the mainland tries its utmost to protect the rights and interests of Taiwan businesspeople and to maintain the peace across the Taiwan Strait," Xie Kunzhong, president of the Association of Taiwan-Funded Enterprises in Beijing, told a news conference on Saturday, according to Hong Kong's Ta Kung Pao newspaper.

    "But the supreme leader of the Taiwan administration tries all means to make troubles for Taiwan businesspeople."

    China's Taiwan Affairs Office said on Sunday that Chen had made his move at a time of peace and stability.

    "Since 2005, thanks to the common efforts of compatriots of both sides of the Taiwan Strait, relations have emerged with a good momentum of peace, stability and development, making it more difficult for the line of 'Taiwan independence' pursued by Chen Shui-bian," an official said in a statement.

    "In this situation, Chen Shui-bian not only refuses to mend himself, but puts the peace across the strait and interests of Taiwan people at stake."

    Chen Guoyuan, secretary general of the businesspeople's association, said the wishes of Taiwan businesspeople were very simple.

    "We require only the rights of existence and development, and we don't want Taiwan to dictate any dismal future for us."

    Not everyone that showed up in the rally organized by the Democratic Progressive Party (DPP) were there of their own will, as some party lawmakers complained they had been ordered to go or would face disciplinary actions, according to a newspaper report.

    President Chen Shui-bian, Vice President Annette Lu, Premier Su Tseng-chang and DPP Chairman Yu Shyi-kun joined some 100,000 supporters as they marched along the streets in Taipei in protest of China's military threat.

    In a show of solidarity, the DPP legislative caucus Friday night issued a "mobilization order of the top degree" to its members, demanding they all join the demonstration.

    According to the party rules, any lawmaker ignoring such an order would be fined NT$50,000, and would be subject to further disciplinary actions.

    DPP Chairman Yu called each and every party lawmaker, asking them to mobilize more supporters to the march than the quota they had been assigned, according to the United Evening News.

    The party last week already meted out quotas for each of its lawmakers, requiring them to bring in a certain amount of supporters.

    The quota requirement at the time already sparked complaints from many DPP lawmakers who had had no intention to participate in a demonstration that they had deemed unnecessary

    The mobilization order had the lawmakers protesting further.

    A lawmaker was cited by the United Evening News as saying the mobilization denied them the freedom of choice.

    The DPP chairman was trying to not let the president lose face, the lawmaker from southern Taiwan was cited as saying.

    A DPP lawmaker from northern Taiwan said the people of Taiwan in general are not interested in demonstrations, and yesterday's rally lacked a clear focus, according to the newspaper.

    The lawmaker said the mobilization order gave the impression that few were actually willing to take part. A party worker complained that the DPP leaders were being very picky about the number of participants this time, according to the paper.

    In the past, the party would approve NT$8,000 to NT$13,000 in travel subsidies for each of the tourist buses arriving at the demonstration, assuming that they were full of supporters, the worker said.

    This time, party workers had to go on the buses and count the number to passengers to make sure that the lawmakers had gone.
