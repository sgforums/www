    Point 1. Do you seriously think everybody is equal before the law?
    That's the most fundamental principle governing human justice and should be adhered to wherever possible. Of course whether it is really practised in real life is debatable. Politics frequently come into the picture, and hence we have the concept of 'telephone judges' - judges who change their sentencing after receiving instructions over the phone from the politicians. But that's another debate altogether.

    Point 2. Nobody loses respect for the law just because discretionary judgement comes in.
    I did not argue against this point did i? Mitigating factors certainly play an important part in determining the sentence, within the limits (max and min permissible sentence) set in the penal code. This is where the 'human face' of justice comes in. But i would like to know what is it u think about this man that deserves a lighter sentence compared to another bank robber.

    Point 4. Sentencing IS arbitrary
    The judge does not have complete freedom in deciding the sentence. He has to stick to the sentencing limits prescribed in the Act for any particular offence, and he also has to base his sentence on precedent cases whereever possible. Which means if a bank robber in the same situation was sentenced in the past to 4 years imprisonment, he can't sentence this fella to 2 years without some very good mitigating factors. Otherwise would it be fair to the robber who had to endure 2 more years in jail for committing the same offence under the same circumstances? This was what i meant by consistency in sentencing. Without it, it's just like a classroom whereby the teacher has a favourite pet. When everyone else is punished for talking in class, the pet is spared. Would the students henceforth respect the teacher anymore?

    Point 3. The psychological trauma of the hostage should only be a tangential factor.
    To be precise, the trauma suffered by the victim is an aggravating factor in deciding the sentencing. For example, if one were to maim another person psychologically or physically thereby causing the victim to lose his capacity to function normally in everyday life (lose his job, unable to sleep due to depression and flashbacks for instance), he can be assured of a stiffer punishment compared to another fella who did not cause as much trauma to his victim. This is what i meant when i said that the sentencing has to reflect the gravity of the crime. And this is because the law seeks to avenge the suffering of the victims.

    Point 5. I am taking issue with the entire mindset of the prosecution that we must protect the reputation of Singapore.
    Isn't that true? As a Singaporean, i wouldn't want to see the image of Singapore tarnished by bank robberies. Is this considered 'fascist' thinking? I certainly hope not.
