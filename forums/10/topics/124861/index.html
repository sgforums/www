    There are lots of people who're dead broke out there, and just imagine what kind of message we would be sending to these folks if this fella were to get off with a light sentence.     First point, unwittingly you have become submerged in this entire order-obsessed culture that Singapore is.

    If you study public administration, you will learn that discretionary judgement is very much a part and parcel of the discharge of public duties on the part of civil servants. The reason? Civil administration does not exist in a vacuum. Civil administration, as the word "civil" implies, involves dealing with civilians who live in a complex society governed by emotions, rules and quirks.

    In discharging his duty, the civil servant has to take into account the environment and circumstances, something Singapore's civil servants are quite incapable of. How else do you explain the Boxing Day tsunami debacle in Thailand where Singaporeans whose belongings were swept away were asked to pay a charge at the Singapore Embassy just to get a new passport?? Ridiculous? Yes. But I think it's a mindset steeped in Singaporeans that there are rules and we have to follow them strictly unless there are clear instructions from the top.

    How is public interest not protected by giving a lighter sentence of maybe 2 years to that man?
