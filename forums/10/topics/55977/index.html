    The truth however, is that the old and successful economic paradigm that has been responsible for the rapid progress and development of the 60s to 80s frame is definitely being overridden. And it is also eventual that the Singapore economy will begin to fail and falter, as with the economic life-cycle of other similar developed markets. This process was merely being hasten up by the above mentioned events.

    Unlike the frame from the1960s to the 1980s, the former economy strategy of injecting extensive capital intensive activities is now fast being copied and rendered irrelevant.

    Aspired by the sight and smell of the Singapore success, the regional third world economies are currently pursuing a common economic strategy which is typically expected of from developing countries, and that is to first revolutionalized upon their internal core activities from post agricultural and into a heavy base of light and medium-heavy industries.

    With the electronics and general manufacturing industries falling under such classifications and aided further by the ever-existing demand for their low production cost products, it had been clear to the Singapore Government that the reliance on these pillars would have to be eventually shifted upon a higher curve, and into more complex and intensive-coordination required fields.

    As researched and revealed upon by the Kruskal-Wallis Test which was conducted to find out on what were the primary determinants that had attracted Japanese MNCs to invest in Singapore over the other available SE Asia countries in the region, it was concluded that the main attractiveness that had been offered by Singapore, laid within the rigid but transparent structure of the island country.

    And unlike the common expectation that Japanese investments had flown in here mainly as a result of attractive Policy variables such as tax holidays or relaxed profit repatriation regulation, it was actually the Non-Policy variables that had played a greater role as the right bait.

    As identified within the Kruskal-Wallis study, the Non-Policy Variables included factors such as :

    1. Political & Economic stability
    2. Country knowledge
    3. Local market size
    4. Establishment of export base
    5. Raw material availability
    6. Import / tariff / barriers
    7. Free trade zones
    8. Labor cost
    9. Tax deductions / incentives
    10. Location / presence of Competitors
    11. Local content exemption
    12. Governmental subsidiaries / state incentives, protection etc.

    While the factors highlight in blue represents favorable conditions that Singapore is able to offer, many of the former strengths are also fast being eroded by the rapid improvement and evolution by our surrounding competing foreign economic systems.

    On political stability, things in SE Asia are now different from what it had been twenty years ago.

    Vietnam is now well recovered from its former internal political in-fighting. Although sporadic handful of resistance still exist against its PM, the governmental structure is now more or less stable, though not entirely transparent.

    Thailand has seen its military influences within the government, as well as the former propaganda fear of the country being suddenly seized over and turned into a military controlled state by the army as popularly fantasized upon back in the 1980s, significantly reduced.

    And in the Philippines, the impeachment of Estrada and the subsequent rise of Arroyo into power has also been symbolic in its conveyance of the message that the country is sick of its former corruption flamboyant running, and that the civilians there want true accountability and honesty from its government from now on.

    Within the economic frame, the former Asia Four Dragons are also fast losing their shine from the inescapable theory of diminishing growth and returns. And by contrast, having been vigorously slapped right in their kisser by Soros during his currency hedging game back in 1997, the issue of financial loopholes that were in existence and unmonitored within SE Asia markets were diligently revisited and repaired.

    While it cannot be said that true transparency and accountability over both the flow or management of funds truly exist, it cannot be denied that there are now, more whistle blowing systems in vigilance guarding.

    National fiscal policies, both successful and unsuccessful, had been amended following the post 1997-98 era in a bid to redirect lost investments back into the respective SE Asia countries, as evident from the Malaysian pegging of the Ringgit to the USD, and the unsuccessful attempt by the IMF to implement upon Indonesia, itÂ’s economic restructuring package.

    The depreciation of the Thai Baht in irony, has even turned the country more competitive in the post Asia Financial Crisis years, as evident from the flock of even local F&B investments into the country as lured by the low cost of investment startup required.

    And coming into the issue of state protection or incentives, we have seen the Malaysian Government granting a monopoly to its domestic carmaker, and India giving a certain soft drink giant, the monopoly over the entire countryÂ’s market.
