    Hi,

    Firstly, basic knowledge of politics and governance is not privy to people of high paper qualifications; anyone can become an expert in it because at the end of the day, it is merely theory.

    I would like to express my personal "theory":

    Whether a parliament has zero opposition is alright or otherwise depends on individuals and the kind of systems the country has. Singaporeans appear to mind less than, say, a Brit, American or Thai.

    However, there are implications of it for a democracy. Theoretically, democracy means about the right of people making a choice.

    A party that wins everything causes the survival of other parties to be at stake and hampers their build-up and this in turn discourages competition and leads to walkovers.

    In my view, elections with walkovers mean lesser people get to exercise their choice and almost rids the purpose of having an elections. For this, all seats have to be contested and this role ends up in the opposition's plate. Activists and renowned bloggers can neither block the ruling party's power nor do they wish to.

    However, for all the desire opposition parties have, limited success increases their difficulty in getting good people to join them and become candidates, causing the walkovers.

    It is a vicious cycle, as I have wrote in an article in 2005:

    http://melvintansg.blogspot.com/2005/03/vote-for-chance-to-vote-again.html

    There are almost no examples in the world of zero opposition in a democracy. Effectively, even Singapore has 3 opposition MPs.

    Regards

    Also, in relation to your question, one interesting point to note is the fact that the President of Singapore was conferred certain powers with a view to act as an independent check of the government. That is why the government must actually consult the President whenever they desire to draw from the country's reserves or make any major decisions involving State-owned companies.
