    Feb 27, 2006
    > NEWS ANALYSIS
    > Near $3b Budget gap sparks fresh debate
    > Govt's accounting is conservative and S'pore is not really in
    > deficit, say economists
    >
    > By Narendra Aggarwal
    > Economics Correspondent
    > AS PARLIAMENT sits today to consider Singapore's Budget for 2006-07,
    > weighing heavy on its mind would be the fact that the huge $2.9
    > billion deficit projected by the Finance Ministry will be the largest
    > in at least 20 years.
    >
    > Yet private-sector economists, academics and public-finance experts
    > will remain, as in previous deficit years, totally unflustered by the
    > figure.
    >
    > The reason for their indifference is that many argue that the rather
    > gloomy picture painted of the Government's finances is not the result
    > of unsustainable fiscal largesse, but rather the result of the
    > Government's rather conservative accounting methods.
    >
    > It's an old chestnut of a problem that has bugged everyone from the
    > humble economics undergraduate to economy watchers at the
    > International Monetary Fund (IMF).
    >
    > The numbers, this time around, look like this: Aided by what is
    > expected to be strong economic growth, a surplus of about $0.7
    > billion is expected for the year ending March 31, 2007.
    >
    > But because of a handsome $3.6 billion worth of giveaways in the
    > new 'Progress Package' Budget, an overall deficit of $2.9 billion is
    > estimated instead.
    >
    > Economists have been quick to point out that there really isn't any
    > red ink at all.
    >
    > For example, long-time Singapore economy watcher Song Seng Wun, a
    > senior economist with CIMB-GK Research, says there is 'under-
    > reporting' of government revenues by what he thinks is a sum of about
    > $10 billion.
    >
    > 'So in fact, by my estimates there should be a surplus of about $7
    > billion,' he concludes.
    >
    > Whether there is a deficit boils down to 'really a definition issue',
    > says Standard Chartered Bank (Stanchart) economist Joseph Tan.
    >
    > 'And Singapore takes a very, very conservative view of revenue
    > compared to other governments,' he explains.
    >
    > In this conservative approach, two major items, typically regarded as
    > current-year government revenue, are not included in full in
    > Singapore's income statements.
    >
    > The first is capital receipts, and in particular, land sales. This is
    > totally omitted.
    >
    > According to an appendix that accompanied the Budget numbers released
    > last week, capital receipts for the coming financial year 2006 are
    > estimated to weigh in at a whopping $4.08 billion. Land sales will
    > account for the lion's share of this at $4.04 billion.
    >
    > The second item is net investment income (NII). Only up to 50 per
    > cent of NII from the investment of past reserves are 'available for
    > use by the government of the day', says the Finance Ministry. For the
    > coming year, 'investment and interest income' is estimated to be
    > $7.65 billion, but only $2.39 billion has been budgeted in the
    > accounts.
    >
    > Both of these accounting moves are problematic, say economists.
    >
    > IDEAglobal senior economist Nizam Idris says 'not including land
    > sales revenue is one glaring omission'. He adds that going by
    > international standards, money from all land sales should be included
    > in government revenue.
    >
    > Like other economists, he also points out that the return on
    > investments made from past savings of the state shown in the Budget
    > are very small.
    >
    > The new Budget includes $2.39 billion as NII contributions, but Mr
    > Nizam notes that even if Singapore's near $200 billion foreign
    > reserves were invested in United States Treasury bills with a 4 per
    > cent yield, the state's investment income would be much higher.
    >
    > CIMB-GK's Mr Song says that if IMF standards are applied, Singapore's
    > financial position is quite strong.
    >
    > This hypothesis was put to the test in a study presented at an
    > Institute of Policy Studies conference in 2002.
    >
    > Using IMF conventions, public finance guru Mukul Asher of the Lee
    > Kuan Yew School of Public Policy estimated the excess in the state's
    > total revenue over total expenditure to be between 6 and 17 per cent
    > of gross domestic product (GDP) between 1991 and 2001.
    >
    > If one uses last year's GDP figure of $195 billion and applies the
    > low end of his findings, Singapore would have had a Budget surplus of
    > more than $10 billion for the year just gone by, instead of the $0.43
    > billion reported.
    >
    > In the past, the Finance Ministry has defended its conservative
    > accounting, arguing since capital receipts from land sales could be
    > highly volatile as a result of changing market conditions. Since it
    > is not a steady stream, it is not prudent to include land sales as a
    > revenue item.
    >
    > In an e-mail response to the Straits Times, the ministry emphasised
    > again that this accounting method is designed to produce an overall
    > Budget balance that includes all the regular income and revenue
    > available for expenditure by the current government.
    >
    > 'The portion of the NII that is not used for expenditure and all
    > proceeds from land sales are therefore not included in the Budget.
    > Otherwise it will mislead Singaporeans on how much revenues are
    > available for expenditure.'
    >
    > Not everyone, however, is totally convinced. 'Everybody else does it,
    > so there is no reason why Singapore should also not do it,' says Mr
    > Song of CIMB-GK.
    >
    > Referring to the point about volatile land sale revenues, Stanchart's
    > Mr Tan says that 'this is not a valid argument as the market will be
    > able to adjust to the fluctuations of revenue'.
    >
    > He adds that the market has its own ways of adjusting to reality.
    >
    > 'Just consider this: the Budget deficits in recent years has not hit
    > Singapore ratings. We have been able to sustain our triple-A rating.
    > Clearly the market thinks that the country's fiscal position is
    > stronger that what it appears to be.'
