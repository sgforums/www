Income gap will narrow as foreign FT take higher value jobs

  • oxford mushroom

    HO CHI MINH CITY - SINGAPORE's income gap will not continue to widen indefinitely, Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew said yesterday.

    While incomes of the top 10 per cent have shot up, they will eventually be dampened by competition from emerging economies - the same forces that are now putting a lid on lower-end salaries.

    Thus, after another decade or so, a 'new balance' or 'steady state' would emerge, he predicted. One ominous scenario: incomes at all levels depressed by labour from China and India.

    'If we're not careful, the new steady state in 15 years will be an abundance of Chinese engineers, scientists, Indian scientists and so on, and you may well find the Singaporean chaps at the top will go down,' he said.

    'And we at the bottom, the bottom ones will stay put, because we're competing with the lower end. But the top end, we may not be able to compete with high-quality Chinese scientists and engineers. That's life.'

    Mr Lee said that the widening gap between rich and poor at present was unavoidable: 'You can't freeze your society in time'.

    'That's the way the globalisation at this stage is working out,' he added.

    The issue of the widening income gap has preoccupied Singaporeans recently, with the Singapore Government pledging to do more to help the bottom 20 per cent whose incomes have stagnated.

    Among other measures is Workfare, an income support scheme to help low-wage earners.

    While the Singapore Government will buffer the gap, Mr Lee said yesterday that Singaporeans must ensure that they can still fight for the top-end jobs when the 'new steady state' emerges.

    Explaining why professionals and other skilled workers would eventually feel the pinch, he noted that developing countries are training more people for top jobs. This will force wages of Singapore's bigger earners down.

    He cited the example of Singaporean hotel managers in Ho Chi Minh City and Hanoi. They probably earned two or three times what they would in Singapore, plus perks.

    'But in 10 years, will they be required? The locals have learnt the job,' he noted. 'So the managers in the hotel chains will say, right, you either take local pay or you go home to Singapore. So it will equalise. How long will it take to equalise? Depends upon how fast each country climbs up the educational ladder.'

    Mr Lee expects Chinese wages, especially in the coastal cities, to go up in a decade.

    'Singaporeans who go there with hardship allowances will either lose the hardship allowances and stay on, or come back to Singapore. So it will equalise. How long will it take? 10 years? Maybe. Five years? A bit too short,' he said.

    (Straits Times 21 Jan)
