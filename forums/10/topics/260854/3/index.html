    By NANDE KHIN

    THE job market here continued to remain tight in the first quarter as total employment rose by 49,400 and the number of layoffs declined sharply, according to the Ministry of Manpower (MOM).

    However, the resident jobless rate of 4 per cent, seasonally adjusted, in March is an increase from December's 3.6 per cent and the 3.5 per cent in March 2006, as more people were attracted back into the workforce by buoyant economic conditions, said MOM in its quarterly report.

    The resident rate - which covers Singaporeans and permanent residents - is typically higher than the overall rate which also includes foreigners on work passes.

    The overall unemployment rate, seasonally adjusted, also rose - from 2.6 per cent in December 2006 to 2.9 per cent in March this year.

    The resident labour supply increased by 17,700 in Q1, as more people entered the workforce, lured by rosy job prospects as a result of the robust economy, said MOM.

    This is higher than the average increase over the last two years, when favourable economic conditions also led to an increase in resident labour supply in the first quarter but by a lower average of 10,700.

    The buoyant economy also generated a large number of job vacancies which reached 32,200 in March. This is 30 per cent higher than a year ago, but is broadly unchanged from December (32,000).

    About seven in 10 vacancies (22,600) in March were in the services sector. Manufacturing added another 7,900 or 24 per cent of the openings while the remaining vacancies were in the construction sector.

    Nearly half (14,400) of the vacancies were for professionals, managers, executives and technicians (PMETs).

    Weighed down by the increase in job seekers, the seasonally adjusted ratio of job vacancies to unemployed persons dropped to 68 vacancies for every 100 job seekers in March, down from the five-year high of 79 per 100 in December. Nevertheless, this is still higher than the 60 vacancies for 100 job seekers ratio in March 2006.

    Far fewer workers (1,964) were also laid off in Q1 this year, compared with the 3,215 retrenched in the previous quarter and the 3,653 laid off in the corresponding quarter a year ago.

    In particular, the number of retrenched workers in the manufacturing sector fell sharply from 2,447 in Q4, 2006 to 1,393 in Q1 this year, although the sector still accounted for the majority (71 per cent) of layoffs.

    Based on CPF records, 59 per cent of the residents retrenched by private sector establishments with at least 25 employees in Q4 2006 were re-employed by March this year.

    However, as employers continued to hire to meet current and anticipated increases in output, job creation has outpaced the growth in output resulting in lower productivity but higher wages, said MOM.

    This has resulted in a 1.6 per cent dip in productivity in Q1 over the corresponding quarter in 2006. It represents the second consecutive quarterly decline in productivity after the 0.7 per cent drop in Q4 2006.

    Nevertheless, wage pressures have continued to intensify, reflecting the brisk hiring pace.

    Average nominal earnings based on CPF records posted a strong year-on-year increase of 5.5 per cent in Q1 this year, higher than the 3.1 per cent in the preceding quarter. After adjusting for inflation, the increase in real terms was 5 per cent and 2.5 per cent respectively
