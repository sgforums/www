    It really is a matter of perspective, isn't it?

    I love living in Australia. I love being able to pack upfor the weekend, and depending on which direction I point my car in, ski, scuba dive or tour a winery. There's only so much clubbing and shopping you can do in Singapore before you stop being able to tell one place from another.

    The more important point doesn't have anything to do with money. I love being able to walk into any pub, plonk myself down at the bar and strike up conversation with the next bloke, be it about his kids, preferences of local or imported beers or what a bunch of knobs the dickheads in parliament are. We may agree or disagree, but at the end of it we'll agree that it was a good chat. You can't even maintain eye contact with a stranger in Singapore.

    ON THE OTHER HAND ...

    I've got a close friend who's a regional director with a large American firm in Singapore. She moved over from Las Vegas a few years ago, and she absolutely loves Singapore's buzz. She and her husband are in their late forties, have no kids and own a place in Phuket that they pop over to every six weeks or so. When they first arrived, I gave them my perspective on the social and political situation in Singapore, and despite initially finding it very negative, after a few years she acknowledged that my observations were accurate. Still, she loves being in Singapore and has no intention to return stateside. Who am I to question that?

    Anothe close mate of mine is from Sydney, but he spent his formative years in Singapore - his parents still maintain a flat in Woodlands. He spent a few years later working and living in Singapore, and I have my fun ragging him about liking Singapore while he says the same about me and Australia. At the end of the day, though, we respect each others' choices. He says he feel at home in Singapore. Who am I to question that?

    As I said, it's a matter of perspective. People have their own personalities, and different places appeal to different personalities - there's no right or wrong in anybody's choice as long as they are happy with it. What is wrong is when some jackass starts crapping on about what place is best. If someone thinks he's in a position to declare what's best, he's got a seriously misguided view of his place in the world.

    As for work-life balance, depending on the time of year, I work anything between the standard 38.5 and 90 hours a week. The 90 hour-a-week periods are seasonal, usually when my clients want to clear off their projects before the end of financial year, and even then weekends are usually sacrosanct. I make sure I'm in the fortunate position of being able to both make the money and having the time to spend it. It's just as well that I love my work, and an even better thing that the missus understands.

    I don't have kids yet, but having been both a student and a teacher in Singapore, I wouldn't want my kids to grow up in that environment. It's not a matter of competition - if my kids were to be uncompetitive, I'd get concerned. What I do object to is the way kids in Singapore are measured and valued by a one-size-fits-few yardstick. It's dehumanising, although to the credit of the Singaporean education system, they finally seem to be slowly realising this and getting with the program.
