    Read the following article that has compared PM Goh as a "silver backed" gorilla leader of a pack of gorillas that continuously seek directions and security from its leader:

    Are Singaporeans similar to this species of apes ?

    INSIGHT: Workers need protection, not just direction
    By Chua Mui Hoong

    THINKING ALOUD

    GORILLAS in Central Africa live in small bands that revolve around a dominant male called a silverback because of the silver hair on his back and neck.

    The silverback provides the focal point that keeps others in line when the group forages for food. He travels in front, and protects the group from predators. In times of distress, others look to him.

    Human societies are of course far more complex than gorilla communities. But the basic primate need for direction, protection and a sense of order cuts across species.

    In Singapore, these are unsettling times for all. Workers face job loss and wage cuts. An impending 'retuning' of the Central Provident Fund rates is likely. Wage reform means some workers will end up with less pay.

    For companies and employers in Singapore, the environment is also unsettling, as the entry of India and China into the global market system brings in new, low-cost, medium-skill competitors.

    In these times, people look to leaders for direction, order and protection.

    Singapore's leaders do provide a clear sense of the first two. Those who follow public discussions on economic restructuring know the direction ahead: To get more jobs, Singapore needs more flexible labour markets and workers need to retrain.

    The message has been driven home. The results are not in yet, but there is a sense of coherence in the restructuring that is under way.

    Each major group - workers, unions, employers, government officials - knows its part in the overall scheme of things, and leaders in each of these groups understand, at least at the conceptual level, what needs to be done.

    The trouble is that the changes necessary require more than just leaders to make the changes.

    The real work of change is adaptive, requiring individuals involved to change the way they live and work and understand the world.

    To do so, people need to buy into the vision articulated by leaders. But to buy into the vision, they need first of all to feel secure and protected in the group.

    Imagine the silverback telling his band of gorillas: 'We need to get to a new feeding ground. This area is now too arid for our food.

    'I don't know where the new ground is, except that it's in this general direction and that we will get there. The journey will be long and hard. Some of us will end up worse off. Some may even die on the journey. We'll face many dangers along the way.'

    Gorillas will clamour: Do we really need a new ground? Can't we make do with what we have?

    The Singaporean equivalent: What's wrong with the past high-wage, high-growth strategy? Why can't Singapore continue with its exports-led model of growth? Why must companies retrench when they're still profitable? Why don't we all just settle for being less profitable, less attractive as an investment destination, a less dynamic economy?

    If gorillas overcome this first resistance, and see that they do need a new feeding ground, their next concerns will be: Where are we going and how do you know that's the right place?

    The Singaporean equivalent: Must we restructure the economy? Do wages really have to be made more flexible? Must we retune the CPF? What's the assurance that after all the pain of the journey, there'll be a new and better feeding ground at the end of it?

    On this, the People's Action Party government has a sterling track record and will be able to command the trust of people that indeed there'll be a fresh, more abundant feeding ground ahead.

    After buying into this vision of a brighter future, gorillas will want to know: What are you, silverback, doing to protect us on the journey? What do you mean, some of us may die, when it's your job to protect us? Why should we agree to go on the journey in the first place, unless you can guarantee our security?

    The Singaporean equivalent: Okay, we realise we may need to make all those changes. But those changes are painful and come at a cost. What are you doing to cushion us from the pain?

    We want job protection for Singaporeans. We want unions to prevent companies from retrenching. We want you to promise to keep costs of Housing Board flats, bus and train fares and health-care costs down. If you don't promise us these, we won't go along.

    This dramatisation of the gorilla-Singapore conflict is not meant to be frivolous or disrespectful. It aims to throw into sharper relief the underlying conflict that has emerged in public discussion about economic and wage restructuring.

    The silverback and other leaders know what needs to be done. But the change that is needed requires effort from every single one in the team: every gorilla must go on the journey.

    Contrast this with the situation if the silverback were to say: 'This feeding ground is getting fallow. But I have the solution: I know a place where I can get a magic fertiliser that will turn this ground into a fertile feeding ground again.'

    This requires no change from others: the leader diagnoses the problem and provides the solution.

    Harvard leadership guru Ronald Heifetz distinguishes between technical leadership where the leader provides the answer, and adaptive leadership, where the leader has to mobilise everyone to make the change.

    Adaptive leadership requires more than just the ability to diagnose the problem and point to the solution. It requires leaders to regulate the level of distress felt by those undergoing change and to respect their pain.

    Prof Heifetz wrote: 'The pains of change deserve respect. People can only sustain so much loss at any one time. Leadership demands respect for people's basic need for direction, protection and order in times of distress. Leadership requires compassion for the distress of adaptive change, both because compassion is its own value, and because it can improve one's sense of timing. Knowing how hard to push and when to let up are central to leadership.'

    In today's context, workers are feeling real pain and anxieties. They are adjusting as best they can. People like computer operator Katherine Chow (on facing page) are tightening their belts in case they're laid off. This newspaper has featured graduates who became hawkers or waiters because white-collar jobs are scarce, and twenty-somethings reconciled to a decade of slow growth.

    Leaders must and can do more to acknowledge the pain felt and give people a sense of protection and security. Without giving false cheer, they must instil confidence.

    More effort must be put into creating systems that protect people while change is under way.

    Even as ministers talk about wage reform, which gives relief to employers, workers want social reform, which will mend the existing glaring holes in the social safety net and help them through periods of unemployment.

    To help workers focus on the journey to the new feeding ground, leaders have to meet workers' emotional need for protection.

    It may well be that no leader can guarantee security against global economic realities. In that case, workers need to feel a sense of confidence that they can make it.

    When the nation listens to Singapore's silverback - Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong - deliver his National Day Rally speech tomorrow, it will be looking not only for a sense of direction and order, but also to feel secure and protected.

    The writer is a senior correspondent on The Straits Times Political Desk.

    To be frank, I dun really like the notion of having the CPF rates cut further as mentioned in the NDP rally speech.

    However, I also believe that I would further dislike the notion of being out of a job, and having lesser jobs around me over the former alternative.

    The rational of the CPF cuts actually reflects the most basic fundamental of economics, and which is in the eyes of foreign investors, cost is and will forever be an elastic factor of consideration.

    Although the amounts of saving will be reduced by a relatively significant sum, the painful cut however, will serve to draw in more foreign investors from all over the globe.

    Aidded by the achievement of more FTAs than compared to decade ago, SG is now offering a far greater and more attractive deal for evaluating firms to contemplate upon us as their next best choice of investure.

    However, we must also realize that the strong SGD trend may not e exactly what these contemplating investers would like to see, since a strong SGD will inevitable make exports more expensive as compared to other lower cost centres around in the region.

    Faced on one hand where the strength of the dollar would actually constitute as a push factor to potential investors, but yet faced with an equally pressing circumstance to not weaken the dollar ( since that would in fact, richochet out more shock into our system ), the next best approach would hence be to operate on the next biggest operating cost component, and which is the wages and salaries.

    I hope I can interpret this correctly, but to me, the decision to have the CPF rates cut and enforced with a ranged system is really meant to provide this trade-off.

    Though painful and not easily adaptable by many in the short term, this painful measure will however, slowly evolve eventually into a very major and critical supporting infrastructure for the local economic scene, and serve the people well with its role as a better 'shock-absorber', in additional to the saving feature that the system is currently fulfilling.

    S'pore needs more than just CPF cuts: analysts

    http://business-times.asia1.com.sg/sub/latest/story/0,4574,91420,00.html?

    SINGAPORE - A plan to cut company contributions to Singapore's state-run pension fund should slow down retrenchments and boost corporate savings, but more cost-reducing measures are needed to sharpen the Republic's competitive edge, analysts said on Tuesday.

    The struggling property sector, once a pillar of the local economy, is expected bear the brunt of the proposed changes to the Central Provident Fund (CPF), which Singaporeans use to fund home purchases, they said.

    Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong in a speech to the nation on Sunday said the government has no choice but to push for CPF reforms in order to save jobs as the country faces a mounting challenge from countries manufacturing the same products at cheaper costs.

    The proposals, including a cut in the amount companies are legally required to contribute to the CPF for each employee, is expected to be tabled for debate when parliament sits on August 28.

    Touching the CPF has always been an emotional issue for Singaporeans because the fund -- whose three million members had a combined balance of more than US$55 billion (S$96.2 billion) at the end of 2002 -- is the cornerstone of the so-called nanny state's social compact with its citizens

    CPF funds are used to buy homes as well as pay for medical bills and children's education, with enough money left for retirement. Part of one's CPF savings can also be put into approved investment schemes like unit trusts.

    But current contributions totalling 36 per cent of a worker's monthly salary -- 16 percent from the employer and 20 per cent from the employee -- has also helped make the cost of doing business in Singapore higher and if left unchanged could divert investors to other countries.

    The government wants to cut the total package to as low as 30 per cent.

    Paul Schymyck, an economist with research house IDEAglobal, said the proposed pension cuts could help companies go slow on job layoffs and boost corporate earnings.

    However, this would not be enough to make Singapore competitive in terms of costs.

    'At the end of the day, no matter how much you cut the CPF, your wage cost here will remain higher than China and India,' he told AFP.

    Mr Schymyck said corporate taxes which have earlier been reduced to 20 per cent remain high.

    Other costs such as transportation, consumer taxes which are to be raised to five per cent next year from the current four per cent, and industrial land rentals are higher compared to neighbouring countries.

    'I think they should address the entire cost structure,' he said.

    Mr Goh in his speech made a stark illustration, saying that for every manufacturing worker hired in Singapore, a company could employ three in Malaysia, eight in Thailand, 13 in China and 18 in India.

    The Singapore Manufacturers' Federation described the proposed cuts as an 'important step in the right direction,' but said it hoped the government 'will implement more measures in the near term to reduce business costs further.'

    It said rents, taxes, and administration or service fees paid to government agencies form a 'large portion' of business costs here.

    Kenny Yap, executive chairman of ornamental fish exporter Quian Hu Corp who welcomed the proposed cuts, said his company employs more than 100 staff in Singapore and 300 in China, but its wage cost is 20 per cent lower in China.

    'I think it has come to a point where the people have to decide for themselves how much they want to save rather than rely on a government structure to do that,' he said.

    Low Ping Yee, an economist with United Overseas Bank, said the proposed cuts should ease retrenchments but would not result in new hirings.

    'But what it signals to investors is that our wage system is becoming more flexible and that will make it more attractive for foreign investors over the long term.'

    Kim Eng Securities said the proposed cuts 'should help reduce the economy's cost structure and help accelerate the nascent economic recovery,' but is likely to dampen consumption demand in the short term.
