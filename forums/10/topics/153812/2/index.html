    You have to look at it both ways.

    For an economy that prides itself as 'non-strike', a single strike can be devastating. It can be speculated that a one-party dominant govt may be starting to lose its grip, that NTUC no longer has the usual swagger it conducts itself with.

    Similarly, a country which strikes all the time will suddenly set investors rolling if it is non-strike for a month.

    This is how fickle-minded investors or existing customers can be as to the situation. And anyone who permits a strike (NTUC chief) has to weight the options carefully, at the same time, ensure that regardless of the outcome, there will be no row with the cabinet.

    The direct terms 'right' and 'wrong' refer to issues which may or may not be negotiable. Let's face it, people complain for all kinds of reasons. You will always have union heads who conjure up enough dissatisfaction among the ranks over meagre issues. For example, if the economy slumps and companies exercise the MVC component in order to save jobs, and people are upset over the drop in salary and want to strike, then it would be a WRONG (no wait ...STUPID) reason.

    As for right, there are plenty of tenable arguments. But most of them can be candily discussed at union level and then corporate level. All this avoids demonstrations.

    Only when all avenues are exhausted do extreme measures take precedent.

    Whether SG has benefited from a non-strike status is for everyone to gauge. Less disruptions mean a solid foundation for corporates to build and improve. On the flip side, many will cast a doubt whether companies have solicited govt aid in legislating numerous MOM directives to their beneift such as lower medical benefits or reduced leave, but then again, that is for the employee to judge.
