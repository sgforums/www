    KL to show S'pore new designs for causeway
    KUALA LUMPUR -- Malaysia will submit fresh proposals to Singapore to try to resolve an impasse over a new bridge Malaysia wants to build to connect the two neighbours, news reports said on Monday.

    Prime Minister Abdullah Ahmad Badawi is expected to soon present Singaporean officials with new design ideas for the bridge, which would replace a causeway that nearly 100,000 Malaysians currently use to commute to work in the city-state, said Foreign Minister Syed Hamid Albar.

    The official Bernama news agency quoted him as having said: 'We will then see what Singapore's response is. I think if there is a right spirit on both sides, these are matters which are not impossible to resolve.'

    Malaysian officials have said the new bridge, which it hopes to complete by the end of 2005, would ease congestion and boost passenger flow between the two countries.

    The project had earlier been part of a package deal that included an extension of Malaysia's water supply to Singapore beyond 2061.

    But the plan stalled when Malaysia called off talks in 2002, after it alleged that Singapore was not prepared to pay fair rates for the water. The two sides did not agree on a design for the new bridge before the talks collapsed.

    Malaysian officials said last year they did not need approval from Singapore to begin work on the bridge. But without the Republic's approval, the new bridge could end dangling in the middle of Johor Strait, which separates the two neighbours.

    Singapore's Foreign Minister S. Jayakumar recently said that Malaysia would be violating international law if it unilaterally pursues the plan to demolish its side of the causeway and start work on a new bridge.

    Leaders of both countries said last month they wanted to work to settle a string of disputes that have hampered relations for years, including territorial claims, the price of water that Malaysia supplies to Singapore, and the use of military airspace. -- AP

    Currently, ships entering the Straits of Johor from the eastern opening, must sail through the sea channel between Pulau Tekong and Changi Point - through Singapore territorial waters.

    The sea channel between the northern side of Pulau Tekong and Pulau Ubin towards Johor coastline, is too shallow for any ocean going vessels to sail through the Malaysian side of the Straits of Johor.

    All ships sailing through the Straits of Johor will have to sail on the Singapore side of the Straits.

    With the Western side of the Straits being narrower, between the shores of Johor and Singapore, there will be navigation problems when ships sail pass each other in opposite directions.
