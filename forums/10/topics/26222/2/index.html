    kenhor:

    Continuous military expenditure is required to ensure that our armed forces remain on par with our neighbors. Obsolete and aging equipment must be replaced, and new capabilities acquired just to keep up. It is a treadmill. This is the price of security. You can call it an arms race if you like -- same difference.

    While we have one of the highest per GDP expenditures on defense and one of the highest per capita GDPs in the world, our defense expenditure is actually quite low, due to our low population. $4b a year really is not that much, and I never cease to be amazed at how much is done on this shoestring budget. Our military and intelligence apparatus are very well-adapted to its mission, including counterterrorism and external defense against likely agressors. I have no complaints about how the money has been spent.

    You advocate spending this money instead on the economy. How? This is not a stupid question, since "spending money on the economy" is not an obvious concept. You propose handouts for local businessmen. This will create moral hazard and destroy their incentive to excel -- we are not Malaysia (no offense to our neighbors). You propose reducing the chilling effect that GLCs have on entrepreneurship. The government does not give the GLCs free money, by the way. The GLCs compete freely and fairly, and dominate the market through excellence and economies of scale. Singapore is one of the friendliest countries for SMEs (small-medium enterprises), with affordable capital, accomodating laws and good infrastructure. The reason why large enterprises find it hard to compete in Singapore is not because of government interference, but because they just do not make the cut. The telecoms market (M1, StarHub, etc.) is one of the few sectors where they did manage to compete -- and it looks OK to me.

    So where will this money be spent? As a previous poster points out, the government already spends extensively on infrastructure and training -- more and better than any other country I have seen. A tax cut is possible, but Singaporean taxes (including indirect taxes like COE and ERP) are already one of the lowest in the world. This means that a tax cut would greatly reduce our defensive capabilities for only a tiny economic stimulus. If anything, our military expenditure is simply on top of our government's already extensive and prudent expenditures on the economy.

    You seem to have a problem with foreign talent. The government did not "create" foreign talent, by the way. It exists out there regardless of whether you like it or not. It is just a fact of life that there are people out there who are better than Singaporeans and willing (in some cases) to work for less pay. So what do we do about it? Wishing will not make them disappear. We can either welcome them and try to benefit from them, or we can shut them out, either selectively or completely. Shutting them out just means that we will fall further and further behind, as other countries welcome these individuals. You cannot hold back the tide.

    In the long run, the solution is for Singaporeans to improve enough to beat these foreign talents in all fields. The government is already spending extensively on education to do this -- more than Singaporeans are able to absorb, in fact. Only so many people can be in school at a time, and Singaporeans are only so smart as a whole. However, as long as Singaporeans harbor your escapist attitude toward the issue of foreign talent, we will never catch up.

    You also have a problem with the Singaporean standard of living. A typical middle- to upper-class Singaporean, if he were to go overseas, would be able to own an estate with a mansion, and several cars. We are not poor, it is just that certain things here are expensive. Why is this so? Another poster mentioned supply and demand, and this is the key. Things are expensive here simply because supply is so low. Homes are expensive because the land they need to stand on is in short supply. Cars are expensive because the land they need to drive on is also in short supply. This is just another fact of life. There is nothing that any form of financial stimulus can do to rectify this. Reducing military expenditure will not help anyone get a car. After living in the USA for a total of 10 years, I can say from personal experience that, if anything, it is easier to own a car and a house in Singapore.

    I did not mean to sound harsh, and I apologise if I did. However, I am tired of Singaporeans complaining about things that cannot be changed. It is probably due to a lack of basic economics knowledge that Singaporens do not realize they do this.

    I really have been enjoying this discussion. Keep it up!
