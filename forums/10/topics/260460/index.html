    Replies from disgrunted subscribers:

    Hit StarHub where it hurts most - cancel your cable TV subscriptions

    DURING the 1960s, when a black woman, Rosa Parks, was arrested for taking a seat reserved for whites in a bus, many blacks led by civil rights leader Martin Luther King started a trade boycott against not only the bus operators in Birmingham, Alabama, but also all the city's merchants for their segregation policies.
    Blacks carpooled or walked to work. They refused to buy from white stores and merchants. Their action brought the city to its knees and paved the way for the Equal Rights movement in the US.

    StarHub subscribers can do the same by cancelling their cable TV subscriptions once the terms of their contracts are up. Don't wait for the subscription fees to go up before you take action.

    StarHub has said that it has no choice for the hikes. We consumers do.

    Send the management of StarHub a message: 'If you want to hurt our pockets, we can do likewise to you.'

    Perhaps then, the EPL programmes can look for ways to cut back, like not having so many highly-paid soccer experts each week.

    Why should we pay for StarHub's bid especially when most of the money eventually goes to soccer players, not the clubs which are forced to pay inflated wages?

    Every extra dollar we pay goes to 'feed' some English player who is paid $40,000 to $120,000 a week.

    Even the spectators in Europe have not seen lower-priced tickets at the turnstiles despite the hefty fees earned by the clubs through TV rights.

    A vote not to watch soccer on StarHub is a vote against greed at the players', club, football association and corporate levels.

    It's as simple as that. Use the money and donate it to a charitable cause instead.

    As for news, go to the Internet and catch the video clips from CNN and BBC online.

    Leow Aik Jiang

    and more:

    StarHub's planned fee hike shows that it does not value customer loyalty

    I REFER to the article, 'Cable TV fees to go up; biggest hike for sports channels' (ST, June 12).
    As expected, StarHub is raising subscription fees, claiming that it had no choice but to do so.

    How can this be when, on May 10, it announced a net profit of $70 million and was able to declare an interim dividend of 3.5 cents per share.

    In February, it declared a full-year operating revenue of $1.8 billion and net profit of $360.2 million. Does it mean that every time the company declares a net profit, it needs to raise prices?

    Its behaviour mirrors that of public transport operators ComfortDelgro and SMRT, both of whom proceeded with fee hikes previously despite what are, in my opinion, healthy profits.

    I disagree with the view by StarHub's Mr Thomas Ee that it is not a monopoly in Singapore when, in fact, there is no other competitor for cable TV content.

    How can it not be a monopoly when it is the only channel that screens live English Premier League matches and Formula One races?

    I also find the method of raising fees very high-handed. StarHub imposes a penalty to prevent those on contract from terminating it. These folks have 'no choice' but to bear with the fee increase.

    What is exactly the role of the Competition Commission of Singapore? Isn't it to regulate and investigate such price increases to promote fair competition? In this case, I would be surprised if it is considered fair practice.

    I am upset with StarHub which, in my opinion, does not value customer loyalty. I am even more upset that nothing seems to have been done to curb such unfair practices.

    Jason Chiam Chiah Sern

    ST Forum
