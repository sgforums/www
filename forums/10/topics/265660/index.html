    Feeling the strain
    Rising dengue causing bed crunch; minister says elective surgery may be postponed

    Tan Hui Leng
    huileng@mediacorp.com.sg

    AS THE number of dengue cases increase, the bed crunch at public hospitals here may get worse, warned Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan.
    .
    In a deja vu of the dengue outbreak in 2005, Mr Khaw said elective surgery cases, which are non-emergency, would be rescheduled if dengue cases in hospitals hit an unmanageable level.
    .
    "For planning purposes, I just assume we'll get a repeat of 2005, so we have to gear up for that," he said at a dental event yesterday.
    .
    "The last few weeks' data is not that optimistic."
    .
    Last week, the dengue outbreak hit epidemic levels of 381 cases, up from 349 the previous week.
    .
    So far, the disease has killed three elderly men in Singapore.
    .
    As of Monday, dengue patients occupied 113 public hospital beds, or about 2 per cent of the total 5,267 beds in service. Currently, about 70 per cent of dengue patients are admitted to the hospitals.
    .
    "All public hospitals are actively monitoring dengue admissions and discharges to manage the bed situation, and this may include cutting back on elective surgery," said a Ministry of Health spokesperson.
    .
    Assuming a worst-case scenario of more than 500 dengue cases a week, this would mean more than 400 hospital beds "clogged up" by dengue , she added. This is about the size of Alexandra Hospital.
    .
    Public hospital occupancy rates range from 82 to 90 per cent now.
    .
    Two years ago Â— when dengue hit a record 14,210 cases and 25 deaths Â— up to 300 beds were taken up by dengue patients in the peak periods.
    .
    Changi General Hospital, which at one time had registered over 100 per cent occupancy, opened another ward and made arrangements to transfer patients in stable condition to neighbouring St Andrew's Community Hospital.
    .
    It was forced to postpone or refer to other hospitals some 22 per cent of its elective surgery cases between 13 July and 12 Aug 2005.
    .
    If hospital resources are strained by dengue patients again, the "only practical way" is to reschedule elective surgery, said Mr Khaw, adding that life will then be "very miserable and inconvenient" to affected patients.
    .
    "I hope we never reach such an extent but we can't tell," he said.
    .
    Mr Khaw also touched on how Singaporeans can expect to benefit from lower costs of dental treatment from next January, when amendments to the Dentists Act kick in.
    .
    This is because dental therapists will be able to move out of school dental clinics to the private sector.
    .
    "Dental care is a wide spectrum of activities and much of the simpler work can be done by staff like dental hygienists. That helps reduce costs," he said.
    .
    Some 230 dental therapists, some of them graduates from Nanyang Polytechnic, now serve the primary and secondary schools here.
