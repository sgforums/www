    This all sounds very familiar. Quite a number of my relatives are teachers. Like all professions there are the bad apples. The dedicated teachers spend "after school" hours grading papers, preparing the next day's lessons and worksheets, planning lesson notes for the next term, etc. Not only do they have extra curriculum activities to deal with, they have meet the parents sessions (younger kids) which require co-ordinating with parents of 30 to 40 students. They have to attend enrichment classes, plan funfairs, sports days, collect school fees, etc. They don't just teach, go home and relax. Tuition teachers deal with a handful of students, very often individuals. Teachers deal with many more students and they have many classes to teach.

    Many young teachers don't have the dedication of the older, more experienced teachers but S'pore being what it is rewards those with paper qualification. Many young folks choose to go into teaching thinking it's a bed of roses - short hours, tons of holidays, good pay. Many quit after they realise it's not as easy as they'd initially thought.

    It's sad to see people diss teachers when they haven't a clue what teaching is really all about. And parents these days ... geez ... some of their kids just need a good spanking is all I can say.

    Good evening,

    I used to teach in a SAP school and am currently teaching at an international school in Shanghai.

    The biggest killer of a teacher's passion is not the pupils themselves. It's the support from above. When you have a principal and head that backs you and gives you the freedom to discipline the pupils and teach in the way you deem best, it really makes life a whole lot easier.

    The second biggest killer of a teacher's passion is the system. Many of us teachers who believe in education found ourselves becoming factory supervisors turning out a standard quality product for the workforce instead. Each child is different and nobody should be treated as a 'product'.

    Many teachers who believe in what we are doing do not mind the extra hours we spend, the money we take out of our own pockets for the little gifts that motivate our pupils and so on. But as a head of department (both in Singapore and here in Shanghai), I've seen my fair share of teachers who really should have no business teaching.

    Sigh...I suspect those who dish the work of teachers tend to be problem parents themselves. I've seen so many cases in my short teaching career (less than 6 years).
