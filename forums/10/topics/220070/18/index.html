    Not quite complete.

    A chicken rice hawker makes a $X out of every plate of chicken rice he sells. Now that the cost of the chicken rice has increae by a%, we would assume that he would counter this by increasing the selling price by the same amount (assuming he doesn't round it up). By doing so, the hawker will continue to have $X to spend.

    However, since the cost of other things (clothes, food etc) that he will be buying will be increased too, his propensity to spend is now less. With the same profit ($X), he has less to buy with.

    To maintain the same lifestyle and expenditure, he will have to factor in the increase into his selling price also. Thus, the adjustment will be much higher than just a +a% increase.

    Ever wonder why a bowl of noodles went from $2.50 to $3.50 when the GST increase from 3% to 5%? It was just a mere 2% increase in GST.

    It an snow ball effect.

    I WOULD like to commend Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong for sticking to the Singaporean economic model.
    I read with interest his views regarding Scandinavian welfare states. I am from one of them: Finland.

    While it is true that there is more 'welfare' in Scandinavia, it comes at a price. Public spending in my country stands at 25 per cent of GDP, twice that of Singapore.

    The government in Finland spends vast amounts of money on free health care and education, nearly twice the 8 per cent of GDP spent in Singapore. Having been to Changi hospital I can say that health care here is no worse than in my country and the charges are very reasonable. My point is it doesn't have to be free.

    It's a question of choice and pricing. The public sector typically does not run the most efficient services, because the services are non-competitive.

    Singapore runs a tighter ship, because it's only partially subsidised. Compulsory savings schemes for health care and pensions are a far better way. So is taxing consumption over income.

    Many people in my country give up half or more of their gross incomes to finance the almighty welfare state. This serves to promote equality of sorts and creates a vast middle class. It also stifles entrepreneurism and leads to voluntary unemployment. The cost of living is higher too, with GST at 22 per cent.

    I disagreed with the crushing taxes in my country. It is for this reason that I came to Singapore, and was happy to give up the benefits I had paid for over the years.

    Long live the Singaporean model.

    Mika Sampovaara

    Straits times 16 Nov)

    Increased spending ahead of GST hike expected
    By Mira Kashinath, TODAY | Posted: 16 November 2006 0734 hrs

    SINGAPORE: That urge to buy a car right now may just have become stronger.

    Within days of Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong saying that the Goods and Services Tax (GST) is likely to be raised by 2 percentage points - the official announcement is expected in February - economists predict an interesting side-effect.

    Those who have been contemplating buying a car may bring their plans forward.

    "Consumers will likely start spending on big-ticket items, such as cars, high-end electronics appliances and other luxury goods, before the GST hike kicks in next year," said Mr Leslie Khoo, regional economist with Forecast Pte Ltd.

    This urgency to beat the hike could strengthen a trend that has already started to pick up.

    According to the Automobile Association of Singapore, there were 10,971 successful bids for car ownership in August and 10,701 in September. The total rose to 12,428 in October.

    Falling oil prices have got more people looking at buying cars again. The GST hike around the corner could speed up their decision.

    But the increase in Certificate of Entitlement prices could stand in the way, said industry watcher and Today's motoring correspondent Colin Yong.

    "COE prices have gone up by a significant amount in recent weeks," he said. For smaller cars, the rise is by as much as $2,500, which is an unnaturally high level. He felt that buyers would wait for COE levels to drop before committing themselves to a purchase.

    Meanwhile, the retail sector as a whole is waiting for an injection of growth.

    Seasonally-adjusted retail sales in September grew by just 0.9 per cent from August to $2.30 billion - far less than the 3.3 per cent rise predicted by a Dow Jones Newswires poll.

    The World Bank and International Monetary Fund meetings in September were expected to give a big boost to the retail sector, but that did not materialise.

    In fact, some popular shopping malls suffered a fall in foot traffic due to tight security around the meeting venues. Tourist arrivals also fell.

    The one clear winner was the restaurant industry, which saw sales go up by 12.4 per cent compared to the same period last year. - TODAY /dt
