    An application of some 'Robin Hood' chivalry from Sherwood Forest? But first you need to spend ...

    Maybe one can consider the significance of the 'safety net - 2% gst increase' this way ...

    The average household expenditure is the key to understanding the 'social safety nets' from the 2% GST increase. The other component is demographics.

    Its true that the 2% gst increase is going to affect every consumer (personal or industrial) at all levels ie low-spending, mid-spending or high-spending.

    By the law of averages, peppered with a touch of randomness, the amount of total gst collected will go up probably quite linearly by at least 2%, maybe up to 20% if you consider to include cases of 'non-linearly' increases in some B2C or B2B2C industries.

    If you just consider the 2% increase, what you can get is a very big pot of money. I do not know what the total yearly consumer spending of Singapore is. Say X dollars. I am guessing maybe $12 Billion (churned out like this: $500 per month, 2M (households), 12 months). 2% of that would be a cool $240M.

    The eventual figure by way of the 2% GST increase would be much much more than $240M. I am guessing maybe $1B.

    Now the trick is knowing how many households are categorised as under the safety net. Name a figure ... 10,000 households?. Offer $1k to them*, it will only knock $10M off the first figure of $240M, a percentage point of bout 4.2%. For $1B, it woud be 1%. You can extrapolate that for a larger net of needy households.

    The remaining 95.8% of $240M or 99% of $1B ... goes into reserves, more investments, infrastructure, creating jobs, future thinking ...

    One of the many applications of the Robin Hood economic models ... just an exercise for casual reading ...

    * Households spending $500 monthly incurs a $10 more GST (2% increase); $120 a year. So a safety net package of $1k a year is 8.3 times more than what's put upfront in $120.
