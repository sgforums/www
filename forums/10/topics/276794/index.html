    A career in the SAF is only good if you can continue to be in top physical and mental form but only as good as you can be up till 45 years MAX.

    Somehow, the SAF do not seem to appreciate those over 45 years - except for Technical Specialists with their accumulated years of experience to keep the high-tech equipment running.

    Whether PP1 or 2, does the SAF need any excuse to give any golden handshake ?

    For those with financial difficulties or poor middle-school grades, life can be tougher in Civvy street, and the SAF maybe about the best option insofar as it will open doors to help in one's further education up to Polytechnic level.

    Under such circumstances, the seeming disadvantage maybe the extent of bonded contract with the SAF, which can be turned into an advantage with further upgrading of skills, or with available spare and idle time to pursue further self-studies.

    By the time one come to the age of 28 to 30 years old, it is best to get back into Civvy street, unless one is being assigned to some high-tech specialist or skilled trade in any units of the SAF that will bring in even higher wages that cannot be expected in Civvy Street given the status quo to one's experience.

    At the age of 28 to 30 years old, it is not too late to start or to change a new career, and it is better then being pushed into civvy street past this age band - especially when not mentally prepared for such a surprise.

    However, SAF pays well, and its wage structure maybe superior to Civvy Street for one's age and experience, as the SAF has to compete with the Private Sector for skilled or educated personnel to fill the needs of its organisational manpower structure.

    Unfortunately, only a few skills picked up in the SAF is applicable in Civvy Street - other than Man-Management skills, the other suitable SAF trade skills will be the truck driving skills for various classes; the Combat Engineer Skills in explosive demolition works and heavy earth moving equipment handling; Naval Scuba Diving for off-shore deep-sea maintenance work; the vehicle and engine repairs; gun maintenance and repairs skills are also sought after by some gun clubs and the Singapore Police Force - as well as local GLC owned Shipyards involved with naval shipbuilding; aircraft technicians will find niche employment as ground crew with local and foreign airlines - the engine specialists absorbed by Aircraft Engine manufacturers with local facilities.

    If one is to expand the horizon beyond this Little Red Dot, one should be able to see a wider range of opportunities that the SAF background experience may directly or indirectly come into positive effect.

    The problem with a career in the SAF is that it is a conscript army. In a conscript army where the conscripts vastly outnumber the regulars, it will be an army designed for and run for conscripts.

    Take the issue of the army taking liberties with your time/weekends. Because the vast majority of the people in the army are lowly paid conscripts, it costs the army next to nothing to mobilise them en-masse for rubbish like NDP. The fact that their time is cheap also means that the army can be very inefficient in the way it uses its soldiers. It can, for instance, get 300 soldiers to wait even though there is nothing to do. After all, anyone who does a simple risk analysis will conclude that it is better to have them on stand by and not need them than need them and not have them ready at hand - especially as they cost next to no money!

    As a regular specialist, you are tainted by association as you are just one level above them and is needed to look after them.

    As well, many of the roles that a regular soldier fills can also be performed by a conscript. Hence, your value to the SAF is diminished as your role gets narrower and narrower.

    Having so many conscripts also mean that your complaints about condition of service will have less impact. Whenever, you complain about pay, for instance, someone only needs to compare your pay to the conscripts to undermine your argument.

    The point of this rambling post? Don't be a regular soldier.
