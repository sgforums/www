    Half the question centres around yourself if you've actually thought about it. What you choose to study at college and what you ultimately decide to do for a living are two seperate entities altogether - while you could choose to study something that prepares you for a career in that field, you're not obliged to do so. Herein lies the paradox : what happens if you choose to pursue a career in a field unrelated to your area of specialisation?

    Truth be told, degrees in the relevant discipline really aren't that sought after if you were to consider the practices of many of the developed nations ( I must stress this approach does not extend to include industries requiring professional certification like law, medicine and engineering ). One of the reasons why the finance industry proves so appealing ( besides the financial lure ) to all and sundry stems from the fact that most of the world's top financial institutions would actually - and quite readily - hire graduates from across a vast array of disciplines for numerous positions.

    In truth, I think that has served them pretty well considering what such firms need are human beings equipped with the skills to analyse, think and make rational decisions ( known to many in such industries as "life skills" ), rather than a machine with a photographic memory capable of regurgitating knowledge/facts. In that sense, successful applicants for such jobs are deemed - at least, in the eyes of the employer - to have acquired sufficient skills to make these decisions in school ( while one may further contend that it isn't realistic to restrict such employment opportunities to college graduates, they unfortunately have to use some sort of benchmark to determine if you've been schooled sufficiently and competently to have acquired a reasonable grasp of life skills ). At the end of the day, that's what most employers care about.

    Of course the abovementioned wouldn't apply to someone without a degree in a relevant field wanting to work as a doctor/lawyer/engineer/accountant...

    What you've observed, in essence, was what I addressed earlier. While you could self-learn the rotes of the trade on your own, you can only practise as an engineer if you're certified/accredited by the body that governs your particular specialised profession (if applicable) - that, in truth, is how things work for every industry that requires professionals (ie.people without accreditation are not recognised in that industry). In our case (assuming you and I decide to become practising engineers in Singapore), we - through our degrees - are accredited by the PEB (Singapore) to do that job. That is what seperates us from those who aspire to be an engineer - and may even be more knowledgeable than us - but do not have the qualifications to allow them to practise the trade by PEB (http://www.peb.gov.sg/peb/html/index.html).

    What I'm trying to say is, if your alternative career is not a job that requires professional qualifications and accreditations, you're absolutely not disadvantaged just because you decide to work in a field that is unrelated to your studies...
