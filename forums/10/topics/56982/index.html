    'Militants are our brothers-in-arms'

    KUALA LUMPUR - PAS chief Abdul Hadi Awang yesterday embraced Hamas and Islamic Jihad militant groups openly as brothers-in-arms and said that Palestinian suicide bombers were martyrs giving up their lives to save thousands.

    His declaration is a measure of how much Muslim sentiments here have shifted in the two years since Sept 11.

    Sympathy towards the United States over the tragedy and fear of being tagged a militant sympathiser have given way way to open support of organisations viewed by the West as terrorist groups.

    While the conservative Islamic party has always had links with Hamas and Islamic Jihad, it has preferred not to trumpet it in public for fear of being seen as too radical.

    There was no such inhibition yesterday.

    Datuk Hadi Awang said: 'PAS will always be with our friends from Hamas and Islamic Jihad, even though they have been labelled as terrorists or criminals by the imperialist power United States and the Zionist regime in Israel.'

    His statement drew a chorus of Allahu Akbar (God is great) from 2,000 PAS delegates at the assembly.

    He ridiculed the US-led war on terrorism, saying that the Bush administration's designs in Afghanistan and Iraq were motivated by a thirst for oil.

    During a press conference, the PAS president said the Jemaah Islamiah bombing of the JW Marriott Hotel in Jakarta 'is not allowed in Islam', drawing a distinction between such attacks and Palestinians and Chechnyans fighting oppression. -- Brendan Pereira

    'They don't exist'
    Head of Islamic group sparks controversy by saying JI was created by US to put pressure on Indonesia

    By Devi Asmarani

    JAKARTA - A leader of Indonesia's largest Islamic group, the Nadhlatul Ulama (NU), has sparked an uproar by saying that the Jemaah Islamiah (JI) terror group does not exist.

    He made the remarks last weekend, a day after the United States froze the assets and accounts of 10 suspected terrorists and shortly after a court acquitted militant cleric Abu Bakar Bashir of the charges of being JI's spiritual leader.

    'The verdict of Bashir is proof that JI does not exist in Indonesia, even if it exists in other countries,' Mr Hasyim said in Surabaya, East Java on Saturday.

    He said that the US was playing the JI card to put pressure on and control Indonesia and other Muslim countries.

    JI is a label applied to Muslim movements, especially fundamentalist ones, in order to push them into a corner, he said.

    His comments raised eyebrows as many feared that it could signal a setback in the country's fight against terrorism.

    They came as a surprise as he and his 40-million strong organisation had been at the forefront of efforts by religious leaders to condemn militancy and support the government's efforts to fight terrorism.

    On Wednesday, three foreign envoys separately met the cleric at his office to seek a clarification on his statement.

    US Ambassador to Indonesia Ralph Boyce said: 'There has been some confusing statement (made by Mr Hasyim) in headlines of several print media which recently said that JI did not exist. So I want to seek clarification and hold a regular meeting to clear the confusion.

    'People might think that terrorism is not a serious problem. Indeed, terrorism is a very serious problem,' he said.

    Later, the cleric met separately Australian Ambassador to Indonesia David Ritchie and a member of the European Parliament, Mr Hartmut Nassauer.

    What emerged was an agreement between him and the foreign envoys that terrorism was not associated with a particular religion and that the US and Indonesia should continue to work together to fight the threats of terrorism.

    As for his earlier remark, Mr Hasyim said he believed there were terrorist organisations in Indonesia, but he did not know for sure whether JI did exist in Indonesia.

    'In the past 30 years I have been in NU, I have never known of such an organisation, where its office is and who its figures are,' he said in a joint press briefing with Mr Boyce after their meeting.

    'It could be an underground group, but that is still based on intelligence reports. We still don't know the truth,' he said.

    He added: 'I never meant to undermine our efforts to fight terrorism by saying this.'

    While the ambassadors tried to smooth over the differences of opinion, observes said that they appeared to have been disappointed at the way Mr Hasyim contradicted himself and his organisation's stance on terrorism in Indonesia.

    A Western analyst said: 'The US government and its allies have relied on Mr Hasyim as the leader of the largest Muslim organisation to support the campaign against terrorism.

    'He should have known that this kind of statement would send the wrong signal both to Indonesians and the international community.'

    Many believe that Mr Hasyim, like other Indonesian politicians, including Vice-President Hamzah Haz, was merely playing to domestic audience with his latest anti-US statement.

    Analysts and foreign observers said his statement was probably politically motivated, citing the fact that he was considering running for presidency or the vice-presidency.

    Mr Muniem Sirri, a Muslim scholar from the Paramadina Foundation, said: 'By saying that JI did not exist, Mr Hasyim only gives room to the hardliners, making it seem like he is protecting them.'

    PAS: Malay vote is key to ruling Malaysia
    PAS vice-president says wresting all of Umno's 72 seats in Parliament can be done by focusing on Malay Muslims

    KUALA LUMPUR - Slice away all the religious rhetoric surrounding Parti Islam SeMalaysia (PAS) and this is what's left: another Malay-based political party searching for the quickest way to power.

    That path to power was sketched by party vice-president Mustafa Ali on the final day of the party's four-day assembly.

    A skilled backroom strategist, he cut through all the fluff from the floor about winning over the non-Muslim electorate by pointing out that PAS needs to wrest all the 72 Umno seats in Parliament to form the next government.

    His reasoning is anchored on the unassailable assumption in Malaysian politics - that whoever controls the Malay vote rules the country.

    This same assumption has given Umno the managing partner status in the Barisan Nasional coalition and reserved the position of prime minister of Malaysia for the president of Umno.

    'The reality is that our country must be based on the Malay Muslims. This means that if there is no Umno, PAS and Keadilan will be the basis of national politics to Malay Muslims,' said Datuk Mustafa.

    A fortnight ago, the unflappable politician experienced 25 minutes of provocative and intense questioning by Mr Tim Sebastian, the host of BBC talk show, Hardtalk.

    Datuk Mustafa then declared that the session was not challenging enough.

    In the 1999 general election, PAS won 27 parliamentary seats on the back of deep divisions in the Malay community over the sacking and jailing of former deputy prime minister Anwar Ibrahim for abuse of power and corruption.

    Umno's representation in Parliament dropped from 94 seats in 1995 to 72.

    Between 1995 and 1999, the ruling party's share of the popular vote declined from 36.5 per cent to 29.5 per cent.

    In 58 parliamentary constituencies in West Malaysia with more than two-thirds Malay voters each, Umno's share of the votes fell from 62 per cent in 1995 to 49 per cent in 1999.

    Since that electoral success, PAS members have been setting their sights on the ultimate prize: ruling Malaysia.

    Over the past four years, party leaders have worked hard on the Muslim ground and tried to get closer to Chinese and Indian voters.

    During the party assembly, Kelantan delegate Hassan Mahmood urged the party leadership to work doubly hard to attract the Chinese vote. 'To attract the Chinese, PAS needs to raise issues about the economy and education and give assurances that their lifestyle will not change,' he said.

    The party is also considering allowing non-Muslims to be associate members.

    But realistically, leaders like Datuk Mustafa know that many non-Muslims will always be suspicious of the Islamic state agenda of PAS.

    He also knows that it will be difficult for a party with limited resources, reach and appeal to win any of the parliamentary constituencies in Sabah and Sarawak, where indigenous non-Muslims still form a significant proportion of the electorate.

    So the best way to come to power is to target the 72 constituencies currently held by Umno.

    Said Datuk Mustafa: 'We win the Umno seats, we become the backbone.'

    That goal will not be possible in the coming elections, he knows.

    But party insiders are counting on demographic changes, continued infighting within Umno and growing religious interest among the Malays to deliver the votes in Malay-majority constituencies.

    They believe that PAS will achieve power through this route one day.

    Datuk Mustafa yesterday signalled the party's intentions to travel along this path.
