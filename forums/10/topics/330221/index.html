    Malaysian ruling party punishes member over race row
    Posted: 11 September 2008 0051 hrs

     
     

    KUALA LUMPUR: A member of Malaysia's ruling party was on Wednesday suspended for three years for "stoking racial tensions" with incendiary comments about ethnic Chinese that shook the governing coalition.

    Ahmad Ismail, a division chief of the United Malays National Organisation (UMNO) which leads the Barisan Nasional coalition, had called Chinese "squatters" and warned of repercussions if they tried to seek political power.

    The furore has highlighted rifts between majority Muslim Malays and the ethnic Chinese and Indian minorities, and raised fears of a split in the coalition of race-based parties which has ruled for half a century.

    "The supreme council has found that Ahmad Ismail's statement and actions have caused much anger and protest from the BN component parties," Prime Minister Abdullah Ahmad Badawi said after a meeting of the policy-making body.

    "We have decided that he will be suspended from the party for three years," he told a press conference.

    Abdullah said that racially-charged comments threatened the future of Malaysia, which has seen bloody racial riots in the past and is determined to maintain ethnic harmony.

    "The racial situation in our country looks like it is showing signs of strain and increasing tensions among races," he said.

    "We for so long have been able to control the situation so that nothing untoward happens, but we can't allow a situation like this to continue... which could affect our peace and stability."

    Despite the punishment, Ahmad remained defiant, saying: "I will not retract, I made no mistake, I will not apologise."

    "I will be staying with UMNO, which is my party... Make no mistake, I will make a comeback," said the member from northern Penang state, who is one of 191 division chiefs representing the party's grassroots.

    Koh Tsu Koon, acting president of the Chinese-based Gerakan which bore the brunt of his outburst, was lukewarm in his response and said police must now take action over allegations of threats made by Ahmad.

    "UMNO's decision to suspend Ahmad Ismail is a step in the right direction. We hope this will serve as deterrent against similar irresponsible and insensitive actions," he said.

    The row has erupted as opposition leader Anwar Ibrahim - whose Keadilan party is the first pan-racial party in Malaysian political history - attempts to woo the support of enough coalition lawmakers to topple the government.

    In March elections the opposition dramatically increased the number of seats it holds, and the race row was seen as jeopardising support for the government among its Chinese-based member parties.

    "The patience of the Malays and Muslims has a limit. Do not push us to the wall, as when we turn back we will be forced to push the Chinese in the interests of our own survival," Ahmad said in a press conference on Monday.

    "The Chinese should not try to be like the Jews in America - it is not enough they control the economy, now they want political control," he said.

    Abdullah, who has faced calls to quit since the elections debacle, was presented with a further headache on Wednesday as a top minister said he should consider handing over power to his deputy earlier than mid-2010 as planned.

    "I'm being very frank and open because I have no qualms about saying what I think is correct: 2010 is too long," said Trade Minister Muhyiddin Yassin, who is a potential challenger. - AFP/de
