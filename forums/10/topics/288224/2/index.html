    >>Why PAP in 1962 hell-bent on wanting merger with Malaysia?

    Reply by Gandhi Ambalam:

    I hope you don't mind if I bring in a few relevant historical developments before answering your question.

    In 1961, the PAP was at its lowest ebb in popularity, having lost the Hong Lim by-election to Mr Ong Eng Guan and the Anson by-election to Mr David Marshall of WP. Not only these unfavorable events affected the PAP under Lee Kuan Yew's leadership, but the biggest blow that took place the same year was the split within the PAP. The splinter group calling itself the Barisan Sosialis took away almost the entire PAP branches, together with members and supporters, leaving only Lee Kuan Yew and his cabinet colleagues. The Barisan split came about because of unfulfilled promises of the PAP in its manifesto in the 1959 elections. As far as the Barisan leaders were concerned, the PAP under Lee Kuan Yew was deviating from its pro-people, pro-worker policies -- a betrayal of its socialistic principles.

    In this changed political environment, the then Malayan Prime Minister, Tunku Abdul Rahman announced for the first time the Malaysian concept of merging Independent Malaya with the British Crown Colony Singapore and the British Borneo Territories. This announcement by the Tunku to the foreign correspondents in Singapore came as a boon to the beleaguered Lee Kuan Yew and his almost "skeletal" PAP.

    Lee Kuan Yew quickly latched on to this Malaysia concept which had the blessings of the British. The PAP propaganda machine was quickly set into motion with the claim that "Malaysia is as sure as the Sun rises".

    Historically, Malaya, including Singapore existed under the British as a single entity until after WW II. It was only after the War that Singapore came under the direct rule of London as a British Crown Colony. Malaya became Independent in 1957, but Singapore still remained under the British with its excellent harbour and with the highest concentration of British troops east of Suez.

    Almost all the political parties in Singapore were not against the reality of the Island becoming part of Peninsular Malaya. But the only objection were that of the unfair terms of merger and above all the inclusion of the Borneo Territories. Indonesia under Seokarno objected to the formation of Malaysia because it saw it as an imperialist plot. The Philippines under President Macapagal, the father of present president Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo, also opposed Malaysia for its historical claim over Sabah.

    The PAP under Lee Kuan Yew had no alternative but to quickly promote merger to the people to prevent an imminent demise of the ruling party from the onslaught of the mass-based Barisan Sosialis headed by its charismatic leader Lim Chin Siong. The hastily arranged unfair merger and its subsequent breakup within two years stands testimony to the Barisan's initial objection to PAP's bulldozed merger.

    Gandhi Ambalam
    Chairman
    Singapore Democratic Party

    The First Battle for Independence

    David Marshall led the first Merdaka Mission to open negotiations with the British for Independence of Singapore. Constitutional discussions began in London in April 1956. On board, representing the PAP, were Lim Chin Siong and Lee Kuan Yew.

    The mission returned in failure and their demands for independence were refused. The British felt that the Labour Front government was too weak and the Communist elements in Singapore too powerful. If there was to be independence, the British fears needed to be calmed. David Marshall resigned and Lim Yew Hock took over as Chief Minister. He had two objectives. Firstly, he had to prove to the British that Singapore was able to resist Communism. Secondly, he wanted to purge the trade unions, schools and political parties of pro-Communist and Left Wing Leaders who were beginning to threaten the rule of the moderate politicians such as himself and LKY. Thus began a series of arrests under the Public Security Ordinance. Lim Chin Siong, Devan Nair and Fong Swee Suan were some of the prominent politicians being detained.
