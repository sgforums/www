    By Todd Hartman And Ellen Miller, Rocky Mountain News

    Donor Alliance was so concerned about the impact of Young's assertions on prospective donors that the group organized a press briefing that included two metro-area coroners and a neurosurgeon.

    In addition, a top executive of St. Mary's Hospital in Grand Junction - where the patient was declared brain-dead and the organs were removed - flew to Denver to participate.

    On Monday, Young, a part-time coroner who is not a licensed physician but makes his living as a paramedic, announced in a news release that he had ruled the death of William Rardin, 31, a homicide.

    Young said that St. Mary's, along with Donor Alliance, made a declaration of brain death that didn't meet "acceptable medical standards" after Rardin shot himself in the head on Sept. 26.

    But other coroners closed ranks Tuesday around Donor Alliance and St. Mary's, saying that initial indications suggest all guidelines were adhered to and that Rardin was legally brain-dead.

    "From what I can tell preliminarily, it does look like all the procedures were followed," said Arapahoe County Coroner Michael Doberson, who serves as the president of the Colorado Coroners Association and expects to participate in a more detailed review of the case.

    Also weighing in was Mesa County coroner Rob Kurtzman, who conducted the autopsy on Rardin's body. Kurtzman, a forensic pathologist, said he found damage to the brain "typical for a gunshot wound."

    "His injuries were not compatible with sustained life and the findings were typical of brain death," Kurtzman said in a statement. "They were typical of any individual who was pronounced brain-dead."

    Kurtzman performed the autopsy because Rardin's body was in Mesa County, after he was declared brain-dead at St. Mary's.

    Young, however, took jurisdiction of the case away from Kurtzman, saying in his news release Monday that "jurisdiction of the case was transferred from the Mesa County Coroner to the Montrose County Coroner per (state statute)."

    Â• The brain-death note was reviewed and accepted by Donor Alliance, as well as by three other doctors.

    Â• The donor was listed on the Colorado Donor Registry, and family members cooperated with the organ donation coordinator.

    Prinster also noted that Young was the official who gave final clearance allowing organs to be recovered from Rardin even though he had the authority to delay the process pending his own review of the situation.

    "One has to ask, why didn't (Young) address the issue at that time?" Prinster said.
