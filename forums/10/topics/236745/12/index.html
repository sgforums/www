And therefore the vast majority of Singaporeans who support organ transplant will also accept brain death.

    No issue with the above.

    My objection relates to this statement of yours:
    'Therefore legal guardians have a right to object still as long as it is prior to the person's death. After all it is prior to the utter demise of the poor soul when the doctor asks his family to consider donation .'

    First, there is a difference between relatives and legal guardians, as I have mentioned. Not every relative is a legal guardian or vice versa. Secondly, legal guardians, who have the legal right to make decisions on behalf of the patient, can only make a declaration to refuse organ donation BEFORE the certification of brain death. Once brain death is certified, such a declaration cannot be made. Third, unless one is mentally incompetent or is a minor or is incompetent as a result of illness eg. Alzheimer disease (http://www.alzheimer-europe.org/?lm4=E1714A531042) , most people will not have a legal guardian. This applies to the small number of people in society who are not legally competent to manage their own affairs. A corollary might be someone with power of attorney. You can delegate the power to make legal decisions for you to another person, such as a relative. In the case of organ donation, you can grant a relative or trusted friend durable power of attorney. In the event that you fall into a coma (bit not yet brain dead), he or she can make the decision for you and opt out of organ donation or request that the ventilator be turned off etc...Of course, if you opt out now, you won't have to worry about that in future.

    In summary, the opt out system in Austria is very similar to that in Singapore. Relatives in Austria do not have the right to object to organ donation in the event of a brain dead patient who had not previously made a declaration to opt out.
