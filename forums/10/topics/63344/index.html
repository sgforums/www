    The Workers' Party: Romance, Reality, Revival?

    By Sylvia Lim

    Sylvia Lim, 38, law lecturer, former Police Inspector and lawyer, is the Chairman of the Workers' Party.

    In late June 2003, I received an email from a school student. He said:

    "I am secondary four student currentlyÂ…Â….Though I am still below the legal age to vote, I guess people of my age group would [be] the target group for votes in the near future.

    [O]ne thing I realise about people of my age group is that they have the impression that PAP = Government and Opposition Party = Anti-Government. I believe that each individual party should be known as it is. But currently, any other parties other then the PAP is known as the opposition party. Not many people of my age know what constitutes "opposition party". They know nothing about the WP or SDP or any other party.

    If they do not know the opposition party now, I don' think they will in the future. I believe that being known as WP and not the Opposition Party is the first step to reach out to people. This will help to eliminate hopefully the feeling that any other party other than the PAP is anti-government.

    These are just some of my views and I hope they can be of some help to you."

    On June 1, 2003, the Workers' Party cadres voted for a "mega" Party renewal at our biennial Conference of Organisers. Eight out of the 14 Central Council members were not even Party members at the time of the previous Conference in May 2001. Since the announcement of those results in the press, we have had renewed interest from Singaporeans including those overseas. While I believe the 2003 Conference will historically be seen as a watershed for the Party, we have still a long road ahead. We, as a Party, must earn the people's trust as an organisation of committed Singaporeans who are not anti-government but have our own brand of constructive politics, as so well-advised by the student above. However, we have yet to reach the critical mass which is necessary for us to be a significant force in advancing the welfare of Singaporeans.

    Singapore claims to be first world, but, in the field of politics, it does not seem to be so. The ruling People's Action Party, the Opposition Parties and the People have all played a part in creating this state of affairs. If we are to mature politically, we must remake ourselves.

    WP has since 1957 not flinched from the challenge of opposition politics in Singapore. In our next phase, it makes sense for us to concentrate on building our organisational capabilities and not rely on individual heroes. To this end, there are 3 key areas we would want to enhance:

    First, our intellectual capability and resource pool so as to provide constructive, timely and relevant input on public policy (in short, brainpower);

    Second, our work on the ground, so as to convince Singaporeans of our sincerity and to canvass their support ahead of the next General Election (in short, manpower);

    Third, our pool of funds, so that we can acquire resources and expertise to broaden our public outreach (in short, money).

    Brainpower

    Our world has become very complex, and the continued economic and political survival of Singapore is no longer the "napshot" it was 20 years ago. The PAP government is now facing unprecedented challenges in an uncertain world and seems to be dependent on the USA to stimulate our economy and supplement our national defence.

    The country needs to have input from varied sources in moving the country forward, and even the PAP admits this by having many committees of "outsiders" debating the remaking of everything in Singapore (except remaking the PAP, which Prime Minister Goh says is not necessary).

    Any government, however apparently benign, must be effectively checked. The omnipresence of the PAP government in business and the media has already weakened the nation in its capacity for independent thought. This needs to be urgently corrected by getting experienced and/or qualified Singaporeans to participate in politics in opposition parties.

    We are often asked questions like: "How will voting for the opposition help the country to solve problems?" Well, two answers: First, it will pressurise the government to work harder and keep its promises / obligations to the people, and secondly, with adequate brainpower, we can consistently go into the details and offer alternative policies across many areas of public policy.

    I recently had an informal chat with some Nominated MPs, and shared with them that WP is not so much concerned with making political capital per se from the PAP's mistakes, but with the larger question of how to solve the pressing problems facing Singapore.

    In order to be able to constructively question the PAP, we need to address challenges such as:

    Information Access. Due to official information often being classified, we need alternative sources of information and people who know how to search for it. Unlike PAP ministers, we are unable to get civil servants to spend sleepless nights providing us with data and analyses within a short turn-around time. We do all the work ourselves from scratch through copious reading of materials, searching web-sites and consulting. If resources improve, we could have dedicated teams looking into specific areas.

    Analyses and Positions. Each of us is limited by our own knowledge and life experiences, and will not individually have the necessary multi-disciplinary knowledge or experience to propose solutions from a macro-perspective. Teamwork is essential for this.

    Manpower

    In order that we may reach our potential voters in preparation for the next General Election and beyond, here are some realities.

    A Group Representation Constituency currently consists of about 120,000 voters. Assuming each household has between 2 to 4 voters, this could easily work out to between 30,000 to 60,000 households. Would anyone like to place bets as to whether the PAP will further expand the GRCs by amending the Constitution to allow for 10, 20 member GRCs at the next General Election?

    The PAP has successfully entrenched its grassroots mechanisms through the formation of Residents' Committees and the use of the People's Association e.g. through Mayors and the Community Development Councils. You only need to look around you in your neighbourhood, at your workplace, or among your friends to suss out people with PAP connections. Breaking through these mechanisms to win the people's votes is possible, provided that we have a team of people with the "can-do" spirit.

    Moreover, only the "relevant few" would know how the electoral boundaries will be re-drawn ahead of the public announcement. At the last Election in Nov 2001, the report of the Electoral Boundaries Committee was released ONE DAY before Parliament was dissolved and the election called. In a Parliamentary sitting in May 2002, Minister Wong Kan Seng told the House that the PAP would "try not to do it again". Note that it was not phrased as a promise! Hence, working with the real possibility of a sudden re-drawing of boundaries, we will need people to work with us on large areas.

    Funding

    People sometimes ask how much we are paid by the Party and are then shocked that we are doing work voluntarily. Our Party is not rich, unlike the ruling PAP which has vast funds. As such, the challenge to produce quality work remains since most of us have to work full-time at other jobs. We need funds!

    It is perfectly legal to donate to opposition parties, and we do not have any duty to report to the authorities who our donors are, unless you give us a sum of $10,000 or more in one financial year.

    Political parties are required under the Political Donations Act to return anonymous donations if the total received by the Party from all anonymous sources in one financial year adds up to $5,000 or more. Hence, anonymous donations while appreciated may have to be surrendered to the Registrar of Political Donations; trust us, we have had to surrender donations in this way before!

    Therefore, if you are a Singaporean and can afford to support us in this way, take that step of making a donation! No amount is too small to show your support and encouragement.

    Why bother?

    Is it worthwhile to join or support the Workers' Party, with no guarantee of electoral victory or material advancement? The following extract from an editorial in an Indian newspaper was passed to me by a Singaporean. Although commenting on India, it bears consideration in the local context:

    "The long reign of popular favourites, without any significant opposition, is probably the main cause for the collapse of independent thinking (in this country). But a healthy democracy require[s] an Opposition that thinks differently and does not just want more of the same, a group of vigorously thinking citizens which aims at the general welfare, an Opposition that appeals to reason.... [S]uch an Opposition, even if it did not succeed in ousting the ruling party, might yet control and humanise it."

    Do consider whether you can spare some time and effort to join us or assist us. Everyone, and every little effort, counts.

    (1) Education Policies that placed unreasonably high standards that resulted in wasted talents at an early age, resulting in a high rate of psychological casualties amongst the young, which can only cause future social problems.

    (2) Economic Policies that provide huge incentives for foreign MNCs to establish their facilities in Singapore, to provide a high absorption rate of the large number of tertiary and secondary, academic and technical graduates that annually join the labor pool.

    (3) Government participation in local economic activities through Government Linked Companies, and partnerships with MNCs, that killed local enterprise and any further interests in entrepreneurship.
