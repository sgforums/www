    Even if 'the world has changed', as pro-casino advocates put it, Singapore CAN afford to take a different path from regional competitors like Malaysia and Macau, and yet not be left behind.

    Even with a resort-cum-casino projected to pull in nearly $5 billion a year in revenues and taxes, adding as much as 1 per cent GDP growth and spinning off 10,000 jobs or more, the economic numbers eventually won't hold up.

    The simple reason: Long after the high-rolling visitors have left our shores, the locals will end up mopping the bill for the broader fallout - bankruptcies, job losses, crime, divorces, dysfunctional families, rehabilitation programmes.

    Australian and US studies, after all, have shown that even if tourists are the prime targets, some two per cent of locals will almost always become problem gamblers - with or without 'social safeguards' in place.

    In the Singapore context, according to a government survey released this week, that adds up to 55,000 residents, not counting the spillover impact on their families.

    So no, society won't 'break down' overnight. But looking ahead, the casino's detractors stress, it's not a gamble worth taking.
