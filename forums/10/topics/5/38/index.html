    Some thoughts on reservist (operationally ready national service if you like) training, the SAF, war and peace.

    I don't like the SAF as an organisation (in fact, i quite detest it) because i have had some very bad experiences whilst under its power, and so I don't like having to go for my annual ICT. But after thinking about it, i have come to the conclusion that although i don't like the SAF, i should take my training seriously. If it has to be done, then i should do it properly and not go into reservist boh-chap mode.

    I am not a propaganda writer for the SAF or the PAP, this is just my honest opinion.

    Singapore's defence policy rests on a number of basic strategies: total defence, diplomacy, and deterrance. The deterrance bit is the part that the SAF is responsible for. In order to deter potential adversaries from going to war against Singapore, we need to show that have a credible war machine. We sometimes think that we can bo-chap during reservist time, and that in the event of war breaking out we will then be on the ball because Singapore's defence will then be at stake. However, what we should be trying to defend against is not so much some potential outside enemy, but war itself. And what we should be defending should not be limited to Singapore, but rather peace for all mankind, including other countries. And so it is actually the peace-time training that is more crucial for defence. When we are doing our reservist training, we are actively defending Singapore and keeping the peace at that point in time. If a war ever starts, then in a sense we have failed in our defence of Singapore and in our defence of peace.

    If war is the enemy, then we also have to take precautions that we do not contribute towards starting one ourselves. Armed forces can be used as a deterrant, but they can also be used for aggression. They can also be used as political tools, to put pressure on others by acting as an implied threat. And although they can act as a deterrant, they can also be perceived as a threat which needs to be attacked and eliminated. If we want to defend against war, then we need to find some kind of balance. Armed forces should not be used as a tool for applying political pressure. They should not adopt an aggressive or threatening stance towards others. Buying too many offensive weapons will only encourage an arms race. If the SAF becomes too powerful relative to the armed forces of other countries, Singapore's politicians may find it more easy to start a war in a moment of folly, or to start a war under lesser provocation than would otherwise be necessary to induce them to start one. Thereofre i view the recent announcements that the Malaysian are buying more modern arms with mixed feelings. Those arms can be seen as a potential threat to Singapore, but on the other hand, they may help maintain the regional balance of threats and help make it less likely that Singapore will start a war.
