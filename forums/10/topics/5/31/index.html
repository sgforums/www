    Title: Poland lobbying hard to sell battle tanks to Malaysia

    By: Syed Nadzri

    POLAND is vigorously trying to sell battle tanks to Malaysia, showing off to Prime Minister Datuk Seri Dr Mahathir Mohamad on Thursday what its PT- 91 can do.

    The PT-91 is an upgraded version of the T-72, popularly known as the Russian Tank which, defence experts say, has capabilities that are quite unmatched.

    Dr Mahathir, who is on a three-day official visit to Poland, was taken to a military camp at Wesola on the outskirts of Warsaw after lunch to see the PT-91 and other armoured vehicles in action.

    The Prime Minister braved chilly conditions and spent almost two hours watching the demonstration and even climbed onto a PT-91 with Defence Minister Datuk Seri Najib Tun Razak.

    The tank has impressive mobility and is said to have a top speed of 60km per hour, very fast for a 45-tonne chunk of steel.

The Prime Minister said decisions on the purchase of arms were not easy to make as many factors had to be taken into account.

    Does the Malaysian media believe that the country will become more powerful after the PT-91 purchase? I don't think there's any right or wrong answers, but one can draw his own conclusion from the following article:
    ==========================================

    Source: The Malay Mail
    Date: 25 March 2002
    Title: More power for Malaysia?
    Writer: Marhalim Abas

    WILL the impending purchase of the Polish-made PT-91 main battle tanks (MBT) for the Malaysian Army tip the military balance in her favour?

    Ananlysts told The Malay Mail that they would not.

    "Everyone in the region knew that the army had planned to acquire MBTs since the 1980s after the Communist Party agreed to lay down their arms," said an expert.

    "If the numbers are correct, 72 MBTs will not tip the scale in our favour. If we bought 1,000 tanks, that's another matter."

    Prime Minister Datuk Seri Dr Mahathir Mohamad announced on Saturday that the Government had agreed to purchase the tanks from Poland.

    Though he did not specify their numbers, a local daily reported that 72 PT-91s would be acquired.

    They are an upgraded version of the T-72 MBT which is the mainstay of the Russian army and its allies.

    The analyst said the Singapore Army, with its three Combined Arms divisions, was probably the most potent force, "pound-for-pound" in the region.

    He said with 72 tanks, the best the Malaysian Army can do is to set up an armoured or Combined Arms brigade.

    "It will be like the Combined Arms division in Singapore. A Combined Arms brigade will have at least two tanks and infantry batallions supported by an equal number of artillery, engineers and signal troops."

    "The brigade will also need an integral airlift capability."

    He said there is also the need for close air support with attack helicopters or fighter bombers or both. The three Singapore Combined Arms divisions have at least two brigades attached to each.

    He said Singapore had taken steps to counter the perceived threat by buying at least 12 AH-64D Long-bow Apache attack helicopters from the United States. They are expected to be delivered in 2004, two years ahead of schedule.

    The analyst said the Malaysian Army needs another RM3 billion to ensure they could form a combat-capable Combined Arms brigade.

    He said the Malaysian Army needs at least RM10 billion to upgrade its six divisions.

    ==================================

    Source: BERNAMA The Malaysian National News Agency
    Date: 1 Apr. 2002
    Article: BATTLE TANKS WILL NOT DAMAGE ROADS, ASSURES NAJIB.

    KAJANG, April 1 (Bernama) - Defence Minister Datuk Seri Najib Tun Razak today assured the public that the use of main battle tanks would not cause damage to roads in the country.

    He said the 45-tonne PT-91 main battle tank, Poland's version of the Russian T-72, would not damage the roads as it will transported on trailers for mobility.

    "We have bought low-loaders (trailers). Apart from that, the tanks would be fitted with rubber tracks," he said after performing the ground-breaking ceremony for the Defence Science and Technology Reserach Institute (IPSTP) in Semenyih near here.

    The rubber tracks may be installed on the PT-91 tanks by local firms which had developed the technology. Such tracks have been used by the army's tracked vehicles like the Scorpion light tanks and Stormer armoured personnel carriers.

    In the past, plans to use heavy battle tanks came under public criticism as it was feared that local roads and geological structures may not be able to withstand the tanks' massive weight.

    Najib said he expected Malaysia to have a regiment of PT-91 tanks, to be purchased from Poland as announced by Prime Minister Datuk Seri Dr Mahathir Mohamad during his recent visit to the country.

    A tank regiment has some 50 battle tanks.

    On the IPSTP, Najib hoped it would catalyse the local defence technologies which would in turn enhance Malaysia's rapidly developing defence industries.

    The first phase of the IPSTP, developed at the cost of RM26 million, is due for completion July next year.

    Title: How the PT-91 measures up against the rest

    From: The Malay Mail (25 Mar 2001)HOW would the PT-91 fare in battle? One should look at the performance of the Russian T-72s since the Polish-made PT-91s were modelled after the former.

    The T-72, an updated model of earlier T-54 and T-62 MBTs, was among the first to dispense with the fourth crew member - the gun-loader - when the manufacturers came out with an auto-loader.

    Experts disagreed whether the auto-loader gave the T-72 crew an advantage over other tanks.

    It helped the designer to reduce the tank turret to just 2.19 metre. But some pointed out that the auto-loader could be damaged in battle or due to wear-and-tear.

    The T-72 was the mainstay of the tank armies in the Middle East. It saw combat in the Iran-Iraq and the 1991 Gulf Wars.

    The T-72 first saw action in Lebanon, with the Syrian Army opposing Israeli tanks.

    Though the Israelis claimed that the Russian tanks were not up to par with the US or British-made tanks, many of the vehicles captured were later modified and thrust into its own service.

    During the Iran-Iraq war, the T-72 served both sides with mixed results.

    Some 10 years after that war ended, the Iraqi T-72 faced the Coalition forces equipped with the most advanced tank forces from the US and Britain.

    The Iraqi armour was ground down and most of the tanks were destroyed by airplanes which enjoyed air superiority.

    Western military experts again cited inept leadership and lack of training as reasons for the poor showing by the Iraqi armoured forces.

    They also said that rounds fired from the 125mm gun of the Russian tanks failed to penetrate the armour on the US-made M1A2 Abrams and British Challenger MBTs.

    Newspaper: The Malay Mail (12 Apr 2002)

    Boeing had proposed a similar scheme if the Super Hornet was selected by Malaysia.

    It was learnt that both jets would be in service with the RMAF by 2007. The Super Hornet costs in the region of US$40 million (RM152 million) apiece. The RMAF operates 17 MiG-29N Fulcrums and eight F/A-18D Hornets.
