    Let's not get too carried away with the US Army's vision of the FCS. First of all, we have to recognise that the requirements of the US Army are different from ours. They are an expeditionary force, having to deploy to conflicts anywhere in the world when called upon. This means that in addition to requiring tactical mobility, their land forces requires strategic mobility. Their lack of rapid deployability is glaring in the fact that it took 6 mths to build up their forces in S.Arabia during ODS. (only 101st arrived early, and they had no stopping force against any attack) Its understood that should China try to invade Taiwan, China will prevent the entry of American forces into the theatre. In fact, as recently reported, China has been trying to acquire such a capability for some time, though its more directed towards America's carriers.
    America thus came up with the FCS. Its supposed to be a system which is highly air-deployable, and will have an offensive capability disproportionate to its size. It will rely on maximum situational awareness achieved through UAVs, the Wolfpack system (whole range of other sys lah), and exploit the info gathered to engage at places and times of their choosing. Currently, the concept is that engagement will be 'stand-off', as they would not be survivable against close-in engagements againts heavy armour. However, I believe research is going on to improve the defenses of the FCS vehicles to enable close-in engagements too, but this will most probably rely on 'active' defense measures like Boeings SLID. At 20 tons, insufficient amt of current armour can be installed, and even new concepts of passive armour in development would not be able to handle a direct hit from a tank's main gun, if they still wanna keep to the 20-ton limit.

    Now, the question is, do we really have to follow the US Army's vision? We must understand that should war break out, the theatre of operations would be right HERE. There is no requirement for strategic mobility, tactical mobility is more than enough. We should, however, aim to reduce the logistical tail of our forces. Electric drive systems, decreasing the size of our ammo while increasing their lethality and Pks etc are some of the technologies we should invest in.

    Another point of the FCS is their over-reliance on situational awareness. Due to their lack of armour, they try their best to engage at a stand-off distance. In other words, FULL situational awareness is not just important to the survival of the FCS, but critical. Should they come unexpectedly into contact with the enemy, they will be at a serious disadvantage. On a land terrain, with uneven ground limiting LOS, land features like trees, etc, situational awareness is not acquired as easily as at sea. Add this to the fact that the enemy will try their best to not be seen, and you will have hell of a trouble trying to get and maintain your situational awareness. The US, even with all their ISR equipment, (which is by far the best in the world) would be seriously challenged. If possible at all, all this can only be done with an extremely comprehensive network of sensors with extensive integration between sensors and shooters. This leads us to the question of
    1. do we have the technology to integrate our systems to such an extent required by an FCS like concept? And if so, make the links robust enuff to continue to function under heavy jamming conditions? Its important to note that should the links fail to work for whatever reason, the whole system will actually be LESS capable than the systems today.
    2. Do we have the financial capability to develop the extensive number and types of systems required to carry out the concept?
    The US will have to utilise all the resources available to them, like the J-STARS, U-2, RC-135, Prophet EW system, future Wolfpack system, Space Radar, blah blah blah. Can we afford to put in place such a system?
