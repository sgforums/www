    Eyezer, your points are valid. However, we have to look at a greater schema of things. Where the battles would be fought, what terrain would the tanks be fought on, what battle doctrine, etc.

    Most MBTs today are designed during the cold war period suitable for European terrain and environment. They are pretty well suited for flatter terrain, eg. plain and desert environment than jungle, hilly terrain like that of tropical environment.

    In terms of tropical region like ours, we probably be encounter lots of jungle type warfare or FIBUA.

    Protection in terms of thickness of armour can never be absolutely replaced by electronic defensive aids. This is evidence in the Israeli Merkava design. However, there is NO tanks in the world that are not penetratable. If the theater of battle usually involves close tank vs tank battles, then the level of armour becomes the primary protection for the tank crews (such as the middle east warfare in 60s, 70s and the 80s between Israeli and Eygptians, Syrians and Jordanians).

    Sweden and Russians however came up the idea of active defensive system to defend themselves. The Swedish CV90 and the Russian tanks are usually more lightly armoured than the NATO ones. The design attempt to use those defensive aids and general mobility and agility to survive. This is evidence in their speed compared to say the British Challenger tank.

    In Singapore, Indonesia and Malaysia terrain, tanks that are too heavy will often find themselves too difficult to move around. There are too many hills, creeks, ruggered terrain to cross. They also place too much reliance on Combat engineers to have sufficient resources to built bridges or clear obstacles for them to advance. Not to say they also consume more gas, more transport of fuel are necessary to refuel these heavy MBTs. Lighter tanks (especially ambipbous ones) are much more easier to support. Wheeled ones are even better. Of course all these mean that they better not get hit in the first place. The defensive aids also help them to get hit easily.

    Of course, here we are assuming that these light tanks are sufficiently armed to counter heavier tanks. They should be armed with at least 105mm gun (preferrably 120mm smoothbore). Alternatively, they could also be armed with ATGM on their turret, something like that of M2 Bradley.

    Just my 2 cents...

    The problem with trying to defeat ATGMs is that sooner or later one is going to get you, thick armour of not. With the advent of smart ATGMs with new attack profiles(hitting from the top etc), there are limits to what ever-increasing armour can do. The trend today is to go with electronic means to defeat ATGMs. I'm not a armour expert(metal birds are more my thing), so my knowledge on this is limted. Maybe one of the other guys can give you more info on this.

    What I do know is in jungle fighting, the smart way to go about it is through stealth and mobility, not brute force and static lines. The Japanese, and to a certain extent the NVA/VC, demonstrated this in the Malayan and Vietnamese jungles. The Japanese plan focused on speed and mobility, to the extent that they were told to ignore logistics in part and take what they need from the British(who were extremely helpful on this point). They bypassed the fixed British lines of defence and hit them hard from the sides or the rear. Since they are no LSVs in 1941, the Japanese used the next best thing for traversing the terrain: every bicycle they could lay their hands on. Their tactics got them so far that they defeated a force 3 TIMES their number in 3 months. Admittedly training and other factors played a part as well, but tactics and mobility was important too. It still applies today.
