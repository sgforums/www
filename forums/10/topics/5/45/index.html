    I want to reply first to whether Singapore can win a war.

    I say YES to a large extent.

    There are several advantages in Singapore's favour.

    Singapore is able to field a 300,000 strong defence force within six hours, supposing our NSman turn up. Its technology, which includes many force multipliers such as air-to-air refueling aircraft and early airborne warning aircraft, will enable to have an edge over its potential aggressor. Singapore currently has the most effective force in South-east Asia. However, there are certain points to be considered, especially in the event of war with Malaysia, our closest and not-so-very-friendly neighbour. They have 18 Atos II MLRS while Singapore does not have any MLRS. They also have 8 F/A-18 fighter/attack planes. These may severly damage, if not cripple the army later if not taken out by the airforce fast. Also, Malaysia is able to turn what is usually a tactical weapon, artillery, into a tactical weapon simply by placing it at the southern most tip of Johore, where it will be able to strike at virtually the whole of Singapore.

    By missile attack, I assume ICBMs, SLBMs and other ballistic missiles. While it is true that Singapore does not have the ability to defend against such weapons, it is improbable and highly unlikely that such an attack will be made against Singapore in the forseeable future. This is for the simple fact that no country in the region possess any ballistic missiles. The closest owners of such weapons would most likely be India and China.

    Another important point is the morale of the people of Singapore. Many posts on this forum think or even 'know' that SIngapore will not survive any attack. This only goes to show the fragility of many Singaporeans' state of mind. Not having the confidence of the people in the SAF, rather than the effectiveness of its force, should be Singapore's biggest fear in the event of an attack. This is bause there is a physical cure to improve the effectiveness or 'potency' of SAF, but none for the psychological state of mind of its people. To attempt to build up the confidence of the people in the SAF, the SIngapore government in 2000 paraded many pieces of military hardware. However, the comments of many people in this post have clearly shown that the government has failed. How many of the 350,000 NSmen will turn up to defend SIngapore, none of us will know. Hopefully, the negative posts here will not reflect the number of NSmen who turn up.

    Singapore would certainly have to launch an attack and not solely defend in any war. This is because Singapore does not have any hinterland to fall back on and give up some land to the enemy hoping it can regroup and later launch an attack to take its land back. Proof that this is included in planning for defence includes the large number of A-4SU aircraft which, like the 'A' in their name suggests, are generally for attacking of land targets. In the event SIngapore faces off with with Malaysia, it will most certainly capture up to a certain part of Johore. This is because Singapore has to ensure its supply of water first (until NEwater comes out). Also, Johore's large population of Chinese in comparison with the numbers of its Malays mean that there will be less change in its racial balance.

    A point in relative to war with Malaysia and Indonesia would be the loyalty of the Malays in the SAF. Would Singapore's Malays be willing to fight against Malays of other countries to defend their own homeland? This has become less of an issue lately as seen by the number of Malays who stood on the side of SIngapore when facing critisicms from Malaysia and Indonesia. Some Malays have also become pilots and others have been trusted with many high-tech and often vital hardware of the SAF. This clearly shows a very positive change of attitude toward Malays in Singapore.

    SIngapore also has many defence links with other countries. For example, Singapore enjoys a close defence relationship with Brunei, which also shares its borders with Malaysia. Thus, Malaysia also faces the possibility of having to wage war on 2 fronts if it invades other Singapore or Brunei and the other comes to the aid of the ocuntry being attack. Malaysia is certainly not capable of having such a war given its current capabilities.

    While many may argue, correctly, that Indonesia's large population may prove decisive in any war it wages with SIngapore, Indonesia's armed forces do not enjoy the same standards of training and technology as the SAF. It was also crippled by civil and political unrest which has only began to subside recently.

    This is not a complete list of Singapore's advantages and disadvantages but it should be sufficient for us to believe that Singapore capable of defending itself against any aggressor, but only if we are willing to do it and have the confidence in the SAF.
