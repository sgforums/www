    Malaysia summons Singapore's ambassador over Lee Kuan Yew's remarks on ethnic Chinese

    By PAULINE JASUDASON
    Associated Press Writer

    KUALA LUMPUR, Malaysia (AP) -- Malaysia summoned Singapore's ambassador Thursday to explain elder statesman Lee Kuan Yew's claim that ethnic Chinese minority citizens in Malaysia and Indonesia were being marginalized, officials said.

    "We have called the high commissioner and we want to know what exactly is the nature of the statement," Malaysian Foreign Minister Syed Hamid Albar said, noting that Prime Minister Abdullah Ahmad Badawi has also sent a letter to Singapore's government to seek an explanation.

    Syed Hamid urged Singapore to apologize for Lee's remark.

    "I think that statement went beyond fair comment, describing something that is not true within our country," Syed Hamid told reporters. "In all honesty, it should be right for them to say that they are sorry. ... But we will wait."

    Lee, Singapore's founding father who holds the Cabinet post of minister mentor, told a forum in Singapore on Sept. 15 that Malaysia and Indonesia "have problems with the Chinese. They are successful, they are hardworking, and therefore, they are systematically marginalized."

    Singapore's High Commissioner T. Jasudasen met with Malaysian foreign ministry officials Thursday, and Singapore "will respond through appropriate channels in due course," a Singapore High Commission official said on condition of anonymity.

    Indonesia also summoned Singapore's envoy earlier this week to explain Lee's remarks that Malaysia and Indonesia "want Singapore, to put it simply, to be like their Chinese - compliant."

    Malaysia's prime minister has expressed displeasure at Lee's remark, suggesting that it could stir up Malaysia's Chinese, who make up about a quarter of the country's 26 million population and are its second largest ethnic group after the Malay majority. He has also noted that race relations in ethnic Chinese-majority Singapore are also not perfect.

    Malaysia and Singapore have close cultural and economic ties, but a history of quarrels, bad blood and rivalry. In recent years the neighbors have sparred over the price of water, and took a territorial spat over a tiny islet to the World Court.
