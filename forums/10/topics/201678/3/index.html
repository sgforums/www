    Why is it irrelevant? If you had a background in research, you would know this, but I'll enlighten you.

    If research is done right, it will meet accepted standards of quality. These standards are designed to ensure that the conclusions reached are sound enough to act upon. The methodology must be designed in such a way that no other explanations for the results is more plausible than the one being put forward by the researchers. The analyses must follow a clear, cogent process that pertain directly to the matter being studied. The interpretation of the results must not be extended beyond what the results state.

    The above is a summary of the requirements of proper research, and obviously the devil is in the detail. The research process is examined in detail and critiqued at two key points, namely at the time of application for approval to proceed with the research, and during the editors' review before the research is published. JH has had plenty of experience over the decades of meeting these standards, and indeed their reputation hinges upon this continuing - they have nothing to gain and everything to lose by cutting corners.

    While the process of ensuring that research is a complex one, competent researchers would know the rules and be mindful of them at every step of the study. As long as the rules are adhered to, the research is sound and the results and conclusions are solid enough for applied purposes.

    Bottom line: if you're interested in research, keep your eye on the process and standards, not who's conducting the research. The brand name is therefore irrelevant and A*STAR's insistence on brand names is therefore misguided.

    And your words make me wonder, what are prof and all research students for if research is that easy?
    Well, by the time the students get to the research stage, they would typically have become the top 10% of the top 5% that get past Honours to postgrad.     Why did we need good students for research?

    why did research seems endless??

Getting past the research approval stage, especially the ethics commitee, takes a long time because all the angles crucial to the study's success have to be considered. Depending on the nature of the data to be collected, it may take some time. Typically, getting participants for the studies is the most time-consuming part of the process. Once you have your data, it's a piece of cake.

    Johns Hopkins retracts allegations against A*Star

    By Liaw Wy-Cin

    TOP US medical institution Johns Hopkins University (JHU) has retracted allegations that Singapore's Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*Star) did not keep to its side of the bargain in supporting the university's research arm here.

    The two parties have also ended their public war of words over the break-up of their eight-year, $82-million marriage, opting instead for a joint statement to The Straits Times yesterday.

    In it, JHU said that it had ''no intention to impugn the reputation and standing of A*Star and Singapore, and any such impression is regretted'' - an about-turn from its earlier position.

    When the news broke about two weeks ago that dozens of faculty and staff would lose their jobs and four postgraduate students would have to go without scholarships in the US university, the Baltimore-based institution pointed fingers at A*Star.

    It alleged that the Singapore agency did not fulfil its financial and educational obligations; and called into question Singapore's and A*Star's reputations.

    Yesterday's one-page statement told a different story, saying that ''Johns Hopkins recognises A*Star's reputation for excellence and integrity''.

    It added: ''Johns Hopkins acknowledges and thanks A*Star for the financial and other support provided in keeping with the agreement with DJHS.''

    When contacted yesterday, the university declined to comment further on its new stance.

    DJHS, the Division of Johns Hopkins in Singapore, is JHU's research and education arm here. A*Star decided in May to pull the plug on its funding of the division when it failed to deliver on requirements like bringing in top researchers and training students.

    Yesterday, Johns Hopkins said it respected the termination and was ''working with A*Star to wind down the existing DJHS programmes and develop appropriate transition plans''.

    Both sides said they would like to work together again.

    While this phase of the experiment has failed, there was still scope for future partnerships, said the director of A*Star's Biomedical Research Council, Dr Andre Wan.

    ''Scientists on both sides have already been floating up common areas of interest and discussions on further collaborations are already taking place.''

    But first on the agenda is the 12-month ''winding down'' of operations at the division which was closed in June.

    This includes finding jobs for staff and sourcing funding for the four postgraduate students.

    The Straits Times understands this will cost A*Star substantially less than the $14 million needed to run the division annually.

    Equipment and the space occupied by the facility in a building at the Biopolis in North Buona Vista will go to A*Star's other research institutes.

    News of the A*Star-Johns Hopkins split, reported in top scientific journals like Nature, has resulted in some local scientists questioning the strategy of courting big foreign names.

    Said Dr Ting Choon Meng, 47, chairman and chief executive of medical devices firm HealthStats International: ''We can't expect to just jump-start a medical hub here by luring the big names and giving them what they ask for. We can't just create franchises.''

    But A*Star's collaborations with at least six other foreign institutes, like Sweden's Karolinska Institutet, may prove otherwise.

    Its president, Professor Harriet Wallberg-Henriksson, said defining clearly the expected goals at the outset was critical in such collaborations.
