  • mancha
    14 Jul 07, 17:18
    Lets say you print something offensive, like racist remarks, slander against a person, or vulgarity on paper and distribute it at MRT Stations to passersby.
    That is clear you are asking for trouble with the law.
    You are publishing offensive material and distributing it to the public.

    But if you post in the Internet, in blogs, forums chat rooms, are you not publishing and distributing offensive material to the public sans paper.

    You can turn off the computer if you do not like what you see.
    You can just as well crush the paper and throw it into the trash bin.
    That does not negate the offence.

    BUT

    What if you print the flyers in Sydney and distribute it there, the contents of which are offensive in Singapore.
    Or you post the offensive remarks in overseas webpages.
    Do they constitute an offence in Singapore?

    We very well know what contents could get us into trouble with the law here.
    But how long is the long arm of the law?

    Tap the keyboard in Singapore, post overseas.
    Tap the keyboard overseas, into Singapore webpages, blogs, etc.
    How would such actions affect us?

    Do we have a general law, that says that if a Singaporean commits an offence overseas, he would be dealt with as if the offence is commited in Singapore.
    There is one, for sex offences against a child. But for other offences, I am not sure.

    I say it is safe to speak out in Singapore, provided you do not threaten National Security, ie be careful when speaking about racial and religious matters, and about inciting hatred and violence.
    National political issues that is likely to cause disquite, will be swiftly challenged, we all know that, but there is no need to fear that "knock".
    And the other famous matter, and often misdirected to the government, is slander against the person. This will render you privately liable to the defamed, and is not a government action, but many do not see the distinction.
    And, of course, if you wish to "entertain the public", please be mindful to the laws of the land, and apply for a police permit. This is for speaking physically on land.
    In cyberspace, the same precautions apply, and personal blogs do not offer any measure of immunity.
