    Let's put it this way ,

    Health of the population -> sick individuals -> demand of healthcare delivery -> healthcare costs

    Acute illnesses - don't need to say so - they come and they will go . They won't affect the person for life. Of course lah , there are the big problems like SARS and avian flu everyone is scared about.

    Chronic illnesses - affect the person for life and the largest burden on healthcare costs. Hospitalisation bills are usually the largest on the medical expenditure list not coughs and colds.

    MOH :Better Health Outcomes for the Chronically-Ill Through Structured Disease Management Programmes

    Chronic diseases are a major source of morbidity, suffering and misery to patients and their families in Singapore. They are also the major causes of death. This is a phenomenon common to all developed countries.

    Four common chronic diseases affect about 1 million Singaporeans: diabetes mellitus, hypertension, hyperlipidemia (lipid disorders) and stroke.
    For example :
    You can have a diabetic person who does not control the diet -> unstable diabetes -> increased monitoring which includes blood tests and HBA1c tests -> need to see the doctor more who advises this person-> gets more prescribed medication since blood sugar is not controlled -> heavier burden on healthcare costs -> still does not control diet or do exercise or even check the feet regularly -> complications -> diabetic nephropathy ( kidney disease) , eyes affected , feet has sores -> see the doctor or nurse educator -> gets advised on diet again etc etc -> not listening -> may end up with renal disease , blindness or lower limb amputation -> heavier burden on the healthcare system since this fellow probably needs multiple admissions to the hospital , needs more specialist focus i.e. nephrologist, opthamologist , orthopedic surgeon etc as well as allied health support like dietitions, podiatrists etc -> might need insulin on top of the diabetic medications -> might need amputation -> loses the job probably because the person is too unhealthy to even work -> social costs on top of healthcare costs .

    You have another diabetic person who controls the diet and exercises regularly . Less risk of kidney disease, and just needs to see the diabetic nurse educator regularly . Cost is not too bad - sees the doctor once every 3 month to get medications which might be just one or two types. Cost is kept low even though this person has a chronic illness.

    Preventative health care will cut healthcare costs very efficiently and probably more than you think that the nurse practitioner policy might.

    Specialist nurses are helpful in a way if you use them in a preventative type of setting in a chronic disease setting . Will help the costs to drop down. However to use nurse practitioners in diagnosis and treatment for Singapore might not help costs at all. First of all Singapore is small and the population is also small compared to larger countries . There are enough doctors going around for a community. You just need to refocus on preventative health strategies.
