    BANKRUPTS WILL FIND IT HARDER TO BUY HDB FLATS Those about to go broke, loan defaulters and people laid off are also in the same boat BANKRUPTS, those about to go broke and loan defaulters or those who have been retrenched will find it hard to buy a HDB home - until now considered a basic right of any Singaporean - unless they qualify for a concessionary loan from the Housing and Development Board. A bankrupt hoping to pay off some of his debts by downgrading from a five-room to three-room flat may also be unable to move because banks do not, as a rule, lend to bankrupts. Those who are retrenched and who want to downgrade their homes to reduce their instalment outlays may also be in a similar predicament because banks are unlikely to lend to those who have lost their jobs. Downgraders, under the new HDB rules for market rate loans which start on Jan 1 2003, have to take bank loans. Sum Foong Yee, HDB senior executive public relations officer, said that bankrupts are allowed to buy up to four-room HDB flats. However, if they wish to purchase bigger flats such as a five-room or executive flat, they are required to seek special approval from the Official Assignee's Office prior to the purchase. The Official Assignee's website notes that if a bankrupt wants to buy a larger flat he has to show both the means and the need for such a flat. Others who have to take bank loans include permanent residents, private property owners, those whose monthly household income exceed $8,000, home buyers who have already taken two concessionary loans, and second-time buyers who have enjoyed one concessionary loan but are buying a similar-sized or smaller HDB flat. It is expected that about 20,000 families a year will be borrowing from the banks to buy their HDB homes. Up to now, Singaporeans facing bankruptcy could always take comfort in knowing that there would be a safety net in the form of a HDB flat. It may also be disconcerting for white-collar and middle management employees to know that buying or selling and downgrading a HDB home may no longer be so easy. Tay Chin Seng, an analyst with ING, said: 'It's another whammy for the sandwich class.' It is not just bankrupts who will face a brick wall with bank loan applications; those who have consistently defaulted and discharged bankrupts will find it impossible to pass banks' strict credit checks. Banks generally require credit checks going back three years, at a minimum. Finance companies, traditionally the small man's lender, say they may lend to discharged bankrupts, but only after a period. PropNex chief Mohamed Ismail who handles about 35 per cent of HDB resale transactions said those without a good financial record may now find themselves out in the cold when it comes to bank loans. He worries especially about those who want to downgrade. He said it has always been that one could buy HDB regardless of one's financial commitments. http://www.stproperty.sg/articles-property/financial-guide/bankrupts-will-find-it-harder-to-buy-hdb-flats/a/58763
