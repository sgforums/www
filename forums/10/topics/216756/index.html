    If you can convince Singapore to accept the jurisdiction of the ICJ in this matter, and subsequently secure a standing legal order to halt the execution by the UN Special Rapporteur on Human Rights under the said and accepted argument that the death penalty is barbaric and unconstitutional due to its mandatory nature.

    Do not forget to mention also, you have to make a case that the mandatory death sentence shifts and removes a large element of having to prove actual guilt and to concur that this case was not a fair case since the onus of prosecution was said to be diluted.

    If you can acheive these two aims, Took will have a fair chance to live. But as long as the commutation policy for capital sentences is decided by the Cabinet, I'd place a bigger wager in the state lottery.

    This is one reason why mandatory punishments have to be struck off, we cannot allow Parliament and the Cabinet to pass sentence indirectly by virtue of effect of a Bill tabled into law without the Judiciary's discretionary powers to say yes or no.
