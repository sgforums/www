    The following appeared on Channelnews Asia website on 7 October 2007 - and will be archived within the next 24 hours.


    Transport grievances one hot issue at dialogue session
    By Asha Popatlal, Channel NewsAsia
    Posted: 07 October 2007 2003 hrs

    SINGAPORE: Transport grievances were one of the main issues dominating a dialogue session held after Transport Minister Raymond Lim's community visit to the Thomson Division.

    "We don't mind, as vehicle owners, to pay increased ERP (fees). But we are frustrated when we pay the ERP (fees), we don't see any ease of traffic flow on the CTE," complained a resident.

    The answer? It may be frustrating, however there is no one magic solution but a range of measures adjusted over time as the situation changes.

    Mr Lim explained: "As a first step, we will do what we call traffic engineering solutions. So if you look at the Central Expressway, we have widened it through the years and only recently we did it again.

    "What's the problem here? Let's say we widen the road. When we first widened the road, the traffic might improve. When it improves, what happens? People who previously didn't drive on that road, the Central Expressway, will now drive on it.

    "So, some say, 'build more roads'. Yes, build more roads, that will help. Some say, 'better public transport'. Yes, better public transport will also help. But at the end of the day, you also still need this traffic management, which involves not just the ERP, but as you know in our system, we also have COE."

    But it wasn't just about the ERP. A whole range of transport issues came up during the dialogue session.

    There were suggestions ranging from having premium bus services to 24-hour senior citizen concession passes.

    There was also a suggestion to have the Public Transport Council (PTC) review public transport fares only every four years, just before the elections.

    But, Mr Lim said: "If we freeze the whole thing for four years - no fare increase - the pressure will say, 'why not extend another four years?' The experience of other countries is that the minute you allow the fare increase to be politicised, that over time, the service of your bus standards goes down. If they can't get these returns to renew the bus fleet, the bus fleet will start to shrink and as the bus fleet starts to shrink because they don't replace it, there will be more and more overcrowding. So the situation gets worse and people will complain." - CNA/ir

    Originally posted by the Bear:
    okay.. all that being said, what do you guys suggest then?
    Allow the expressways to function as an expressway.

    The Traffic Police should not be speeding down expressways and randomly nabbing supposedly errant goods vehicles for minor infractions, but should instead ensure the proper lane and speed discipline - {i.e. : ensuring road hoggers move aside}.

    Any device - that unnecessarily strike psychological reaction to affect drivers to suddenly change speeds on the highways - should be removed.

    How often have we seen vehicles slowing down to 70-80kmh before a speed-camera on a 90kmh expressways, only to speed off again at 90+kmh after a safe distance of 300 meters past the camera ?

    Every day and night, most cars are already doing 100kmh along all expressways, with some even going higher at 110kmh on clear roads with minimal traffic at different times of the day or night.

    The speed limits should be variable at different times of the day and night.

    As matters stand, it is ridiculous to post a 90kmh speed limit during peak hours, when traffic load is so dense on the expressways that restrict speed to a snail paced 60-70kmh bumper-to-bumper traffic.

    Expressways should have slip road exits designed in a logical way, to allow vehicles to slow down prior to exit on the LEFT.

    At the PIE exit into Jalan Anak Bukit towards Clementi and BeautyWorld, the exit from PIE is on the RIGHT - which require slow traffic to criss-cross with through traffic moving at a higher speed towards Jurong and Tuas.

    The crown of the roads on most of our Expressways do not help drivers to negotiate the curves and bends along the expressways even at 90kmh.

    When moving towards Jurong-Tuas, there are several sections of the PIE that are dangerous for vehicles taking the long left curving bend - even at 90kmh, as the road surface is ''sloping downwards'' towards the right - which accentuate the center-of-gravity push towards the right as the vehicle negotiate the long left curving bend.

    The first section of the PIE with this poor road design is the long left-curving bend when travelling towards Jurong-Tuas, and this is after the ''PIE exit to BKE'', with this long curve stretching for at least 500 meters.

    The other section of the PIE with this similar poor design feature is at the PIE fly-over that is adjacent to the Bukit Timah Shopping Mall, when the road surface is similarly banked towards the wrong side of the curve.

    Surprisingly, the long curve along the Benjamin Sheares Bridge - in both direction - is superbly designed to allow cars to negotiate the long curving bend at speeds of 110 kmh, as the road surface has been designed to compensate the center-of-gravity push in the opposite direction of the vehicle taking the long curving bend.

    LTA has consistently deny that road bends are poorly designed to handle such center-of-gravity issues, but the fact remains glaringly exposed if one compare the road bends along these two sections of the PIE to that provided along the Benjamin Sheares Bridge - the difference is remarkable.

    The Singapore Traffic Police should not be obssessed with speed restriction, but should instead encourage the use of safe higher speed limits to move traffic off the roads quickly.

    They should adopt the philosophy that the longer vehicles stay on the roads or on the expressways, the chance of accident always exist.

    Vehicles today are designed for higher speed, and it is ridiculous for the Traffic Police to expect cars to travel at a speed of 50kmh that is arbitrarily blanketted over all roads in Singapore unless otherwise stated.

    Moving at 50kmh will require a vehicle to travel on 3rd gear all the way.

    Singapore Drivers - new ones especially - should be taught that driving vehicles with auto-transmission still require them to make decision to change gear manually if certain conditions require them to do so.

    I have witnessed at least one spectacular accident in which a BMW driven by an inpatient young kid taking a short curving bend at a high speed. Did he think that the vehicle will automatically shift down to 3rd or 2nd to handle the curve at a more manageable speed ?

    In a manual transmission car, an experienced driver will manually shift to a lower 3rd gear when taking on a bend - but an auto-transmission car will not do so and will need the manual input of the driver.

    Unfortunately, this fact is not in the minds of new drivers, who should be sent to a higher level of learning that include such defensive driving skills so as to make Singapore roads safer.

    Many accidents that have happened along road bends are a result of misjudgement that is due to simple ignorance of basic car handling.

    Road sense and presence of mind is essential not only with road users, but also with road designers.

    At times, I wonder if the road designers are drivers at all.
