    Iraq used Scuds against Israel and Saudi Arabia in the 1991 Gulf War and some Soviet-era Scuds have ended up in terrorist hands.

    US intelligence officials said there was no link between the North Korean ship and al-Qaeda or other terrorist groups.

    The So San was boarded early on Monday about 960 kilometres south-east of Yemen.

    Finding the missiles, hidden under sacks of cement, can only deepen the strain in the already tense relationship between Washington and Pyongyang.

    It may also chill US relations with Yemen, which backed Iraq in the Gulf War, and was the site of an attack on a US destroyer, the Cole, that killed 17 American sailors in October 2000.

    The US Deputy Secretary of State, Richard Armitage - who arrived in China yesterday on an Asian tour to drum up support for a likely Washington-led war against Iraq - said the interception of the So San was further evidence that North Korea was an important proliferator of weapons.

    But the discovery was unlikely to affect US policy on North Korea.

    "I don't think there's any change. This is not exactly a development that is new," he said.

    Two Spanish warships, the Navarra and the Patino, signalled the freighter to stop around dawn on Monday. The Spanish ships were part of a seaborne operation initially established to halt al-Qaeda supporters who might be fleeing the war in Afghanistan.

    When the So San tried to evade capture, the Spanish ships fired warning shots across its bow. When it continued on, Spanish special forces troops used helicopters to board the boat.

    The crew of about 20 was then put under guard and the ship searched. It quickly became apparent that there were problems with the legal status of the ship, officials said, noting that its original name had been painted over to conceal its North Korean origin, and its registry papers were not in order.

    A search uncovered discrepancies between the ship's manifest, which listed a cargo of cement, and the actual load. The Spanish soldiers opened large containers partially hidden by the cement and found missile parts. They then called in US military experts in handling explosives.

    USS Nassau, which usually carries Marines, helicopters and Harrier jump-jets, was standing by the freighter, which remained off the Yemeni coast, an official said. Additional searches still were under way.
