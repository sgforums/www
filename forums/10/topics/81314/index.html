    just to play devil's advocate...

    while i do feel that more CAN be done, i guess the perception is that for special children, the emphasis is to teach them how to take care of themselves and blend into mainstream society...

    if we go by that mission, then i guess one might argue its never enough since the goal itself is rather impossible... we are judging them by standards that do not apply to them.

    however, it is a more realistic goal than unearthing the idiot-savant talents hidden amongst the few who might be fortunate enough to have them.

    i've read medical articless about certain autistic geniuses who have amazing memories and power of perception, who are able to play musical instruments with uncanny ability WITHOUT formal training and sketch portraits with amazing realism, again, without formal training, but these are exceptions... how many are there? and how are we to unearth these gems?

    and if we did, would we not be applying OUR standards to judge them again? how feasible is that?

    the movie "mercury rising" raises this possibility rather well... an autistic boy who is able to de-code the most sophisticated NSA encryption just by looking at it. but he is mostly unable to take care of himself in any "normal" way.

    so what else can be done for special education?

    This may sound cold and perhaps, a little unfeeling. But I see validity in CX's views.

    The extend of assistances to the special education requiring cluster is unrealistic when the overall objective becomes too microscopic - that is when it is targeting more towards specific 'talents' aside from the majority makeup.

    I have read thru Foolish Girl's post and while I can feel the commitment that she has in her vocation, the most realistic possibility that we, as society in general, would not probably expect the day to come when similar proportions of funds and attention will be channelled towards special education as compared to the regular mainstream.

    However, in my own humble view, the question of assistance to the special education cluster does not have its improvement solutions lying within the spectrum of Governmental grants or assistance alone.

    While the above would certainly help, a bigger decisive and contributory factor is actually the acceptance of society for these lesser previllage group from society.

    Acceptance and the gracing of simple but precious Opportunities.

    These are the 2 factors which can play a truly substantial and greater impacting role into the re-defination of purpose, societal role as well as survivability for the special education cluster.

    While we think of the above 2 factors, we should also bear in mind that another existing barrier to the better resolution of difficulties currently present within special education work is also the willingness and availibilty of special education teachers and supportive manpower.

    Though we have the educational infrastucture to train special education teachers, do we have the means to retain these personnel in Service over the middle or long term perspective?

    Why do such trained personnels leave the Service?

    What difficulties / demotivation did they face to make them leave?

    Are these difficulties or demotivation originating from existing policies? Or is it structure? Or is it the general societal mindset?

    To effectively attempt on a feasible and retifying solution to the issue, perhaps there is only this group of personnel who can accurately feedback to us what is wrong, limiting or lacking with the current system and protocols.

    They are the special teachers who operate on the floor, someone like Foolish Girl herself.
