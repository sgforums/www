    If any of our volunteers test positive, our response will not be to blame and chastise him or her, but to ensure that they receive the best medical and psychological support. If they express a desire to continue offering their help, we will not turn them away.

    If we hear of any volunteer repeatedly engaging in high risk behaviour, we will investigate the matter and ask the person to leave the organization if the allegations are substantiated.

    US poll: Support grows for gay marriage

    The gay marriage movement may be gaining momentum in the United States,
    as new poll results show an increasing number of Americans support
    marriage rights for gay couples - the highest support since July 2003.

    According to the new Pew Research Centre for People & the Press/Pew
    Forum on Religion & Public Life poll, 35 percent of Americans polled
    were in favour of allowing gay couples to get legally married.

    Fifty-three percent still opposed marriage for gay couples, but that
    same number of people polled, 53 percent, said they wanted gay couples
    to have some kind of legal arrangement, such as civil unions.

    "This is exactly what the right wing is afraid of," Evan Wolfson,
    executive director of Freedom to Marry, said. "People have had a year
    of legal marriage in Massachusetts to see how ending marriage
    discrimination helps gay and lesbian families and hurts no one."

    During 2003, Massachusetts' high court ruled that gay couples in that
    state could get married, which they began doing in 2004. Following that
    decision, 11 states passed anti-gay marriage amendments, and poll data
    showed a slip in support for marriage rights.

    The poll also showed a slight increase in religious groups' support for
    marriage rights.

    The poll questions were part of a broader study of national issues that
    may face the incoming US Supreme Court nominee. Marriage rights for gay
    couples did not figure in the top five issues people were concerned
    would come before the Supreme Court. Abortion was the biggest interest,
    followed by the rights of imprisoned terrorists.

    The poll also touched on stem cell research, religious displays in
    public forums, affirmative action and physician-assisted suicide.

    Gay rights advocates such as Wolfson suggest these latest poll results
    on the marriage issue show the effectiveness of gay activism and the
    impact of equal marriage rights in Massachusetts.

    "The long-term trend in America's civil rights discussion, including
    this recent rebound of public support for marriage equality, which
    follows the ferment and barrage of the past several months,
    demonstrates the power of engaging the public and showing them the
    reality of marriage equality, rather than right-wing rhetoric and scare
    tactics."

    Only the Netherlands, Spain and Belgium offer full gay marriage access,
    while Canada is expected to introduce new legislation in the coming
    months.

    40 years of Singapore's nationhood

    Singapore will celebrate its 40th anniversary of nation-building today
    with a great spectacle that perfectly symbolises the story of its
    economic success and the social harmonisation among its ethnically
    diverse population. Forty years ago, Singapore separated from Malaysia
    with an uncertain future. Today, Singapore has emerged as one of the
    most developed economies of Asia an object of envy among its
    neighbours.

    Forty years of independence has witnessed a gradual process of
    Singapore's national identity construction. Reborn in the age of Cold
    War, Singapore imagined its sense of nationhood as a society where
    political and economic centralisation was looked to as a prerequisite
    of national progress. This aspect of Singaporean nationhood responded
    well to the Cold War norms whereby one-man rule was believed to be able
    to achieve rapid economic and social development.

    In the 1990s, the promotion of economic nationalism that became
    foundational to Singapore's national identity in the early years was
    transformed into a more internationally-oriented economy with an
    attempt to enlarge its economic presence on the global stage.

    Singaporean corporations have played a predominant role in
    strengthening the local economy not only in laying strong business
    foundations domestically but being assertive in international trade
    through overseas investments too. Singapore's economic success also has
    very much to do with the government's support for the private sector
    and the maximum use of human resources in ultimately placing Singapore
    in the world-class league.

    Throughout the four decades of nation-building, Singaporeans have
    enjoyed a high living standard and been voted the best city in Asia,
    alongside Tokyo, in terms of quality of life. It is known as one of the
    most globalised cities in the world on the basis of its superb
    infrastructure and the dependence on state-of-the-art technology
    connecting it to the global network.

    Singapore has retained its status of one of the world's largest oil
    processing centres. Singapore ranks among the top three of the world's
    busiest seaports. In the aviation sector, Singapore International
    Airline has been voted the world's best carrier for 10 consecutive
    years by the Travel and Leisure Magazine and offered the fourth
    position by the World Airline Award of 2005. Changi International
    Airport has also been listed as the most efficient airport in the
    world.

    Singapore is eager to declare itself as the global hub of various kinds
    of business. It houses the world's best R&D operation in biomedical and
    pharmaceutical sciences. Despite its lack of water resources, Singapore
    has been chosen to lead the world research on water recycling and
    desalination of water. Singapore has truly become a preferred
    international meeting point and a favorite regional and international
    headquarters for many multinational conglomerates.

    Here, business achievements were made possible due to the successful
    policy of ethnic harmonisation. Singaporeans have been segregated at
    birth into ethnic groups but conditioned to think of ethnic diversity
    as one Singaporean-ness. The government has upheld a strict ethnic
    pluralistic policy with a guaranty of a freedom of expression of ethnic
    identities. Apparently, during the National Day speech, the prime
    minister is expected to give his remarks in English, Chinese and Malay.

    However, what is so extraordinary about the 40th year anniversary of
    Singapore is not about its socio-economic accomplishments.

    This year's National Day slogan, "The Future is Ours to Make" loudly
    conveys a message of a higher level of political openness. Since Prime
    Minister Goh Chok Tong stepped down in August 2004, his successor, Lee
    Hsien Loong who is the son of Singapore's founding father Lee Kuan Yew,
    has adopted a more liberal approach in the handling of both domestic
    and foreign affairs.

    A greater political openness is closely related to improvement in
    certain aspects of social and political life of Singapore, such as
    political participation, national pride, success values and social
    mobility. In the past, Singaporean society was reluctant to talk about
    politics. Nowadays, people more openly discuss government policies.

    A emerging new political model also means that the government has
    loosened grip on some social issues. Though law and order is still in
    place, Singapore is no longer "sanitised and lifeless" society, much
    the butt of jokes in the past. Nightlife is ever more vibrant to serve
    the need of globalised consumers. And although homosexuality remains a taboo subject within Singapore's society, the gay community is
    increasingly visible and seeking to augment its voice in politics.

    Ideologically, the new perspective of Singaporean politics sits
    comfortably with nation-building in a capitalist economy a wonderful
    reason for Singaporeans to rejoice the National Day this year.
