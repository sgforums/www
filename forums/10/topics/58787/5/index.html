    The Present COE have the following features - as was mentioned in my post on this thread - Page 4 - 22 October 2003 - 12:39P.M.
    printed below:

    1. The COE is a quota system by itself, by limiting the MONTHLY Quantity of Vehicles allowed to be sold Per Month. (It is NOT a Tax System - and is unfortunately not a fair way) .

    2. Anyone is allowed to submit a bid for a COE and there is NO AGE LIMIT, EXCEPT that the insurance certificate must cover the successful bidder who is the certified driver and owner of the vehicle.

    3. The COE price is NOT determined by the TOP VALUES of ALL COE received, but taken from the LOWEST VALUE of the last REJECTED BID PRICE - (which LTA termed as "Reserve Price" ) .

    4. COE is awarded by a complex system of accepting the APPLICANTS submitting the TOP VALUES, but they need to PAY ONLY the price of the LAST REJECTED BID PRICE.

    For example, if there are only 5000 COE available for this month:

    First group of 3000 Persons (Buyers or Dealers) submit bids of $10,000
    Second group of 2000 Persons submit bids of $11,000
    Third group of 1000 Persons submit bids of $12,000
    Fourth group of 1000 Persons submit bids of $15,000
    Fifth group of 1000 Persons submit bids of $20,000
    Sixth group of 1000 Persons submit bids of $50,000
    Seventh Group of 1000 Persons submit bids of $60,000

    The 5000 applicants submitting the TOP VALUES will be awarded, and those people from the Third Group to the Seventh Group - each of 1000 persons will be the successful winner.

    However, they DO NOT PAY the values of what they have submitted, as the COE Price is determined at the LAST UNSUCCESSFUL BID PRICE - which is the value submitted by the SECOND Group at $12,000.

    LTA will charge $12,000 PLUS $1.00 - (GST to be separtely paid).

    This is the mechanism in which COE determine who amongst a LARGE group of BUYERS will be awarded with a limited quantity of COE.

    IS THIS A FAIR APPROACH IN DISTRIBUTING THE COE ?

    There is no assurance that the system is TRANSPARENT, as some people do experience a problem of entering a GENUINE LOW PRICE BID, which the system reject at a fixed time within the day.

    When DEALERS are allowed to bid for COE to allow them to sell their stocks of vehicles, and by allowing them to pay only a TOKEN FEE with their submission, they will simply join in the mad rush to be at the top of the heap of desparate COE bidders.

    The nett effect is for the wild speculative swings of COE prices over a period of six months.

    In the SUPPLY SIDE Quota System, it will feature the following:

    (1) The DEALER will only bid for a limited stock of approved vehicles to be sold.

    (2) The limited stock of vehicles that are allowed to be sold can be auctioned off in Quota Lots of fixed quantities of cars, which the DEALERS can bid for the number of Quota Lots that they dare to hold in stock.

    (3) The DEALERS must pay UP-FRONT the FULL VALUE of the bidding price per car quantity, or per Quota Lot of cars.

    This is intended to cut out the speculative element by forcing the DEALERS to behave in a rational and responsible manner when bidding for the right to sell the limited quantities of cars.

    (4) The successful bid price for the Quota Lot, will be determined by the largest MEDIAN of hits at which the prices are received.

    The nett result will be those who are alone bidding at the High End or at the other Low End, will be the ones being unsuccessful.

    The SUPPLY SIDE Quota Control System will serve as a DAMPENER to any market manipulation and provide stability in an otherwise speculative market for COE during the weekend Turf Club Races.

    As was previously mentioned, this proposal may require further development and tweaking, but its application is very wide.

    No-one told me about it

    Mazda Motor: It is not up to us to inform buyer of COE prices
    By Faith Teo
    Picture/ MOHD ISHAK

    REMEMBER that unusual price for a certificate of entitlement (COE) two years ago?

    Car buyers received a windfall when the Category A COE for cars of 1600cc or below dropped to just $101 in July 2001.

    They would have been expecting to pay up to $30,000.

    Depending on what car they bought, they would have got a huge refund based on the COE rebates set by car dealers.

    For instance, if a dealer had set the COE rebate at $25,000 that month, his customers would each have got back $24,899 ($25,000 less $101).

    But there's one person, and likely some others as well, who did not enjoy that windfall.

    Instead, Mrs Mary Ng now stands to lose a big amount - all because of that unusual bid.

    She had paid $27,048 for her COE, which is for 10 years, when she bought her car from Mazda Motor two years ago.

    Today, if she scraps or sells the car, she won't get back 80 per cent of that amount.

    Her COE is worth $76 now. This is because she had made her purchase with an open category COE that month.

    This category is valid for six months and some car dealers offer it if a customer wants a car in a hurry or can't be bothered to bid for a COE.

    But the value of an Open Category COE is based on the COE for a car of that size in the month in which the customer buys the car.

    Usually, the difference is small.

    But for Mrs Ng, 50, the difference turned out to be nearly $27,000.

    She had bought a 1.6-litre Mazda 323 hatchback, and that month's COE for a car of that size was $101.

    And that was all her COE would be worth.

    She said she didn't even know about this till now as she had just returned to Singapore then, after 11 years in Papua New Guinea on business.

    She claimed no-one told her anything about the implications of owning an open or Category E COE.

    It was only when she wanted to buy a new car last month and tried to sell the old one that she found out.

    The dealer she wanted to buy the new car from informed her that her old car's resale value would be affected because its COE was now virtually worthless.

    A furious Mrs Ng went to Mazda Motor to demand an explanation.

    She said she bought the Mazda because the price of $86,000 seemed attractive and she had to wait only two weeks for it.

    She claimed no-one at Mazda had told her the COE would be an open category one. She felt the company was to blame for the situation.

    When asked if she should have taken more responsibility for what happened, she said she had assumed that Mazda would do what was necessary to safeguard her interests.

    She said: 'The lady who sold the Mazda car to me told me I had to bid for a COE, but she never said it was going to be an open category one. I told her I had just returned to Singapore after 11 years, but she still didn't explain the implications to me.'

    But Mazda Motor says it is not responsible for her predicament. (See report below.)

    Mrs Ng claimed the dealer's general manager told her it was not his company's responsibility to educate buyers about COEs.

    'Nobody at Mazda apologised, they pushed all the responsibility to LTA (Land Transport Authority). That's what makes me so angry,' added Mrs Ng.

    She then approached LTA. In a letter sent to her, LTA explained the workings of the vehicle quota system and informed her there was nothing it could do to help her recover the lost sum.

    She claimed she was told that more than 300 car owners were in the same position, but LTA would not confirm this.

    LTA told The New Paper that such an extreme situation is unlikely to occur again, with the current open bidding system.

    With the previous 'closed' system, buyers submitted bids without knowing what others were bidding.

    This led to rare but extreme discrepancies in price such as the July 2001 bid of $101 and a December 1997 bid of $50.

    Now, buyers can see how much others are bidding and adjust their bids accordingly.

    This transparency has helped to stabilise COE prices.

    Mrs Ng's car was bought using an open category COE that Mazda had purchased in June 2001.

    Now, it's worth $76 after two years of depreciation.

    Now, her only alternative is to keep a car she can't wait to sell.

    'Even my car insurance company has told me that if anything major happens to my car now, they will only pay according to the COE value of $76,' she said.

    'It's so stressful to drive this car now.'

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------
    WHAT MAZDA MOTOR SAYS:

    MAZDA Motor's marketing manager told The New Paper that it was the responsibility of the Land Transport Authority to inform the public about the different types of COEs.

    Declining to be named, he said: 'LTA has the social responsibility to do that, and it has done so in many relevant publications.'

    He reiterated the company's stand that there are just too many regulations on the sale of a car to explain everything.

    'We try as much as possible to explain things at the point of sale, but there's no standard list of points that we follow.'

    He claimed that at the time when Mrs Ng bought the car, she was informed her COE would be an open category one. That was the reason why she got her car in about two weeks.

    'It's not like we bought the COE at $101 and charged her $27,048 for it. We bought it at that price too.'

    Out of six car dealers The New Paper approached, only two were willing to comment on whether their sales teams are trained to inform buyers of the different COEs.

    Borneo Motors, which sells Toyota vehicles, said its staff members tell buyers about the different COEs but declined to elaborate.

    Kah Motors, dealer for Honda vehicles, said it had not met an incident like Mrs Ng's so far.

    Its marketing manager, Mr Ricky Teo, said he understood 'a high percentage of people don't know about the open category situation'.

    He added: 'COE information is open, it's just that a lot of people don't pay enough attention to it.

    'LTA should highlight it more clearly. Even on their website, it's not very evident.'

    Mr Teo said that as a standard procedure, his staff members explain the different COEs to buyers, and ask them which category they want to bid for.

    The buyer's choice is then reflected in a written pre-sales contract.

    Mr Andy Lau, sales consultant for used car dealer Car Times Automobile, said the value of Mrs Ng's car would definitely be affected by the COE, although he did not say by how much.

    LTA's spokesman confirmed there wasn't anything they could do for Mrs Ng as COE values are the result of a transparent system determined by market forces.

    She added that out of the 1,340 people holding the July 2001 open category COE, only those owning cars with an engine capacity of 1600cc or below would be severely affected.

    That was the category in which the $101 bid was made.

    Information on COEs can be found on LTA's website at www.onemotoring.com.sg.
