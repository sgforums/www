    Singapore as compared with some countries such as US, UK and
    Australia, etc. But since I am merely proposing a new way to look at
    Singapore's personal income tax rate, I am sure readers will be able
    to judge the correctness and accuracy of the points I am going to
    make, and, with their more informed knowledge, be able to confirm my
    observation.

    First, let me define 'personal income tax' in general terms, that is,
    that Singapore's 'income tax' is the tax monies that we pay to the
    Government based on how much we earn.

    Thus, if you are prepared to agree with me that our 'income tax' is
    all the monies that are taxed from our income and given to the
    Singapore Government, then you are going to be very shocked by my
    observation.

    First, many wage earners in Singapore do not pay income tax. Or so it
    seems. That is, they do not even earn enough to fall under the income
    tax rates. But nevertheless, as I will show, even these low income
    wage earners are paying income tax [in the general sense of the term]
    and at a very high rate, too.

    Furthermore, for those who fall into the income tax brackets, they not
    only pay their usual income tax [up to the highest marginal personal
    tax rate of 26%, I believe] but they, like their poorer Singaporeans,
    pay another income tax on top of the usual one, that is also very
    high.

    What is this 'additional income tax'?

    Well, it is the CPF-HDB Great Ripoff Scheme.

    Let me explain.

    We all know that when we are having a salary, 40% [for most years in
    the past, now 36%, I believe] of every single wage earner goes to his
    CPF. I repeat, every single Singaporean has his/her wages deducted by
    36% every payday, without exception.

    You may say, Oh! That's OK because it goes into OUR CPF and not the
    greedy Singapore Government. So, it is not another income tax, right?

    Wrong. Because the CPF allows you to use your CPF funds to pay for
    your HDB flat.

    So what, you may say?

    Ah, here comes my observation.

    Now, the Government takes 36% of every single wage earner's pay every
    month and puts it into the CPF, under your name and 'ownership' BUT
    TAKES IT OUT TO PAY FOR YOUR HDB FLAT. AND BY INFLATING THE PRICE OF
    YOUR HDB FLAT, THE GOVERNMENT THEREBY TAKES AN EXORBITANT AND ALMOST
    ILLEGAL PORTION OF YOUR CPF, THAT IS, YOUR MONIES, THUS ALMOST
    DIRECTLY FROM YOUR INCOME EVERY MONTH.

    Now, again, you may say that that is OK because the HDB flat will be
    yours after you have finished paying for it.

    True, that is, if your flat can be sold at the same or higher price
    than when you paid for it. Or, if selling were an option -- if you
    sell, where could you live? So selling is not really an option.

    We now come to the point of HDB flat prices.

    It is now irrefutable fact that a 4-room HDB flat costs less than
    $30,000 to build. We have seen direct tender prices by contractors to
    build HDB flats and we know that flats cost less than $30,000 to
    build. BUT THE PRICE OF A 4-ROOM HDB FLAT IS ABOUT $280,000 [my wife's
    colleague paid that]. Thus the HDB makes a massive profit, which goes
    to the Government. Even if we have to pay for the administrative costs
    of the HDB organisation, each 4-room should not cost more than
    $80,000. The extra $200,000 is simply obscene profit.

    Thus, we now have a clearer understanding how this evil scheme works.

    First, the Government, by law, forces every single wage earner to put
    36% of his salary [or income] into his CPF. Then, it 'allows' these
    CPF monies to pay for your HDB flat. And by inflating the price of
    your flat to obscene levels, it takes most of your CPF and puts it
    into the Government kitty.

    Thus, this CPF-HDB combination directly takes your income monies [via
    the CPF Board] and credits it into the Government coffers. Is this not
    a ripoff? Is this not another income tax? That is, a direct tax on
    your income?

    Another point. Precisely because 36% of our wages goes into the CPF,
    which we cannot withdraw, we are 'prepared' to pay higher prices for
    our flats than would be if we had to pay in cash. If everyone had had
    to pay cash for their flats, the HDB could never have raised prices to
    such obscene levels.

    Thus the CPF Home Ownership Scheme is merely a sleight of hand to take
    our income and transfer it to the Government coffers. Through the
    indirect means of our HDB flat.

    We own the flat, no? True. But if there were no such CPF Home
    Ownership Ripoff, we would have rebelled against paying $280,000 for a

    But the Government spends its obscene profits wisely, no? That is

    Ministerial Buildings, the new Durians on the Esplanade, etc, the high
    spending on the military, to the tune of US$4 billion a year, and ask
    yourself if you can trust the Government to spend the money wisely,

    creating jobs and opportunities for foreigners; hardly any is spent
    locally to benefit Singaporeans]. The list goes on...

    And what do we get for paying these highest income taxes in the world?

    No social security. No free health service. No unemployment benefits.
    No everything.

    We pay more than the developed countries in income taxes and get
    nothing like what they enjoy. To hazard a guess, our income taxes [as
    I have discussed above] probably exceeds 40% of our incomes, counting
    both the normal income tax and this abnormal tax on our income.

    On top of that, we also pay GST, etc.

    Any wonder why Singaporeans are poor while the Government is rich?

    The difference between the SUPPLY side quota control and the DEMAND side COE quota control is that a Dealership will have to commit not to the payment of ONE vehicle, but for the entire quota of a fixed quantity of vehicles - either 500, 1000, or any numbers lower or higher.

    The Dealership will have to be careful that he does not end up with a stock of overpriced "Quota Allotment" of vehicles that may end up being non-competitive against another make of vehicle, as well as the possibility of a lower cost for the next Quota bid.

    The Dealership will have to weigh his sales potential, his cash flow position, present stock levels vs additional stocks, financial cost of holding vehicles in stock for too long a time, competitor's pricing, and a whole host of consideration pertaining to their specific industry.

    The DEMAND side will only involve the Car Owner, who is only concerned in getting his single vehicle, and do not care necessarily be overly concerned with the financial aspects as would a Dealer that is involved with purchasing an entire quota approved quantity into his stock.

    The SUPPLY side quota control of the car population can be finely tuned to ensure that Dealerships do not monopolise all the quotas by out bidding the other Dealerships. The system can be fine tuned to the point that any second quota allotment awarded to a previously successful Dealership, will carry a Premium Value added to the bid price.

    Unlike the present COE bidding system - which has now become an additional financial collection system for the Government Treasury - the SUPPLY side quota will see a definitely fixed quanity of vehicles being allowed to be sold on a monthly basis.

    There are more then this few suggested ways of tweaking the SUPPLY side quota allocation to control the size and growth of the Car Population in Singapore, without the need of implementing financially punishing direct and indirect taxes called under different names.

    Let us not forget that the objective is to regulate the growth and size of the vehicle population in Singapore, and this entire exercise is NOT about generating additional Cash for the Government Treasury.
