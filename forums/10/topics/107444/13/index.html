    Fiscal Argument for casino here overplayed?

    A MEGA-RESORT could still hit the jackpot in Singapore even if it does not have casino, according to a report from United States investment bank Morgan Stanley.

    The report also questions some of the assumptions underlying the arguments
    for a gaming palace, including the notion that it would attract large numbers of foreign punters and provide funds to cross-subsidise other attractions.

    It conceded that a resort as a whole would boost the economy with an initial start-up investment of 0.9 to 1.9 per cent of gross domestic product (GDP) and the addition of 6,000 to 9,000 jobs.

    It also estimated that if the mega-resort is built and Singapore meets its tourist-visit targets, the GDP by 2015 will be 15.4 per cent higher than it was last year. Without the resort, the GDP would grow by just 6.1 per cent.

    But whether a casino is crucial to these gains remains questionable.

    "The key question is whether the resort and the economy could do just as well without the casino. We think the answer is probably yes." said Morgan Stanley economists Deyi Tan and Daniel Lian in the report.

    They also examined the Government's view that revenue from a casino could be used to "cross-subsidise the other non-gaming attractions, which in turn attract the large number of visitors."

    Calling this argument "somewhat shaky", the report argued that if the resort's non-gaming components - hotels, convention centres and luxury retail outlets - command a price premium and generate profits, "it detracts from the need for cross-subsidisation to attract tourists".

    The Atlantis in Bahamas - a model the Government has cited - is a case in point, the paper said.

    Non-gaming revenue accounts for over 70 per cent of total revenue and there does not appear to be any cross-subsidisation for accommodation and food, both of which had sizeable profit margins of 84.2 per cent and 33 per cent respectively in 2003.

    As well, the report also questioned whether a Singapore casino would be able to attract the base of foreign tourists it is targeting.

    It cited the fragmented Asian market, wth casinos in Genting and Australia relying largely on locals. Even in Las Vegas, in the year ended June 2003, only 9 per cent of casino visitors were foreigners, the paper said.

    A casino in Singapore would also face competition from Macau, which opened up its gaming industry to foreign operators in 2002 and enjoys first-mover advantage.

    "This suggests to us that Singapore's proposed casino would by itself struggle to attract a critical mass of foreign clientele, thus undermining the economic logic of this project," the paper said.
