    Gambling addicts unsure about casino ban

    Many at 'gamblers anonymous' meeting accept ban but say it won't stop them

    By Krist Boo
    Feb 21, 2006
    The Straits Times

    GAMBLING addict John (not his real name) will be thankful if he is barred from the casinos, even if the ban is initiated by a family member.

    It will be his wake-up call to do more to regain the trust of his loved ones, said the sub-contractor in his late 30s. He is now hounded by loan sharks for the mountain of debts he racked up to finance his soccer betting.

    'If my family does that to me, it means they still can't trust me. I would have to commit myself more to these sessions,' he said.

    John was among 13 recovering gambling addicts who spoke about the exclusion order at a recent 'gamblers anonymous' session organised by One Hope Centre, a non-profit organisation.

    The Casino Control Act passed last week allows the National Council on Problem Gambling and casinos to issue orders barring addicts from entering the gambling halls.

    Addicts can apply for exclusion orders from the council or the casinos, while their relatives can send their request to the council. The council is also empowered to bar social assistance recipients, bankrupts and those with poor credit records.

    It was the first time gamblers have shared their views on the ban. Most of the debate so far has been dominated by politicians and community workers.

    There have been concerns that gamblers will turn on family members if they feel they are 'squealing' on them.

    One gambler at the session said he would squabble with his wife if she had him banned.

    A former sales executive admitted he would be annoyed at relatives who sought an order against him. The unemployed 27-year-old is struggling under a mountain of debt totalling more than $100,000. However, he said: 'I'll be frustrated. Why do the authorities treat me differently from others? We are already very miserable. Why impose more misery on us?'

    However, many of those at the meeting last Thursday backed government plans to keep the casinos out of their reach, though they said it will not stop them from gambling.

    Said one: 'It is in the right direction. I will take it as an extra safeguard.'

    President of One Hope, Reverend Edward Job, said his counsellors will explain to gamblers that their loved ones had them barred out of love.

    The ban keeps compulsive gamblers from 'spur-of-the- moment' gambling and enhances their recovery, he said.

    But not every gambling addict may benefit.

    One hardcore punter, who has wagered at spots ranging from illegal gambling dens to offshore casinos, laughed when asked how he would feel if his family had him barred.

    Speaking in Mandarin, he said: 'There's no one to do that. My older and younger sisters are themselves gambling like crazy.'
