    Heeren mother
    If I don't work, WHO'LL FEED MY SICK CHILD?
    February 05, 2006

    YOU would have seen them near The Heeren on Orchard Road.

    Madam Rani has been there for about five years. Her routine: She'd be there with her daughter to sell packets of tissue from a big, red plastic bag.

    The 50-year-old woman would hold three or four packets in one hand, raising her other hand to her mouth to show she needed money to eat.

    Some people would shake their heads and walk away. Others would simply ignore her.

    She would then go to her daughter, who would be standing in a telephone booth. 'Today no luck,' Madam Rani would say to her daughter, Miss Bawani, and return to her work.

    Slipping her hand under her T-shirt and rubbing her stomach, Miss Bawani, 26, said to this reporter: 'No luck, we drink plain water. Got luck, we eat chicken rice.'

    Miss Bawani looks healthy, but Madam Rani said she does not allow her daughter to work.

    'My daughter is sick. She had a brain operation and she can't work. Don't talk to her too much or else she'll get a headache.'

    Her sickness could not be independantly verified.

    Raising her voice, Madam Rani added: 'Don't talk to us. So many people came to talk to us and said they wanted to help, but also never help. Some called the police to chase us away.

    'We did not cause any inconvenience. I'm only selling tissue paper. When people tell me they don't want to buy, I never force them. I walk away.'

    She recalled how a woman had once approached her.

    'She talked to me for one hour. She asked me so many questions and said she wanted to help me. I waited and waited, there was no help.

    'A few months later, I saw her again at Orchard Road so I stopped her and asked her 'How?' She told me, 'Sorry lah auntie, I can't help you. You go look for MP.'

    'Never mind, I depend on myself. I help myself.'

    So there they are every day, taking the bus or MRT from Yishun where Madam Rani claimed they live in a friend's rented one-room flat.

    'We come in the morning and leave around 3pm to 4pm,' said Miss Bawani.

    'Here is good. Nobody comes to disturb us although some people gave me nasty looks and showed me their middle fingers. But I ignored them because I don't want to offend anyone or else they'll call the police.'

    A teenager distributing flyers at the junction claimed she had seen the two women leaving in a taxi.

    On good days, Madam Rani said she can make up to $14 selling packets of tissue. Sometimes it's only $5.

    'People are always rushing. Some people give me $1 and ask for 12 packets of tissue,' said Madam Rani.

    Before her operation, Miss Bawani said, her mother used to work in the service industry and she worked at a fast-food restaurant.

    She could not say when she had the operation, but said her father left the family about nine years ago.

    'We lead a simple life and have enough to get by,' said Miss Bawani.
    - Joyce Lim

    Orchard Road confidant
    Tissues for tears & ears for their fears
    By Zubaidah Nazeer

    February 05, 2006

    YOU would have seen him in his dark glasses and baseball cap, which reads: Jesus Blesses You and Your Kindness.

    You may have heard his voice singing hymns and serenading passers-by.

    Mr Goh Siang Leng, or Coco as he is known to friends, is almost blind, and a jack-of-all-trades.

    He's a gospel-singing evangelist, a kerbside tissue-seller, an entertainer to passers-by, a confidant of sorts - someone who is willing to provide a listening ear to anyone who needs one.

    'People tell me all sorts of things, from marital problems to work woes,' the 65-year-old told us outside Meritus Mandarin hotel, where he usually sells tissues.

    For example, a 40-something woman once approached him for advice because she suspected her husband was having an affair, he claimed.

    'I could tell she was nearly in tears and didn't know what to do.

    'I told her not to idolise one man, to be patient, to pray to the Lord. I also told her to communicate with her husband because men do not always know how to tell you that they want your love.

    'Two months later, she looked for me near Novena Square and told me that she and her husband are doing all right. She sounded radiant.'

    Another time, a young engineer was so stressed with life he wondered to Mr Goh whether he should quit to devote himself to church-work.

    He told him he should continue to work and save money so that he can be stable and devote himself to church-work later.

    Mr Goh said he left school at Secondary 4, was a clinical assistant in a hospital for four years and then worked as a clerk in various ministries for the next 14 years.

    This was followed by a stint selling stationery for a printing company. He was also a passenger services officer at a cable-car station.

    Then he became a tour guide and park assistant at the Jurong Crocodile Park.

    He appeared in shows and handled the reptiles when visitors wanted snapshots.

    But he hasn't had a regular job since 2000 when diabetes-induced glaucoma affected his eyes.

    Now, he is about 95 per cent blind. He recently also had prostate surgery, but said his diabetes is under control.

    The eldest of six siblings, Mr Goh separated from his wife 26 years ago and does not have children.

    He lives on his own, renting a room for $120 in a friends's three-room HDB flat in Eunos. Can't he get a job?

    His tired reply is that he has tried.

    'I applied to work as an ice-cream seller, but my application was rejected because of poor eyesight,' said Mr Goh.

    'I have also applied to do simple clerical or reception work, but the employers told me they don't employ someone older than 35 years old.'

    Despite his old age and failing eyesight, Mr Goh is fiercely independent, travelling everywhere by MRT and bus, tapping his cane.

    He is on his feet for four to five hours a day, five times a week.

    He gets up to $70 on good days, selling tissues at $1 for four packets.

    Said Mr Goh: 'I can't stay at home. I find such joy meeting and interacting with people.'

    Singaporeans, he said, are a kind-hearted lot. 'A few times, a doctor and a company manager have driven me home.'

    He even squirrels away some money to travel to Batam, Penang and parts of Thailand to visit churches and do missionary work.

    'Selling tissues is not just about making a living for me. I see it as one way for me to pass my time and to reach out to people, and hopefully, to touch them in some way.'

    Are they begging & breaking the law?

    PEOPLE who solicit for money in return for packets of tissue paper can be fined for illegal hawking, said the National Environment Agency (NEA).

    It can also be considered disguised begging, which is illegal.

    Under the Environmental Public Health Act, those found peddling tissue paper can be fined up to $5,000 for illegal hawking.

    Repeat offenders can be fined up to $10,000, jailed for up to three months, or both.

    They can also be charged with begging under the Destitute Persons Act. Those convicted face a fine of up to $3,000 or up to two years in jail.

    In recent times, there has been a call for the authorities to be more lenient with the elderly selling tissues.

    The NEA declined to comment on whether laws will be relaxed, but said they are working with the relevant authorities to tackle the issue.

    An NEA spokesman told The New Paper that they understand some people in financial difficulties want to hawk for a living.

    But they advise people to apply for street hawking licences. That would allow them to sell ice cream, canned drinks and tidbits within their town council estates or parks.
    - Maureen Koh
