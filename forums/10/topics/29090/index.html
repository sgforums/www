  • YourFather

    "The Republic of Singapore Navy (RSN) laid the keel for the first in a series of six frigates on 14 November 2002 in Lorient, France. Permanent Secretary (Defence) Mr Peter Ho officiated at the ceremony.

    Under a contract signed with Direction des Constructions Navales (DCN) in March 2000, the shipyard will be delivering six modern frigates to the RSN. These ships are intended to replace the six ageing Missile Gunboats (MGB) that have been in service for more than 25 years.

    The first of the class is to be built in France while the remaining five ships will be constructed locally by Singapore Technologies Marine.

    The Defence Science & Technology Agency (DSTA) is the overall programme manager and is also undertaking to integrate the combat systems and platform in this challenging programme. The first ship is expected to sail back to Singapore in 2005.

    The frigates are built to be stealthy, using the latest ship design and building technology. The addition of the frigates to the RSN Fleet will expand the capabilities that the navy can employ in the defence of Singapore.

    The keel laying ceremony marks another milestone in the strong bilateral defence ties between France and Singapore. The defence relationship between Singapore and France is an extensive one which includes regular dialogue at the policy level, professional interactions in bilateral and multilateral exercises, and defence technology collaboration. "

    Some personal observation:

    1. The design is quite different from La Fayatte, but the principle of stealth design (ie. the superstructure is sloped at about 10 degree) has been applied. However, the design of this Delta frigate is not as "clean" as the original La Fayette and it will definitely produce higher RCS than the La Fayatte.

    2. The frigate is armed with a Oto breda 76mm gun with a new stealth mounting.

    3. The two triple torpedo tubes are fitted in the front just behind the VLS system.

    4. Sixteen tube VLS (Aster 15 missiles) are fitted.

    5. Two masts are present, the first mast on top of the main superstructure will carry some sort of advanced Air/Surface search radar - possible even some 3D phased array given the "flat" surfaces. Second mast most like will carry the navigation radar, radio antennas, and possible some ESM/ECM anntena. The SATCOMM white radar dome is mounted just behind the second mast.

    6. The top of the helicophter hanger has some sort of mounting, possibly for CIWS type weapon.

    7. Helicopter depicted in the computer generated graphics is either the NH-90 or EH-101 (more like the former given its size).

    8. The main superstructure that contains the wheelhouse and the CIC are much lower than that of the orginal La Fayette design.

    10. The weight quoted is just 300 tons lighter than as the original La Fayatte. I don't see how this Delta frigate will steam much faster than the La Fayattes if the same CODAD engines are used. RSN will have to really look for better and more powerful engines.

    11. The frigates have stabliser fins fitted, as well as the sonar mounted on the middile rather than at the bow.

    Given the quoted 3200 tons weight, and vast larger size and capabilities, these delta frigates are not going to "simply replace the MGBs". In fact, they are going to be introducing a totally new capability to the RSN, namely being able to, for the first time, engage multiple targets in multiple dimension, eg. engaging SSMs, enemy aircrafts while hunting for an enemy sub. In fact, a whole new doctrine will have to be written to integrate these frigates into the current RSN ORBAT.

    It is more likely that RSN will use the MCVs to replace the role played by the MGBs while La Fayette will replace the MCVs role of being the main fighting ships. In all, RSN will be more all rounded to deal with multi-dimensional navy threats.

    I also reckon that RSN should continue pursing the stealth OPV project that was put aside when they decided on the Delta frigates instead. A modified ST Marine 81m OPV with a combined La Fayette technology and possibly Swedish Visby technology will see the stealth OPV a real potent ships able to deal with any rouge MEKOs.

    The prototype of Otobreda's new 127mm/54-cal lightweight (127/54 LW) naval gun with a 'stealth'-optimised turret, mounted on the Italian Navy's Artigliere- (Lupo-) class frigate, Bersagliere, was on display in the Port Zayed element of IDEX.

    Designed for surface warfare and naval gunfire support, with a secondary air-defence role, the new gun weighs 22 tons. This is about 15 tons lighter than the current Compatto design. As a result, it can be mounted on ships from corvette-size upwards.

    According to Massimo Gualco of Otobreda's naval systems division, the weapon has "the highest rate-of-fire of any 5in [127mm] gun" on the market. Although company documentation quotes a maximum rate-of-fire of 40rds/min, 35 rds/min is the accepted normal rate.

    The mount features a revolutionary new loading system with up to four 20-round ready-use feeding magazines, although the prototype has only two at present. These can be reloaded while the mount is in operation - considered an advantage during fire-support missions.

    The whole ammunition feed has been reduced in volume and will fit on board narrow-beam ships. Gualco told JDU that with its compact lay-out, the whole gun system could be 'modularised' for mounting in multirole vessels, such as the Danish STANFLEX concept.

    The gun is able to fire all available standard 127mm (5in) ammunition and would be compatible with the US Extended-Range Guided Munition (ERGM) round. Gualco also noted that a 62-calibre barrel was a future possibility, for use with extended-range ammunition natures.

    This prototype weapon completed its land-based firing trials early last year and was installed and integrated on board the Bersagliere by November 2000. It is now undergoing a one-year operational evaluation with a view to replacing the existing Otobreda 127mm/54 Compatto on certain ships of the Italian Navy.
