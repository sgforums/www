    parkour, I really think you seriously misunderstand the issues at stake here. Most rational responses I've seen with regards to the role of GLCs in our economy are not calling for a total liberalisation of various sectors. I for one agree with the opinion that in a economy like ours, GLCs play an important part in maintaining standards and setting benchmarks for competitors to follow. What I disagree with is the penchant for flooding various sectors with nothing but GLCs and calling it "liberalised" and "competitive". Then after the market is saturated, allow a real private company to enter a market and fight the big boys. Is that a fair fight? Have a GLC in each sector, knock yourselves out. But leave the playing field open to private competitors, sure, they might not be as large or well-equiped as GLCs initially, but if well-run, they will grow and the market will flourish.

    Politically, no one is calling for a coalition government nor regime change for the sake of it. What is being asked here is political liberalization of having ombudsmen and watchdogs that are truly independent and have no affiliation at all to political parties. And elections that involve ALL eligible Singaporean voters and not just in selected areas. Another area to be improved is the fine-tuning of election laws, which are currently so skewed towards the Ruling Party that it beggars belief (to me anyway). Finally, a greater oppostion voice to represent alternaitive views. Believe me, constructive alternative opinions help in developing a country, not set it back.

    BTW scare stories and worst-case scenarios do not wash with me. Even if Singapore adopted a proportional representation system of elections (which I think it should), election results since independence will not have any change in government or even coalition governments.

    You say that these are your own opinions, but the way those opinions parrot the party line being offered to us ad nauseum day in day out, as well as your misconceptions on the idea, and motives about what those Singaporeans who do not agree with government policy seem to indicate that those opinions are shaped to a great extent by the message being offered by the party mouthpieces.

    To summarize, we ask for real fine-tuning and loosening up of politics and the economy, not lip service and half-hearted measures towards that end.
