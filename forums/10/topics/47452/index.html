    'There should be no great dilemma between pursuing our passion, and doing something lasting and worthwhile,' Mr Lee told about 510 students at the annual Pre-University Seminar.

    In a keynote address that held the 16- and 17-year-olds in rapt attention, he urged them to seek out and realise their own potential.

    They were mistaken if they thought they were born too late and had missed out on all the good things in life and were now saddled only with bleak prospects.

    'You have choices which your parents never had. Our economy now offers a wide range of jobs. Our society invites and accepts a broad spectrum of ideas,' he said.

    'Remember that nobody else can validate your dreams. Ultimately, it is your life and your choices,' he added.

    Mr Lin, who is now in national service and did not attend the seminar, needs no such urging.

    Each evening, after he gets out of camp in Ang Mo Kio, he heads to his company's office in Changi South Street.

    As a vice-president of Esco Micro, a clean-room equipment company run by his parents, he leads a division of 20 engineers who make biological safety cabinets. He said: 'I work until 10.30pm every night... But it's not enough, I have to continue at home.'

    It all started while he was a Secondary 1 student in the Gifted Education Programme at Raffles Institution.

    Intrigued by glitches plaguing the computer network of Esco Micro, he did some research and stumbled upon the solution. His parents ended the network contract with a sub-contractor and let their son take over.

    Then while he was maintaining the firm's website, international inquiries started trickling in, and that's when he went into export sales. He was 15 then.

    In junior college, he was skipping classes to do business in Europe and the US, and he credits his former JC principal for being supportive.

    'There are so many facets to life, and your studies are just one very, very minute aspect. If you're on your dying bed, I don't think you will look back on your A-level grades.'

    Although most of his friends are now trying for a scholarship, Mr Lin has his future all mapped out. He intends to get into a top US university such as Stanford, MIT or Harvard and set up shop there while he's studying.

    Some of the engineers in his department will go over to the new office, as the US is his largest market, he said.

    His father, Mr Lim Lay Yew, 52, says his son deserves full credit for turning the company into a 'very export-oriented' firm.
