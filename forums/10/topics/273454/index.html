    Shocking! Taken from Melvin Tan's blog http://melvintansg.blogspot.com/:

    09 August 2007
    Care versus cost - lopsided?
    Yesterday, a day before the National Day holiday, I had some drinks with a couple of friends.

    One of them, presently sourcing for a nursing home to place his semi-mobile father in, learnt that the costs of doing so could run up to more than $2,000 per month and lamented the high fees charged by such homes.

    Only in the event where a family has an average income of $1,000 and below per member, the government will subsidise 80% of the costs.

    To me, it odd to expect a family with an average income of, say, $1,100 per member, to fork out expenses of about $2,000 per month if they had to opt for the assistance of a nursing home.

    Having to deal with expenses that outstrip their earnings is an impossible feat for any family even if they could do the impossible of not spending a single cent of their income.

    In my view, the income ceiling to qualify for this subsidy should be at least $3,000 or more instead of $1,000.

    My friend and his other siblings had no problem with the costs but he wondered about the plight of less well-off families who, if under inevitable circumstances like his own, have to put a family member in a nursing home as last resort.

    For their case, it is not their desire to leave their father in a home but they do not have much of a choice as their mother is also semi-mobile and they were worried their maid will be unable to handle both.

    How will poorer families under the same situation, except being unable to afford a maid or hospice costs, cope?

    Another topic discussed was means testing, a policy idea introduced by the PAP (with several u-turns on the implementation deadline) that the Workers' Party was concerned with and became one of the mentioned topics during the General Election in 2006.

    We wondered why government hospitals had to institute means testing when they can maintain C Class wards while reducing or abolishing A and B Class wards, leaving it to the private hospitals to run the latter classes of wards?

    The two reasons we could think of is, firstly, to give the world the perception that Singaporeans are generally well-off for the government to have high-class hospitals and secondly, the usual engrossment with surplus at the people's expense.

    Today, on Singapore's 42nd birthday, let us reflect on the society the country will have in the near and distant future.

    A new video by activist-filmmaker Martyn See lends a lesser-seen insight.
