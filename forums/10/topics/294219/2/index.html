    To be fair, the irony of this whole notion is twofold and the two groups must be considered separately to have any sensible discussion (regardless of your stance on this issue). It surely is no rocket science that one realizes skilled labour and unskilled labour are two completely separate entities belonging to two different worlds.

    Skilled labour that is widely coveted (ie. athletes, entertainers, senior management, professionals, executives) very often possess certain expertise and skills which you can't find in an average Joe. Fair enough, these people - who are incidentally also the greatest beneficiaries of globalization - should be commensurated competitively for the expertise they offer which is in short supply in a labour pool. In addition, it certainly makes sense to pay them wages befitting their skill sets. More importantly, it's certainly not this group of foreigners whom the worldwide hordes of anti-globalization protestors are aggrieved with.

    Unskilled labour, on the other hand, very often provides a source of labour that is widely available that the plane Janes are well capable of undertaking. While even the most developed countries implement legislations and policies to facilitate the hiring of such workers, they are often limited to doing so after exhausting its local supply of unskilled labour (as a matter of fact, most of those from the former Eastern bloc working in the developed EU countries are hired on short-term basis). In addition, by enacting minimum wage legislations, such countries often seek to deter the extensive hiring of foreign unskilled labour to ensure that employers, even if given a choice, see no economic sense to do so at the expense of its local labourers. That, in essence, is the crux of the issue surrounding the whole notion of hiring foreign unskilled labour (it is worth noting unskilled here does not necessarily refer to illiterates; rather, it's more a matter of whether the local labour pool available is capable of performing the same task). Without sensible policies in place to protect the interests of the locals, why should any commercial entity - whose sole existence conforms with and adheres to economic principles - increase its labour expenses by hiring locals over their cheaper foreign counterparts? This issue is grossly compounded by this regime through its extensively generous legislations and policies that actually support (instead of limit) the hiring of such cheap unskilled labour.

    There are actually some economic dissertations on such issues suggesting models like those adopted by this regime, while beneficial in the short term, is unsustainable in the long run. Obviously, the fact most residents and citizens of this regime (especially the unskilled labour force) are unaware and completely nonchalant about issues like minimum wage or the role of active trade unions with regard to safeguarding their interests would only serve to further advance the interests of this fascist regime.
