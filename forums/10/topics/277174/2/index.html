    1 Sep 07, 19:03
    Damn. Where was I when this thread started.

    ERP certainly by no means reduces the traffic problem as the total number of cars on the road remain at least the same. Truth be told, the total number of cars on the road are actually increasing every year.

    The only thing the ERP really achieves, apart from the obvious filling of government coffers, is that it eases a little traffic from implemented zones. This, at the expense of the smaller roads that, under current ERP implementation, are deemed not obstructive to the business district.

    Recently, they wisened to the fact that as a result of the ERP, traffic congestions were occuring in the areas immediately sorrounding "town", like redhill and kallang. Thus ERP is being implemented on those roads too.

    What would be the eventual end of this as the number of cars keep increasing? We find that ERP will have to be implemented on several other roads as soon as people find another way to bypass the ERP.

    While I won't be quick to say only the rich benefit from this arrangement, the folk who are headed to town for work usually are able to claim the ERP back from their bosses, the big multinational companies. Which DOES leave the poorer non-town folk with lesser choices for roads.

    My conclusion is that ERP is a temporary road-diversion solution which in the longer run has to be implemented on all the roads, because of the "pushover" effect. What we are left with effectively then would be "taxes" of sorts but no solution.

    What would genuinely reduce the traffic problem? And what are the side effects?

    1. People take public transport.

    This certainly is the ideal. The slight side-effect would definitely be the increase in costs for public transport as the public transport companies scramble to increase their fleet.

    Greater business costs and sharp demand for public transport would cause prices to increase at least twofold, which translates to some $1-2 extra per trip. Fuel prices can also be expected to increase due to the drop in consumer demand for petrol as there are less and less car owners. The amount is a small price to pay. Estimating a daily return trip at $6, this would translate to $180 monthly per commuter. Still better than paying $700-$900+ a month for a car.

    There is a slight problem with this arrangement. Transport past midnight will be completely dependable on taxi services as no one wants to pay $700 a month for a car they can only use at midnight.

    I hope there wont be additional costs as a result of 24 hour operations or increase in demand for taxis past midnight.

    SBS, SMRT, Comfort DelGro, and their majority stakeholder Temasek Holdings will have some planning to keep costs minimal.

    2. COEs are not given out as readily as they are now.

    The government intervenes by not allowing as many cars to be sold. The great demand for COE, would most certainly push up COE costs to the ceiling. The drop in the number of applications for COE will certainly pose a recorded drop in the government coffers. Our economy will also lose out on the revenue that these car companies generate. Cars become a luxury item and everyone else will have to use the public transportation system which will have to adapt itself to cater to the greater demand. Revenue generated through Road Taxes will also suffer. This scenario seems very uneconomic, the only respite is the public transportation scene.

    Considering the choice of ERP over the alternative methods presented here.

    ERP is easy to implement and is a source of revenue. Singaporeans, being who they are will continue buying cars as cars have become a status symbol of sorts and also a convenience. COE and Road Tax revenues don't decrease.

    People will also choose public transport over driving their car to work, never mind they having paid for it. They'd drive the car after work hours. After all $180 a month for transport still beats the $200-$300 for petrol costs. And less headache. The only minute difference is that the money goes to Temasek Holdings, rather than the MNC petroleum companies.

    As a result, every economic sector makes money. The public transportation sector sees an increase in demand. Temasek Holdings shares will do well. The COE and Road Taxes keep coming in.

    It makes economic sense. Us? We can work till 65 to pay every loan we've taken. While the government helps invest our money in CPF for us, returning us part of the investment profits when we die.

    Frankly why do we even bother thinking about it all. They've got it all planned out for us.
