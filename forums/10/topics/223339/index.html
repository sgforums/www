Controlling the Gini

  • oxford mushroom
    3 Dec 06, 05:10
    Janadas Devan in his ST column on 26 Nov wrote an insightful article about the wage gap challenge facing Singapore; although Hong Kong and China also face the same problem. All are vibrant economies that have seen rising prosperity in a very short space of time. As he pointed out, the market is great at generating wealth but poor at distributing it.

    Communist China used to be among the most egalitarian of all economies. Everyone was equally poor and everyone had access to the same low quality of healthcare. Now it is the world's fourth largest economy and has more millionnaires than Singapore. However, the price is the marked wealth inequality that pervades society. There are farmers who have to sell their children and they have lost even the low quality healthcare provided by barefoot doctors they used to have. The Gini coefficient ( a marker of wealth equality, 0=completely equal, 1= completely unequal) for China is around 0.4 and unofficial estimates suggest it is closer to 0.6 now. The same index for Hong Kong was 0.52 and 0.46 for Singapore in 2001. The latest figure for Singapore has risen to 0.517 (http://www.singstat.gov.sg/keystats/annual/ghs/r2/chap3.pdf) and probably higher for Hong Kong (http://www.hkcss.org.hk/cb4/ecp/pov_rate_91-05.pdf) .

    Yes, there is rising wealth inequality in all the Asian economic powerhouses, from China to Singapore. The ability of national governments to control social unrest and maintain cohesion will determine future success of these nations. Communist China is probably able to suppress the rising rural unrest for some time, but not forever. The Chinese government is only too aware that it was hungry farmers who supported them to power against the KMT.

    Singapore has to suppress the rising Gini index and keep it around 0.4. Studies have suggested that a figure between 0.3 and 0.4 is probably about right. A low Gini index in the 0.2s as in Scandinavian countries promote laziness, destroys the work ethic and erodes competitiveness. On the other hand, a high coefficient may lead to unrest and degrades social cohesion.

    At risk of simplifying matters, you can lower the Gini coefficient by reducing the income of the rich or raising the income of the poor. The former will not work in a globalised world where funds and talent are highly mobile. Taxing the rich to create a more equitable society will only drive away the ones who pay the most taxes and provide the most jobs. As the PM said, Hong Kong has lower income tax than Singapore. Any move to raise taxes will make Hong Kong even more attractive.

    The only way is to raise the income of our poor by increasing their skill sets and re-engineering low-skilled work to increase productivity. But low-income Singaporeans must get off their butts and accept training, work unsociable hours and in uncomfortable environments such as clean rooms. They cannot have it both ways.
