    Multiple accidents ignite glass panel debate
    March 06, 2007

    YESTERDAY's incident was the second close-shave at an MRT platform in just three days.

    On Friday, a woman in her 50s broke her right leg after she was hit by a train at Woodlands MRT station.

    She was pinned under the train but was freed by an SCDF rescue team.

    Northbound train services were disrupted for about an hour on Friday afternoon and close to 3,000 passengers were affected.

    Also in the news recently were two videos containing footage of MRT track incidents - one at Yishun and one at Admiralty - which spread on the Internet.

    This sparked a discussion on the value of protective glass panels on MRT platforms.

    In November, Madam Cynthia Phua, MP for Aljunied GRC, raised the issue in Parliament.

    Minister for Transport Raymond Lim replied that the Land Transport Authority would assess the effectiveness of the panels before making a decision.

    Madam Phua recently told The New Paper that after a trip to Hong Kong, she was impressed with the shoulder-height glass panels she saw at train stations there.

    The SMRT had considered installing similar panels but eventually decided against it. An SMRT spokesman said this was because the panels would not effectively eliminate suicide attempts as people could still climb over them.

    However, the LTA's main concern was that the costs associated with installing the panels might have to be borne by commuters in the form of higher fares.

    I am one of those who do not agree with implementing barriers for SMRT train stations.

    This concept of barrier-less overhead stations is derived from european and western societies, where most have a substantially large network of metro stations above ground. In such a case, introducing a protective barrier to prevent people from 'accidentally' falling onto the tracks will not be cost effective.

    People die everyday, and throwing themselves in front of a train is probably not new in countries like UK and USA. Yet their societies have not seen fit to erect protective barriers to deter would-be suicides.

    In retrospect, placing a barrier may limit the number of people who conveniently use SMRT as a means of committing suicide, but it doesn't take away the intention.

    It was miraculous that in the initial years of operation, SMRT did not already suffer from such incidents. The recent spat of copycat suicides is just something unavoidable.

    If we go back several years, i recall a Mercedes Benz that somehow jumped off the road, plowed through the fencing and ended up on the SMRT train tracks at Lentor, right in the path of an oncoming train. On that occasion, civic- mindedness saved the day when fellow road-users 'rescued' the driver from the car while others ran ahead of the advancing train to warn the pilot.

    Ironically, the driver was later arrested on suspicion of drink-driving (or was it drugs-related?).

    In that incident, sufficient barriers were erected, yet such a freak catastrophe occured.

    Can we safely say barriers will solve all our problems?
