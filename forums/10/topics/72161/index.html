    SIA-PILOTS' DISPUTE: WHY DID SM HAVE TO STEP IN?
    Veteran MP voices disquiet felt by some
    But Tan Cheng Bock is confident that it is no reflection on the ability of younger ministers to manage difficult issues
    By Lydia Lim

    IT TOOK veteran MP Tan Cheng Bock to say aloud what has been on people's minds over a bitter dispute between Singapore Airlines (SIA) management and its pilots.

    Why did Senior Minister Lee Kuan Yew have to step in?

    And what did it say about the younger ministers' ability to manage such issues?

    Speaking in Parliament yesterday, the Ayer Rajah MP admitted raising a sensitive issue, but said he had to do so because of the 'disquiet' he sensed among Singaporeans.

    He also wanted to lay to rest doubts over the younger leadership's ability to handle such crises on its own.

    Mr Lee met members of the Air Line Pilots Association-Singapore (Alpa-S) two weeks ago to sort out the union's differences with management. Both sides agreed to mend fences and rebuild trust.

    The Senior Minister had stepped in personally after the pilots ousted Alpa-S' leadership last November, a move that the Government read as the start of a confrontational stance in industrial relations.

    Dr Tan said: 'Now one question that has been asked is, why is the Government interfering in this matter? Should this not be thrashed out by the management and the union?

    'Why do we allow foreigners to sit on the union executive committee? What if a similar or even a more serious issue involving, say, a pilot strike were to happen in the future and there is no Lee Kuan Yew to crack some heads?

    'I think these are legitimate questions being asked.'

    But Singaporeans should not lose sleep over how the younger ministers would fare without Mr Lee, he was quick to add.

    He himself had no doubts about their ability to manage crises. 'You watched how our new leaders handled Sars, the Asian financial crisis, and how they are managing our economic downturn.

    'They have the confidence, they have the ability and we should support them,' he said, the emotion in his voice evident.

    Mr Inderjit Singh (Ang Mo Kio GRC) also spoke, on Monday, about the direct involvement of 'very senior ministers' in the affairs of state-owned companies like SIA and PSA Corp.

    Unlike Dr Tan, he spoke from the perspective of a businessman who has long objected to the Government's dominant role in the economy.

    'In both cases, we see very senior ministers getting directly involved, despite the fact that both companies already have supposedly very strong management teams and boards.

    'This is not a good signal and is not in line with what the Government has been saying about its role in managing companies owned through its investment arms,' he said.

    He also criticised another minister who 'jumped the gun' to suggest publicly a transport companies merge.

    'Both companies are listed and it should have been their own decision,' he said.
