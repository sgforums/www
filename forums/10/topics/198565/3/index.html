    The New Paper
    THE taxi fare hike is supposed to help drivers offset rising fuel prices.

    --HEDY KHOO
    But could it end up hurting cabbies instead?

    Regular taxi commuters polled by The New Paper say they are likely to switch to taking the MRT and buses.

    More than half of the 100 regular taxi users polled said they would reduce the frequency of their cab rides.

    They were most turned off by the doubling in peak period surcharge from $1 to $2, affecting those who travel to work or school.

    Particularly because traffic is usually heavier during the peak hours, between 7am and 9.30am, commuters felt it does not make sense to pay more to get stuck in a traffic jam.

    Fifty-four of the 100 polled felt this way.

    Mr Justin Zhuang, 22, an undergraduate at NTU, said: 'That increase is very steep. It's 100 per cent more.

    'I am not willing to spend the extra money.'

    Technician Brian Ng, 28, felt the 10-cent rise in flag-down fare is fine, but the peak surcharge hike is too much.

    Property agent Eileen Lee, 29, aims to cut down on her sleep to save. She now takes the cab during peak hours at least three times a week.

    'I will try my best not to take a cab because it is going to be very expensive,' said Ms Lee.

    'I plan to wake up earlier in the morning to make sure that I don't miss my bus.'

    One commuter said he would resort to renting a car.

    Army officer See Wai Ming, 23, said: 'On days I need to go to more places, I would rather rent a car. I will take a cab only if I am very late for appointments.'

    Taxi drivers have got an earful from passengers about the price hike. And they, too, are unhappy. (See other report.)

    One ComfortDelGro driver, who gave his name as Mr Goh, said: 'I had a mother who told her son in my taxi that they have only a few more days to enjoy taking a cab to school.

    COUNTDOWN

    'It was a countdown until next Monday.'

    He was skeptical about whether the fare increase will actually help drivers.

    ComfortDelGro, which runs about three-quarters of the 22,000 taxis here, was the first to announce fare hikes.

    This is the first fare increase in 12 years for the company.

    SMRT and Premier notified the Public Transport Council (PTC) of their hikes on 3 Jul, but no details have been confirmed.

    Operators must give two weeks' notice to the PTC, so their fares will go up on 17 Jul at the earliest.

    Smart Automobile has said it is likely to raise fares, while Transcab will monitor the market for one or two weeks or more to see if the increases are accepted.

    Mr Joseph Lim, 36, a manager, agreed with the cabbies' sentiments.

    He said: 'Taxi drivers do not stand to gain from this hike unless taxi rental goes down.'

    Those who take cabs at least once a week during peak hours will feel the pinch harder.

    That's why more than half of them (55 per cent of 65) said they would change their transport routine to save money.

    Miss Vivian Mok, 19, a polytechnic student, said: 'It gets too expensive because I take a cab every week.

    'I'm going to try and arrange for my appointments to be after peak hour times.'

    Will cabbies lose their more regular customers?

    SPECIAL CIRCUMSTANCES

    It remains to be seen whether their loss will be made up by passengers who cannot stop taking cabs because of special circumstances. (See report at right.)

    Financial planner Tay Mong Seng, 57, has to take a cab during during peak hours when he takes his mother for medical check-ups at the hospital.

    'It is the most convenient way for us because she can't move very well as she suffered from a stroke recently,' said Mr Tay.

    There are also passengers who will continue to pay for the convenience and speed.

    NUS undergraduate Lavinia Selvakumar, 22, said: 'I will think twice about taking the cab during peak hours but if I'm late, I have no choice.'
