    A ''Peranakan'' is a Malay word for ''one who is born locally''.

    This term will actually refer to the descendants of those Chinese that migrated from China to Peninsular Malaya, Singapore and Indonesia. With many of them mainly settling in the Straits Settlement of Penang, Malacca and Singapore; and were soon assimilated into the easy village and town life of equatorial Malaya and Singapore.

    Some of these Chinese men, who left home for many years will soon marry local Malay girls without converting to Islam - as Islam was not as pervasive and had no political support during the 19th Century, as the Malays in Malaya and Singapore were still politically unmotivated nor awaken.

    These Chinese men picked up Malay culture and language, while keeping to their Chinese customs and religious practices.

    A cross mix of cultures, languages, customs, and beliefs created a new communal group that helped to bridge the gap between two rich communities through the Malay language that is spoken with an intersperse of Chinese colloquial words.

    The Peranakan ''Song and dance'' was well known as the ''Dondang Sayang'' and the 'poetic' exchange between male and female in the typical 'Perananakan Patois' resemble closely to the poetic verses exchanged between Chinese couples.

    The ''Peranakans'' community also exist in Indonesia, and is known better as ''Baba Indonesia''.

    Unfortunately, the ''Peranakans'' in Indonesia were assimilated into Mainstream Indonesian Life with the banning of Chinese names, Chinese Cultures, Chinese Education, and Chinese Language - when in 1967, President Suharto began to systematically dismantle the Chinese identity from Indonesian life.

    The word ''Baba'' is a word used by different nationalities or ethnicities in their languages, and each will have different meaning.

    Googling the word ''Baba'' to find its origin will reveal quite a surprising outcome, and much will depend on which site the word will land on.

    In the context of this Southeast Asian Region, it will seem to be that the word ''Baba'' is referring to the males in the 'Peranakan' community, and the females are known as ''Nonyas''.

    The word ''Peranakan'' in the Indian language refers to ''speakers of a foreign tongue''

    Before WW2, the Malay Community was not as political as it is today, and seem to be more political after the revival of Islam that is seen to have occurred during the Oil Crisis of the 1970s - (after the 6-day Israeli War), when the Middle-east Arab Nations found their pride boosted with the political clout in their near monopoly in crude oil production.

    It was during this period of self-discovery amongst the Middle-east Arab-Muslims, that the Malay communities in Southeast Asia awoke to identify their seemingly ''underclassed'' position with that of the Arabs through their mutual faith in Islam.

    Since this discovery of a common thread, and finding their position to be strengthened with a ''Rich Cousin'' holding an 'Ace Card' in the crude oil, the Malay Community began their ''Islamic Revival'' - becoming more Islamic than the Arabs.

    It was about this period - in the late 1970s and 1980s - that the Malay communities in this region began to introduce and inject Arabic cultures, diet, dressing, and values into their own local habits.

    The ''Baba Peranakan'' culture - even in its diluted adopted forms of each culture - can be said to provide an accurate snapshot of the original Malay culture in the old days before this Islamic fervor drove the Malay community from their original form. (This statement will be controversial to some. )

    Amongst the diminishing older generation of ''Peranakan'' communities in Singapore, traditions remained the same. While those in their late 50s are somewhat modernised.

    Recent trips to Malacca and Penang, seems to reveal the same trend of cultural deterioration, in which modern life has taken a toll on the preferred mode of dressing amongst the ''Peranakan" women.

    Fortunately, the ''Peranankan'' interest in good cooking and tradional fares have never diminished; and fortunately - (or unfortunately, for some) - there is a revival in ''Peranakanism'' and in a strenghtening of 'Peranakan' bonds.
