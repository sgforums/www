    Originally posted by soul_rage:

    If I am not wrong, COE is not considered a part of taxes.

    It's a kind of revenue that is classified separately from taxes, so in that aspect, you aren't correct.

    Bottomline is, if you lower the astronomical pay of our leaders, it will go towards having more funds to fund fiscal expenditures.

    You could be right about the classification of COE. But my point (which I admit wasn’t well articulate in my earlier posts) is that when we have a welfare state (where the citizens are entitled to subsidized or free medical care, cheap subsidized housing, free education, cheap transport, etc), there is often a price to be paid (usually in the form of higher taxes).

    Before I elaborate further, I want to stress that I am neither pro-PAP nor pro-opposition. And I certainly do NOT support the current policy of paying millions to our politicians.  I am a true-blooded Singaporean (like many of you here) who supports political changes – but only if the change is for the better.

    I used to work for a German boss. One of the reasons why he chose not to work in Germany is because he had to pay income tax of 50%. He is opposed to the fact that his hard-earned money is used by his government to provide unemployment benefits, medical care and free housings for drug addicts and drunkards who are too sick to work.  There is also a significant percentage of people in Germany who are unemployed (by choice) as there are sufficient welfare/financial hand-outs for them to survive without having to work.

    On the other hand, my Norwegian colleague is happy to pay 48% income tax in his country. That’s because he has many children and all of them are entitled to free medical care and free education.

    Bottomline is that a welfare state is not for everybody.  As the range of welfare and social security services increase, it is inevitable that tax will also have to be increased to fund these services (unless we are oil rich like Qatar or Kuwait).

    Some of you may argue that raising tax will not be necessary if we can cut the millions that we are currently paying our politicians. But if you do the maths, you will realise that even if you can save $100mil by cutting the salary of our politicians, it will amount to only $25 for every Singaporean (assuming a population of 4mil). This is barely enough even for a visit to the neighbourhood clinic. So who is going to pay for subsidised hospitalization, subsidised housings and free education? Does it mean that doctors, nurses, hospital administrators, construction workers, architects, engineers, teachers and civil servants will also have to take a pay cut? That’s not progression, that’s regression into a Marxist system.

    Even if we are to use our reserves to pay for these welfare services (as suggested by some people in the forum), it is not a bottomless pit. Our reserves can be depleted in a generation or two, leaving our children and grandchildren to bear the burden of our lavish and unsustainable welfare system.

    Personally for me, I would love to have free medical care, cheaper housings, cheaper cars, free education for my children, etc (who doesn’t?).  But, on the other hand, I am also opposed to paying more taxes or taking a pay cut. So can such a system be sustainable in the long run?
