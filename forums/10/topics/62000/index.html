    Insightist,

    as the others have mentioned already, the poly route is not as bad as some have thought it to be. I'm from a local polytechnic myself, despite my O-Level results qualifying me for a good JC. I was unable to get into the local Unis on the basis of my results not being in the top 5% (not that I was that keen on local Us) and then went on to a uni Down Under. I don't think my taking the poly route has made me a worse person, in fact, it has given me much more in my field of study as it has given me more hands-on knowledge as well as getting me into my vocation in NS which has increased my chance to get hands-on experience on what I've studied.

    As for emigration, I spent 4 years in Australia, and immensely enjoyed my life there (Melbourne). Initially my thoughts were always with the line that Australia was a stopover, and that I'll always return to Singapore, work, start a family and grow old. However the more time I spent there, the more I questioned the wisdom of that logic, as I found myself disagreeing with more aspects of life in Singapore (social, economic and political) and finding out that I increasingly enjoyed life Down Under. I've made the decision to leave and will certainly apply for PR status once my work experience is sufficient to qualify me. If anything, I've regretted not getting a PR before returning to complete my NS, due to misconceptions about Aussie residency requirements then.

    Whether its the path for you, nobody can decide but yourself. But from what you've typed so far, you and me seem quite alike. We have specialised interests that do not get much mileage in Singapore, and would prefer a less hectic, more carefree life that Singapore neither accepts socially or permits. My advice for you is to make the decision carefully, as obviously, life there is not suited for everyone. IMO your best bet would be to spend some time there (studying?) or to speak with people who have lived, or are living there for a while. I've been to 3 different Australian cities, as well as did research and spoke to people who've lived in others. For me, my choice would be Melbourne, as it offers to me a slower pace of life, yet one which is not overly slow (a common complaint with Perth). Melbourne also has a rich cultural and historical heritage, and is easily the events capital of Australia. In short. its a modern cosmopolitan city, yet offers enough for people with all kinds of interests.

    My take on the pros and cons of Australia (mostly from my experiences in Melbourne but mostly applicable elsewhere):

    Pros
    - Cars/Houses are cheaper
    - A friendly, welcoming people (mostly)
    - Slower pace of life (how much slower depending on where you are)
    - A much more carefree political and social system (and more forgiving and tolerant - this one can cut both ways)
    - Much more space and places to go on your free time
    - 4 seasons (in the case of Melbourne, in one day!)
    - Lots of medical and social benefits
    - Driving does not significantly increase blood pressure

    Cons
    - Security not as good as Singapore. but not much worse
    - Public transport not as efficient or cheap.
    - Not as clean as Singapore (some say not as "sterile" - good or bad? Your call)
    - You're the racial minority (though rarely noticeable)
    - Cost of living higher (Melbourne/Sydney especially)

    Generally, as a Singaporean who speaks good English, you'll be almost universally accepted and welcomed there. And you'll have no trouble integrating. While I was there, I've been mistaken more than once for a local by the Aussies I've come in contact with (and no, not because I speak with an Aussie accent but due more to my command of English). The "racism" issue that rears its head often in the local press (and in the mind of many Singaporeans) here is way overblown, the anger at immigrants is by and large directed towards the economic refugees from the Mid-East and S Asia who claim political asylum to get in, then refuse to integrate and become a burden on the state's welfare system

    'I have heard stories from my Singapore counterparts about working extremely long hours and horrendous stress at work,' said Miss Vanessa Teo, a 24-year-old senior business analyst in Minnesota, the United States.

    She said the more relaxed lifestyle, higher pay and career opportunities were what made her stay on after graduating with a business administration degree from Indiana University.

    Supportive parents also play a part. When Mr James Tan, 26, graduated from the University of Chicago last year with a master's in international relations, he wasn't sure what he wanted to do next. But his parents helped him decide.

    Now based in Washington DC, the analyst in a business-to-business content firm said: 'They wanted me to stay on to get the exposure, though I was pretty ambivalent about where I worked.'

    With more than 20,000 Singaporeans going abroad to study every year, and with more wanting to stay on, is the Republic losing its talented young citizens?

    Of the six overseas graduates The Straits Times interviewed, five said that they wanted to work abroad for at least two years before deciding if they would return home.

    Some are worried they would not be able to fit into the Singaporean work culture, or get used to living at home after living independent lives overseas.

    Mr Er said he was comfortable with his life in London and does not rule out the possibility of staying on indefinitely. 'I have come to realise that living in Singapore was like living in a glass bowl,' he said. 'I have become aware of issues such as crime, race and politics. I used to be neutral about such matters in Singapore, but I no longer am.'

    But for Miss Nora, the choice is clear.

    'I feel like I still haven't learnt enough,' she said. 'But Singapore is home base and I will eventually return.'
