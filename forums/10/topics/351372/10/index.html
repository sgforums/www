    xtreyier, I honestly don't want to perpetuate this conversation, but I feel the need to set the record straight about suicide. This is extremely difficult to do, as your arguments lack clarity, cogency and at times are downright contradictory. Nevertheless, I shall persist as this is such a very sensitive subject.

    Disclaimer: Although my work involves dealing with trauma affected clients in therapeutic settings (therefore the subjects of depression and suicide are ones I am often exposed to). I do concede that most of my formal research work has been centered on PTSD. I do not claim to be an authority on the subject of depression, but I do know more than the average Joe.

    First things first. I am not a moderator, nor am I an arbiter of all things English. If you read my post clearly, nowhere have I suggested any standard by which posts in the forum should abide by.

    I did, however, point out that you made a straw man argument. I'm starting to wonder if you understand the concept, because you have committed the exact same fallacy again. If you are going to construct a rebuttal, it would be helpful if you address the original argument faithfully and not one which you have fabricated.

    Secondly, suicide is not an illness. Yet in this case, it is arguably the unfortunate and final symptom of depression. Depression was the illness I referred to; if you have any doubt at all that depression is an illness, consult the DSM IV.

    Yes, not all who suffer from depression complete suicide. Not everyone who gets the flu is bed ridden. What is your point?

    It appears that your argument is as follows, "Not every who is depressed completes suicide, therefore anyone who completes suicide is not depressed." This is akin to saying, "Not everyone who has HIV develops AIDS, therefore anyone who developed AIDS did not have HIV." In taking this approach, you have committed the fallacy of accident- you have taken a generalization and made it the rule.

    As to why I would oppose the treatment of depression with "medication and counseling" is beyond me. Have you taken leave of your senses? Why would I be against medication and counseling if I see depression as a medical condition? 

    Furthermore, there is no treatment for a completed suicide, medicinal or otherwise. Once suicide is completed (or committed as you prefer), the patient is by definition without life. This is obviously irreversible.

    As a side-note, your duplicity is fascinating, as in one paragraph you can state suicide is not an illness (which is correct, though it's antecedents almost always are), as well as,  a "disorder that can remedy[sic] by medicine and counselling.[sic]" Sheer lunacy.

    On the third and final point, I might have to concede an error of judgement. I am unsure if you deliberately chose not to understand and formulate a cogent argument or if you lack the intelligence to do so. Six to one half dozen to another; the end result is the same.
