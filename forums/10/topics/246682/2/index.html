    Just my 2 cents.

    There are indeed two slippery slopes that one might see from the dialog:

    1. the first slippery slope is that there will be people who can conveniently use the govt or whatever as their crutch (being the word of the day) or excuse to just wallow and not work their way up ie. use their own determination etc.

    This position assumes firstly that the people have the wherewithal to do it (i.e. innate intelligence, skills) and that the innate potential "fits" into the existing rules.

    2. the other slippery slope is that the elite, the policy makers etc can continue to blame the "poor" people for being lazy without relooking at current practices.

    While it is important to be objective and pragmatic, the govt has the understand that it hands-all-over manner of governing and managing the country AND the economy has severe repercussions for the growth and development of the people.

    The second slippery slope that many in this dialog have pointed is more salient, IMHO, than the first which is what hardhall was trying to push hard on. Why?

    In academic terms, Anthony Giddens, Bourdieu and a bunch of other European sociologist have long pointed to the power of structures over human agency and actions. Albeit humans have its ability to influence and mold structures, structures and the inherent values have powerful consequences over human actions as well. They refer to this as the duality of structures i.e. they are created by and yet at the same time recreate by influencing human actions.

    in our case, the all encompassing structures imposed by the government in all areas of our lives be it in education, economy, even sports does and will have powerful influences on all levels of society, especially those in the lower rungs. That, I think is what many of the contributors are pointing to in their various vignettes. Regardless of what hardhall thinks - ie. actions and not speech, there are enough evidence - go talk to social workers, go talk to primary school kids in neighbourhood schools - to show that it is not easy (though not impossible) to break out from these cycles.

    The government's role can therefore be:
    1. To listen closely and rethink and relook at all issues and continue with their tinkering. This decision however will be continually plagued by grievances - simply because they are not omnipotent. Theory and practice in both societies and business have shown that there will be always unintended consequences. This is inevitable due to the complexities of issues at hand. Unlike financial market management where transactions can be reduced to numbers and dealt with alogrithms, governance inexplicably involves human emotions, thoughts, aspirations and actions. This requires ongoing actions to keep things moving on an even keel.

    2. The other option is to adopt a cultivation approach and this requires a major shift in govt mindset i.e. a less hands on approach. This approach recognizes the failings of human cognitions and rationality. This approach accepts that inevitable unintended consequences will arise. However, this approach takes a bottom up view and intervenes to cultivate the growth. It is analogous to growing plants. You can cultivate it (to the extreme would be bonsai planting) knowing that the plant has an innate life force with its ability to thrive with or without your intervention. Yet your interventions while at the one hand may not aid terribly in its growth can have severe ill repercussions that will either hinder or in the worse case destroy the plant and its growth. Because of this understanding, a govt will use kid's gloves to handle the ongoing interventions that it introduces and not slipshodly try to bulldoze its way onto the country that it tries to govern. This is probably in line with Catherine Lim's point to the govt i.e. mind the affective gap.

    Sorry for the long post but this is something that is close to my heart.
