    To lure foreign talents, give them same rights as S'pore citizens

    I would like to thank Minister for Community Development, Youth and Sports Dr Vivian Balakrishnan for the decision to let the Singapore Sports School take in foreign talents.

    I understand that the sports school is for citizens but having made the decision to open it to foreign talents, how can the latter be attracted if the school fees are $25,000 per annum compared to $6,000 per annum for citizens?

    In my view there are two distinct types of foreigners:

    a) PRs who have lived in Singapore for many years, have a home here and have contributed a lot to the economy; and

    b) Those who are non-residents living in other countries and who want to send their children to the sports school.

    While it's fair to ask the latter category to pay $25,000, I feel it is not fair to put the permanent residents in the same category.

    In all the other areas of education and child development, PRs are treated more or less on par with Singapore citizens. So my question is why is there this big differentiation in the case of the sports school?

    On the other hand, the sports school wants to groom the locals to represent Singapore, but aren't today's PRs future citizens of Singapore if they feel that this is the place for them?

    PRs love to live in Singapore because the place is good, safe and clean with many career opportunities. These are the key factors that keep PRs committed to Singapore.

    If they like it, they can apply to become Singapore citizens. The Singapore government doesn't force anyone to become citizens.

    Singapore has done a lot to attract foreign talents as far as the work force is concerned. But in sports, I feel there is still a lot more to be done. The process to upgrade a student's pass to that of a permanent resident and then become a Singapore citizen should be the same.

    Encourage sporting talents to come to Singapore. Please revise the fees for permanent residents to match those for Singapore citizens.

    Suresh Potluri

    Groom S'pore atheletes. Don't rely on foreign talent

    I was at the National Healthy Lifestyle Campaign on Sept 11.

    I visited the booth of the Singapore Table Tennis Association. I was alarmed when I noticed that all the players are from China.

    A few officials were also from China. My friends asked me whether we were at the China Table Tennis Association. I can understand why they say that.

    On Sept 13, I read an article about basketball. I learnt that the silver medal which Singapore won in the 2003 SEA Games in Vietnam was obtained with the help of six foreign players whom I believe were from China.

    Was the women's team entirely made up of Chinese players?

    There are more and more foreign players representing Singapore. But there are many avid basketballers, table tennis players and sportsmen in Singapore.

    Surely, some of them are capable of doing Singapore proud. If not, they can be groomed. For example, silat is doing very well with local talent.

    I agree that foreign players can act as sparring partners and provide competition for local talents. But do they deprive our local talents of their places in the national team?

    Many young sporting talents aspire to represent the nation. Foreign players may win medals for Singapore but I believe not many of us are really proud of them.

    I feel really proud only when Singaporeans, not foreign players, win medals for Singapore.

    Sports is not just about winning medals. We must also not forget the passion of our local talents and fellow Singaporeans.

    I believe that we can do it. We have successful local sportsmen and sportswomen such as Remy Ong, Joscelin Yeo, Teo Wee Chin and Terence Koh.

    We also have what it takes to groom our Singapore athletes. In time, I believe we will have capable athletes who can be better than the foreign players.

    To mould Singapore into a sporting nation, we have to depend on ourselves. We must have our own sportsmen whom we can be really proud of.

    Cheng Zhi Qin
