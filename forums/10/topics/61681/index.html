    Just as my car passed into the Johor state border, I tried to pass a Malaysian passenger coach in front of my car. It then started to swerve from the left lane to the right lane, and I thought that the driver was falling asleep.

    Slowing my car to the rear of the bus, which was already travelling at a speed of about 125 kmh, the bus straightened out in the left lane (on the 2 lane North South Highway).

    With the bus on the left lane, I tried to make a second pass again, then the driver again swerved into my right lane, preventing me from passing him again; and started to weave the bus from the left lane to the right lane over the next 1km - at a speed of 125 kmh.

    Again I slowed my car to the rear of the bus, realising that the driver is out of his mind; and blasted my air horn for a full minute to wake up any passengers in the bus and have them observe the erratic driving that is endangering the passengers' lives.

    The next time when I tried to pass the bus, with my air horn full blast, the bus driver made a less determined swerve to push me off the highway, but I was determined not to pull back, and instead drove closer to the bus - while being alert to the distance between my car and the bus.

    The bus driver realised that I was not giving way, and he stuck his hand out to show me his middle finger.

    I passed the bus, and went in front of the bus, and slammed my brakes enough to slow the bus down, without having it hitting my rear; and slowed the speed down to 90kmh for the next 1 km.

    From past experience on Indonesian roads, I realised that the bus driver may have at least one or two assistants on board, and it will be pointless for me to stop the bus - (to question the Driver for his reckless driving) - even though my car is equipped for any aggressive assault by any road rage driver on Malaysian roads.

    The bus tried to overtake me again but it was no match for the take-off speed of my car, and I taunted him over the next 2 km, pulling myself away after that, as it was pointless to fluster him further.

    It was pointless to report this matter to the Malaysian Police, as they are indifferent to such reports from Singaporeans.

    From his side mirrors, the bus driver could hardly see my car as a Singapore registered car, especially when my two head lamps were on, with additional two spot lights mounted into my bumpers also turned on.

    It seems that some of these bus drivers take the law into their own hands, and behave in a murderous way when irritated by bright lamps - regardless if the cars are Malaysian or Singaporean registered.

    Little wonder that some accidents on the Malaysian highway can occur with little explanations, especially when no one live through a severe road accident.

    The final chapter for Driving on Malaysian Roads - A word of advise by Johor Police Chief for Singapore Drivers being abused in Malaysia

    Cops 'can't seize S'pore passports'
    Johor police chief urges S'pore drivers to lodge report if they are forced to hand over passports or to pay fine on the spot

    JOHOR BARU - Singaporean motorists should file police reports if they are forced to pay traffic fines on the spot or if their passports are seized by Malaysian police, said Johor police chief Musa Hassan.

    'It is not standard operating procedure for police to seize the passports of foreign traffic offenders to make them pay their summonses before leaving the country.

    'It is also an offence for a police officer to seize a person's passport as they have no power to do so,' he said.

    Police officers should only ask for passports in order to establish a person's credentials and update records, the police chief said.

    'After taking down the details, the officer must return the passport to its owner.

    'The officer has no right to seize it,'     he said in an interview with The Star.

    He was commenting on reports in The Straits Times and The Sunday Times last month that motorists from Singapore had had their passports seized by Malaysian police at roadblocks.

    Some of the drivers who were unaware that they had outstanding traffic summonses in Malaysia had their passports held by the police here and were made to go to the Traffic Police headquarters in Jalan Tebrau to pay up.

    Datuk Musa advised Singaporeans who had their passports seized or were forced to pay on the spot at roadblocks to lodge a report immediately.

    'The motorist can always refuse to pay on the spot.

    'If the officers are persistent, the motorist can take down their name and personnel number and proceed to lodge a report,' he said.

    Last month, Johor police set up roadblocks to nab Singaporean motorists who had not paid traffic fines.

    They also upped the ante recently by warning that they would detain those who refused to pay.

    Singaporeans owed RM28.6 million (S$13 million) in outstanding traffic fines, it was said. -- The Star/Asia News Network
