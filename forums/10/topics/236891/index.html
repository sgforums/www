The problem of nationalised 'public' transport

  • BillyBong
    11 Feb 07, 13:11
    Two things that have regressed since SG made the leap forward have been the lack of competition in the transportation system, and the obvious lack of intelligence in problem solving implementation.

    It must read like a red flag each time someone writes in the papers concerning the obviously failed concept of the ERP. In the past week alone there was a flurry of letters denouncing the flawed concept:

    Evening ERP on CTE ineffective after gantry

    MANY motorists choose to pay the evening ERP charges on the Central Expressway (CTE) heading north towards Yishun and beyond, hoping to have a smoother and faster ride home.
    Traffic approaching the gantry just before the ingress into Braddell Road is presently containable and smooth. Pass this gantry and the volume of traffic starts to build up again as one approaches the egress for traffic joining the CTE from Braddell Road. This heavy build-up becomes worse as one approaches the Ang Mo Kio avenues 1 and 3 ingresses and egresses. Sometimes, traffic slows to less than 30kmh.

    To be fair to those who choose to pay the ERP charges, there should also be gantries located at the egresses into the CTE at the Braddell Road and Ang Mo Kio Avenue 1 junctions.

    Many motorists have actually detoured to avoid the gantry point, only to join in at the above two locations.

    Paying motorists should be accorded more fairness so that they do not have to contend with a build-up of traffic again immediately after going past the gantry.

    Chia Yong Soong

    ERP isn't working, rein in number of cars instead

    WHAT is the main objective for the implementation of ERP and the subsequent increase in charges? Is it to reduce congestion on the roads and expressways? If that is so, then it has not been effective. Increasing the charges will cause traffic to be diverted to other roads and consequential bottlenecks and more congestion.
    During my daily drive home on the northbound CTE, it is very common to see motorists driving extremely fast either to get past the ERP gantry before the operational hour or to beat the higher peak-hour rate. They switch lanes recklessly in order to get ahead, posing a hazard to other road users.

    If the Government aims to reduce congestion, it should look at reducing the number of cars by controlling the number of COEs and raising taxes on new cars. At the same time, it could promote use of public transport by improving the infrastructure. Fewer cars on the roads will also help save the earth.

    Cindy Brown (Mrs)

    Installing ERP gantries and raising ERP rates every quarter not the solution to traffic jams

    I refer to Mrs Cindy Brown's letter, 'ERP isn't working, rein in number of cars instead' (ST, Feb 3), and could not agree with her more that the ERP's aim of easing congestion on the roads in Singapore is not working.
    I live in Punggol and it takes my husband and I one hour to get to and from work in Shenton Way. The irony is that it takes the same amount of time to travel via the LRT or bus followed by the journey from Punggol MRT station to Tanjong Pagar station.

    Why drive when you can take public transport, you say? Well, someone from the Land Transport Authority should try taking the westbound train from Tanjong Pagar to Outram station or from Tanjong Pagar to Dhoby Ghaut station (transfer at Raffles Place) during the peak periods of 6pm to 6.45pm. Often, you could not even board the trains as they are packed. I really sympathise with the other commuters' discomfort as their train journey is longer than mine.

    At the end of a long working day, Singaporeans would just like to enjoy a quick journey home to unwind and have dinner with their loved ones. With the Government also encouraging companies to promote work-life balance, they should abolish the ERP gantry (installed in August 2005) on north-bound CTE in the evenings. Why leave office by 6.30pm but in turn get penalised for going home early? Have we not paid enough for the COE and annual road tax?

    The peak-hour traffic is worsening due to more cars populating our roads. With the recent dip in COE prices, many people have decided to buy a car. Perhaps the authorities should spend more time to build an efficient world-class transport system in order to persuade its citizens to switch to public transport. In the meantime, installing ERP gantries and raising ERP rates every quarter is not the solution.

    Wendy Lee Fei Yin (Mdm)

    The problem with the CTE is that by the time it was built, its original plan has been implemented almost five years late.

    As soon as the CTE was opened, we see an almost immediate clogging of this expressway that was supposed to alleviate the traffic woes along other secondary roads linking the north residential areas to the economic zones in the city center located in the South; and the industrial areas in the west.

    If one looked at the AYE and the PIE, it was already going through the second or third stage of improvement during the mid-1980s, when the CTE was under construction.

    The observations and improvements made to the PIE and AYE was not included in the CTE design.

    The width of each traffic lane along the CTE is much narrower than that of the AYE, and certain stretches of the PIE has lanes that are woefully miserly in width.

    Even the design of the flyovers and road profile is outrageously unsafe, as the road camber is designed to create an accident.

    The Benjamin Sheares Bridge that link the ECP to the AYE is about the only concrete structure that has taken into consideration the 'banking angle or slope' of the road profile that allows a car to continue its speed of 80 or 90 kmh as it go round the bend.

    Most other roads are banked the wrong direction that force the car to tilt towards the outer side of the cornering motion.

    Could it be that the Benjamin Sheares Bridge was built by Japanese who understand the road camber and banking angle to assist car drivers to go through a bend without slowing down ?

    While LTA has design expressways to move traffic in volume, one will expect that the speed limits could be similarly raised to get people moving along faster, so as to get out of the expressways sooner.

    Unfortunately, the LTA and the Traffic Police seems to defeat the original purpose of building expressways.

    They will install 'speed cameras' that serves as a throttle-valve that suddenly reduce the fast flow into a trickle at the point of the camera location.

    For every car that slows down suddenly before the camera, and pick-up speed again after the camera location, this proces will involve at least 40 seconds per car. This action will correspondingly be accumulated into a multiplier effect that is repeated by all the cars behind that will respond in the same manner.

    This has caused the traffic to slow down unnecessarily, and have resulted in small pile-ups when drivers unexpected hit the brakes before the cameras.

    Do the traffic planners also drive, and understand the problems of driving in Singapore ?

    Alas, the current number of road works on the Benjamin Henry Sheares Bridge has ultimately served only to clog traffic at their critical times: lane narrowing coupled by traffic merging from the filter lanes is a classic example heading towards the ECP.

    In the case of CTE being opened late, it seems more as a case of too little too late, but better than nothing. I cannot fault the planners for failing to forsee the impact of a surge in vehicular traffic in the 80s to 90s but what i find most disturbing is the lack of a penetrating solution that alleviates the entire traffic problem.

    If drivers are being asked (no, demanded) to pay for smoother traffic, it is doubly likely that many would be willing to part with $3-4 of ERP fees, if the result is 'express' flow to the City.

    The problem is, one pays the levy, and still gets stuck in 30-40 km/h slow moving traffic: where's the incentive to pay and pay if it doesn't solve the problem? In addition, a single accident creates mayhem and ulimately defeats the perceived 'advantage' supposedly created by ERP.

    Any implementation now will be deemed too late, but perhaps the authorities could consider designing a 'skybridge': creating an overhead 'expressway bridge' with very limited filters to create single directional flow of traffic. An example of this can be seen on the Oakland Bay bridge leading into and out of San Francisco. Traffic in this case is limited to a single direction on both the lower and upper lanes, expanding the limited space on the bridge and allowing traffic to flow in the same direction across the entire bridge.

    In the case of the CTE, which at the moment runs on 3 lanes per direction, an overhead sky bridge designed solely to take traffic out of the city could release as many as 6-7 lanes (including shoulders) heading into the city alone.

    If volume of vehicles is the problem, this is one suggestion to maximise the use of the CTE.

    The other aspect is to review the policy of using speed cameras along the expressways. They are clearly counter-productive in their role and i would really like to see the traffic police release the statistics of both speedsters vs the overall accident figures caused by speeding to determine if there is indeed a correlation, of which the TP has put to effective use.
