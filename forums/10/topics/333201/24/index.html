    Having at least a 5-rm, an executive flat or a HUDC unit, aiming to upgrade to a condominium or a private property, or at least an Executive Condominium is a must. Thus, many caught with large mortgage commitments are now trying their best to dispose of their large properties in order to downgrade. 5-room, Executive, HUDC apartments are increasingly out of reach to the new entrants of the middle-class dream. Yet a four room flat for the young middle class is a minimumÂ  - which is quite a large space if they have no children yet. However, even this category of flats and its value remains unpredictable for the future. It is important also to take note that the purchase of houses does go hand in hand with the desire for the best renovations and fittings. Thus, depending on income levels, many take renovation and hire-purchase loans for home re-modeling, household appliances, sophisticated home entertainment systems, computers and furniture.

    Many have been weaned on overseas travel at least once a year to exotic places. They play or aspire to play golf hence country club memberships are desired, if not at least a visit to the driving range. Having a car but expecting to buy a more expensive car even though it is now largely an expense item is a common practice. Hunting for liquid cash for investment and stock speculation are seen as normal. Even tertiary students, before graduating and getting a job, explore credit for overseas travel. The desire for overseas and further education has become a pursuit for many. Parents and individuals take study and personal loans to realize this wish. Others have immediate needs - at least a two-year package for hand phones (with a desire for a yearly upgrade to keep up with the latest models), wearing branded clothes togged with the latest fashion accessories, indulging heavily in movies, Internet and the weekly pubbing and clubbing. Some are fond of going to the gym or thinking of joining one. Others join such establishments but do not use the facilities fully. Then there are the other expanses. Dinning out, owning a palmtop, buying CDs, books and magazines to name just a few. There is also the desire to consume ?culture? as fashion. Hence, some attend art and music performances at regular intervals. How deep their appreciation of ?culture? is off course another matter. Nevertheless it is another set of expenses. The aspiration for the good life is valid, but it needs to be tempered by one?s affordability and responsibility to live within one?s means.

    Once the boon of the middle class, the asset accumulation and life-style policies of the PAP are increasingly narrowing their choices or leaving them in debt. Many apply for credit cards, but credit is not an option. After all if you chalk up large bills on your card that you cannot pay, you are eventually made a bankrupt. Hence it is not surprising that the number of people going bankrupt in the recent years have increased. Those who pursue their material wants, although they cannot really afford them, get into heavy financial obligations and have problems managing them. For Singapore banks it meant that bad debts that they had to write-off reached a 12 year high in June 2003 and could get worse if unemployment rises (31 July 2003, Reuters).

    The consequence is an emotional and spiritual vacuum in society caused by the singular pursuit of materialism. Consumerism has created a status-conscious society where the goods you posses mark the size of your worth. Personal values revolve around up-market, up-grading, up-lift, up-ward looking, etc. "UP" is the buzz word. Life continues in this manner in spite of frequent calls by many to stress the non-material dimension of life.

    Pursuit of materialism has its social impact. For example, it does not support marriage. If you are unable to be materially attractive you chances at marriage are also low. By extension it is having a bearing on the desire to have children, as the cost of raising children is enough to dissuade many from procreating. A middle-class family is usually a double income family with a maid. Some want to stick to one or 2 children, a few even no children. They practice a two or one child policy without government intervention. They do not live with parents because they value privacy. Relationship with parents may be limited to weekend visits or a meal outing. If there are frequent visits it could be for practical considerations such as having a cooked meal or for child caring reasons.

    Work ethic and employer ? employee relations are also affected. It is not uncommon for individuals to expect salary increases every year otherwise the decision is to job hop. Jobs are treated in terms of the market value of the job. A job is seen as a means to a high flying career path. Similarly, working environments where employers are not sensitive to the needs of working parents can also emerge. It was painfully obvious when the foot and mouth disease broke out in October 2000. A majority of the employers wanted to make sure that their employees came to work - never mind if his or her child is sick. The welfare of the employee and his family does not feature prominently in such a working culture.

    Materialism has also given rise to a self-centeredness that is becoming the hallmark of the young. Public service and duty is not a priority, thereby creating a dearth of young people in civil and social organizations. This occurs at a time when the financially and socially displaced rely heavily on charitable organizations to help them tie over their various predicaments. Materialism has led to a lack of social empathy. While the young might be articulate, they also presume themselves to be of high standard and tend to look down on people who may have made mistakes in life.

    Other reasons include Singaporeans non-awareness of the problem and its significance. Those below 35 have been brought up naive in political knowledge. Many don't know what's going on around them both socially and politically. Others are reluctant to be involved in alternative policy formulation exercises apart from those sanctioned by the PAP. Some in this category might even criticise the political opposition, and try to demonstrate they are better. But have no courage to show how good they can be by making a public contribution and take a differing stand vis-?-vis the PAP.

    Health care, housing, utilities are all essentials. As such, long-term mortgages for public housing, loans for further education (locally) or even health care that place heavy financial burdens on people are major concerns. Whereas cars, overseas education and travel maybe not for some. However given the PAP policies of pursuing materialism, loans for overseas education and the seven-year lease on car (now even 10 years is allowed) has made it also a concern for those who want it and it figures importantly in their financial plan.

    Because the social cost of materialism are all linked to a generation of PAP policies Singaporeans have all become and can be classified as ?poor? in certain ways.


    Unemployment, dipping wages and rising costs
    According to quarterly reports by Singapore's Department of Statistics, unemployment has been steadily increasing. By June 2001, unemployment had reached 3.4%, breaching the international satisfactory unemployment level of 3.0%. Singapore?s unemployment rate worsened to 4.7 percent in December 2001, the highest in 15 years, as companies laid off workers to weather the recession.

    Unemployment stood at 4.5% in the first and second quarters of 2003 as confirmed by the Manpower Research and Statistics Department report on ?Labour Market, Second Quarter 2003?. The rate exceeds the 4.4 percent recorded in December 1998 during the Asian financial crisis. The Manpower Research and Statistics Department published a report on "Labour Market, 2001" in March 2002 in which overall unemployment rate was projected to reach about 5.5 - 6% by the second half of 2002 ? an estimate that has been confirmed with the 5.9% figure for the third quarter of 2003. The record rate of 6.0 percent was in March 1986 resulting from the mid-1980s recession.

    It is not usual to have government lead surveys of wage to show that since the Asian crisis wage growth has reached pre-crisis levels. However a comparison of the pay increase of the rank-and?file employees will show that throughout the 1990s average annual wage growth has been declining from 10% to 8% in the early half to 7% to 5% in the later half (see page 4, Report on Wages in Singapore, 2002). This is largely due to attempts to keep business costs down, as employers see a cut in wages as one way of keeping costs down.

    In fact, total wages comprising of basic wages, annual wage supplement and variable bonuses came to standstill in 2002, a drop from the 1.1% rise in 2001 (Report on Wages in Singapore, 2002). For the rank and file worker there was a cut in wages by 0.3% in 2002 down from a 1.2% gain a year earlier. The variable wage component of all workers (which includes the 13th month allowance and other variable bonuses) fell 15% from 2.09 months of the basic salary in 2001 to 1.77 months in 2002 (Report on Wages in Singapore, 2002). Needless to say, the rank and file workers experienced a drop of 19% in 2002.

    It has been highlighted that the Per capita GDP in 2000 at around US$23,000 was higher that many First World countries but the remuneration received by Singapore workers or wage share in GDP was about 42 percent, which is unusually low compared to developed countries and lowest among the Asian NIEs. Some economists have therefore remarked that Singapore, while having attained a First World per capita income, in reality has kept in place an income structure that is closer to that of a Third World country (Department of Statistics, 2001).

    What has been a trend for many years now is increasingly kicking in - the cost of living has risen and continues to rise. Health care, housing, utilities, education and the purchase of consumer goods are expensive and are eating into one?s income ? that is if you have one. Coupled with the prolonged economic downturn, the loss of jobs and a drop in the value of assets has made many financially vulnerable leading to a new kind of poverty situation.


    The rise of the new poor
    Singapore is facing an emerging problem of relative poverty called the new poor - where societal expectations of continued economic growth and prosperity are increasing replaced by a sense of economic uncertainty and long-term financial insecurity.

    ?Absolute poverty? which refers to a lack of the basic requirements to sustain physical life - the subsistence poverty of not having sufficient food and adequate shelter are largely accepted in Singapore as have been adequately tackled. This is the popular understanding of poverty in Singapore even though there are scattered reports of homeless families and destitute individuals in the local media.

    A close examination of socio-economic conditions in present day Singapore shows that much has changed under the economic policies of the PAP ? there is the rise of a new phenomenon. Other than the traditional poor, there is now the new poor, the middle-income people who find themselves in a bind. The ?middle-class? which at times was portrayed as the ?new rich? is finding that the cost of living for maintaining a certain life-style associated with being middle-class is outstripping income.

    If we just say cost of living has gone up, the PAP will argue, based on the CPI, that the cost of living has not increased much or is even lower now. For instance they are likely to argue that the average household income by work rose by 156 per cent in 1990 over 1980 and by 61 per cent in 2000 over 1990. And that these increases are much higher that the corresponding increases of 18 and 16.4 per cent respectively in general food prices (see ?Food Prices Today, Singapore Statistics Newsletter, March 2002). However, it is the cost of living associated with ?Life Style? as promised by the PAP is what the middle class is having problems with.

    Many in this stratum of society have lost jobs. Suddenly they find that all this while they have been living on a thin line of affordability in terms what they were assured they could afford as a middle class life-style. They have accumulated long term financial commitments they now have to honour even though they are unemployed. Others who seem, in material terms, well-off are challenged in terms of financial obligations, ability to manage them adequately and capacity to cope with the accompanying social fallout. Meanwhile, the gap between the rich and the poor continues to widen. It has created a condition where there is now real poverty among the middle-class once perceived to be affluent.

    Asset accumulation policies have created a condition where many Singaporeans were made asset rich but cash poor. For instance, the purchase and speculation of prices of both public and private housing is one example. Underlying this development was the steady rise in land costs in Singapore and a general upward swing in the cost of living.

    This is where the relative definition of poverty comes in ? the new poor. ?Relative poverty? is used to demonstrate the inadequacy of definitions of absolute or primary poverty by referring to the cultural needs (social expectations) of individuals and families within the context of an industrialized society. It is a relativistic definition that relates poverty not only to physical needs but also to the norms and expectations of society. In the Singapore case ? it?s the ever rising expectations of a higher standard of living and life-style promised by the PAP which unfortunately resulted in binding many Singaporeans into long term financial obligations which they are unable to honour in an increasingly uncertain job market ? hence making them ?poor?.

    In Singapore, the new knowledge-based economy means that many of our unskilled and semi-skilled workers, especially those in their forties who still have families to support are becoming ?redundant? unless they retrain, upgrade their skills and use information technology. The sad aspect is that the PAP government does not understand that even for subsidized and allowance giving retraining programs, people still need enough hard cash to stay alive. Many in this category have long mortgages (up to 30 years) for public housing and with upgrading, incurring additional long-term payment expanses. Further, if workers are retrenched, the number of years they have served in a company may not count towards their retrenchment benefits. For example, if workers are paid retrenchment benefit at the point of retrenchment, the number of years of service does count, the benefit is usually calculated based on number of years of service. However, some company may mot be able to pay retrenchment benefit due to losses.

    For the younger workers or fresh graduates, the recession not withstanding, jobs are harder to come by, have to settle for less pay and work longer hours. But there is no guarantee that tertiary education locally or abroad translates immediately into a job. Many graduates spend months, some over a year, looking for a full-time job. Others work temporarily in between - if they can find something. Relief teaching and giving tuition is the most common type of work undertaken by unemployed graduates.

    It is increasingly evident that the financial commitment elicited by the new economy will not be matched by a guarantee of life-long employment and financial security. For those employed, the presence of personal savings may not be large enough to cushion unexpected long-term unemployment prospects. If made redundant, the only option is to find stopgap measures of work such as being a security guard, cleaner or taxi driver that will take them away from their traditional area of skills and training to a saturated unskilled employment market where there is competition with school leavers, retirees and foreign workers. What about others who do not work - housewives, retirees, the handicapped, students and children?

    When the concept new poor was first mooted by the Workers? Party in the run up to the Singapore 2001 General Elections, both PAP politicians and journalists (Tan, 2001) from the mainstream media claimed that that everyone was a new poor if they experienced a reduction in usual earnings. In particular some tried to link the New Poor phenomenon to people who choose to be unemployed or refuse to adjust their ego by accepting jobs with a relative lower status. Others argued, the situation of this group is exaggerated. However a distinction has to be made between the ?behaviour or choices? of some who are affected by relative poverty and those really financially burdened. In fact, the psychological behaviour of some who are unable to adjust to the new social-economic reality and who suffer from material acquisition withdrawal symptoms should be in part be blamed upon the PAP?s 40 year policy of material acquisition.

    The new poor is larger ? it is a social and economic phenomenon that has been inflicted on the middle-class as a result of the PAP policy of asset accumulation and asset inflation. It has brought to a sudden halt this asset accumulation/inflation?s upward lunge and has left a significant portion of the middle class in a financial bind. Coupled with this, is the problem of maintaining an expected standard of living and life style of the middle class.

    It has been noted that since late 2001, professionals, managers, executives and technicians have formed nearly 40% of those laid off (16 September 2003, Today). There also been a rise in the number of middle-class cases seeking help at selected welfare organizations (see also 16 September 2003, Today). Hence, it had earlier prompted PAP MP Ho Geok Choo to plead that the ?new poor of Singapore? such as the professionals, managers, executives and technicians who have been affected by job losses be also considered for assistance programmes (Business Times, 2 September 2003).

    For our purposes I want to stress that the New Poor concept refers to middle class and NOT the upper rich class though they suffer as well, may be poorer than before, but still remain relatively rich.

    The articulation of poverty is central to any discussion of social inequality, including the debate of who is poor and the reasons for their poverty. The poor have often been blamed for their poverty, which is seen as the consequence of some form of personal inadequacy such as ficklessness or idleness. However, this is not the case. The existence of poverty in terms of the social and economic structures of industrialized societies is an important feature to consider. In the Singapore case, it's the plight of the new poor.


    PAP?s Response
    Part of the problem resides in PAP government?s policies that seek profit. Wealth redistribution is cast aside in favour of forcing high growth to mediate poverty levels ? an ineffective solution. A scrutiny of the rich to find ways to accommodate the poor is prematurely discharged as the ?politics of envy?.

    The PAP government?s response to the increasing financial difficultly faced by many Singaporeans has been a series of short term responses. Its has dished out New Singapore Shares, Economic Restructuring Shares, re-training grants and a series of financial assistance packages for those who are hard hit.

    However, the impact on the household size, official unemployment status, overseas resident Singaporeans who may not be experiencing the hardship of those resident locally and age must be evaluated to ensure that taxpayers? money distributed as handouts are indeed reaching those who actually need it and not the other way around. Further, these are only short-term measures using taxpayers' money to temporarily relieve the need to adjust to cost of living increases, they are not a long-term solution to keeping the costs of living down. Instead the PAP government should evaluate whether its government policies are the culprit behind these price increases.

    Meanwhile, the PAP government has asked Singaporeans to bear the brunt of cost cutting measures by taking wage cuts and lowering their expectations in life. The Central Provident Fund ? (Singapore?s social security system) has been re-structured. Now there is even talk of devaluing the Singapore dollar and bringing property prices down. But it is unclear whether in the long run jobs will be saved or Singapore will attract more investments. Nevertheless, the above short term solutions currently remain as the PAP governments? response for ensuring cohesion in society.

    Conclusion: building social cohesion ? including everyone
    Nevertheless, an alternative formula has to be found. Singaporeans need to review their old compact with the existing government under the People?s Action Party.

    A new social cohesion compact is needed to create a bond among the people that goes beyond materialism, emphasises genuine multiculturalism and includes meaningful civil and political participation. Towards this end two recommendations may be considered as starting points.

    First there is a need to bring to public attention to the phenomenon of new poor and explain to Singaporeans the implications it holds for social cohesion. Unless the problem is recognized as being there and identifying why it arose and who is responsible, it will be difficult to frame the opportunities for introducing solutions

    Second, there is a need to introduce a wide consultation process to find out what Singaporeans want and how they would like to go about addressing the problem. While it might be easily to think up solutions in the abstract, it is important the ownership over policy making is re-introduced into Singaporean society and people get the feeling that there are a different set of actors who will be willing to politically implement them.

    With these two options as a modest step forward, we can begin to deliberate how to face up to the challenges of the new poor and social cohesion in Singapore.

    Singapore: Rising Costs, Dipping Wages

    By amrc
    Created 09/13/2007 - 16:59

    By James Gomez

    Singapore, the only Southeast Asian country to avert a recession during the Asian Crisis, became the only Southeast Asian country to fall into a recession, or to quote the Trade & Industry Ministry on 18 May 2001, a âtechnical recessionâ. The city-state enjoyed a prolonged period of economic growth between 1986 and 1997 averaging 8.6 percent per annum. However, after the Asian financial crisis, Singaporeâs GDP is more volatile -

    According to quarterly reports by the government, unemployment has been steadily increasing in the run up to the recession. By June 2001, unemployment had reached 3.4 percent, breaching the internationally accepted unemployment level of three percent. The official unemployment rate worsened to 4.7 percent in December 2001, the highest in 15 years, as companies retrenched workers. The rate exceeds the 4.4 percent recorded in December 1998 during the Asian financial crisis. The Manpower Research and Statistics Department published a report, Labour Market 2001, in March 2002 in which the overall unemployment rate was projected to reach about 5.5 - six percent by the second half of 2002. The record rate of six percent was in March 1986 resulting from a recession.1

    Meanwhile, the cost of living in Singapore has not been reduced by much even in the economic downturn. During the second half of 2001, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) rose 0.5 percent for households in the lowest 20 percent income group but fell by 0.3 percent for the top 20 percent income group. For households in the middle 60 percent income group, the CPI remained stable in this period. Households in the lowest 20 percent and middle 60 percent income groups registered lower inflation rates of 1.3 percent and 0.4 percent respectively in 2001, the rises being due to dearer cooked food and higher electricity tariffs. The CPI for households in the top 20 percent income group dipped by 0.2 percent in 2001, due largely to lower car prices.2 In a study done by the Relocation Journal & Real Estate News in August 2000, Singapore was the fifth most expensive city to live in after New York, Seoul, Tokyo, and London.

    It is with this backdrop that one should understand the wage situation and the impact on workers. Except for unionised companies in Singapore, there is no legal minimum wage. Therefore there is really no jurisdiction over how much an employer should pay an employee or offer a job-seeker.

    Between an employer and employee, there exists a contract of service and the terms and conditions cannot run contrary to the Employment Act, which stipulates the minimum standards for an employee, e.g. overtime, annual leave, and maternity benefit, but nothing governs the actual salary that should be paid to an employee, apart from compensation calculations in the Third Schedule of the Employment Act and that employees who earn more than S$1,600 (US$869)3 are not entitled to overtime etc.

    In unionised corporations, the minimum wage is spelt out in a collective agreement (CA) between company and union, legally endorsed and renewed once every two to three years. It also provides for an annual increment, being negotiable on an annual basis after National Wages Council (NWC) recommendations. But if the company can prove that it is not doing too well, any agreed increment can be smaller or even waived for the year, and the CA can be re-negotiated before it expires. There is also an Industrial Arbitration Court if employers refuse to up salaries in the CA to a reasonable level.

    However, wage negotiations between employer and union rest on the employerâs right to a final say, and typically in Singapore, success rests much on the usual day to day âgood relationsâ between employers and unions, that help foster the possibility of securing better benefits for workers. Direct confrontation between employer and employees, using the courts, is generally frowned upon.

    The Ministry of Manpower in December 2001 backed a call from the NWC for severe restraint or cuts in wages. Citing worsening economic conditions and uncertainty in 2002, the government urged companies to freeze or cut wages in the city-stateâs worst recession yet. A survey quoted in the Straits Times revealed that 18 percent of Singapore-based companies had already frozen pay levels in 2001 and 28 percent planned to do so in 2002. During the financial crisis in 1998, the NWC asked workers to accept wage cuts of between five and eight percent.

    At the height of the 1998 financial crisis, the government announced a blanket policy of cutting the employersâ share of monthly Central Provident Fund (CPF) payments to employees from 20 percent to 10 percent. This policy was aimed as a cost saving package to revive the economy but it placed the burden on ordinary wage earners and not on the stateâs coffers. This 10 percent cut was applied to all companies regardless of profitability and financial situation. It penalised workers even in companies that were breaking profit records. Overnight, many workers had trouble keeping up their mortgage payments that were serviced through the CPF. Instead they had to dig into their salaries to make up the difference. There was a public outcry and some quick fix measures were assembled to assist those who were unable to continue paying mortgages.

    It was then that the NWC recommended the monthly variable component (MVC), to allow companies to adjust monthly wage bills quickly to remain competitive and minimise job losses. This move exempted the government from involvement and becoming the target of bad publicity and collective unhappiness of the workers whose wages are cut. Over the years the number of companies that use the MVC has increased and wages in general have become more flexible. For instance the variable component of total wages was 15-16 percent in 2000 compared to 11 percent in 1997. The Ministry of Manpower is now seeking to speed up the implementation of the MVC even further and to promote performance-based pay structures to replace seniority-based systems. In addition, other components of the employment benefits system, including implementing medical co-payment and changing retrenchment compensation so that companies have greater flexibility in managing wage costs.

    The central issue in introducing and operating the MVC is how to define a justifiable cut in each industry, especially in large MNCs where profit transfer between countries could be easily carried out. Since financial situations and management capabilities vary from company to company, it is unclear whether the Ministry of Manpower and the National Trades Union Congress (NTUC) are sufficiently informed about the technical details of industries to function as a watchdog against unjust wage-cuts or to set out intelligent guidelines for a MVC cut.

    Major concerns are, how much to allow companies/government to cut the MVC? How to calculate and define a cut? Does a well managed economy require frequent MVC wage-cut to rank and file workers to keep employment levels? Can a company convert part of a workerâs present salary into MVC?

    As cited earlier, Singapore is the fifth most expensive city to live in. Of all components of cost, workersâ wages are not the highest contributing cost factor. Workersâ wage levels have actually fallen behind other Asian countries such as Japan, South Korea, and Hong Kong. If factors other than workersâ wages are the biggest contributors to high costs, it is not logical to pursue wage cuts as a priority. More should be done to lower government charges and duties.

    There is a concern that adjusting the workersâ wage structure for the sake of keeping business costs low may be abused, leaving workers more vulnerable. For instance, it is not usual for government wage surveys to show that since the Asian crisis wage growth has reached pre-crisis levels. However a comparison of pay increases of rank-and-file employees shows that throughout the 1990s average annual wage growth declined from eight to ten percent in the early half to between five and seven percent in the latter. This is largely due to attempts to keep business costs down, and wage cuts are one way of doing this.

    Employers are suspected of using the economic crisis as an excuse to restrain wage increases, creating an employerâs market. For instance, the starting salary of university graduates in the latter half of 2001 fell to pre-1997 secondary school-leaver levels. Without a minimum wage, employers, even when making profits, can offer graduates salaries below what used to be the graduate benchmark of $1,600.

    Wage issues remain problematic as they are not regulated by law. The NTUC is said to play a pragmatic role, but a tame one in the perception of some. Part of the problem is that the NTUC is aligned with the ruling Peopleâs Action Party (PAP) and therefore its independence is questionable. Changes arising from economic restructuring, which included job cuts and new forms of employment relationships, are unsettling workers and testing worker-employer relations. But the PAP wants to deepen the tripartite partnership between its administration, the employers, and the NTUC, to preserve âharmoniousâ industrial relations.

    The push for more MVC is in the name of minimising retrenchment in a volatile economy. The increasing attack on wages is explained in part as a result of a drop in workersâ productivity. So workers are presented as receiving less wages because they are less productive and not because the PAP administration has introduced a policy that penalises workers first.

    The MVC is a political solution to a political problem. It is of no help in saving jobs at all. It is unclear whether MNCs will stay in Singapore simply because they can cut wage costs by five to ten percent when wages constitute less than ten percent of total costs to most MNCs. Such cuts are increasingly painful for workers when the CPI does not fall in the same period. If workersâ wages are cut by more than ten percent without corresponding reductions in other living costs, more people will find it difficult to make ends meet.

    A thorough review of all other aspects of the Employment Act is thus urgently required to meet the fast changing job environment. It is worrisome that historically the government has a tendency to chant policies that favour large MNCs and government linked companies (GLC) and ignores the needs of small- and medium-sized enterprises (SME). Tremendous efforts have to be made to regain the balance where SMEs can co-exist with MNCs and GLCs for a more balanced and sustainable economy.

    Because Singapore is small, housing space, land use, transportation, water, and electric supply, must become more efficient. But they are increasingly costly and without subsidies. Additionally, health care, education, food, and recreation are also becoming expensive. There is increasing public pressure to review policies to include further concessions on state subsidies and relief.

    With increasing anxiety to make ends meet, more workers are treated for depression as Singapore slips into its deepest recession. The Institute of Mental Health reports that the number of patients has almost doubled since April 2001. Psychiatrists confirm that more people are now treated for depression. Men, especially 30 to 49 year-olds, feel the most heat in the current economic meltdown.

    The government has long rejected the notion of a welfare state, preferring short-term incentives to those in need to avoid relying on welfare. In Singapore, families shoulder part of the responsibility and more than 260 private welfare organisations take up the rest, some aided by public money. However the challenge is for the government to provide a safety net for people when the global economy is volatile, impacting the local one, and family and community support is gradually being eroded.

    In the run up to the general election in November 2001, the government announced budget measures that included tax relief and rebates for conservation charges and utilities. To boost PAP chances in the elections it introduced New Singapore Shares which is essentially a staggered cash handout. However, such rebates and handouts are not efficient ways to redistribute accumulated state wealth and are not targeted to reduce basic household needs or to create jobs. Most see them as short-term stop gap measures motivated by electoral politics.

    There has been some decentralised and delegated social service through the government administered Community Development Councils (CDC) aimed at identifying and helping the poor. Short-term loan schemes have also been introduced. The budget for CDCs grew from S$19 million in 1997 to S$153 million in 2001, largely due to social assistance schemes. But eligibility for government assistance is stringent so as not to encourage a dependence mentality. The Ministry of Community Development and Sports, together with other agencies link short-term financial assistance closely with job counselling, job seeking, training, and placement.

    However, short-term assistance is generally judged as not guaranteeing that the unemployed can become economically stable. Job placement services have on many occasions not been successful. A Jobs Task Force has been set up to give training, job assistance, and counselling to those affected by economic restructuring. There have also been promises of jobs in new âgrowth areasâ. But what âgrowth areasâ are, how many they will employ, and when, is not clear. Areas such as wafer fabrication and biotechnology that were celebrated as the way forward are high-tech and need specialists so their contribution to job creation is questionable. The promise that more Singaporean companies operating world-wide will facilitate Singaporeans to work overseas is also speculative. Most Singaporean companies overseas are GLCs and it is unclear how good they are at creating new jobs at home.

    In this context, there is a need to review the CPF scheme, to ensure that in addition to providing members with adequate savings for housing, medical, and retirement, that there is a scheme where some form of social security is available for sudden job loss and a means to tide over times when jobs are scarce. In the absence of new jobs for older and retrenched workers, rules need relaxing to allow the growth of a âregulated informal sectorâ. Singaporean workers need to be able to operate from home to keep costs down and at the same time to be gainfully employed to make a contribution to the economy and society.

    SINGAPORE

    By YAROSLAV TROFIMOV

    DURING rush hour in Singapore's ultramodern subway system, wrinkled old ladies squat on the underpass floor with handfuls of small tissue packets that they sell at one Singapore dollar, or 65 US cents, apiece.

    These are the poor of Singapore, left behind by the boom that has transformed this tropical island of 4.5 million people into a regional business, finance and technology hub. Poverty here isn't nearly as desperate as in its Asian neighbors, or even many Western societies. But it has become an increasingly sensitive political issue, prompting new tax and social policies that the government is expected to unveil in the annual budget presentation Feb 15.

    According to official statistics, at least 40% of Singapore's households saw their real incomes decrease from 2000-2005 -- while the overall economy posted some of the world's highest growth rates, surging by 7.7% last year.

    "The question is, who is all this growth for?" says Manu Bhaskaran, an adjunct fellow at Singapore's Institute of Policy Studies and a partner at the Centennial Group, an advisory firm. "Shouldn't the government be aiming for lower growth, but better distribution? "

    The government's strategy so far is to pursue a balancing act of generating more revenue for helping the poor without scaring off the global investors who have long been attracted by the city-state's low-tax regime. "The dual objective is to ensure that the Singaporean economy continues to remain competitive, and to strengthen the inclusiveness of Singaporean society," says Lim Swee Say, secretary-general of the National Trades Union Congress and a government minister.

    Most of Singapore's poor are in their 50s or older, and many are onetime manufacturing workers who lost their jobs to cheaper competitors in China, Vietnam or India. Many lack education and English-language skills that are needed to prosper in expanding sectors such as services and finance.

    It is only in recent months that senior officials began to speak out on the problem of structural poverty, recognizing the potential perils for a multiracial, multireligious society such as Singapore's. "It is essential for us to tilt the balance in favor of lower-income Singaporeans, because globalization is going to strain our social compact," Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong said in a speech last fall.

    Singapore's People's Action Party, founded by Mr Lee's father Lee Kuan Yew -- who is still a senior government member with the title of Minister Mentor -- has controlled the island since independence in 1965, winning nearly every seat in parliamentary elections. The country's mixed population of Chinese, Malays and Indians has accepted decades of the PAP's often authoritarian rule in exchange for phenomenal economic growth that has transformed this former British trading outpost into one of the world's most prosperous states.

    While providing subsidized housing and health care, Singapore's government has long resisted calls for European-style unemployment benefits for the poor, describing welfare as a "dirty word" and rejecting the idea of a minimum wage. Instead, the government has come up with the concept of "workfare" -- a large-scale program to top off the incomes of those Singaporeans who accept low-paid work. Details of the program will likely be given in the budget presentation.

    "We can't stop the income gap from widening because the wages are determined by the global competition for knowledge workers, and by the excessive supply of unskilled workers," says Mr. Lim, the minister and union leader. "But what we're trying to do is to make sure that the widening income gap doesn't necessarily translate into a widening social gap."

    Most developed economies fund such social programs by redistributing wealth through progressive taxation: the top-bracket federal income tax rate is 35% in the US, and at least 40% in most of Western Europe. But Singapore is ideologically opposed to this model: the island-state has been steadily slashing its top income-tax rate, from 55% at independence in 1965 to 28% in 2000, to 20% today. Making up for the shortfall in revenue, Singapore established a general sales tax, currently at 5%.

    It is the sales tax that the government is planning to tap again to pay for the "workfare" initiative. According to Mr Lee, with the new budget the sales tax is likely to rise to 7% -- a measure that, economists calculate, will generate some S$1.5 billion of extra government revenue.

    The plan is far from popular. In the two months since Mr Lee in November first mentioned a likely sales-tax rise, the proposal elicited unusually vocal criticism from Singapore's corporate world and opposition leaders alike. The influential Singapore Chinese Chamber of Commerce and Industry complained the sales-tax increase is "likely to have an immediate and detrimental effect on local spending," undermining Singapore's long-term competitiveness.

    Sylvia Lim, leader of the Singapore Workers' Party and an opposition Parliament member, also pointed out that the poor -- the ostensible beneficiaries of the proposal -- would be hurt the most. "We need to strengthen our safety nets through some redistributive policies," she asserted, adding that consumption taxes, such as the sales tax, "are generally regressive and likely to compound the underclass problem."

    While the government usually ignores the minuscule opposition in this tightly regulated country, the business community's pleas found a receptive audience. Aware that corporate taxes are even lower in rival business centers such as Hong Kong and Ireland, Singapore signaled it would sweeten the pill for businesses: To compensate for the higher sales tax, the corporate tax rate, currently 20%, would be shaved by at least one percentage point.

    "This is a tough and competitive world," Minister Mentor Lee told Singaporean journalists as he explained the planned corporate-tax cut. "People don't come here because they like Singapore -- they come because the returns are better."
