    I REFER to the proposed GST increase from 5 to 7 per cent, to provide for those who are poor. There is indeed a pressing need to help lower-income Singaporeans caught in the globalisation tide.
    Being a small-business owner, I can attest to this. I have many employees who earn less than $1,000 in basic salary and are the sole breadwinners (One such staff member hails from a family of 11). It is heart-rending hearing them bemoan the cost of living, utility bills, household expenses, etc. Much as I would like to pay them more, it is not possible to do so without affecting cost and the business' survival.

    The minimum-wage argument is not tenable. Should employers be forced to pay a minimum wage, many like myself would choose either not to hire because we cannot afford it, or not to set up shop in Singapore. This will result in lesser employment.

    I am glad the Government has heeded the call to take care of the lower-income. Workfare is unique to Singapore and is a far better principle than welfare. Under workfare, hardworking Singaporeans are given a chance to level up whereas welfare destroys all incentive to work.

    It will take some time for Singaporeans to accept the GST increase. I urge everyone to take a measured and rational approach and to support it as it is designed primarily to help poorer fellow citizens. In order to make the reason for the GST hike more persuasive, I suggest the Prime Minister gives a full account of the two-point increase by Budget 2007 - what is the exact dollar value and how much of each dollar will go into workfare.

    Theodore Yeo Guan Huat

    (Straits Times 21 Nov)

    What do you think are the reasons that small businesses can't pay a higher wage rate?

    It's "their policy" of taxing the lower and middle income earners more instead of the
    higher income earners

    Higher income earners have more disposable income, if they make a million a year, after
    taxes of 20%, they will be left with $800,000 a year. They won't spend everything, they
    will most likely save a large substantial portion of this. High propensity to save (save
    more)

    Low income earners have less disposable income, if they make $20,000 a year, after CPF
    contribution, they will be left with $16,000, with the $16,000 they have left, they
    are most likely to spend everything on perishables and necessities. After the GST, they
    will only have like $15,238 left. Low propensity to save (save less).

    When you spend more and save less, you tend to stimulate the economy.
    Assume you save only 5% (low income earners) of your income.

    ABC spends $100 in DEF's shop, DEF spends $95 ($100 x 95%) in GHI's shop, GHI spends
    $90.25 ($95 x 95%) in JKL's shop, JKL spends $85.74 ($90.25 x 95%) in MNO's shop, ....

    If you add up total income = $100 + $95 + $90.25 + $85.74...............

    It will add up to $2,000 = $100 / 0.05

    When you perform this with a savings of 50% (high income earners).

    You will get $200 = $100 / 0.50

    This means less income will be generated.

    The growth of our economy was based on Solow's model of savings and growth. That's why
    CPF still remained despite the completion of our MRT project. But as the economy matures
    to a stage, I think it would be counter productive to have too much savings.

    Pre-mature economy (low savings):

    1 computer (capital stock) to 1,000 people

    Mature economy (balanced savings):

    1,000 computer (capital stock) to 1,000 people

    Mature economy (high savings):

    1,000,000 computer (capital stock) to 1,000 people

    Having too much capital will bring diminishing returns, because you can't utilise the
    capital efficiently. That's why you see earlier in the year, Singtel did a share buyback,
    so they can return excess capital to shareholders as they can't find good investments.

    I don't understand why they always have to have a budget surplus, sometimes a balanced
    budget, or a slight budget deficit will be more favourable. I think it should only be right
    that you tax the higher income earners, as they make more money, they should return to
    the society what they have taken.

    Maybe to enticed multi-national corporations here, they can use tax incentives.
    Instead of cutting the top bracket of income tax to 20% for all income tax payers.
    They should distinguish between public listed company (with a certain capital and turnover)
    and non listed (privately owned enterprises)
