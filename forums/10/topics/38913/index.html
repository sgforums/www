    U.N. Envoy: N. Korea Preparing for War
    1 hour, 7 minutes ago

    By JOE McDONALD, Associated Press Writer

    BEIJING - North Korea (news - web sites) is preparing for possible war with the United States and is concerned about Washington's intentions after its attack on Iraq (news - web sites), said a U.N. envoy who returned Saturday from the North.

    North Korean officials say missile tests and other recent steps that have unsettled its neighbors were meant as preparation for such a conflict, said Maurice Strong, a Canadian aide to Secretary General Kofi Annan (news - web sites). Strong was visiting Pyongyang as part of U.N. efforts to mediate the North's nuclear crisis with the United States.

    South Korea (news - web sites) put its military on heightened alert this week, and on Saturday the North retaliated by canceling economic talks scheduled for next week. That could mean it will also suspend more important inter-Korean talks next month aimed at easing nuclear tensions.

    The United States says it wants a peaceful settlement to the dispute. But the U.S. war in Iraq is getting intense study from North Korean officials, Strong told reporters.

    "They are watching it very carefully and with deep concern, and questioning what this means in terms of the U.S. ultimate intentions toward them," Strong said.

    Asked whether North Korean leaders feared they would be the next target of U.S. military action after Iraq, Strong said, "Fear I do not believe is in their vocabulary. Concern, yes, real determination to seek a peaceful settlement. At the same time, preparation for war, if necessary."

    Strong did not say specify how he knew about the military preparations, and gave no details.

    He said officials expressed "deep concern for the threats that they perceive to their own security, and a determination to defend their security and their integrity."

    Tensions on the Korean Peninsula have been high since Washington said in October that the North had admitted starting a nuclear weapons program in violation of a 1994 agreement.

    Strong said he met with "very senior people," but wouldn't give their names or other details. He said he was conveying "guidance and information" between the two sides but couldn't discuss it before he reports to Annan.

    Both Washington and Pyongyang have said they want a peaceful settlement, and Strong said North Korean officials expressed optimism that one was still possible.

    But Washington has rejected the North's request for direct talks, saying instead other governments must be involved Â— a condition that Pyongyang has rejected.

    "Until those discussions occur, the risks that the process will degenerate, without the intent of either party, into a conflict still remain," Strong said.

    Washington and Pyongyang, which fought on opposite sides in the 1950-53 Korean War, have never had diplomatic relations.

    With the United States focused on Iraq, experts fear North Korea might use the opportunity to test a long-range missile or reprocess spent nuclear fuel to make atomic bombs. That would be viewed as an attempt to force Washington into direct negotiations.

    Asked whether North Korea might try to use tensions over Iraq to try to force a compromise, Strong said, "I don't think forcing the U.S. is anything they realistically consider. They want the U.S. to engage them in direct negotiations."

    The two Koreas had planned to begin talks in Pyongyang Wednesday to discuss ways of boosting economic cooperation. Topics included opening territorial waters to commercial ships from each side.

    The South put its military on heightened alert Thursday, saying it wanted to guard against any effort by Pyongyang to raise tensions during the Iraq war. The North reacted by calling off the meeting.

    "When our dialogue partner is threatening us with a dagger, we have no option but to conclude that we must delay the talks," said Pyongyang's state-run Central Radio, which was monitored by South Korea's Yonhap news agency.

    Asian nations jittery over North Korea
    Charles Whelan
    Friday, 21 March 2003

    AS THE United States focuses on war against Iraq, jittery Asian nations are casting a wary eye on North Korea, to many a more clear and present danger than Saddam Hussein.

    During its five-month stand-off with the United States, North Korea has ramped up tension at regular intervals through action and rhetoric.

    Threatening to turn South Korea into a "land of ashes", firing off missiles into the Sea of Japan and predicting "nuclear war at any moment", the Stalinist North Korea has maintained a level of menace that has unnerved the region and exasperated the United States.

    A consensus is growing among analysts that North Korea will seize on the Iraq war as a chance to raise the stakes further, seeking to force the unwilling United States to bow to its demands by perhaps testing a ballistic missile or even carrying out a nuclear test.

    Japan and South Korea have called for stepped-up vigilance, both fearing North Korea's next move, and China, without singling out North Korea, has cautioned against any steps that would escalate the crisis. US Secretary of State Colin Powell warned Pyongyang on Tuesday, March 18, that provocation during the coming war would be a mistake.

    "It should be clear to North Koreans right now that while we look at these provocations with concern they are not going to provoke us into a bad policy choice and the world should be united on this one," he said in Washington.

    North Korea sees matters differently, and what Washington views as provocation, Pyongyang sees as a bargaining tool.

    "Expect a lot more bellicose talk and more efforts to raise tension," said Yu Suk-Ryul, of the Institute of Foreign Affairs and National Security, affiliated to the South Korean Foreign Ministry.

    On the day Bush gave Saddam 48 hours to quit Baghdad or face war, Pyongyang called on South Koreans to join with the North to launch a "vigorous struggle" to expel US troops from South Korea.

    Tension has been building on the Korean peninsula since last October when the United States accused North Korea of running a secret nuclear-weapons program based on enriched uranium in contravention of a 1994 agreement.

    The Pyongyang government has demanded face-to-face talks with the United States to defuse the crisis, but Washington insists the stand-off can be solved only in multilateral talks.

    North Korea has ramped up pressure on Washington to come to the negotiating table by expelling UN inspectors, firing up a reactor at its mothballed Yongbyon nuclear plant, withdrawing from the Non-Proliferation Treaty and firing missiles into the Sea of Japan.

    North Korean fighters intercepted a US spy plane in international waters off the North Korean coast in another escalation of the crisis.

    Seeking the highest price from the United States in the form of concessions for scrapping its weapons drive, North Korea will watch the unfolding Iraq conflict ready to seize any chance to drive a harder bargain, according to a South Korean government analyst.

    "North Korea has a few more cards to play. And the remaining cards are more serious than the ones used before," says the analyst, who would not be named.

    He says North Korea will quickly choose one of a number of options: test-firing a ballistic missile, starting to reprocess plutonium from spent fuel rods, launching a border skirmish, or even carrying out a nuclear test.

    Professor Yu says the longer the war lasts, the better for Pyongyang.

    "Maybe they will start reprocessing spent fuel to produce nuclear weapons.

    That is a big card for North Korea, and I am convinced they are going to use it,'' Professor Yu said. ''Then they will just let the tension rise, one notch at a time." AFP

    N. Korea likely to exploit war in Iraq, analysts warn

    By Michael Zielenziger
    Knight Ridder Newspapers
    March 21, 2003

    SEOUL, South Korea -- The U.S.-led war against Iraq is likely to speed North Korea's efforts to develop nuclear warheads or launch another provocative act to force Washington to the bargaining table, analysts here warned Thursday.

    Reflecting the government's consensus, a senior foreign ministry official, who asked not to be named, said: "Some sort of brinkmanship to induce negotiations" is likely from secretive North Korea leader Kim Jong Il now that American missiles are targeting Saddam Hussein. "They want to accelerate the pace of dialogue."

    The remarks underscored a widespread fear that by focusing U.S. military and diplomatic efforts on Iraq, the Bush administration is ignoring a far more dangerous threat to U.S. security. Bush so far has refused to engage North Korea in direct negotiations.

    Analysts predict that the North Koreans might even set off an underground nuclear test to step up the pressure on the United States and make clear to the world that it possesses nuclear weapons and must be taken seriously.

    Whatever the tactic, North Korea is expected to exploit the situation in Iraq.

    "For the time being, the North Koreans, while threatening to test-fire missiles, will wait and see, gleefully gloating over the sight of the Bush administration becoming an odd man out in the Western camp," said Kim Myong-cho, a Tokyo-based researcher with close ties to Pyongyang. "The end of the Iraq war will find the North Korean nuclear arsenal expanding," while U.S. support is draining away, he predicted.

    "There's a lot we don't know about North Korea and what it might do," cautioned Robert Gallucci, the former State Department official who negotiated the 1994 Agreed Framework with Pyongyang under which it stopped developing plutonium for nuclear warheads.
