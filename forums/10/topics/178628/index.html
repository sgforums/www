    I feel that Hitler was an idiot to have attacked Russia. His reasons for attacking Russia aren't at all compelling - he simply regarded the Russians as 'subhuman' and that his people needed more 'lebensraum' (living space) if they wanted to be successful economically.

    If he had been less bigoted and had a better understanding of basic economic theory, he certainly would have see the madness of invading Russia.

    I remember reading a pretty interesting book (forgot the title) about what Hitler should have done to win WWII. Instead of invading Russia, he should have just reinforced Rommel in Africa. Even with the limited resources available to him, the Afrika Korps came pretty close to winning. If they had been properly reinforced, I don't see why Rommel win and capture the Suez Canal, along with the whole of North Africa.

    At the same time, he should have invaded Malta, whose strategic location was harassing Rommel's supply line. Also, he should have ordered an airborne invasion of Gibraltar at the same time. If Gibraltar's too small for that, Rommel can just set up lots of artillery on the western end of North Africa. Either way, the Straits of Gibraltar would have been blockaded.

    With the Rock of Gibraltar and the Suez Canal captured, the Mediterranean is all theirs.

    Once they've consolidated their gains, they could have rolled on to the Middle East and capture the oil there. The Brits would be pressed to intervene, but any reinforcement of the scarce military resources they've place there would take months to arrive now that Gibraltar and Suez have been captured.

    Heck, after that Hitler could have coordinated with the Japs and conducted a twin-pronged invasion of India. Once again, India can't be reinforced easily because of the loss of Gibraltar and Suez. Down goes the British Empire's crown jewel.

    I know the scenario I've just described sounds simplistic, but hey, history is filled with 'what ifs'.

    Adolf Hitler should have been more diligent in school, and not miss out in the interesting history of Europe.

    He will probably have picked up the disaster that fell on Napolean Bonaparte's expedition into Russia occurred during late summer of 1812 and unexpectedly stretched into the bitter cold Russian winter.

    Adolf Hitler's invasion of Russia had also began in the summer of 1940, and can only lead to the same scenario that befell on Napolean a hundred years earlier.

    Extracted from the BBC referenced material "History - Hitler's Invasion of Russia in World War Two"

    In the summer of 1940 Adolf Hitler, despite his swift and dramatic victory over France, faced a major military and political problem. The British would not do what seemed logical and what the FÃ¼hrer expected - they would not make peace. Yet Hitler was frustrated by geography - in the shape of the English Channel - from following his immediate instincts and swiftly crushing the British just as he had the French.

    Hitler did in fact order preparations to be made for an invasion of England, but he was always half-hearted in his desire to mount a large seaborne landing. Germany, unlike Britain, was not a sea power and the Channel was a formidable obstacle. Even if air superiority could be gained, there remained the powerful British Navy. And there was another, ideological, reason why Hitler was not fully committed to invading Britain. For him, it would have been a distraction. Britain contained neither the space, nor the raw materials, that he believed the new German Empire needed. And he admired the British - Hitler often remarked how much he envied their achievement in subjugating India.

    Worse, if the Germans let themselves be drawn into a risky amphibious operation against a country Hitler had never wanted as an enemy, every day the potential threat from his greatest ideological opponent would be growing stronger. (It was just ironic that he was not yet at war with this perceived enemy, since in August 1939 Germany and the Soviet Union had signed a Non-Aggression Pact.)

    All this meant that, from Hitler's point of view, there was an alternative to invading Britain: he could invade the Soviet Union. Both Hitler and his military planners knew that Germany's best chance of victory was for the war in Europe to be finished swiftly.

    Hubert Menzel was a major in the General Operations Department of the OKH (the Oberkommando des Heers, the German Army headquarters), and for him the idea of invading the Soviet Union in 1941 had the smack of cold, clear logic to it: 'We knew that in two years' time, that is by the end of 1942, beginning of 1943, the English would be ready, the Americans would be ready, the Russians would be ready too, and then we would have to deal with all three of them at the same time.... We had to try to remove the greatest threat from the East.... At the time it seemed possible.'

    (The above paragraphs are taken from chapter one of 'War of the Century' by Laurence Rees, published by BBC Publications, 1999.)

The real fact was that the Russians did mass their forces at the borders, but in defensive positions and not offensive, this despite Red Army intel insisting the Wehrmacht were preparing to mount a sustained campaign of agression against them.

    Stalin's complacency in the Red Army's capabilities and the transitional period of defensive preparation in which the Wehrmacht caught the Russians literally with their 'pants down', contributed significantly to Babarrossa's early success.

    Subsequent mass encircling tactics by the highly mechanised German Army Groups A and B enabled them to sustain their lightning thrusts into Russian territory with minimal effort, resulting in huge losses for Russia in both troops and weapons.
