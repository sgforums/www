    I would like to add my two cents' worth:

    A Republic is NOT a political SYSTEM. It is the arrangement of political leaders and heads-of-state. It is the manner a country decides to organize its political unit. Apollo said:
    republic - a political system in which the supreme power lies in a body of citizens who can elect people to represent them

    But your definition could just as well define the word 'democracy'
    democracy -a political system in which the supreme power lies in a body of citizens who can elect people to represent them

    So is a republic a democracy? The answer is obviously an emphatic NO since many non-democratic countries are republics as Silverkris pointed out earlier.

    Examples of political systems include: democracy, theocracy, monarchy, stratocracy, plutocracy, oligarchy etc. "Republic" is not one of them. It may also interest you that for political scientists, academia and economists, communism is less a political system, than an economic system. Many users in this forum use 'communism' loosely of course when referring to the "communist governments" in China, Cuba or North Korea.

    I used the dictionary as well:

    reÂ·pubÂ·lic ( P ) Pronunciation Key (r-pblk)

    A political order whose head of state is not a monarch and in modern times is usually a president.
    A nation that has such a political order.

    A political order in which the supreme power lies in a body of citizens who are entitled to vote for officers and representatives responsible to them.

    Both meanings appear in all 4 dictionaries I looked up even though you say "the first definition rarely comes up in the dictionary." And then you appear to contradict yourself when you then go on to add "however, internationally, republic is just plain old, "a non- monarchial type of country." as if to suggest the first meaning is the more common and regular one too.
    The differences in meanings between 'system' and 'order' are subtle but crucial. The two do not mean the same thing if one is pedantic.

    I think not, and that was what Silverkris was trying to point out. Your second post and what you purport to claim comes out of the blue. A democracy is really about having the right to vote for leaders in an election. It's less about voting for 'issues'. I think what you were trying to refer to is a 'referendum'. As you acknowledged yourself in the second post, in a democracy, elected leaders are given the responsibility to decide on issues in the interests of the country. They do not leave to the citizens who elected them to decide on every issue. However if they do, they hold a referendum.

    Even in democracies, national referendums are RARELY held. Those which have occurred are politically significant like voting to join the EU (many East European countries), or voting to become independent (East Timor), or voting to become a republic (Australia).

    Lastly, the origins of the Democratic and Republican parties have little to do with their literal meanings. For the Republican party in the US, it was borne out of deep-seated beliefs in liberalism and anti-slavery in the mid-1850s.
