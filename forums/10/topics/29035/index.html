    call me skeptical but i still think its overkill...

    yes, SAF suffers from shortfall of manpower, but are u sure that they require TWICE the number they have now???

    it will be expensive, unnecessary and inefficient.

    i think the the current shortfall is more due to inefficiency in managing human resource than actual quantitative shortfall. if this inefficiency persists, i don't see how inceasing the quantity of manpower available by 100% will actually lead to more effective operation.

    what is the problem ultimately? one of the issues is that too many irresponsible guys are pulling a fast one and getting downgraded.

    on top of that, MINDEF seems to have a peculiar habit of not allocating manpower according to their physical capabilities.

    hence, we have PES A clerks who do nothing all day, and PES C pending riflemen who just might crash out halfway through their term of service.

    attrition due to training incidents are not that high actually... it depends on where u are but thats nothing we can do about. making safety more of a concern then it already is now would make training unrealistic and ineffective. NS is not a boys scout camp.

    u can't post in a new rifleman halfway through a unit's training cycle... it happens sometimes, but is that effective?

    the law of comparative advantage states that resources (be they human or material) are seldom perfectly substitutable. hence, increasing the pool of manpower to include women won't actually remedy the situation.

    u want a meaner, leaner army, the way to go is to maximise the productivity of the organisation so that enough people do the work much better and people are allocated work which they are capable of performing effectively. u can't just add more people and expect productivity to rise accordingly.

    in the same way, how would increasing the pool of people to include females help if they are to be allocated to non-combat vocations IF it is combat vocations who require the most manpower?

    the men freed up from non-combat vocations may not actually be fit for combat vocations in the first place.

    so what happens? u end up with TWICE as many clerks and medics and storemen and still not enough riflemen...

    and opening combat vocations to females would open another can of worms altogether... even the US is not too thrilled to do that cos of psychological effects women in combat might have to their male peers.

    not that i'm a sexist misogynists, but these are very real issues involved when contemplating NS for women.

    either way, there is no real need unless u're contemplating issues like nation building or equal opportunities.

    than again, would anyone contemplate providing equal opportunities if its gonna cost a lot more money and have not much to show for?

    Here's another:

    Fertility rates could be affected
    I REFER to Mr Han Yong Yuan's letter, 'Women should do national service as well' (ST, Nov 15).

    At first, I agreed that in the interests of equality of the sexes and giving women the same benefits men enjoy, women should take on some national service responsibility.

    But such a move should be taken with extreme caution and forethought.

    As it is, we are facing a severe fertility crisis because working professional women are putting off getting married and getting pregnant in favour of establishing a career first.

    I believe that the median age of marriage is increasing, as is the age of those giving birth for the first time. Indeed, the number of childless couples, from personal experience as well as reports, is quite worrying, spawning a multi-million-dollar industry in fertility treatments.

    If national service were to be implemented for women, it must be for at least 12 months for it to have any meaningful effect on Singapore as well as themselves.

    Then, we would be looking at an additional delay before these women get married and become mothers.

    If because of higher qualifications, like the A levels for men, the NS stint is extended by six months, the delay in bearing children would be further extended.

    This could mean that when these women finally want to have children, they could run into fertility problems associated with conception at non-optimal ages, which they may not have encountered a mere 12 months earlier.

    Singapore could be faced with a worsening baby shortage.

    Looking at the current situation, we do not have the critical mass of population that would be able to absorb any delay in producing of babies in favour of implementing national service for women, no matter how fair it may seem.

    MICHAEL LOH YIK MING
