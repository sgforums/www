  • BillyBong
    8 Jul 05, 17:37
    If either the defence or prosecution appeals the sentence, the matter is referred to the court of appeals, in which the CJ sits together with 2 other presiding judges.

    They will review the case files and determine if there is any grounds for the appeal. Alternatively, they can increase the sentence if they deem it insufficent. Thus its a gamble for either side should an appeal be lodged.

    This appeal only includes existing evidence. If new evidence is introduced, the relevant side can apply for a new trial. It will not go to the courst of appeals.

    This is essentially an issue of the separation of powers. For the esablishment and maintenance of political liberty the government be divided into three branches, the legislature, the executive and the judiciary. Each branch of the government must be confined to the exercise of its own function and not allowed to enroach upon the functions of other branches. i.e. the judiciary must function independent of the executive.
    Furthermore, the persons who compose these three agencies of government must be kept separate and distinct, no individual being allowed to be at the same time a member of more than one branch. In this way each of the branches will be a check to the others and no single group of people will be able to contol the machinery of the state.
    The separation of powers is clearly committed to a view of political liberty, an essential part of which is the restraint of governmental power.

    Some systems of government exhibit a great deal more in the way of separation of powers than our own. In the United States the president as chief executive, the congress as legislator and the supreme court as the most important repository of the judicial power. All perform very different functions and each acts as a check upon the excesses of the others.
    Able and ambitious people will be attracted to positions in the various branches of Government. They will all want to do their jobs well, to the limits of the power which resides in the office. Tensions will arise. Those tensions will be healthy because they cause the behaviour of one branch of Government to be modified by the attitudes of others. Power will be spread around and shared. The chance to be free from concentrations of arbitrarily exercised power is increased.
    Complete fusion of power tend to make control of the Government by the people impossible and would tend to produce tyrannical government. Governmental power can be made less oppressive by dividing it up between various branches.
