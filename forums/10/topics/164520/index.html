    'Us vs them' view of students disturbing GEP a boon for talented pupils

    Tuesday Â• November 22, 2005

    Letter from Kelly Koh Ben-Lin
    Letter from Chaitali Tarafdar

    In her zealous defence of Gifted Education Programme (GEP) students, Michelle Qiu ("A second look at the gift", Nov 21) displays a sense of, at best, brash arrogance and, at worst, condescension and a superiority complex.

    While I do not want to believe she represents the typical GEP student, it would be worrying if many "GEPers" (as they call themselves) had such an "us-them" mentality.

    Aside from proclaiming "mainstreamers" (what a patronising term!) to be immature, she is thrilled the programme allowed her to interact exclusively with "people like us".

    For a "gifted" student, she is quick to make sweeping generalisations that "everyone" in the mainstream follows trends, almost insinuating that "ungifted" people have no minds of their own.

    It is fine if Michelle and her group of like-minded friends continue to live in their ivory towers, associating only with members of this chosen fraternity, keeping out of the lives of the "mainstream". Unfortunately, many may end up with scholarships and eventually hold public office, where their policies will affect the "mainstream" and the "masses".

    I shudder to think what kind of empathy there can be when, from the onset, these young people display such an elitist "us" versus "them" mentality.

    This is probably an alarmist scenario, but picture this: A group of key decision makers decide that the general population is rather immature and cannot possibly have minds of their own.

    As such, everyone with an IQ below 150 should be systematically eradicated. Such a mentality would please Adolf Hitler.

    My daughter will join the GEP in January. The GEP branch sent us materials highlighting the curriculum. The briefing was also elaborate, giving a clear idea that students would have to work hard.

    I asked my daughter if she was still willing to go for it Â— she enthusiastically and repeatedly nodded.

    Five other parents I know whose children are joining the course likewise did not pressure their children. Another parent of a child who is in his third year of GEP says he enjoys it.

    Strangely, though the programme is criticised as a heavy burden for small shoulders, there are many siblings in the programme. If parents felt it was a nightmare, it is unlikely they would allow another child to enroll.

    As Singaporeans, it is easy to overlook the plight of talented young people in other countries who, lacking the chance to nurture their talents, are forced to leave their homelands. Here, talented children are identified and nurtured.

    So let us try to see the merits of the programme, rather than only the demerits.
