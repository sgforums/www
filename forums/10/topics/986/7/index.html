    I think the cobra is kinda confused with a similar manuever. I forgot the name but this is how it goes.

    The flanker first enters a steep climb, 90 degrees vertical. Once that is established, cuts the engines down to idle, slowing the climb. It will reach a point where there is not enough thrust to climb, and it actually slides back down on its tail. The nose dips, heading down towards the ground, re-engaging the engines back to full military.

    This maneuver can be used to temporarily confuse a tracking AWACs. They might think that the aircraft stalled while attemptin to climb.

    However, the point in all this, is not demonstrating how the manuevers are useful in combat. But rather, how the aircraft can withstand high angles of attack without stalling. This allows the aircraft to turn tighter at lower airspeeds.

    TVC aside, most western aircraft can only turn at a certain rate. The F-16 bein one that can turn the fastest, with minimal loss of airspeed. Once the limit is reached, the F-16 cannot continue to increase its turn rate without runnin the risk of losing all its airspeed and stalling. The Su-27 and the MiG-29 were designed to push the limit. The had high AOA tolerances, to allow to push the turn rate beyond the limits of conventional aircraft, without stalling.

    If you were an F-16 pilot, looking back, u will suddenly realise the flanker managed to actually "OUT TURN" you beyond the physics of conventional flight. This being a normal sustained turning flight. Combined with a simple High G Yo Yo, the flanker can almost out turn the F-16 easily. ( of course, the F-16 pilot can counter with a High-G Yoyo of his own.)
