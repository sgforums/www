    Handcuffs will leave a stigma for life
    Treatment should be commensurate with severity of offence
    Tuesday Â• November 15, 2005

    Letter from Narayana Narayana
    Letter from Jimmy Ho Kwok Hoong

    Caught at the checkpoint with less than a three-quarter full tank of petrol, reader Lawrence Seow Kuan Yong (Nov 11) only agreed to pay the $50 composition fine for first-time offenders after he was told that if he refused, he would have to undergo the necessary procedures to be charged in court Â— such as being handcuffed and fingerprinted at the police station.

    Readers have since weighed in on the debate as to whether minor offences warrant the handcuff treatment.

    Mr Lionel de Souza pre-empts the official reply with his letter "Fear cuffs? Obey rules" (Nov 14).

    With due respect to his experience in the Police Force, he voices what is probably the official view, and the law, that "regardless of the severity of the offence, an accused person should be handcuffed".

    Although there have been rare cases of "an accused person who escapes", his days of freedom can be limited at best.

    Mr de Souza does not seem to feel much empathy for the common people who view the "mata-mata" with a sense of dread, even if they have done no wrong or have no cause to fear the police.

    Any individual's hard-built reputation can be shattered if he or she is led away by the police, even without the mandatory handcuffs. Even a subsequent verdict of innocence is unlikely to wipe away the stigma entirely.

    I recall my similar experience with Singapore Customs more than 10 years ago. Then, the rule called for half a tank of petrol, which I ran short of for the first time.

    A customs officer led me to the administration office where I waited for 45 minutes on a couch while my wife and children waited in my car, wondering what was happening.

    Two customs officers handling my case were chit-chatting instead of working. When I finally lost my patience, I stood up and started reading some papers pinned on a notice board Â— but was told by one officer to sit still and "behave" myself.

    I count myself lucky that I had accepted to pay the $50 fine outright.

    The law seems to reflect that our system does not have the patience and time to deal with petty offences committed, deliberately or not, by ordinary Singaporeans.

    It seems like anyone who is reluctant to accept a composition penalty and wants to appeal may be strongly discouraged with the prospect of being handcuffed and locked up.

    Possibly, such measures exist to ensure that the police and the courts are not unnecessarily bothered.

    Mr de Souza's rationale that it is safer to handcuff everyone accused of committing a minor offence "in case he is guilty of a serious crime" is absurd.

    The treatment an accused person receives must be commensurate with the severity of the alleged offence.

    We don't go around castrating all Singapore men just to prevent the rise in rape cases!
