    Jan 18, 2005
    Strong economy but jobless still struggling

    MPs afraid the unemployed will grow disgruntled and cause rift in society
    By Li Xueying

    EVEN as the economy grew by a healthy 8.1 per cent last year, there are still many 'heart-breaking' stories of Singaporeans struggling to find jobs, two Members of Parliament said yesterday.

    Both urged the Government to redouble its efforts to keep its focus on 'jobs, jobs, jobs' lest the problem of the unemployed, especially the long-term or chronically jobless, leads to further divisions within society.

    One of those who made this call was Dr Amy Khor (Hong Kah GRC), who said that jobs remained the persistent worry of the average worker.

    The other who raised the issue at yesterday's parliamentary debate of the President's Address was fellow Hong Kah GRC MP John Chen.

    Opposition MP Low Thia Khiang (Hougang) also spoke about the plight of the poor.

    Dr Khor, who is mayor of the Southwest Community Development Council, reported seeing an increase in the number of elderly job applicants with little family support and savings.

    'Some have been unable to find a job even after repeated job matching attempts, although the economy has picked up and more jobs are available,' said Dr Khor. 'These are the chronic umemployable. Some of their circumstances are quite heart-breaking.'

    Dr Chen said the improving economy, which grew on the back of an expansion of the high-tech sectors, had left some groups of Singaporeans, especially the older and lower-educated workers, out in the cold.

    'The feel-good factor is still absent among many, especially those who could not keep up with the progress of the knowledge economy,' he said. 'The lack of this feel-good factor, despite our good growth, is a real concern.'

    With many lower-skilled jobs permanently lost to low-wage countries, the spectre of structural unemployment and under-employment will haunt them for a long time, he added.

    'Even those who currently hold a job but are less skilled constantly fear losing their jobs,' he said.

    If they are not helped to remain employed and employable, there would be growing disaffection between them and those who can cope with the demands of the changing economy, warned Dr Chen.

    'If we manage this divide poorly, the resulting disaffection will cause our society potential problems in the future,' he said. 'National unity is at stake.'

    The two PAP MPs suggested a slew of solutions.

    First, redesign and upgrade low-skilled jobs. Employers in sectors such as construction, hotel and shipyard should follow the lead of the cleaning industry, where automation has helped increase cleaners' productivity and salaries.

    Second, help workers start small businesses such as hawker stalls, or 'work-at-home' trades such as baby-sitting or telephone services.

    Third, provide the unemployed with social assistance till they find a job.

    And to give the efforts a time frame, the Government should set a target to bring the current unemployment rate of 3.5 per cent 'down to, say, below 3 per cent within a year', Dr Chen said.

    Also tackling the subject of the poor, Mr Low, speaking in Mandarin, criticised the Government for not providing what he said was a 'proper or systematic social safety net to protect the poor people, the workers'.

    Likening the New Singapore Shares and the Economic Restructuring Shares to 'a cup of water to douse a large fire', he said the Government should review its social welfare system to decrease the overall burden of living costs for the people.

    He said the President's Address dwelled on 'utopian visions' but glossed over the livelihood problems of the 'new poor' - Singaporeans living from hand to mouth.

    'If the Government neglects this problem and talks about the so-called promised land, what is the meaning of all this?' he asked.

    However, Mr Seng Han Thong (Ang Mo Kio GRC), who also spoke in Mandarin, said Mr Low had been 'critical, but has not provided any specific solutions himself'.

    He reminded Mr Low that the Government had indeed addressed Singaporeans' concerns, including by working with employers to redesign jobs in sectors such as horticulture, health care and precision engineering to encourage the unemployed to take them up.

    The NTUC also had a range of schemes to help place and train Singaporeans and over the past few years, 'thousands of unemployed have been helped this way', he added.

They summon us to these meccas of commerce, huge stores where almost anything may be purchased. In a typical year, clueless relatives supply me with cards to bookstores and, every once in a while, one to a Best Buy. I love the gift card concept. While some might argue that it removes the personal touch a present should possess, the intention expressed in a gift card is apparent: this person obviously wants you to be very happy and is willing to empower your happiness by giving you the choice. When you get a gift card on Christmas morning, all you have at that moment is love. That doesn't mean the damn things can't bite you in the ass. Currently my wallet is stuffed with several cards containing about a dollar each left to be spent in these stores.

    This year my uncle gave me a card for Wal-Mart, based on the admittedly bulletproof argument that I would have no trouble finding one. My hometown of Manassas, Virginia has two. In fact, the new one, located a mile from my house, is even listed as being in "East Manassas," which I had no idea even existed! Now my hometown's geography is split and defined by the Wal-Marts it contains. So getting to one would obviously pose no significant problems.

    The store, however, scares me beyond tangible reason. Wal-Mart elicits complicated feelings from me, ones I never even knew existed. While there are certainly a number of reasons to be against the company on principle, namely their diabolical global strategies and notoriously awful treatment of workers, there is another element to my fear, one that doesn't arise from its peers like Target. The source of my worries had never been properly divined; it was something that was simply out there, a phantom I did not want to touch. But there was no way I wasn't going to get my free stuff.

    My new Wal-Mart had dramatically affected the surrounding roads. We had several confusing lights and turn lanes. It rose ahead of my sister and me like a colossus on the horizon. Approaching it in the parking lot, it just loomed ever larger, consuming my field of vision. My mission, however, shone like a beacon against the deep dull blue: I would make my uncle proud. Somewhere in this house of pain, I would find my happiness. The doors slid open and I jumped into the breach.

    Any large store in the holiday season is an ungodly mess. Fortunately we have greeters to lift our spirits! I have major objections to the greeter system in general. When I walk into Best Buy I try to elude them as best I can, doing my part to fit in with Georg Simmel's modern society of the alienated. How dare they try to establish personal contact with me! They utterly fail to put me in a sunny mood, ready to spend. They mask their basic security function in pleasantries, and this is an insult to any shopper. They are judging you as they welcome. Wal-Mart complicates the problem, because their greeters seem to be almost entirely comprised of the aged. This should inspire remarkable pity and sadness. Imagine suffering life's slings and arrows, only to arrive here, wishing thousands a good day and a happy shopping experience. If I were ever to become a Wal-Mart greeter, I would know that my life has been for naught. I've sapped the world of valuable natural resources. Instead of a summit, this is the pits.

    Why are the elderly here? I can think of two possible reasons. First, some may be unable to retire for financial reasons. Second, others may see a job as a welcome time-filler. I would hope for better solutions to either dilemma. Being a greeter requires less than a person. In that lies the truth about Wal-Mart. The company doesn't even try. I have seen customers herded in like cattle, and they bray accordingly. The aforementioned blueness of the store does not flatter it. They peddle in banality. At Target, you receive the sensation that the store might be trying to pull a fast one, stunning customers through its bright, flashy design. Wal-Mart exposes everything, including the pipes on the ceiling. Smoke and mirrors keep the customers from seeing what they really are, but at Wal-Mart this becomes all too clear. It's like seeing the Matrix code: numbers everywhere being shifted around. As long as there is movement, everything is fine. Wal-Mart has risen above the need to cater to the customer. The blandness of color, the smiley face logo, are merely signs that their customers need no convincing; they are resigned to an ordinary life, filled with unstylish, ordinary things and five-dollar Hulk Hogan movies in the bargain bin. The company does not aim for a false exclusivity or hipness. It just exists and forces you to accept it.

    My happiness did lie inside: a copy of Pulp Fiction. A work of flash and brilliance trapped in a box, buying this felt like a prison breakout. I waited in one line, which decided to close. Then I moved to another. At that moment, less patient customers than I revolted, addressing the employees and calling them names. Wal-Mart sat back and laughed. It realized we had no choice.
