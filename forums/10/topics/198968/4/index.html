    Some accreditation is necessary but too many hampers proper hospital administration. One reason for these accreditation programmes is that US patients can be transferred here for treatment in JCI-accredited hospitals, paid for using their US-based medical insurance. The US is likely to be our biggest customer and they call the shots. If your hospital has JCI accreditation, they will treat your hospital like one of their own. If your lab is CAP-accredited, then they will accept your standards as equivalent and even allow your lab techs to sit for their examinations, since they consider someone working in your lab to have the experience as someone in a US lab.

    It tends to be local private patients that go for the cheapest rates. US patients tend to be more discerning and certainly their medical insurance companies will be. That's why the private hospital in Bangkok has done so well after they got JCI accreditation ahead of Singapore hospitals.

    The problem about accreditation in Singapore is that we have large public hospitals like SGH, TTSH and NUH who are already accredited with multiple organizations. Smaller private clinics, labs and hospitals do not have any accreditation at all and do not wish to pay the high price for signing up with one of these overseas accreditation schemes. So Singapore started running local accreditation schemes so that these smaller establishments will at least have some form of quality assurance. But the big hospitals are also required to sign up to local schemes in addition to overseas-based schemes. That is ridiculous and a waste of time and resources.

    Originally posted by sourketchup:
    Ah, but that is assuming that accreditation would definitely result in a higher standard of care. That is debatable, to say the least. But it does definitely result in lots of paperwork(to "prove" that the necessary steps demanded have been taken), which eats into time and effort that could have gone into proper care of patients.
    Correct - the assumption is that accreditation results in better care.

    Let's break this down into several aspects of hospital accreditation.

    1. Care itself - some are eveidence-based medical care and are standards heaped onto hospitals so that they can compare themselves with one another - eg. if you came in through the ER and was diagnosed as having penumonia -----> the standard will be expected to receive IV antibiotics within a certain time period, I think it's 4 hours - I forget 'cos Docs precribe as soon as they make a diagnosis. It is the hospital "process" of translating that management decision into action, ie. pharmacy gets order, process it correctly, sends the right drug to the right patient in the right location, have the nurse set the patient up for IV antiobiotics and then actually delivering it to the patient ......

    So - accreditation also measures and improves care - when you have to report how many percent of patients presenting to ER with pneumonia receive antibiotics within 4 hours of arrival/diagnosis. If you suck - you will try to improve the process.

    2. Documentation - things that get done or not done - no documentation means not done, right? Can argue until the cows come home but no proof - accreditation is done on hospital procedures.

    3. Ethical aspects - how the hospital handles patient information, protect the patient from medical research studies, etc. So improves the patients' rights for care, not from exploitation.

    4. Recommendations for standardized short-forms: many docs and ancillary staff use a lot of shortforms and these cause errors. Medical mistakes hurt or kill patients - these clerical errors also monitored by accreditation agencies. They have proscribed standard shortforms to avoid these erros and hospitals get ding on it because of personnel habit - thus training and education is needed. Things will change over time when newer docs/personnel are imbued with the right attitude and right training for this.

    5. Training/accreditation - eg. CPR - BCLS/ACLS certification for essential personnel. Prior to accreditation many docs did not have routine Basic/Advanced Cardiac Life Support Training but I heard that JCHAO (or JCI for Singapore) monitors that so training invariably is conducted to satisfy the criteria for accreditation. Of course this does not mean that care improves but logically it should. Why if you don't know what you are doing then you don't know. If you were taught it before - at least if you encounter it the first time - you will have a basis to act and to know where to find the solutions to problems.

    Accreditation is not the solution for all hospital ills but allows standards to be established for comparisons.

    Some are evidence-based changes in hospital proceudures. I think inevitably some good comes off. Documentation is time-consuming, so costs will definitely go up. But is there any other way of moving into the next phase of development??
