    Foreign students will only pay the same amount of fees as per local students if they choose to sign a Tuition Grant Bond with MOE.

    For those who chooses to do so, these cluster will be bonded to serve in a Singapore incorporated company or business for 3 years after their successful graduation from the respective institute.

    Incidently, it is usually also a common practice for this group to be offered a first generation PR offer subsequently after graduation.

    For the other group who decides against the offer of Tuition Grant, they will then pay the full unsubsidized amount of school fees, which would most probably run up to sums around prehaps some $7K above.

    Just a personal view - with no offence to anyone, I think the term 'talent' in fact too hype up a word to describe anyone who just comes in to Singapore with more than a work permit and a different passport from another country.

    If the two criterias are sufficient to justify the word 'talent', then by the same standards, we would readily have three million local talents ready for economic activation in Singapore at any moment right now.

    So if we or the top brass wish to continue with the above defination, then I guess the contributions that FTs can contribute for Singapore, will probably be those that of ordinary achievements - made along the current curve of performance.

    Ideally what we hope from an ideal FT might be something different.

    Perhaps in face of the still grim and uncertain future, we would prefer FTs to contribute more differently from how they may be currently contributing now.

    That could possibly be a reverse from the current trend of competing jobs with the local, to one which will help new contracts and hence, new job opportunities for locals through such new contracts' domestic spin-off effect on the domestic home front.

    Perhaps in the drive for regional expansion and the emerging call for entrepreneurship, the role of FTs can be better reshaped into one which will enable local firms or businesses, to expand on their respective operations with greater effectiveness and efficiency as compared to a local might preform in the FT's country of origin.

    The employment of FTs and locals must be managed as a balanced equation.

    Given the unstoppable trend of globalization, Singapore has already danced too far deep into the rhythm of its rapid pace to have rendered the option of sanctioning off the existence of all FTs in the domestic context to be a sane and feasible option.

    And just as unavoidable, there will also come this day when the global village will result in governments all over the world to find certain FTs being more loyal to their country, just as these same governments will also find growing numbers of their own citizens - being the FTs in another foreign land, to habour deeper loyalty for their host country as well.

    And until the day that we reach that day, the FT-Local Equation would then have to undergo reconstruction and study so as to ensure on its role of relevance to us - in the same way as itmust be done so now.
