    I think people have missed the issue. Prof Asher is not saying for a moment that we should opt for state welfare instead of the CPF system. The issue is how the CPF system is run.

    By allowing and in fact encouraging Singaporeans to use their CPF for housing, we have seen the dramatic rise in property prices over the years, so much so that Singapore becomes non-competitive. Residential property prices do have a bearing on commercial property prices as well in land-scarce Singapore. Whether it is the doctor's fees or the cost of ice cream, a significant part of it goes to pay for the rental of the clinic or factory.

    To reverse that trend, the government is trying to bring down infrastructure costs but that will have a detrimental effect on our retirement savings, since much of it is tied up in that HDB flat. In the past, conventional wisdom is that when you retire, you downgrade from that executive flat to a 4-room flat and live off the profits. Looking at the prices today, how much profit do you get? But many do not have anything else in their CPF savings apart from that HDB flat. If you don't get a windfall from the sale of your flat, or worse, if you suffer a loss, then what are you going to live on in your retirement?

    The other very valid point by Prof Asher is that there is no transparency in the way Temasek Holdings invests our CPF funds. Going by international industry standards, the returns we get for our CPF savings is below par. Why is that so? Apart from the few leaks such as that disastrous investment Ho Ching made in the US, we will never know...
