    By Liew Hanqing

    August 25, 2005

    THERE could be more foreign students than Singaporeans taking some engineering and science courses at the National University of Singapore.

    Students tell The New Paper that this is particularly true in electrical and computer engineering, with local students forming less than a quarter of the class sometimes.

    For instance, out of the 48 students in one industrial and systems engineering class, only 10 are Singaporeans, they said.

    As many as 31 are from China, and seven are from other countries.

    When asked for the details, the university said 20 per cent of its entire student body is international.

    'International students are not evenly distributed across all faculties,' its spokesman said.

    'Faculties such as engineering and computing may have a higher proportion of foreign students depending on foreign student interest in such courses.'

    In going international, NUS has gone into partnerships with seven universities worldwide - including big names like Peking University, Australian National University, UC Berkeley and Yale.

    This means there will be more exchange programmes and more collaborative research.

    More competition too, as foreign students flock to our shores.

    And the locals seem to be feeling the heat. One 23-year-old computer engineering student said foreigners made up about two-thirds of his class.

    Three other students who are doing computer engineering confirmed that the majority of students taking the course are foreigners, mostly from China.

    NUS would not comment on this figure.

    Fourth-year environmental engineering student Audrey Huang, 22, said: 'With more foreign students, local students aren't given enough space to develop.

    'Maybe the competitive local students would like being spurred on by these foreign intellectuals, but personally, as a normal student, it gives me excessive pressure.'

    Second-year computer engineering student Chen Kaiwei, 22, felt that admitting foreigners would be meaningful only if more interaction between them and the locals was encouraged.

    'Local students don't mix with the foreigners, so they don't learn anything from them,' he said.

    'The only effect, then, is the intensive competition.'

    But that is the key to success, the NUS administration believes.

    The NUS spokesman said: 'We impose a highly competitive selection criteria for international students, more so than we would for local students.

    'This is to ensure that our international students are among the most talented, and that they are truly deserving of a place in NUS.'

    Some local students are taking the competition in their stride.

    'There's no running away from competition,' said second-year mechanical engineering student Zeng Xian Hong, 22.

    'If we don't meet foreign competitors in school, we'll meet them in the working world.

    'There's no difference.'

    But will this make an NUS education less accessible for local applicants?

    NUS, Nanyang Technological University and Singapore Management University now have separate admission systems, the spokesman said.

    'There are, therefore, sufficient places and a variety of courses - no deserving local students will be turned away.'
