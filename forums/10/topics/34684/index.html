    Thursday - 6 February 2003 Straits Times reprinted a Malaysian New Report that:-

    Malaysia is testing a plan that relies on the use of recycled water from households to boost supplies, as it prepares for a possible water shortage in 20 years' time.

    The Malay-language daily, Beritat Harian Malaysia, reported thata a team of engineers from Universiti Sains Malaysia in Penang and the Drainage and Irrigation Department was working on a pilot project to process used water from homes.

    At the project's launch on Tuesday, spokesman Nor Azazi Zakaria said the recycled water could "be used for many domestic needs except for drinking".

    He said the team would monitor the results of the Bio-Ecological Drainage System over a 10-year period.

    There is a possibility that if successful, the model would be implemented nationwide.

    Dr Nor Azazi said recycling water was a cheap way to obtain clean water.

    "This can help save up to 90 percent of our piped water from going to waste, as we only use about 10 percent for drinking."

    He added that the development of the system was significant because of a need to find alternative water sources ahead of a looming worldwide water shortage in 2025.

    Last year alone, Malaysia approved more than 62 water projects costing RM 51.9 Billion (S$23.7 billion) under a master plan to meet rapidly growing deman for water.

    The projects were aimed at meeting the expected increase in the daily demand for water at a rate of 6 percent every year until 2010.

    In 1997, seven of Malaysia's 13 states were hit by a water shortage brought on by a severe drought.

    Singapore has also put in place a system of tapping new sources, including reclaimed water and plans for a seawater desalination facility.
