    AFP - 15 minutes ago

    Interpol issues alert for Singapore militant escapee

    SINGAPORE (AFP) - - Interpol issued an urgent worldwide alert on Friday for an alleged leader of the Jemaah Islamiyah militant network, two days after his escape from a detention centre in Singapore.

    Mas Selamat bin Kastari, who was accused of planning to hijack a plane and crash it into Singapore's Changi Airport, escaped Wednesday from the Whitley Road Detention Centre.

    In a notice on its website, the global police body said it issued the Orange Notice alert following a request by Singapore.

    Orange Notices can be issued by Interpol's general secretariat in France for any event posing a risk to the safety and security of citizens worldwide.

    "Singapore is clearly doing all that it can on a national level to locate this fugitive and through issuing an Orange Notice, Interpol and all of its member countries can support these efforts on an international scale," said Jean-Michel Louboutin, Interpol executive director of police services.

    Kastari's photograph and fingerprints will be sent to Interpol's 186 member countries, the agency said.

    Analysts said they believed Kastari would try to flee to Indonesia.

    Sutarman, chief of police of Riau Islands province which shares a sea border with Singapore, told Indonesia's state-run Antara news agency that the city-state "sent us an urgent notice requesting intensified patrols to prevent the terrorist from entering Indonesia."

    He said Kastari "possesses good knowledge" about entering the country illegally.

    Indonesian police chief Sutanto told reporters in Jakarta that his force would help Singapore if Kastari makes it to Indonesia.

    "We've taken all important measures," he said, without elaborating.

    Authorities have blamed Jemaah Islamiyah (JI) for a string of regional attacks including the 2002 bombings on the Indonesian resort island of Bali, which killed 202 people, mostly tourists.

    Singapore's Ministry of Home Affairs said Friday its manhunt continued with extensive police, armed forces and other security resources deployed.

    It said Kastari, a Singaporean who allegedly led JI in the city, walks with a limp and was not known to be armed.

    In a refocusing of the search, authorities withdrew the dozens of soldiers, special operations police and paramilitary Nepalese Gurkhas who had been deployed around the detention centre.

    An AFP photographer later saw a group of soldiers and Gurkhas investigating an area beneath a highway about three kilometres (two miles) from a bridge leading to Malaysia.

    Meanwhile, police arrested another man whom they said made threats after claiming he was the fugitive.

    The 58-year-old used a public phone to claim he was Kastari and "threatened to attack multiple locations," a police statement said.

    Kastari fled Singapore in December 2001 following an Internal Security Department operation against JI, the home affairs ministry said.

    He was arrested on the Indonesian island of Bintan near Singapore in 2003, reportedly for carrying false identification, and was jailed for 18 months.

    He was later released but rearrested in Indonesia in January 2006 before being handed over to Singapore, where he was held under the Internal Security Act, which allows for detention without trial.

    The 2001 security operation in Singapore led to 15 people being arrested, 13 of whom were suspected JI members who allegedly planned to attack a bus full of Americans.

    Sidney Jones, who has extensively studied JI for the International Crisis Group of analysts, said Kastari was a hardliner but currently had little power in the group, whose Indonesian leadership now rejects "Al-Qaeda tactics" targeting foreigners.
