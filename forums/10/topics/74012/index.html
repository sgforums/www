    Before we rubbish our medical expenses as exhorbitant, let us take a a peek at how other nations administer their medical practices.

    For example, in France, the taxes are extremely high at ard 45% in terms of overall income compared to ours. However for the french, that takes care of most education bills and all medical check-ups, including specialists treatment (yes they dun pay a cent). This practice is such that some among the french abuse it to an extent that has caused growing concern within the french parliment, with calls from the leftist conservatives to review the current situation with a suggestion to increase taxes to cover the added burden brought on by these irresponsible 'malingerers'.

    Over here, our gahmen introduced medisave to help our pre-dominantly aging population cope with future medical expenses. By creating a special account under the CPF scheme, they hoped that a portion of our monthly CPF would be sufficient to allow partial or full payment of any hospital treatments incurred. Obviously, this cannot cover the full cost of major operations such as chemotherapy or magnetic resonance scans needed for cancer treatment, if partially at all.

    The consideration here might be to moderately increase the percentage contribution in our CPF special accounts, with an view at increasing the distribution in our medisave. The bad news is, only those who work and earn a monthly income are entitled to this benefit and that cuts off the jobless community completely.

    Our medical bills here, while comparatively expensive in our eyes, are relatively cheaper compared to other established countries like germany and US. This coupled with our graft-free reputation and our competent hospital practices makes Singapore a medical destination for many ppl in the region seeking professional advice and treatment. One major reason why the iranian conjoined twins came here for their op was due to the relatively low cost of the operation. Since then, many others have visited our shores for medical assistance.

    If that is not enough, then we should also consider the fact that hospitals have reduced the prices of various outpatient treatments and consultations last year to aid Singaporeans with the rising cost of living standards.

    If we were to adopt a method similar to the french, will our ppl agree to the huge increase in income taxes that will curtail it? Will our ppl use the previlage that comes with this practice honestly and not try to abuse it as some within the french population have?

    Let's face it, no system is full-proof. No method can cater to all the needs of a nation. Some will undoubtedly be left in the cold. At the moment, medisave is as good as it gets.

    The following article appeared in Streats a couple of days ago, I believe.

    -----------------------------------------------

    Doctors, heal thy suffering sector

    By CONRAD RAJ

    WHENEVER you ask about the high cost of services in Singapore, whether in health care or at one time in port services, the standard answer is that you have to pay for quality.

    It seems to me that this argument has worn thin.

    Singapore once used to be a shoppers' paradise. No longer. Escalating rentals and other costs have pushed up prices here. At the same time, elsewhere in the world, products of the same or even better quality are being offered at far lower prices than in Singapore.

    Yet, many here still cling on to the "we are better" stance, implying that others can't match our overall package because they fall short in quality or flexibility, and hence we can maintain our higher costs.

    That surely is a wrong attitude to take.

    PSA Corporation (formerly the Port of Singapore Authority) learnt at great cost and some embarrassment the fallacy behind that approach.

    Its inability or failure to agree to the demands of the AP Moller Group, parent of Maersk Sealand and the world's largest operator of container ships, resulted in the Copenhagen-headquartered company not only moving most of its operations to the Port of Tanjung Pelepas in Johor but also taking a stake in the port.

    With the help of Maersk's expertise, Pelepas is now aggressively competing with Singapore and was also able to lure Evergreen, then the second biggest user of PSA's facilities, to follow it to Johor.

    The port stake offered by Pelepas may have weighed heavily in the Maersk decision but observers say that Pelepas, also held out the promise of lower fees.

    Talk is that Maersk is still doing a lot of business at PSA wharves, but the initial shift to Pelepas should have been a signal to other Singaporean companies and industries.

    Alas, anecdotal evidence indicates that the PSA lesson seems to have been lost on our medical and health-care sector, once the region's undisputed leader.

    For example, when a reporter from this paper recently talked to our eye specialists about the disturbing trend of Singaporeans going abroad to seek cheaper cosmetic surgery in places like Bangkok and Kuala Lumpur, the replies emphasised the quality here rather than price.

    The Singapore National Eye Centre told Streats: "The centre/surgeon'strack record, experience, quality control and accreditation need to be considered."

    AsiaMedic chairman and ophthalmologist Low Cze Hong said: "Be aware of risks in places where hygiene and sanitary standards are not what we expect in Singapore. We will not cut costs or prices just to compete and risk patients' interests."

    The fact is that reporters of the various media here who have visited Bangkok or Kuala Lumpur have been impressed by the facilities.

    For instance, they seemed to have the latest in eye surgery equipment and doctors who had no problems discussing procedures in English.

    A colleague, after undergoing Lasik treatment for his eyes in
    Thailand, described Bangkok's Bumrungrad Hospital as a five-star hospital. The hospital, Thailand's largest private health care centre, last year treated more than 3,000 Singaporeans. In all, Bumrungrad treated more than 300,000 foreigners from 140 countries.

    Health care has become big business for Thailand. While the Tourism Authority of Thailand doesn't track medical tourism, it is estimated that up to 800,000 foreigners used Thailand's hospitals last year, bringing in about 19 billion baht (S$815 million) Â– a 25 per cent jump over 2002.

    Malaysia is no slouch either. When the Makhota Medical Centre in Malacca was established in 1998, it had less than 1,000 patients from Indonesia. Four years later it had 15,600.

    While the Government has gone some way in trying to help the
    healthcare industry here reduce costs, the charges are still high relative to our neighbouring competitors.

    According to some estimates, open heart surgery here costs about US$11,500 (S$19,800), compared with US$7,000 in Bangkok and US$6,300 in Kuala Lumpur or Malacca.

    A day's unsubsidised surgery for cataracts here can cost anything from $2,671 to $3,501. A similar procedure costs about $1,274 in Bumrungrad and $1,458 at Mahkota.

    Some of our hospitals here have begun dropping prices, but it seems by not enough.

    There is one group of people who can help make a difference: our surgeons.

    I'm by no means asking them to live up to the extremes of the Hippocratic Oath Â– parting with their knowledge for free.

    But if Singapore seriously harbours (no pun intended) any intention to be once again the region's undisputed healthcare hub, our doctors need to charge less.

    After all, they have a vested interest in our healthcare industry's health. A stream of ill foreigners slipping through their skilled fingers and heading to other shores could create a critical mass that becomes impossible to attract back.

    And it would be a shame if this came to pass just because some top doctors found it too hard to give up their dreams of garages full of Porsches and wine cellars brimming with Petrus.
