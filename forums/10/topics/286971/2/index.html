    GONE UP
    Prices of live chicken rise by 20%
    Higher feed costs in Malaysia see fresh chicken prices up by 30 to 90 cents a kilo here
    By Jamie Ee Wen Wei

    THE soaring cost of chicken feed in Malaysia has sent the price of live birds here up by almost 20 per cent this month, to about $5 a kilo.

    Hawkers say they have no choice but to raise their own prices to offset some of the hefty increases.

    Mr Chew Kian Huat, secretary of the Poultry Merchants' Association, said corn, the main ingredient in chicken feed, has shot up from about $420 to $820 a tonne - making live chickens 20 to 25 per cent pricier.

    Hawkers said chicken prices are at an all-time high.

    'Chicken has never been this expensive,' said Mr Tay Thiam Huat, 48, who has been selling chicken at a wet market in Eunos for 30 years. He used to sell a medium-size one for about $5. Now it costs $6.

    The Sunday Times visited 10 wet markets in neighbourhoods like Ang Mo Kio and Toa Payoh last week. It found that the price of fresh chicken had gone up by between 30 and 90 cents a kilo.

    Those who passed on all increase in costs to customers suffered a dip in sales. One hawker at Ang Mo Kio Ave 10 said sales fell by 30 per cent after he raised prices by 90 cents to $5 a kilo.

    Mr Ng Say Cheng, 57, who runs a stall at Toa Payoh Lorong 4, said: 'Business is already not so good. We just have to earn a little less.' He charges 50 cents more for a kilo of chicken.

    Two weeks ago, Sheng Siong Supermarket raised chicken prices by about a dollar. A small chicken weighing 1 to 1.3kg now costs $4.80.

    Giant hypermart has also increased prices, while NTUC FairPrice is 'holding' them for now.

    The Sunday Times knows of at least one chicken rice stall that has raised prices this month. Ming Kee Chicken Rice at Bishan Street 13 now charges $3 for a plate, a rise of 50 cents.

    Singapore imports about 3.4 million chickens from Malaysia a month.

    While Mr Chew stressed that chicken prices have been kept 'too low' over the past few years due to intense competition, some consumers clearly think otherwise.

    Housewife Tan Poh Yok, 67, said: 'It's very expensive compared to the last few years. I'll just have to eat less of it.'

    Oct 22, 2007
    Caltex petrol, diesel prices up
    Other brands likely to follow suit soon.
    By Christopher Tan

    AMERICAN oil company Chevron raised pump prices of its Caltex petrol and diesel by five cents a litre on Monday - the sixth increase since July and the second in about a fortnight.

    The others - Shell, ExxonMobil and Singapore Petroleum - are likely to do likewise in the coming days.

    Chevron's move brings its pump prices to record levels.

    Before discount, the price of Caltex Silver 95 is now $1.886 a litre, Caltex Gold 98 $1.96 a litre, Caltex Platinum $2.126 a litre and diesel $1.403 a litre.

    Station discount now stands at 5 per cent.

    For some perspective, the price of diesel today is equivalent to what premium petrol cost just four years ago.

    Back then, diesel cost around 67 cents a litre.

    The latest increase comes on the back of crude oil hovering at record levels.

    Last Friday, New York light sweet crude reached US$90.07 per barrel while London Brent hit US$84.88 a day earlier.

    The pump price spiral is expected to have as much impact on individual motorists as public transport operators and taxi-drivers.

    It will also raise business costs all round.

    Title : Report shows job seekers expect higher salaries in Singapore
    By : Wong Mun Wai, Channel NewsAsia
    Date : 17 Oct 2007 2050 hrs (GMT + 8hrs)

    SINGAPORE: Job applicants are expecting higher salaries than what employers are willing to pay, according to the Hudson report - a quarterly report of human resource trends in Singapore.

    The report shows that 57 percent of 723 top company executives surveyed wanted over 10 percent more than the salaries offered.

    The Media/PR/Advertising sector has the biggest gap in salary expectations, followed by Manufacturing, Healthcare and Life Sciences.

    The Hudson report indicates companies in Singapore receive demands for higher salaries than they are willing to pay "often or very often".

    This is higher than that in Hong Kong, Japan and China.

    The report's authors said the demand for much higher salaries is partly driven by the fact that the candidates are bilingual and have the option of working in Hong Kong or China.

    The authors of the report caution that a gap in supply and demand exists in the job market, and employers are overconfident about filling their ranks on their own terms with the right employees.

    Mark Sparrow, Singapore Country Manager, Hudson, said: "I think that is going to become acutely more difficult, moving on from now, unless there are pools of talent that can come into Singapore very easily and probably en masse."

    He is confident that top brands will attract talent to Singapore, so companies need to look beyond the country's shores when hiring.

    - CNA/so

    SembCorp Marine estimates losses of US$248m in forex dealings
    Posted: 22 October 2007 2328 hrs

    SINGAPORE : Sembcorp Marine could see losses of as much as US$248 million in foreign exchange dealings.

    The company said it has sacked its Finance Director Wee Sing Guan, who was responsible for the unauthorised trades.

    The trades were done on the account of Jurong Shipyard - one of its units.

    Sembcorp Marine has appointed Drew & Napier and Ernst & Young to investigate the transactions.

    In a statement on Monday, SembCorp Marine said it has received mark-to-market information on the foreign exchange transactions from the various banks.

    It estimated that the losses could amount to US$165 million.

    This is on top of the US$83 million which Jurong Shipyard had already paid to one of the banks before the unauthorised transactions were discovered.

    Sembcorp Marine is the offshore and marine arm of SembCorp Industries.

    Both counters were suspended from trading on Monday, ahead of the announcement. - CNA/ms

    Singapore's Sept CPI down 0.3% from the previous month
    Posted: 23 October 2007 1320 hrs

    SINGAPORE : Consumer prices in Singapore edged down 0.3 percent in September from the previous month.

    The Department of Statistics says the change in the consumer price index (CPI) in the two months after July 2007 was similar to that in the months before.

    This shows that there is no evidence so far of uptick in inflation for August and September after the one-off increase in the Goods and Services Tax (GST) rate in July.

    The GST was increased to 7 percent from 5 percent on July 1st.

    In September 2007, costs of transport & communication declined by 1.2 per cent as lower car prices and road tax more than offset dearer petrol.

    Housing cost went down by 1.2 per cent due mainly to lower housing maintenance charges and cheaper household durables.

    Reflecting cheaper ready-made garments and footwear, prices of clothing & footwear dipped by 0.9 per cent.

    On the contrary, food prices increased by 0.4 per cent on account of dearer cooked food, fresh poultry, milk powder and fruits.

    Compared with the same month a year ago, the consumer price index in September 2007 increased by 2.7 per cent. - CNA/ch
