    Most varsity cheats are Chinese
    Statement by NZ university causes uproar
    March 10, 2007

    MOST of the cheats at one New Zealand university are from China.

    That's what Lincoln University is claiming. And it has sparked outrage in Christchurch.

    A local newspaper reported last week that a majority of cheating cases at Lincoln University, a rural campus about half an hour's drive from the town that focuses on agricultural studies, involved students from mainland China.

    Almost half the college's 4,000 students are drawn from overseas, 60 per cent of them from China.

    Several students contacted the newspaper after the report appeared to rebut the claims.

    Many studied hard for their qualifications, they told The Press newspaper.

    One said: 'I can only say some Chinese may cheat in the exam but I never cheat during any of my exams, and assignments.'

    Student Rockie Hsu said: 'I was a student at Lincoln and I saw some Kiwi students cheating... My point is most cheaters are Chinese because the supervisors are not catching the Kiwi cheaters at all.'

    Others agreed that students from all groups cheated.

    Student Jaemi Tan: 'Yes, I do believe there are some Chinese students who cheat in the exams, but Kiwi students cheat in the exams as well. You can't deny that.

    'I think it is a little bit unfair to single out Chinese students about cheating at the university. From my own time at a different university I'm aware cheaters come from all groups.'

    UNFAIR ASSUMPTION?

    Another student, Hai Yan Jiang, said it was unfair to make assumptions about a whole race based on just a few people.

    'It is unfair to judge a nation according to several individuals. Can I make conclusion that all the Kiwi are stupid, drunk people because I saw lots of them drunk in the late night standing in the street and making mess?'

    Since The Press published the story on Wednesday several people have also contacted the newspaper to back up the claims made by students and staff at Lincoln University. They believe cheating by Chinese students is widespread, not only at Lincoln but also at other New Zealand universities.

    Mr Rutherford Ward, a former tutor at two leading North Island universities, said he frequently raised the issue of cheating among Chinese students.

    'It is a problem, no matter what people say,' he said.

    'I came across it while I was tutoring.

    'There are cultural differences and Chinese students are under a lot of pressure because they pay a lot to come here but universities do make it clear what the rules are.

    'It isn't just Chinese students who cheat or have poor language skills but they make up a large number.'

    Meanwhile, thousands of British students have been cheating on their university application forms, reported The Telegraph.

    The Universities and Colleges Admissions Service (Ucas) said one in 20 candidates copied material from websites when writing personal statements.

    University applicants have to complete a Ucas form. This means they have to submit a personal statement describing their interests and explaining why they want to study their chosen degree course.
