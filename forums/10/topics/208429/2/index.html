    Hospital faces axe as debts bite
    By Nick Triggle
    BBC News health reporter

    At the beginning of the year, the government said community hospitals were the future of the NHS.

    But crippling deficits are forcing the closure of many such hospitals, say campaigners.

    When you enter the Wiltshire town of Westbury, the signs are clear for all to see.

    "Save Westbury Hospital - join the fight", they say.

    The hospital in question is a community hospital housed a stone's throw from the high street.

    Until inpatient beds were stripped away earlier this year, the building had a specialist stroke unit, palliative care beds, district and school nursing teams, health visitors and social workers.

    The palliative care and stroke beds have now gone and the rest will follow, under plans being consider by local health bosses in a bid to save money and redesign community care.

    But this is happening little over three months since the government unveiled a white paper which demanded more care be carried out in the community, by investing in a new generation of community hospitals and multi-disciplinary care teams.

    Long journeys

    "The Westbury Hospital was everything the government was saying it wanted," said Erica Watson, part of the hospital campaign group and a health visitor.

    Speaking in her campaigning role, she added: "It just does not make sense to close the hospital.

    "There is only one GP practice in the town, so local people relied on the hospital for community health services.

    "When the white paper came we thought, yes, this will be good for the hospital. But it has not turned out like that.

    "It is motivated by saving money, but it will lead to patients suffering by having to travel long journeys for care."

    The Westbury Hospital was everything the government was saying it wanted
    Erica Watson, local campaigner

    The local primary care trusts ran Â£21m over budget last year and, coupled with Â£34m of historic debt, the pressure is non to save money.

    But it has denied cost-cutting is the only motive.

    Carol Clarke, chief executive of Kennet and North Wiltshire and West Wiltshire PCTs, said: "Even if we were in financial balance, we would be making these changes.

    "Community services in this part of Wiltshire have not had the investment and development that they should have had, and it is essential that we bring community services here up to date.

    "However, it is also true that we are in a tough financial climate, and these changes do deliver some savings.

    "The changes proposed, however, strive to strike a balance between the need to modernise and improve our services, and pull back our expenditure. We believe that we can do both."

    Savings

    And the PCTs are targeting community services despite the fact that they spend much more on major hospitals and GP services.

    A fifth of the two PCTs' Â£347m budget is spent on community services, with Â£8m a year being spent on the hospital buildings alone.

    Government guidelines state that there should be only one community hospital per 100,000 - the area has eight for 320,000 people.

    Under three different plans being considered, between five and seven of the hospitals will be shut, potentially saving up to Â£8.1m.

    In each case, Westbury has been earmarked for closure, to be replaced by 24-hour community teams of district nurses, health visitors and social workers.

    The town, like some of the others losing community hospitals, may also get a primary care centre comprising the traditional GP care with a range of extra services such as dermatology clinics, diagnostic tests and minor surgery.

    However, nurses said they were unconvinced about the changes.

    The BBC spoke to several nurses who did not wish to be identified because of fear over their jobs.

    One said: "They have made promises about community teams and primary care centres, but hospitals are closing and as yet there is nothing to replace them.

    PATIENTS at a flagship hospital built through the private finance initiative scheme face the longest waits in accident and emergency in Scotland.

    A census published yesterday of every A&E and community casualty unit in the country found that Edinburgh Royal Infirmary (ERI) scored worst, with patients having to wait over four hours.

    The study found 35 per cent of the hospital's A&E patients were delayed longer than this compared with a Scottish average of 12 per cent. Andy Kerr, the health minister, conceded there was a problem at the ERI but was "confident" it was being addressed.

    The census came as the Executive published a raft of figures on waiting times for a variety of treatments. These show the NHS continued to meet the target of no patient waiting longer than six months, with progress towards a maximum wait of 18 weeks by the end of 2007.

    But the figures also revealed an increase in people on the "hidden" waiting list - those given "availability status codes" (ASCs), with no six-month treatment guarantee.

    The A&E survey was carried out during a seven-day period in April, covering almost 30,000 attendances across Scotland.

    The Executive has set a target that, by the end of 2007, 98 per cent of patients must be seen within four hours.

    In April, almost nine out of ten (88 per cent) of A&E patients in Scotland waited less than four hours before discharge or admission, with a median wait of 97 minutes. Just 65 per cent were seen in four hours at the ERI - no other unit scored below 72 per cent. The ERI also had the longest median wait at 189 minutes.

    The figures revealed that in 51 per cent of cases where patients were waiting more than four hours, the reason was a wait for a bed in the hospital. There was controversy about a reduction in bed numbers when the hospital was being built.

    NHS Lothian claimed it had not scored better as it was "extremely busy" when the survey was carried out. Dr Casey Stewart, clinical director of medicine, said: "The weekend of the census also saw a multiple shooting in Edinburgh that placed unprecedented pressure on the A&E."

    Mr Kerr, speaking as he released the figures at the Southern General Hospital in Glasgow, said long waits at the ERI were being dealt with. "I am reassured, and I have looked at this matter closely, that they are taking action that will resolve this."

    Earlier this week, the ERI was one of the hospitals named and shamed by the Executive for failing to meet the expected high standards of cleanliness.

    Margaret Watt, chairman of the Patients' Association, said it was not acceptable that people, sometimes with serious conditions, were left to wait hours in A&E: "Patients don't think the current system is good enough."

    Mr Kerr visited Southern General's endoscopy unit yesterday with Jack McConnell, the First Minister, and said that overall waiting times in Scotland were now at their lowest-ever level.

    There were no patients waiting longer than six months for inpatient or day treatment at the end of June, compared with 1,118 in June 2005.

    There were 4,027 patients with treatment guaranteed waiting more than 18 weeks, down from 8,866 in June last year. But out of 104,791 patients waiting, 35,233 were exempt from guarantees because they had an ASC - handed out if someone misses or cancels an appointment or cannot be treated. This was up from 33,869 at the end of March.

    Mr Kerr said the rise was down to people rescheduling appointments because of summer holidays. The Executive plans to abolish ASCs by 2007 to make the system "more transparent", Mr Kerr said.
