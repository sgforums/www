    No reason to raise bus and train fares

    Aug 03, 2006
    The Straits Times

    I NOTE with apprehension that SBS Transit and SMRT have applied for bus- and train-fare hikes.
    I don't understand why SMRT and SBS Transit have decided to raise fares when SMRT registered operating profits of $68.9 million in 2004 and $70 million in 2005 on its bus and MRT services. SBS Transit, too, enjoyed healthy operating profits, of $52.6 million (2004) and $57.15 million (2005). Both companies enjoyed operating margins in excess of 10 per cent in both years.

    According to Statistics Singapore, residents in lower-income households and smaller HDB flats and holding jobs of a lower status rely more heavily on public transport; 4.8 per cent of working people with monthly incomes below $2,000 commute to work by car, as compared to 43.7 per cent of their counterparts earning $8,000 and above in 2005.

    According to Statistics Singapore, working commuters who switch between MRT and buses take on average 51 minutes to journey to their destination vis-?-vis 27 minutes for their car-commuting counterparts.

    A fare hike will hit lower-income groups harder, and raise income inequality as well as inflation.

    Although attempts to counteract this have been targeted at the lowest income groups, there will always be a marginal group that lies just above the cut-off line for assistance.

    A higher income inequality means that while the economy develops, the lower-income groups do not get a share of the progress.

    People in Singapore, a densely populated country, rely heavily on public transport to commute, leading to less traffic congestion. The social benefits of having low-priced and efficient public transport are thus enormous.

    Huang Junxiang
