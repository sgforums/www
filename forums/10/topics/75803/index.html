    Originally posted by nismoS132:
    so how is this different from us, japan or malaysia?
    As I mentioned above, in virtually every other country in the world, being a Member of Parliament or Congressman / Congresswoman or Senator is a full-time job.

    These national-level representatives are not allowed to hold down other jobs or directorships, they are expected to devote themselves full-time to serving the interests of their constitutents.

    Only local-level councillors, e.g. town hall representatives, etc are allowed to work in other jobs while in office. Once a politician moves into national-level representation, he / she is expected to have no other jobs or appointments, because the duties and responsibilities of their political office are supposed to be particularly onerous, and also because any other jobs they hold down may generate conflicts of interest with their political obligations.

    As a previous poster suggested, many companies in Singapore probably try very hard to get an MP to sit on their board of directors. Having an MP on its board of directors would effectively allow a company to extend its influence into Parliament, in an extremely blatant way!

    Can you imagine what would happen if an MP who also sits as a director of say, a telecoms company, were to stand up in Parliament one day and propose a Bill to give all telecoms companies major tax relief? Wouldn't any right thinking person believe that there would be something seriously wrong with that situation?

    Actually, I'm pretty sure that sort of thing already happens in our Parliament, it's just that we're not allowed to hear about it.

    In any other civilised country, this would be considered utterly scandalous, and a gross perversion of the democratic process. Parliamentarians are supposed to represent the interests of the people who elected them, not the interests of corporate masters.

    Goh to MPs: Keep public, private interests separate

    PRIME Minister Goh Chok Tong on Wednesday reminded Members of Parliament belonging to the People's Action Party that they should keep their business and employment interests separate from their service and duties as MPs.

    He added that the decision to take up such positions was solely that of the MPs, and that the Party would not - and could not - vet or approve these decisions.

    Noting that it had been several years since he last wrote to MPs on the matter and that many MPs were relatively new, he said he had decided to revisit the issue.

    His Thursday letter to party MPs explained that he was updating and consolidating his previous comments on the matter and revising the reporting requirements for MPs who take up company directorships.

    Mr Goh, who is also the Secretary-General of the PAP, noting that MPs were increasingly being invited to serve on the boards of private companies, wrote: 'This is a good sign that private sector companies value PAP MPs' integrity and service, and reflects the high standing of the Party and of PAP MPs in general.

    'The Party supports this practice, provided MPs rigorously separate their private and public responsibilities.'

    Whether making public statements on behalf of the companies they serve, raising questions in Parliament or dealing with government departments or public officers, they should make clear their interests and whether they are speaking as MPs or in their private, professional or business capacity.

    Warning MPs who are in business or sit on company boards to be 'especially vigilant', he said they must not exploit their public position or close contacts with ministers or their access to Government departments to lobby for their business interest or for their employers' benefit.

    MPs also should not solicit for directorships, wrote Mr Goh, lest they appear to be exploiting their political position to benefit themselves.

    They should also not sit on the boards of companies owned or chaired by grassroots leaders they have appointed, because this might give rise to the perception that the MP is obligated to the grassroots leader.

    And while MPs will continue to have access to ministers who are their parliamentary colleagues, MPs cannot expect policies or the decisions of ministries to be changed without first looking at the merits of the MPs' case.

    Mr Goh also reminded MPs that they were to keep the party Whip informed of all the directorships that they hold - a requirement since 2001 - and that they have till the end of January each year to declare their director's fees.

    He also warned his party's MPs to be on guard against companies who invite them to serve as directors, 'hoping that this will facilitate their business interactions with the government, or lend respectability to their operations', and said they should not sit on the boards of companies owned or chaired by grassroots leaders they have appointed.

    The advice for MPs wondering if a directorship may adversely impact his political life: 'Where he has any doubt, he should decline involvement.'
