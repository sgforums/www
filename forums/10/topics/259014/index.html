    No QC for Feer case, rules judge

    Defamation suit against it 'not complex enough'

    THE Singapore High Court has rejected the Far Eastern Economic Review's (Feer) application to use a British Queen's Counsel to represent the magazine in a defamation case.

    According to court documents obtained yesterday, Justice Tan Lee Meng ruled on Friday that the defamation suit filed against Feer Â— filed by Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong and Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew Â— was "not sufficiently difficult and complex" and will not require the services of a Queen's Counsel.

    The Hong Kong-based magazine had said in its affidavit it needed libel specialist Gavin Millar, who has successfully argued several high-profile cases in Britain, because of the complex nature of its defence.
    .
    The judge noted that the fact that Queen's Counsels have been admitted in the past for defamation cases cannot, by itself, justify their continued admission except in the most exceptional circumstances.
    .
    Said Justice Tan: "As the issues in the libel suits are not sufficiently difficult and complex and can be handled by local counsel ... the application to admit Mr Millar on an ad-hoc basis to represent the defendants in the libel suits is dismissed."

    The two Singapore leaders had sued the magazine's editor Hugo Restall and Hong Kong-based Review Publishing, its owners, alleging defamation in an article based on an interview with opposition leader Chee Soon Juan.

    Mr Peter Low, Feer's local lawyer, said the magazine has not decided to appeal against the decision. It has one month to do so.

    Meanwhile, a separate appeal filed by Feer is scheduled for hearing at the Court of Appeal early next month. The magazine is challenging the decision to have the Singapore courts preside over the defamation suits.

    Singapore banned the distribution of the magazine in September as it had failed to comply with media regulations. Â— Agencies
