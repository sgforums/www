    This will help Singapore raise new breed of entrepreneurs, says SM

    THE public sector is too talent-heavy and up to half its scholars should be released into the private sector after six or seven years, said Senior Minister Lee Kuan Yew.

    They should be sent into the private sector to become entrepreneurs and not just managers, he added.


    Right now, too many of Singapore's able are professionals when the country actually needs more business people.

    While the system has worked well before with the Government retaining the best brains, the next phase of development must focus on producing more entrepreneurs, he said.

    This way, they will not only have a solid knowledge of how government works but also 'the brain power and the drive' in the private sector.

    He made these points in an interview with Chinese daily Lianhe Zaobao, Channel U and Channel NewsAsia, broadcast last night.

    He noted how in global competitive rankings, such as those by the well-regarded Institute for Management Development (IMD), Singapore scores very highly on its public sector systems, from its laws to services.

    But it does not fare well on other criteria such as the level of entrepreneurship and venture capital.

    'What does that mean? That means too much concentration of talent, drive and energy in the public sector,' the Senior Minister said.

    It meant Singapore had the systems right, which was necessary at the beginning. But now, the Government could still provide youths with scholarships, but release them after they have worked for six or seven years.

    'Let half go, let one third go and get them to be entrepreneurs, not just managers.'

    Every year, the Public Service Commission awards 50 to 60 scholarships but there are also several hundred others given out by other government agencies and government-linked companies.

    The idea of pushing scholarship holders back into the private sector has been raised before, most recently by sub-committees within the Economic Review Committee.

    The sub-committees had debated whether it was time to change the scholarship system but no decision was taken, other than an affirmation that the public sector still needed its share of the top brains.

    The subject of entrepreneurs emerged during the interview Mr Lee gave to talk about the 10th anniversary of the Suzhou Industrial Park (SIP). He is now in Suzhou for the celebrations tomorrow.

    On Suzhou, he said one lesson learnt has been that there must be 'total alignment' of interests at the central, provincial and local levels before starting projects.

    When the SIP started in 1994, Singapore controlled 65 per cent of the stakes, and China the rest. The idea was for Singapore to transfer its software and knowledge to the Chinese, using SIP as a model development.

    But the park soon ran into problems, which were only solved after the controlling stakes were reversed.

    Mr Lee said that if this had been done from the start, there could have been a higher level of software transfer. Still, he added: 'We never walked away from the project, we put our best efforts in and solved the problem. Therefore, I would say because we had all these difficulties, there is a deeper appreciation on both sides that we are long-term players. We are not fly-by-night operators. When we promise, we deliver, whatever the difficulties.'

    He said the value of being involved in Suzhou is to have it now as an icon of the 'Singapore quality, integrity and capability'. He also revealed that China now wants Singapore to go into projects in the west and north-east. It is considering them but must not take on more than it can deliver, he added.

    He also underscored how the Suzhou experience had forged relations at the top: 'If we have not had Suzhou, they would not have understood or they would not have been so convinced that we have something to offer. Just think of it, we're three, four million with the workers from abroad, and they're 1,300 million.

    'So, why should they meet us at that high level? But I think that they have found that although we are small, we have systems which they can apply throughout many of their hundreds of cities.'

    SME association to set up attachment scheme for government scholars

    SINGAPORE : The Association of Small And Medium Enterprises is setting up a "surrogate scheme" that will allow government scholars to do attachments at one of its 1,000 member companies here.

    Senior Minister Lee Kuan Yew's radical idea to release up to half the Government's scholars to become entrepreneurs has found support.

    The Association of Small and Medium Enterprises, which is made up mostly of entrepreneurs, says it is a good idea, especially since scholars are bright, can add value and know how the government works.

    "These scholars should then be taught entrepreneurial skills to enable them to move into entrepreneurship, to transit smoothly into entrepreneurship. This takes time and it's a challenge for scholars as most of them are actually fresh from university into the public sector and the mechanics of the private sector needs to be made known to the scholars," said ASME's Aloysius Tan.

    So the group will set up a "surrogate scheme" in about a year for government-sponsored scholars to spend time at blue-chip SMEs.

    Other entrepreneurs Channel NewsAsia spoke to feel that the irony is that a high paying position of a top scholar is a deterrent to entrepreneurship.

    They feel that if a scholar is already making good money, there is less incentive to take the risk, come out and make his own money.

    Multi-award winning entrepreneur Kenny Yap says a scholar first needs to have the passion and drive to make it on his own.

    He is no scholar, but now heads Qian Hu, a multi-million dollar, publicly-listed ornamental fish firm.

    "I think you have to ask those scholars who want to become businessmen, do you really want to become businessmen, rather than the question of, hey it's time for you to become an entrepreneur, let you become an entrepreneur," said Mr Yap.

    "II think for entrepreneurship, you cannot further structure it; you should provide the environment, you should provide the freedom for people to act, to think, to choose." - CNA
