    Striking a balance

    Singapore feeling its way forward in establishing a system that works best for it, says PM Lee

    By Aaron Low

    For Singapore, Mr Lee said, having a good system is important, but getting good people to run it is equally crucial. -- ST FILE PHOTO

    SINGAPORE'S model of governance is one that tries to achieve a balance between delivering quality leadership and giving people the opportunity to voice their dissatisfaction with government policies, said Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong.

    It is a system that has worked well and one that has not let the people of Singapore down, he told a German newspaper.

    'How do you keep a system which delivers an outstanding quality of leadership and government, and yet at the same time, have free play and the opportunity for people to say, 'I want this government, but I do not like this policy or the other'?'

    The ideal system, he said, balances both. It delivers a good government, while providing for aspirations to be fulfilled and voices - the full range of them - to be articulated. 'This is the ideal,' he said in an interview published in the Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung last week.

    The newspaper wanted to know if Singapore would become 'even more democratic' in future, as it had recently relaxed its rules on public assemblies and was introducing changes to the political system to enable more opposition voices in Parliament.

    In his reply, Mr Lee said Singapore, like other countries, is feeling its way forward in establishing a system that works best for it. What would not work, he stressed, is to simply rely on the simplistic approach that 'if you develop, you will need democracy'.

    For Singapore, Mr Lee said, having a good system is important, but getting good people to run it is equally crucial. He rebutted the suggestion that the system was unfair, saying that the opposition had a fair chance to compete and contest in general elections.

    In fact, he pointed out that in a small country like Singapore, the ruling party would get booted out very quickly if it lost the mandate of the people. 'It is for us to make sure that we continue to merit their confidence and their support and deliver a good government,' said Mr Lee, who is also secretary-general of the People's Action Party.

    The German newspaper also asked Mr Lee why there were 'no signs of dissatisfaction' with the Government, despite the country facing one of its worst recessions since independence. There were three reasons for this, said Mr Lee.

    The first is that many workers see what the Government is doing to help them, such as through training, which keeps them employable. Another reason is the special measures like the Jobs Credit scheme, which helps employers defray part of the wage bill of local workers. This has helped companies keep workers rather than retrench them, he said. 'And thirdly, when there are problems, they are able to talk to us and we respond and deal with these problems,' said Mr Lee.

    These measures have helped buffer the impact of the recession on Singaporeans, preventing unemployment from shooting up. Said Mr Lee: 'If you look at the people on the streets and in the shops, life goes on. It is a serious situation, but it is not a calamity.'
