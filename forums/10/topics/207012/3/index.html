    But if you take a few large steps backwards, and you take a long look at the whole picture, wouldn't you wonder about the following:

    Evidently, it's an unglamourous job: getting your hands dirty and reeking of filth. Which like-minded Singaporean would wanna slob a living in this manner?

    But if it pays well.....who cares? You've struck a chord by suggesting that managing trash collection pays dividends. Now it's a question of sharing the profits with your garbage collectors.

    I really believe that if you pay garbage collectors 1K a month, give them additional responsibilities such as catagorizing the garbage (eg: recycle products vs biodegradable) and rename their vocation into something less demeaning, our locals may just hop on the bandwagon...

    But i'm just an idealist.

    Originally posted by ShutterBug:
    >> Escalating costs of living is the main factor that people aren't taking up certain jobs, but now, with all the FTs in abundance, even high level position salaries are devalued by FTs - who the hell can support a family much less have a baby if one is expected to outperform and take a lower paycheck as FTs or even lower?
    Competition has stressed out the market. Everyone is talking about cost-cutting measures. It's always easier to cut costs then venture out and make MORE MONEY to manage your overheads.

    This is something i never understood. But it's the standard practice in every company, be it GLCs or Private enterprises. It's generally recognized (though foolishly exaggerated) that locals are fussy, high-maintenance and unwilling to step out of their comfort zones. FTs are way easier to handle, ask for less and almost always give more.

    So why not? Employers always lament that locals have only themselves to blame by refusing to accept pay cuts and jumping ship at the earliest convenience when 'greener pastures' come beckoning.

    If our people break out of this stereotype, they may yet make a difference. Till then, i simply see FTs as the competition. And it is a competition i have to beat to stay relevant.

    Originally posted by ShutterBug:
    >> Maids are imported, why not let local aunties take the role? No levies to collect maybe?
    Maybe this is a question meant for the aunties. Again it's a paradox. By mass importing maids and making it an industry onto itself, aunties have the mistaken impression that by taking on cleaning jobs in direct competition to maids, it 'degrades' them to the same level of the 'maids'. And that itself is demeaning and unglamourous.

    Who would have seen these turn of events 20 years ago? Back then, for a nominal fee, these aunties would work the day shift cleaning the house, ironing the clothes and buying food for the kids when the parents were not in.

    Originally posted by ShutterBug:
    >> Why do they say Medical and Health Care is affordable when most employers still shun old workers because of the need to provide medical benefits, and potential loss of man-hours due to health.

    We shouldn't kid ourselves that the gov is taking care of our interests.. if so, most of the above scenarios would have been different.
    While never willing to admit it, many companies are still sex and age discriminatory. Women who are likely to get pregnant and employees above the age of say 50 tend to be 'high-maintenance', and companies measure their productivity by the man-hours put in. The yard stick comes when critical periods are filled with maternity leave or long-term illnesses.

    I don't think health costs and medical benefits come into the picture just yet. It's just a matter of the company's driven culture to demand the best out of each employee that forces their hand in such circumstances.
