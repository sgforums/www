    Drive for foreign talent to become citizens may create greater rather than lighter socio-economic burden

    THE drive for foreign talent to become citizens - or even the subject of national economic survival - cannot be a mere matter of boosting the population in absolute figures, as the letter, 'Could Singapore survive another 80 years?' (ST, Aug 31), seems to be dwell on repeatedly.
    This is because, if that were the real consideration, there is no lack of foreigners without requisite qualifications who want to become Singapore citizens. The lengths to which illegal workers go to smuggle themselves into Singapore just to find menial jobs shows this is so.

    In any case, if we are not careful about the new drive for foreign talent to become citizens, Singapore could end up with a greater rather than a lighter socio-economic burden.

    This is because for every immigrant who becomes a Singapore citizen, there are family members who will tag along, including the aged and infirm who will become citizens or permanent residents with certain privileges conferred by the state such as subsidised health care.

    And given there is officially no state welfare in Singapore, it also follows that the added burden of an increased population will fall on society in general. How will society cope, seeing that presently the community welfare system is already under strain?

    Viability of the economy is more about the appropriateness of overall economic strategies and less about population size. Smaller populations than Singapore, like those of Monaco and Macau, thrive precisely because of this economic strategy factor.

    There are many ramifications to this issue of attracting foreign talent to take up citizenship. Some issues the Government and society can look into include:

    * How many of the 100,000 Hong Kongers said to have migrated here after the 1989 Tiananmen incident stayed put and how have they contributed to the economy and society?

    * There are many fields of endeavour and in-depth knowledge of these fields needed to screen citizenship applicants expertly is hard to come by. Thus if we are not careful, instead of talent we end up with 'pseudo talent' - which society will have to put up with simply because they are now Singapore citizens.

    * Talented individuals without paper qualifications, which the Government now also wants, will be difficult if not impossible to identify before they become successful and well known. But if they are already successful, it is doubtful they will be bothered with the citizenship issue as they will be welcome anywhere in the world.

    Is it not too sweeping to say that because our forefathers were immigrants, we should be open to new immigrants? There is a wide disparity between the state of development of Singapore then and now.

    * Skilled manpower needs can be met as done presently by employing foreign professionals. In other instances, outsourcing or sub-contracting to foreign companies at costs that are lower than localised operations has proved a viable approach. Foreign call centres and software development are two good examples.

    There is more flexibility with these options because in an economic downturn, society does not have to cope with a high unemployment rate.

    Chia Hern Keng
