    SINGAPORE : Singapore's media industry is undergoing a major shake up.
    The island's largest broadcaster MediaCorp and main-board listed Singapore Press Holdings will merge their mass-market television and free newspaper operations in what the two media giants called a 'rationalisation move' to stem losses and improve shareholder value.

    On Friday afternoon, top management from the two media giants signed agreements for SPH to buy a 20 percent stake in a new television company to be called MediaCorp TV Holdings.

    MediaCorp will manage this new company and own the remaining 80 percent.

    MediaCorp TV Holdings will have a net asset value of S$50m.

    SPH will also buy a 40 percent stake in MediaCorp Press, which publishes TODAY.

    TODAY will continue to be managed by MediaCorp - and will operate independently of the stable of newspapers under SPH.

    The deals were struck on a "willing buyer, willing seller" basis and should receive formal regulatory approval by year's end.

    Since Singapore's media liberalisation in May 2000, both SPH and MediaCorp have incurred losses - after their respective forays into each other's businesses proved unprofitable.

    SPH will pay S$10 million for their 20 percent stake in the new TV company MediaCorp TV Holdings.

    The new company will comprise MediaCorp's Channels 5 and 8 and TV Mobile - as well as SPH MediaWorks' Channel U and I.

    The new television company will also include MediaCorp Studios - which produces the bulk of MediaCorp's local programming.

    Channel U will continue to operate but Channel I's commercial viability has to be reviewed.

    On the newspaper side, MediaCorp will sell a 40 percent stake in MediaCorp Press, which publishes TODAY, to rival SPH for more than S$19 million.

    Once the deal is completed, SPH will merge its own free newspaper Streats with TODAY.

    TODAY will continue to be managed by MediaCorp, and will operate independently of the stable of newspapers under SPH.

    The rest of MediaCorp's businesses, which are largely profitable are unaffected by this merger.

    Besides Channel NewsAsia, these include radio and the other television businesses of Suria and Central as well as its film production arm MediaCorp Raintree Pictures.

    The mergers will affect staff on both sides.

    A staff rationalisation exercise will be overseen by a Manpower Synergy Task Force.

    It will comprise an independent director from MediaCorp's board and senior HR managers from the two media companies.

    Alan Chan, the chief executive of SPH, said the merger will bring rationality back into the TV and free newspaper markets and should ultimately lead both businesses to flourish.

    He said the merger is also a win-win solution for both parties - with SPH maintaining its presence in the TV platform and having a stake in the only free newspaper in Singapore (TODAY).

    He added that when SPH ventured into the TV business in 2000, the outlook and potential seemed positive then.

    However, a slackening economy, intense competition and a shrinking advertising market, led to losses for both groups.

    The merger has put a spotlight on what's to come for advertisers.

    Concerns have been raised as to how much advertising rates will go up.

    On this, Mr Chan said: "We can only charge what the market can bear. But having said that, because of the steep discounting that has been going on during these four years of competition, actually the rates have gone below the pre-competition era. And so I think there is great impetus for the new companies to survive, and we have to adjust the rates to a level where the losses are gone."

    On its part, MediaCorp assures advertisers that the rates won't be increased drastically. - CNA

    Media release by MediaCorp .
