    Singaporeans trained to work, not to retire: elderly
    By Loh Chee Kong, TODAY

    They are well educated and established in their respective professions, but when it comes to growing old, their worry is as basic as this - jobs.

    At a government feedback session organised for the elderly last Friday, the 30-odd participants - ranging from consultants to senior engineers and company directors - agreed on one thing: Singapore is "not a good place to retire in".

    It is hard to find and sustain employment in one's old age because of the ageist sentiments of employers, they said.

    A retiree who spent a large part of her life in the United Kingdom noted that "the old are being marginalised".

    "Singapore is not a good place to retire in," she said. "The old should be enjoying their time, not working as cleaners."

    Another participant, a 55-year-old consultant, added that Singaporeans are "trained to work, not to retire".

    "It seems that people like me are being forced to work overseas. I'm planning to go to Malaysia through its 'silver-haired scheme'," he said.

    Mr Phua Kok Tee, the 68-year-old CEO of Singapore Action Group for the Elderly, said that more should be done to tap into the service industry to provide jobs for the elderly.

    "For example, in Borders in the United States, 16 per cent of its staff are 50 years old and above, as compared to just 6 per cent in 1999.

    "This only came about after a conscious and concerted effort to provide employment for the elderly," he said.

    This "constant cry for employment", given the participants' ages and relatively successful backgrounds, surprised Tampines GRC MP Sin Boon Ann, who chaired the session.

    The 47-year-old MP asked the participants: "It's interesting how many of you are still thinking about finding or sustaining employment. Why not retire early and enjoy life? If it's a case of finances, what are your expectations?"

    He added: "I suspect in Singapore, our expectations are set by the pace of life. A lot of pleasures don't have to cost the sky. For me, it can be just bringing my family to the beach."

    If it is a question of financial security, many Singaporeans are putting unnecessary stress on themselves, he said.

    But isn't the high cost of living a real issue?

    Speaking to Today, Mr Sin said: "The cost of living could be a concern but I do not think that it is such a worry as to cause the elderly to be pre-occupied with staying employed.

    "With adequate savings, planning and careful budgeting Â… they should have enough to see them through their old age."

    He went on to assure Singapore's greying population that the country would not leave them behind.

    "What they should perhaps realise is that we have a social safety net to look after them. They should take comfort in the fact that, should they run out of savings when they are older, there is always the family to fall back on.

    "If there is no family support, then of course, the society and the community will look after them. And this is what making Singapore as a nation is all about." - TODAY/sh
