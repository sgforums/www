    Get out of Singapore, Peter

    By Chris Smith

    July 24, 2008 12:00am

    I CAST my memory back to an Australian-born blonde, who was arrested in Bangkok in 1996 for smuggling drugs.

    With 4kg of cannabis and 500 amphetamine tablets, she faced a death sentence. There she sat, curled up and weeping, inside Lard Yao women's prison, also known as the "Bangkok Hilton".

    To describe this facility as dangerous and disgusting is supremely generous. She was sitting with 120 other women, in a rat-infested dorm.

    Soon after, her rich daddy came to the rescue, posting $80,000 bail. An Australian Embassy car took her from prison and she was never seen in public again.

    Twelve years later, Lisa Marie Smith remains one of the world's most wanted fugitives.

    Some reports say she could be in Europe, maybe Greece, where she has undergone cosmetic surgery to avoid detection.

    Australian Peter Lloyd, a well-read journalist who has reported on the fate of Schapelle Corby and the Bali Nine, may well have thought of Lisa Marie Smith's escape when he was granted release from a Singapore jail yesterday after posting $45,000 bail.

    Escape is a risky manoeuvre. If it backfires, it could see him slapped with 20 additional years on whatever sentence he got if found guilty for drug trafficking and/or possession of the drug ice.

    Is there room, secretly, to adopt the Michelle Leslie strategy, and pay his way out of the more serious charges? In the days after her arrest, Leslie's boyfriend made a series of cash transfers, which have since been alleged to be the first steps towards bribing her way out of police custody.

    That is justice Bali-style, but in strict Singapore that could see him face further charges.

    Instead he could defend his serious charges of seemingly indefensible proportions - a perilous route to traverse.

    Not for me, not ever. He should flee the region at the first opportunity. I would, without question.

    Walking into one of Asia's most filthy and intimidating jails will immediately convert anyone to angelic pursuits. Whether it's Singapore's Changi Prison, or Bangkok's Bang Kwang maximum-security jail, it's enough to bring on acute nausea.

    At the latter jail, nearly 8000 men in death row conditions serve sentences of 20 years or more for big-ticket crimes - from drug smuggling to murder.

    I met convicted drug trafficker Paul Hudson there in the '80s. Maybe it was the ball and chain that threw me most; then again, the decrepit and putrid group cells turned my stomach.

    Then there was the sight of the late Australian League player Paul Hayward, shivering and sickly in his cell. The armed guards had this sneering nastiness, like some tatty gang of bandits ready to maim the next prisoner who stepped out of line.

    These are the realities now facing our alleged drug trafficker and popular TV reporter as he contemplates life in his own unmitigated hell hole. He has lost his career. He now risks contracting disease, such as HIV through prison rape. And reports of his status as a gay man will not help.

    My advice is to run, Peter, run - because there's a ball and chain with your name on it.

    Why your first-hand experiences of life inside such deadly squalor, gained as a reporter, were not enough to steer you clear of trouble in this region, is an unfathomable mystery to me.

    Chris Smith is the afternoon announcer for Sydney radio station 2GB.
