    Another letter from a fellow doctor incensed by the 'hopeless' police:

    When does an assault become a criminal case?

    Aug 17, 2006
    The Straits Times

    I REFER to the letter, 'Assault 'a civil case', so no police action' (ST, Aug , by Ms Liew Sok Kuan.
    Singapore reputedly possesses an excellent legal system and public security. I am therefore astounded as to why the police would label the assault on the doctor at a food stall as merely a civil case, and not criminal activity.

    If, indeed, a group of hooligans can approach any peace-loving citizen in a food centre and give that person a beating without infringing any criminal law, I would fear for the safety of my family and friends.

    As the incident was earlier classified as a civil case, the victim would theoretically be required to garner evidence if he wanted to pursue the issue in court. On top of that, he must be able to cough up $10,000 to $20,000 to file a civil suit against the perpetrators. Therefore, if the victim is unable to gather the required witnesses, or to pay for the civil suit, the perpetrators would go scot-free.

    Although the case was subsequently reclassified under Section 325 from Section 323, I agree with Ms Liew's observation that 'the attitude of the police, as demonstrated in this case, will only serve to embolden hooligans to commit even more such offences'. Hypothetically speaking, a person can beat up anyone in the street, and the police cannot carry out any arrest, investigation or prosecution because it is only a civil case.

    This begs the question: Should law-abiding citizens call the police if they witness such cases of assault? Or should they fold their arms, and let the victim settle the dispute in the Magistrates' Court?

    Furthermore, an assault is presumably not criminal unless there is outrage of modesty, rape or grievous injury, or death - in which case it would be manslaughter or murder.

    Should we, then, avoid calling the police until the incident evolves into a more serious scenario, that is, until the perpetrator sexually abuses, maims or kills the victim?

    The police should clarify what constitutes criminal assault and what constitutes a civil offence. In this way, victims in a similar case would know what is the best course of action to take.

    Dr Vincent Chia Wei Meng

    Implications on public security troubling

    I REFER to Ms Audrey Ang's reply, 'Police explain actions taken in assault case' (ST, Aug 16). While Ms Ang may have clarified Dr Liew's case, I find some statements relating to general public security more appalling than appeasing.
    I always look up to our men in blue as our first line of defence when things go wrong. I shudder to think that our law enforcers can become, or seem to be, powerless, as in the case of violence involving Dr Liew, who suffered a bloodied nose.

    If all policemen were to follow this 'standard operating procedure', are we not allowing the culprits more time to escape, thus enabling and emboldening them to repeat their crime somewhere else?

    Daniel Chan Wai Piew

    ST Forum

    Revise the penal code to give police more powers to fight crime
    Aug 18, 2006

    It is disconcerting to read the explanation of Ms Audrey Ang, Assistant Director (Media Relations), Singapore Police Force (ST Aug 16) on police inaction regarding Dr Liew's report of assault (Ms Liew Sok Kuan's letter "Assault a civil case, so no police action", ST, Aug .

    The police force is and should be seen as an effective guardian of public order and security.

    Ms Ang's reply that the police requires a magistrate's authorisation to commence investigations into a simple report of assault is sadly incongruous with the lofty and noble work that policemen are expected to do by ordinary citizens.

    Does the police have to operate within the shadow of a magistrate, a junior court officer, in investigation work, notwithstanding its public standing and organisational structure?

    Can the police inspire public confidence when its investigations are hindered and the victim of an offence is made to suffer pain and injustice as a result, or when some bad hats escape and go scot-free due to the delay?

    Clearly, the penal code needs to be revised for our police force to be more responsive and competent in tackling crime.

    Above all, Dr Liew's case demonstrates that the devil is in the law as contained in Section 323 of the penal code, which has made chickens of the police officers at the scene, albeit they "should have treated the incident more seriously based on the circumstances that led to the assault on Dr Liew."

    It behooves the Singapore Police Force to scrutinise and update its enabling legislation from time to time in order that its work is not compromised in any way. The police should strive for no less than excellence in this respect.

    Yap Swee Hoo
    ST Forum

    I refer to the letter "Assault 'a civil case', so no police action" by Ms Liew Sok Kuan (ST, Aug .
    The attack reminds me of an elderly man who was assaulted by a volunteer at an old age centre in Bukit Merah two years ago. The victim was repeatedly pounded on his face and right temple until his head swelled with concussion.

    As his caregiver, I reported the assault to a nearby police post but was told that a magistrate court's order was required for the police to act. The reason was that the police would not investigate unless there was bleeding and/or bone fracture.

    Despite being puzzled by the bureaucracy, I complied and obtained the magistrate's order for the old man. Unfortunately, the police merely followed up with some questioning of related parties and told me later that they would not pursue the case further. The police classified it as a "civil" case.

    How do the authorities expect a feeble and handicapped old man surviving on social welfare money to have the means to take up legal recourse on the matter? In the end, he dropped the case.

    I can vividly remember the smile of satisfaction on the assailant's face the instant we told the Subordinate Court's mediator to close the file.

    From what I gathered, the volunteer who carried out the assault still continued his service at the centre and a staff who stood up as a witness to the assault had been sacked. Where's justice?

    The role of our police has been significantly altered over the past ten years to include security, such as the threat of terrorism. However, a rethink of police duties must be done but not at the expense of public safety.

    Jimmy Ho Kwok Hoong
