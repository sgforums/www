    However, the downside is that democracy threatens the autonomy of those in power who inevitably, through the argument of coherence or otherwise, evoke the legal tool to keep others from wrestling control through increasingly sophisticated and diversified designs to coerce the disenfranchised to comply with the rules established by those in power. This is inevitably transformed into imbalanced electoral designs to perpetuate vested interests. Some countries successfully fight electoral injustice effectively whilst others are less successful. The primary reason behind this is the type of governance of which is shaped by a country’s political, economic and cultural history and the populace’s maturity and understanding of their political autonomy. It is less a problem for countries that adhere to the rule of law where there are ministerial responsibility and accountability and that procedures are transparent; but it would be otherwise for those that do not have possess such democratic norms, especially countries in transition from authoritarianism.
