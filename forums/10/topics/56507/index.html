    Reclamation in S'pore: KL takes legal action
    Malaysia breaks off talks, refers dispute to arbitration; Republic disappointed at sudden decision but confident of its case

    By Lydia Lim

    MALAYSIA has launched a legal battle against Singapore by referring its unhappiness over reclamation at Pulau Tekong and Tuas to international arbitration, instead of continuing negotiations.

    It has also applied separately to put an immediate stop to the reclamation works.

    Expressing disappointment over its neighbour's actions, Singapore yesterday described them as 'premature and unnecessary'.

    'It is regrettable that Malaysia has not made a serious attempt to resolve the matter through negotiations,' said a Ministry of Foreign Affairs (MFA) spokesman.

    Foreign Minister S. Jayakumar also told reporters later that he found Malaysia's sudden decision to break off talks 'strange and disappointing'.

    Speaking on the sidelines of the opening of the Apec secretariat building, he said: 'We very much prefer to have negotiations and an amicable solution but they've now decided to take this route and it is their right.'

    Malaysia has also made the immediate stoppage of reclamation works as a precondition to further talks.

    Rejecting this, the MFA spokesman said that the suspension of works was a very serious matter and would be justified only if there was 'clear evidence of serious and imminent danger'.

    'Singapore has provided Malaysia with technical reports and explained in detail that our studies indicate that no significant adverse effects will result from our reclamation works. Malaysia's demand is therefore not justified,' she said.

    Both the MFA statement and Professor Jayakumar's comments came in the wake of a press release on Friday by the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea. It announced Malaysia's request for 'provisional measures', including to stop the reclamation in the western and eastern parts of Singapore.

    These measures are an interim step that states can take to protect their interests before a case goes to arbitration.

    According to the press release, Malaysia said the works were causing 'serious and irreversible damage to the marine environment and serious prejudice' to its rights.

    Malaysia first raised its objections to the reclamation in January last year.

    Following its complaints, Singapore repeatedly asked Kuala Lumpur to back its claims with technical reports. It did not do so until July 4 this year.

    Meanwhile, Malaysian politicians and officials alleged through their media that the reclamation encroached on Malaysia's territorial waters, narrowed the shipping lane and damaged their country's interests in various other ways.

    Singapore rebutted all the claims.

    On July 4, Malaysia sent a diplomatic note giving Singapore notice that it was referring the matter to international arbitration under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.

    It gave the long-delayed technical reports and demanded that Singapore respond within two weeks, or it would apply to stop the works.

    Singapore gave its reports despite the short notice.

    And at Singapore's suggestion, officials and experts also met here on Aug 13 and 14.

    'It was a good start to understanding each other's concerns,' said the MFA spokesman. She added, however, that given the complex and highly technical discussions, it was clear more meetings were needed as both sides had only identified the issues that divided them.

    Singapore had suggested a second meeting to be hosted by Malaysia.

    But on Sept 5, Malaysia applied to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, signalling it was closing the door to further negotiations.

    Yesterday, the MFA spokesman expressed confidence that 'Singapore's position will be vindicated' when the issue goes before international arbitration and adjudication.

    As Singapore is small and land is scarce, reclamation was necessary for its survival and prosperity, she said, and its reclamation plans had always been in the public domain.

    'In all our reclamation works, Singapore acted in conformity with our rights and obligations under international law.'

    Actions currently taken by Malaysia:

    TWO LEGAL TRACKS

    Malaysia is doing two things on the legal front at the same time. First, it is seeking arbitration to decide whether Singapore has a right to reclaim land in Tuas and Tekong. Second, it wants to put an immediate stop to the ongoing reclamation works.

    TRACK #1

    Under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea, countries have three options to settle disputes. They can go either to the International Court of Justice, the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea, or decide on arbitration.

    When Malaysia and Singapore signed the Convention, neither specified the route each preferred. By default, their recourse will be via arbitration.

    On July 4, Malaysia served notice to Singapore and started arbitration proceedings. The arbitration tribunal, which has yet to be set up, will comprise five members agreed upon by both sides. They have so far chosen one representative each, with the remaining three yet to be decided. The process starts only after the panel is set up.

    TRACK #2

    Pending the setting up of the arbitration tribunal, Malaysia wants Singapore to stop the reclamation works immediately.

    On Sept 5, it applied to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea for what is called 'provisional measures' to stop the reclamation. This process is usually swift. The tribunal may hold a hearing within two weeks and gives its decision soon after. It may rule as early as the end of this month.

    -------------------------------------

    WHO WILL BE IN SINGAPORE'S LEGAL TEAM?

    Foreign Affairs and Law Minister S. Jayakumar said Ambassador-at-large Tommy Koh, who was the president of the Third UN Conference on the Law of the Sea, will lead the legal team.

    The minister expressed 'full confidence' in the team, which will comprise experts from the Attorney-General's Chambers, the Foreign Affairs and National Development ministries, and foreign counsel and experts.

    THE UN CONVENTION ON THE LAW OF THE SEA

    It lays down a comprehensive regime of law and order in the world's oceans and seas. It sets out rules governing all uses of the oceans and their resources. It enshrines the idea that all problems of ocean space are closely interrelated and need to be addressed as a whole.

    January 2002:

    One-and-a-half years later, Malaysia sends the reports and a Third Person Note, which is a diplomatic note, giving Singapore notice that it is referring the matter to international arbitration. It gives Singapore two weeks to respond and an ultimatum to also stop reclamation works within two weeks.

    Sept 5, 2003: Malaysia closes the door on negotiations and applies to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea for provisional measures to stop Singapore's reclamation works.

    DISPUTE OVER LAND RECLAMATION
    Mahathir defends decision to end talks
    Negotiations with Singapore failed so KL referred the problem to arbitration, says Malaysian Prime Minister

    By Brendan Pereira

    KUALA LUMPUR - Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad said Malaysia sought international arbitration in its dispute with Singapore over reclamation at Pulau Tekong and Tuas after a series of negotiations with the Republic ended in failure.

    Defending his country's course of action, he said: 'We have tried to negotiate for years but they have gone and told others that they are right in this issue.

    'Previously, we opted for negotiations but negotiations failed. Surely, you don't expect us to go to war.'

    He used an analogy to explain why international arbitration was a suitable option.

    'When two people fight and cannot settle it, they bring the matter to a third party,' he told reporters after a day trip to his home state of Kedah.

    On Friday, Malaysia referred its concerns over reclamation to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea. It applied to put an immediate stop to the works being carried out.

    This move came as a surprise as both countries were well into the negotiating process after Malaysia sent a diplomatic note on July 4, giving Singapore notice that it was referring the matter to international arbitration under the United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea.

    Malaysia also gave technical reports to back up its claim that the reclamation had damaged the environment and encroached on Malaysian territory, and demanded that Singapore respond within two weeks.

    Despite the short notice, Singapore gave its reports, assuring Malaysia that the reclamation works had not, and would not, have any significant impact on the specific concerns raised by Malaysia.

    At Singapore's suggestion, officials and experts met in Singapore on Aug 13 and 14. A week later, officials from both sides exchanged more technical reports.

    A spokesman for Singapore's Ministry of Foreign Affairs said: 'It was a good start to understanding each other's concerns. However, given the complex and highly technical nature of the subject, it was clear that further meetings were necessary, as we had only identified the issues which divide us.'

    Singapore had suggested a second meeting to be hosted by Malaysia.

    But on Friday, Malaysia chose a different path, signalling that it was closing the door to further negotiations.

    Malaysia has made the immediate stoppage of reclamation a precondition for talks.

    Singapore said that further talks should be held without any preconditions, adding that it was disappointed that Malaysia had decided to submit the matter to arbitration and seek what is called 'provisional' measures to stop the reclamation    .

    Foreign Minister Syed Hamid Albar said Malaysia was forced to take the dispute to international arbitration because Singapore had been unwilling to settle the matter at the negotiating table.

    By seeking arbitration, Malaysia was doing its part to keep bilateral relations on an even keel and prevent matters from becoming tense.

    Singapore, he said, had been given many opportunities to settle the matter but it had refused to budge from its position.

    'They asked for evidence. We have handed over many types of reports and held discussions with them. We feel there is no other way to resolve this matter,' he told reporters after touring his parliamentary constituency in Johor.

    He said Malaysia was not interested in getting involved in an argument with Singapore and would leave it to the international tribunal to decide on the dispute.

    He hoped that the Hamburg-based body would issue a temporary ruling ordering Singapore to stop all reclamation immediately.

    SEPT 10, 2003

    Reclamation puzzle? It's all about white elephants

    Chua Lee Hoong

    PUZZLED by why Malaysia is taking Singapore to court over reclamation that's taking place strictly within the latter's territorial waters? Well, don't be. Malaysia's New Straits Times made the reason clear in its editorial yesterday.

    Commenting that reclamation works around Pulau Tekong would result in a narrowing of what it calls the Tebrau Straits and Singapore calls the Johor Straits, it noted: 'Malaysia is acting responsibly in safeguarding the future of Tanjung Pelepas and Pasir Gudang, which stand to become white elephants if reclamation continues.'

    And there you have it. Beneath the mumbo jumbo about sedimentation, damage to the marine environment and preservation of Malaysia's right of maritime access to its coastline, the crux is simple: bilateral economic competition.

    It's a competition that has become more intense since Malaysia decided in the late 1990s to develop its Port of Tanjung Pelepas (PTP) to rival Singapore's PSA Corp.

    Malaysia thinks reclamation works at Pulau Tekong will stymie its development. Pasir Gudang, opposite Singapore's Pasir Ris, is a feeder port through which a lot of cargo transit, some on their way west to the PTP, and some north either to Senai Airport or the inland peninsula.

    At present, most cargo vessels approach Pasir Gudang from the east through Singapore waters south of Pulau Tekong, instead of through Malaysian waters to the north, as the latter are shallower.

    On Aug 27, Business Times also broke the story that the Sembawang shipyard - a former British base which does mostly repairs now - will be moving to Tuas. The move is part of SembCorp Marine's consolidation of ageing shipward facilities now spread between Jurong and Sembawang.

    For Malaysia, however, the impending move must have sent alarm bells ringing: Once the Sembawang shipyard is closed, Singapore won't need a deep-water passageway for ships in those straits. It would have nothing to lose if it decided to build a bridge between Pulau Tekong and the mainland. Malaysia's Pasir Gudang goose would be cooked!

    Ah, now the penny drops! That is why Malaysia is rushing to replace its end of the Causeway with a bridge. For no matter how crooked or unsightly it would be, it would serve one crucial purpose: allow ships to pass through. A crooked cat is still a cat as long as it catches mice.

    So is this a cat-and-mouse game in which one side is choking off access and the other scrambling to open new access lines?

    Nationalistic Singaporeans with no love for the northern neighbour would see it that way. But Malaysia would be loath to be the mouse to the Singapore cat.

    For one, they would have been caught napping if so. This is because reclamation works at Pulau Tekong and Pulau Ubin were announced in Parliament as early as January 1992, but Malaysia did not voice objections until January last year.

    In fact, then Malaysian Transport Minister, Dr Ling Liong Sik, told the Malaysian Parliament in February last year that Singapore's reclamation works had not affected the navigation line leading to Pasir Gudang. The Marine Department, he said, had investigated the matter, and found no impact.

    As for Tanjung Pelepas, it's too far away from any reclamation works to be affected.

    To my mind, the port factor aside, there's another reason why Malaysia would want to refer the reclamation matter to international arbitration.

    It's related to the Pedra Branca case which Singapore and Malaysia recently referred to the International Court of Justice (ICJ). When there are two cases involving the same two countries, what are the chances of the same country winning both? The law of averages - and of human nature - would say these are lower than that of both countries winning a case each.

    To be sure, the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea (ITLOS), which is hearing the reclamation case, is an entirely different panel from the ICJ. The ICJ is a 15-member bench while the ITLOS has 21 members.

    The fraternity of distinguished experts in public international law is, however, a small one. Members of the two panels must be nominated by member states, and are elected by secret ballot. Politics has featured often in the history of both panels; as many judgments have been made on political grounds as on their legal merits.

    How will the reclamation case pan out in court? The merits of the case are obviously in Singapore's favour; it would take severely contorted legal logic to argue that reclamation works in Singapore waters have caused serious prejudice to Malaysia's rights of maritime passage.

    But Malaysia, clearly, is basing its arguments on more than law, at least to its domestic audience. Editorials in both the Malay and English press flog the old line about the claims of friendship and neighbourliness.

    Putting it with polish but disingenuity, the New Straits Times said: '(Singapore) should decide once and for all if civility should be the order of the day in dealings with Malaysia. Mere lip service will not do. Like justice, friendship must not only be maintained but be seen to be accomplished.'

    Let the law, and only the law, decide.

    The writer is Features Editor of The Straits Times. E-mail: leehoong@sph.com.sg
