    As a postgraduate student, you should have little difficulty getting a job if you you look in the right places - in the employers' perception, you are distinguished from the undergraduates, who tend to be workers of very variable quality.

    As for looking in the right places, the newspaper classifieds would not be the place to do it. In those ads, employers would not primarily be looking to hire international students, and some of them may think that hiring you may involve extra paperwork. Besides, you are competing against applicants who pose no potential work rights issues, and who can commit full-time. I would suggest your university's student job site - you would fit the bill for potential employers there.

    Silly question - if you're at UOSQ, why are you in Sydney? If you know anybody at the University of Sydney, see if you can get access to their job site (I expect it would be a password-protected site) - the serious players tend to advertise at the older universities. My best two roles came from Melbourne University's site, including the one where I got around the 20-hour rule.

    That was actually a bit of an odd case. I got along very well with my potential employers, to the point at which they selected me as a postgraduate student over fully qualified psychologists. I explained the 20-hour rule to my boss, and she said fine - on the books, she would hire me for 20 hours a week but double my hourly rate, so she got the other 20 hours for free. I have found the most receptive organisations to be those that have a staff of about 10-20, and have been around for about 8-10 years. Such organisations that I have encountered tend to be new enough to not be weighed down by bureaucracy, and are old enough to be reasonably stable.

    Apart from the university sites, try either seek.com.au or mycareer.com.au, and look for contract, temp or casual roles. When they ask you if you are a resident, you are in fact considered a resident by the Australian Tax Office, albeit with the work limitations. By the way, do you have a tax file number yet? If not, apply for it ASAP at your nearest ATO office - it should take less than a week to come through and you will need it if you're going to be paid. Anyway, you can explain to employers that you have the right to work in Australia for 20 hours a week. If it is a contract or casual role, that will not affect them at all.

    With your work experience, as well as your postgraduate status, you may as well go for something in finance. Try Seek or mycareer - they should have plenty of part-time roles in that field. You might want to apply for this bookkeeper role that turned up on Seek ($25K a year could ease a lot of pain for a student :mrgreen):

    http://www.seek.com.au/users/viewdetails.asp?Action=jobsearch&JobListAction=ViewOneAd&JobSearch=true&AdID=3259281

    If I were in your position, this is something that I would feel reasonably confident about applying for.

    Another approach is to send your CV to temp agencies, not to the employers themselves (they rely on the agencies for their recruitment). I'm not sure which agencies operate in Sydney, but you can call any recruitment agency and ask if they do temp agency work - even if they don't, they are likely to be able to tell you which agencies would be appropriate.

    A side issue would be your English language skills. Being educated in Catholic schools throughout my education in Singapore, I had absolutely no problems with this, but if you do have language issues, that could be a barrier to be aware of. It's not a racist thing - the fact is that you will need to be able to communicate clearly in order to do the job right.

    Anyway, all the best. Feel free to ask any more questions if you need to. Also, PM me if you want me to take a look at your CV and provide feedback - I used to be a recruiter in Melbourne.

However, since well before I went to Australia, I had already known that I did not identify with Singapore culturally. Since the beginning of secondary school, there were many cultural factors that I disagreed with, and despite it making me a loose cannon on deck, I have stayed true to myself and not regretted it.
