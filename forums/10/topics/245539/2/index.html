    As Singapore becomes a wealthy developed nation, there is regression towards the mean. The poor aren't as poor as we were in the past and young people today tend to talk about their rights and entitlement, rather than fight for every opportunity to better themselves. It is not surprising that foreigners, more hungry and ambitious than we are, will begin displace us from jobs and positions.

    Ranting about foreigners will not halt the competition. Mahatir discovered as much after he complained about the immigrant Chinese who forced the Malays out of their businesses in his book, The Malay Dilemma'. He admitted that the economic powess of the Chinese could be attributed to their hard work and even blamed the laid-back culture of his own race for their inability to compete. He responded by trying to protect Malay interests with extension of the New Economic Plan (NEP), which he admitted was a failure, years later.

    The current Malaysian government is trying to exempt the southern Johor economic zone from the restrictions of the NEP. That is a pragmatic move to attract investment and a poorly-disguised admission of the failure of the race-based state ideology.

    It is ironic that wimpy Singaporeans who cannot compete are trying to urge the government to repeat the mistakes others have already learned from.
