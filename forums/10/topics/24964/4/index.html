    I think it is hard to pit Typhoon against Rafale in terms of technology. They are equally matched if not Rafale actually beats Typhoon in terms of the following:

    1. AESA radar (typhoon still on steered pulse-Doppler radar)

    2. Rafale avionics are currently more advanced. Typhoon will catch up eventually though. Rafale will introduce integration of HMS before Typhoon. Other systems are comparable though, eg. MFDs, Voice Data Input, touch screen (only on Rafale I think), ECM/ESM (I believe Typhoon may be slightly ahead, but it really depends on if you are talking about the German or the British version), etc

    3. Conformal Fuel Tank - flight tested on operational Rafales.

    4. Engines - about the same. Rafale engines have gone through 3 round or 4th roung of uprating, to the current M88-3. Whereas the Typoons only got their EJ200 engines not too long ago. Most of the prototypes were running on RB199 or the GE F404s.

    5. Stealth. The RCS of Rafale is debatably better than that of Typhoon. In fact, Dassault did a study to indicate that Rafale is second to Raptors in terms of RCS. Of course, one can counter claim that the study is done by Dassault and not the EADS consortium.

    So, I think they pretty much match up well. No one should claim which plane is more advanced. The simple fact is that Typhoon is designed as air superiority fighter, well Rafale was designed as a multirole fighter. The whole design difference was the root reason for France to leave the ECP/EF2000 project.

    If RSAF wants a dedicated Air Superiority fighter, by all means, go for Typhoon (provided we can be assured of Amraam integration and delievery when the Typhoons are delivered, what is the chances of that happening - anybody's guesses) . But if we a multirole or deep peneration strike aircraft, I think Rafales or the Super Bugs present better options. Even the Strike Eagles look better than Typhoon (pre Trenche3 of course).

    Read my post carefully. Let me draw out the following scenario: The Meteor BVRAAM, which is the common weapon for the Rafale and Typhoon, is at the moment is due to enter service in 2013 earliest, unless the Germans play more tricks. Now, lets say Singapore orders the new fighters in 2003/2004. Allowing 4 year lead time to delivery(as per first batch of F-16C/D), we get the new planes in 2007-2008. That means that there is a gap of 5-6 years before Meteor comes online. Therefore in this time we need an interim missile. So what are the options?

    Eurofighter has AMRAAMs, Rafale has MICA. Problem is: The US has said that it WILL NOT allow integration of AMRAAM for non-NATO countries which buy non-US made aircraft(US stated this clearly in the Korean FX program). So if Singapore is barred from AMRAAM integration for Typhoon, what are we left with? Skyflash or Sparrow. These are to be honest, 2nd rate missiles compared to the latest AAMs, which means, we're screwed.

    Rafale? It has MICA, whether or not AMRRAM is integrated. The general consensus is that the active radar MICA is not only a rough equivalent of the AMRAAM, it is a more flexible weapon, being designed for close in to BVR combat, as well as being available in IR and active rafar homing versions. Combined with Rafales Front Sector Optronics, which are already operational, the Rafale can carry out totally silent(passive) BVR interceptions (ie the enemy will not even know he is being tracked and indeed fired upon unitil he has been hit), because the optronics does not require radar to be on, and therefore enemy threat warning receivers will not be set off.

    What do you prefer? Second rate missiles(Sparrow/Skyflash) for a first rate fighter(Typhoon) or a first rate missile(MICA) for a first rate fighter(Rafale)?

    Aviation Week & Space Technology/March 4, 2002

    Singapore Fighter Race Begins by Robert Wall/Singapore

    The competition to supply Singapore with the multirole fighter has begun in earnest, with six bidders vying for one of the jewels of planned combat aircraft azquisitions. Singapore initially plans to buy about 20 new aircraft to replace its highly upgraded A-4s. However, a follow-on order might be made to replace the country'd F-5s, although that program could result in another competition, industry officials said.

    Singapore has been typically tight-lipped about the program, prompting often boisterous fighter makers to adopt a similarly cautious attitude. At the start of the competition, Boeing offers upgraded F-15E and the F/A-18E/F;Dassualt its Rafale;Eurofighter its export version, dubbed Typhoon; Lockheed Martin its F-16 Block 60, and Sukhoi with a tailored version of its Su-30MK.

    The process of winnowing down to about three serious competitiors will begin soon. Officers from Singapore's air force are slated to perform flight trials of different suitors this summer. Three contenders are likely to be eliminated next year, with a winner to be chosen a year later.

    Sukhoi, which is widely considered a long-shot, is basically offering the Su-30MK which has been sold to China as the Su-30MKK, India as the Su-30MKI and is competitng in Malaysia as the Su-30MKM. However, there would likely be some difference in avionics, according to Sukhoi's Alexandra Klementyev. The aircraft would feature both thrust vector controls and carnards, something that is part of mostbut not all Su-30s, said the company's Viktor Polyakov.

    Singapore is already an F-16 customer and should recieve the first batch of its 20 Block 52 aircraft next year. The Block 60, which is being put forward for the new program, is still in development and will be for 3-4years. The UAE is the lead customer for the aircraft that features conformal fuel tanks, an AESA radar, integrated forward looking infrared sensor andnew electronic warfare suite. Although Lockheed Martinhas a good relationship with Singapore through the initial F-16 buy, one weapons contractor following the competition said the deal could also be a handicap. Buying as a new fighter one that look so similar to an existing aircraft may present a perception problem, he argued.

    For the F-15E, the Singapore competition could represent a last chance to keep the program alive if the bid in South Korea is unsuccessful. The production line is being kept open by a USAF purchase of 10 aircraft several years ago. The first of these is now in final assembly, although production is widely spaced to avoid shutdown as long as possible.

    Seoul is expected to make its fighter announcement either later this month or early next month, says Michael D. Marks, vice-president for USAF fighter and bomber programs. South Korea has signed provisional contracts with all four competitiors that also include Dassualt, Eurofighter and Sukhoi. The F-15E offered to Singapore would likely be very similar to that offered South Korea, featuring an enhanced electronic warfare suite and new radar.

    However a loss in South Korea might knock the F-15E out of Singapore because of a line shutdown. Marks maintains this is too early to predict. However, if the fighter wins the production line would not stay open, but the pace of production would increase to about two fighters pewr month.

    Boeing's F/A-18E/F offer would include Raytheon's new AESA antenna that is still in development and the Integrated Defensive Electronic CounterMeasures suite. So far the US government hasn't cleared the AtFlir targeting system for export, although that is likely to happen before Singapore makes its final decision. Any F/A-18 buy would be folded into the USN's mulitiyear purchase agreement and should help both customer obtain a lower price for the aircraft, said a program official. Boeing also hopes to find out before year-end whether Malaysia will buy the F/A-18E/F; its up against the Su-30MKM and MiG-29MRCA.

    Although Singapore hasn't ask for a weapons package yet,yet, Europepan missile maker MBDA already has made its thoughts known. MOreover, the company is establishing industrial ties in Singapore. It has begun a codevelopment missile project with Singapore Technologies Engineering , said Fabrice Bregier, MBDA's chief executive officer. The company has partnered with two of Singapore's defense science and technology agencies.

    For Eurofighter, MBDA plans to offer ASRAAM for short range AA requirements, Storm Shadow as standoff cruise missile, Brimstone as a standoff guided missile and the ramjet powered Metoer as BVR missile. The long delayed 6 nation contract to strart Metoer development, which is still waiting Germany's aproval, shopuld be signed this spring, said Alan Garwood, MBDA's chief operating officer. Asraam could be offered for the F/A-18E/F, since Australia is integrating the weapon on its F/A-18.

    For the competing Rafale, MBDA will likely propose the Mica RF/IR, Metoer and Scalp EG(the french name for Storm Shadow), the Exocet AM39 anti-ship missile and countermeasures.

    The European weapons maker will probably face the typical competition from Raytheon in the AA area-Singapore is already an AMRAAM customer and it would be offered the AIM-9X short-range missile as well. Offers are also expected from BOeing, Lockheed Martin and Raytheon air-to-ground weapons. The US government will handle the weapons package seperate from aircraft, said the US industry official.

    The Saab, Bae System team of Gripen is not in the Singapore competition. Instead. the company is focused on East Central Europe where it has already won in HUngry and Czech Republic. The contract for Hungary has been signed and the deal with the Prague is slated to conclude in the next two months. MOreover, Gripen officials believe Slovakia may have an emerging fighter program. There are opportunities for the single engine fighter as well, but not immediately. Down the road, the aircraft will likely compete in Malaysia and Thailand, which is cash strapped and presently getting only very basic F-16 air defense fighters under a deal with Washington.

    So far, the competitions are moving ahead without any sign that the end-or-the-decade emergence of the JSF is having an impact. In fact, even Lockheed Martin expects to be selling F-16s after the JSF enter production, said Dain M Hancock, president of the corporation's Aeornautics Co. The F-16 order book already reaches to 2008 and "will go well beyond 2010."
