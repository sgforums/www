    I find Dr Lee's speech rather blend and does not really focus on the actual basis of 'moderating' the internet. In fact, it is evident that he avoids those topics entirely and prefers to give open explanations that could be interpreted both ways.

    Below are 3 of his comments i singled out for discussion:

    Nonetheless, he felt that policing of new media such as podcasts was necessary Â“to keep the electoral process on an even keelÂ”.

    This seems more like a reaction to 'police' the relative unknown territory of the internet, an area obviously out of direct control of the govt. There are certainly some companies like Google that could be deemed 'untouchable' from lawsuits even by our esteemed sue-happy govt. Therefore, it isn't surprising to notice several of Chee Soon Juan's video podcasts on the google website.

    The 2nd part of his justification was the aim to 'even the playing field of the electoral process'. This point is quite moot. The internet, being an independent body answerable to no one, is perfectly impartial and it is ridiculous to claim that political podcasts would slant viewer's opinion or give unfair tactical advantage to one party. There is no overall webmaster who audits and nip-tucks each and every podcast; moreover it is impossible to filter every shred of information and authenticate them. It is partially up to the reader's maturity of thought to differentiate fact from fiction.

    In fact, podcasts work both ways as the Ruling Party could also post their own to counter those of the alternatives.

    It is more than likely that the reason for this decree is based on the illogical (but true) assumption that, as with PAP-alternative rallies, more people prefer to watch alternative podcasts rather than listen to a parallel but predicatable equivalents by the Ruling Party. And the Ruling Party may have no avenue to manoeuvre given this predicament.

    Hence the 'legislative' measure.

    This was on top of the ample coverage given by the mainstream media.Â”

    Being part of a political party which has virtually full control of state-owned media, it is only natural for Dr Lee to see small snippets of alternative articles (most of them skewed against the alternatives anyway) and claim that is 'ample coverage'.

    These are times where myopia has truly become myopic.

    Â“We have adopted a light touch approach in dealing with the everyday use of the Internet. We will continue to do so and accept that the Internet and new media will remain largely a free-for-all. However during the election period when such free-for-all may result in undesirable situations, we cannot take a completely hands-off approach.Â”

    This statement is as good as no statement at all. As with standard govt approach, Dr Lee has adopted a relatively flip-flop approach. He starts off by saying that the govt will adopt a 'light touch', but neglects to say what he means by light touch, only going as far as to say the internet is at times a 'no man's land'; then he jumps to the other foggy extreme, claiming that during situations such as elections, they cannot adopt a totally 'hand-off approach', but once again, declining to state exactly what he defines as 'undesirable situations'.

    Instead, his press statements leave more questions than answers.
