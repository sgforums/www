    11 Apr 04, 00:00
    Allow me to take you back to the 60's, that is as far back as I can start.

    Those days, economics was poor. One of the things we could not afford was a good, warmth, quality blanket to cover us during our sleep. Our parents would gather pieces of cloth leftover, cut them into regular shapes(usually squares), machines them together and then we have a blanket over us.

    Sometimes it takes months to complete one such blanket. Reason maybe 1)you need to collect enough unwanted leftover cloths 2)hand-stitched(because could not afford a machine) 3)having to work, cook and take care of the children and parents.

    In the end the blanket looks like a rojak of colors and pattern and even cloth material. But it does serves the purpose of covering ones during a cold night. Of course this is no comparision to one of high quality wool blanket.

    One other chance you may get a wool is when, after a funeral you are left behind with some towels and blanket. Some clans send decorated blanket with messages of condolences to the bereaved family instead of floral wreath.

    The Fabric of the Nation I would see is a representation showing people from all walks of life coming in together contributing a little warmth to those in needs during the SARS outbreak.

    It gives us a visual reminder of the outbreak, how anxieties reigned then, how we came together to fight as one against a then unknown and unseen enemies and how some fellow being succumbed to the deadly diseases in the course of their work serving the sickly.

    You must not view the exhibits as a piece of visual arts but feel the warmth that it could provide one.
