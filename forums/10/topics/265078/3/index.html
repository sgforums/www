    Originally posted by matleep:
    hard to explain. but basically if every company is GST-registered, then we pay only 7% GST. Depending on the situation, we could pay more than 7% or even less than that. let me show a situation where it is less.

    Company A, a non GST-registered company, buys its goods for $107 which $7 is GST. He sells them for $207. So only $7 is paid as GST and the selling price is $200. Thus, GST is only 3.5%!
    Hi Matleep

    I am unsure if your above example works.

    Basically, the end product's GST is dependent on the price of the final good that is sold. The price of the final good is never impacted by the GSTs incurred by the companies in the supply chains, but more so by profiteering on the companies' parts.

    So if you go to your above example, it is not really correct to say that you only incur 3.5%. The basic idea of business is to buy for 1 and sell for 2. So technically, if a business cannot claim GST, they would write that into their costs.

    So assuming market conditions allow it, he would sell perhaps at $214 (and not $207), and then impose a 7% on $214.

    If he is a GST registered company, then he could claim $7 GST from the $107 cost, and then instead of calculating it into his costs, remove that, and sell his goods at $200 (Excl GST) (buy for 1 sell for 2), which would then be cheaper for the customers.

    Of coz you have companies that claim $7, and then still include that $7 into his base cost computation. That's why I find it fishy that Case could investigate the cases. Its really difficult to decipher between price increases due to GST profiteering and true GST impact, becoz there are many ways to hide the cost computation of a product
