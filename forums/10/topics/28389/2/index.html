    One small note to Atobe: The "poisonous prawn" doctrine was discarded sometime early in the nineties. That old doctrine meant that we would aim to have the forces to fight a long battle but eventually lose, exacting a tremendous toll on the opponent in the meantime. In a sense, we were "planning to lose"

    Our current doctrine is to train and build up enough strength to reliably *win* a limited war. We intend to be able to completely defeat the armed forces of any potential aggressor, possibly even if we allow them to attack us first (which is good diplomatically but bad militarily). However, we do not aim to have enough force to occupy or annex the aggressor in return (which is likely impossible considering our conscript nature). This new strategic doctrine shapes our defense procurement, force structure, manpower policies and combat doctrines.

    For more details, read the excellent book: Defending the Lion City by Tim Huxley.
