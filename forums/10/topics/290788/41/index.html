    So where are we now?

    In 2006 there were hints of an impending housing related crisis in the US. In July 2007 the crisis become full-blown.

    You can think of it as, in 2006, the US housing market was hit with HIV and full-blown AIDS developed in July 2007.

    The next alarming development was the spread of the disease across the Atlantic to none other than HSBC and Royal Bank of Scotland, among others, like Temasek's purchase, Barclays Bank.

    How Temasek can buy Barclays Bank in July at a time when the equivalent of full-blown AIDS hit the banks, is a mystery. Didn't Temasek, with such a big portfolio of banks, know that the banks were infected with the equivalent of HIV in 2006 and developed AIDS in July, the same month they added positions to their Barclays Bank stock holdings?

    When the problem became full-blown, the first knee jerk reactions of the central banks were to cut interest rates. Well, that, as we all know failed. The markets were in turmoil.

    They could not cut interest rates more to calm the markets because cutting interest rates created other problems, like inflation and problems with the value of the US Dollars, which in turn led to even more problems.

    Therefore they decided to do a concerted central bank intervention and loan unlimited amounts of money to banks at lower interest rates. The banks had a massive liquidity problem caused by the write-downs due to their sub-prime related losses.

    They were also wary of lending to each other just in case the money they lent, yes money they lent to other banks, become bad debts.

    If the problem is contained within the financial markets, the problem could be, well, contained.

    If the disease spreads to the US economy, we could see massive infection rates across many countries before it gets better.

    Last Friday, the US Department of Labor reported very poor unemployment figures and warning bells started toll.

    London was doing pretty well that Friday, smug in the idea that the problem could all go away with the concerted central bank intervention.

    When the report came out, the reaction was swift and furious. It was a straight line down.

    The US market opened, promptly headed south in a straight line and meandered the rest of the night down.

    Last night was quiet. After a big drop you would expect a rebound. A quiet session after a sharp drop is ominous.

    Eager money that believes a sharp drop represents buying opportunities, is buying and smart money is quietly selling to them, and no rebound materializes.

    In Singapore, the same. After the big drop on Monday, it is quiet today when it should be rebounding.

    Big drop, consolidation, big drop, consolidation, is the nightmare scenario to watch out for. It always end with an almighty crash. The more it drops, consolidates, drops, consolidates, the bigger the crash.

    Unless of course, it drops, consolidates... consolidates... consolidates... to the point that no one is really interested in the market... that would signal a time to consider getting back into the market...
