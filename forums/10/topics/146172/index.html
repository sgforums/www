    i read with mixed feelings of sadness and bewilderment the report, 'Sentence upped for man seeking trade-off for caning' (ST, Aug 3).

    Let's look at the facts of the case:

    Iskandar Muhamad Nordin, an 18-yr-old with a low IQ, was sentenced to nine month's jail and 3 strokes of the cane for touching a woman's breast.

    Unrepresented, he appealed, asking for a longer jail sentence in lieu of caning.

    CJ obliged by extending his jail sentence to 2 years and increasing the caning to 9 strokes.

    i have a few questions:

    how did he come to be unrepresented? did anyone in our law-enforcement agency brief him on his rights?

    did the state give him access to a lawyer who could have explained to him the consequences of a failed appeal?

    being diagnosed as "educationally subnormal" and suffering from "conduct disorder" and "attention deficit hyperactive disorder", how was he deemed fit to conduct his own appeal - and, in this connection,

    was a professional engaged to help in this assessment?

    the court scene couldnt have been more heart-rending: there stood the mentally-disturbed and retarded iskandar, helplessly alone and hoping for mercy from a learned judge not know for his munificience. he didnt have a chance.

    everyone connected with the case, except iskandar, probably saw it coming. but how could he with his troubled mind and diminished intelligence, and with absolutely no one to help him?

    the eventual punishment of two years and 9 strokes was no doubt to teach iskandar "so you will remember" never to touch women's breasts again. and this supposed to be appropriate for a man with an IQ of 58?

    surely, there is a more humane approach to helping wayward young people with mental disability.

    but what did give the case a bad aftertaste was the remark by the CJ that iskandar probably wouldnt mind the longer jail term. considering iskandar's mental state, such a derision was quite unnecessary.

    i cant help wondering if the molested woman feels that justice has finally been served.

    Lee Seck Kay

    the above appeared in the forum of The Straits Times, 6 august.

    IQ tests have a mean of 100 and standard deviation of 15.

    If someone scored 58, he's effectively 3 standard deviation away from the mean.

    IQ is distributed on a normal curve (a bell shaped curve). There are only 1% of the population falling under the 2 extreme ends (extremely intelligent/ extremely retarded). Hence, he's only more intelligent that only 0.5% of the overall population.

    The next important question is "Is this IQ test taken independently?" It's easier to fake bad than to fake good on an IQ test. If he had taken this IQ test much earlier before the court case, this piece of information should be used to send him to IMH, under the principle that such a person should not be able to function properly in the real world and should require help from the mental professionals.

    But you ask "Is IQ test a reflection of acedemic performance?" No, even though academic achievement might slightly influence how a person perform in a test. An IQ test usually has 2 components to it. A numerical components and a verbal components. For both components, there are many different tests falling under these categories. Most of these tests would be written in a language as simple as possible to ensure that language would play a small part in determining the overall intelligence. These tests tried to examine an individual's understanding of the concepts behind the language.

    But last and most important question of all. WHY IS HE NOT REPRESENTED? Any psychological undergraduate will be able to help CJ to understand the IQ scores. Unless CJ himself is so sure that he could understand the IQ scores by himself. Something wrong with our legal system here?
