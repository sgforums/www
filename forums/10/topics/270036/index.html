    Organised crime 'thriving' in Qld

    The "Mr Bigs" of organised crime in Queensland were thriving untouched because law enforcement agencies could not tap phones, a federal parliamentary committee has been told.

    Criminal investigators were virtually powerless to stop flourishing criminal networks and were forced to rely on interstate policing bodies for phone surveillance, the committee heard.

    Queensland is the only state in Australia where crime fighting authorities do not have telephone interception or phone tapping powers, despite more than a decade of lobbying from police and the Crime and Misconduct Commission.

    The State Government had "repeatedly refused to act" because it wanted the independent public interest monitor to guard against abuse of power.

    Police Minister Judy Spence last year announced changes in Commonwealth laws meant the obstacle had been cleared, but a dispute between the two governments has delayed a resolution.

    CMC director of intelligence Chris Keen said the impasse was diminishing the power of Queensland law enforcement agencies.

    "We are significantly impeded at the moment by the lack of telecommunications interception," he said.

    The CMC's submission to the inquiry, which is examining the effects of organised crime on society, said the lack of phone tapping powers was letting crime bosses go unpunished.

    "Organised crime in Queensland is characterised by a number of significant criminal identities who have been involved in organised crime activity for a number of years and have successfully evaded prosecution," it stated.

    "Were TI powers available in Queensland it is highly likely that a number of these criminal identities would have been successfully prosecuted or at the least had their activities curtailed."

    Investigative journalist and expert witness Bob Bottom told the committee the CMC's complaints were "shocking".

    "That's in effect saying there's an official attitude here that has allowed Mr Bigs to re-arise in Queensland untouched and making a laughing stock of authority," he said.

    Mr Bottom said drug lord and fugitive Tony Mokbel's recent arrest in Greece had relied on thousands of hours of tapped phone conversations.

    He said it was outrageous Queensland police had gone so long without the same powers as police in other states.

    "There could be Tony Mokbel's walking down the Queen Street Mall and you wouldn't have a clue they were here," he said.

    "It's the Florida of Australia, because they know they can operate here, they can't be phone tapped and with modern communications it's a haven for them."

    Queensland Police Commissioner Bob Atkinson said he had not seen the CMC's submission and would not comment on it.

    However, he said organised crime was an ongoing concern in the state and there was no doubt phone tapping powers would help police.

    "There's some good things happening but it is a concern to me and I'm not prepared to say we're on top of it and I don't know that we ever will be," he said.

    Police Minister Judy Spence said Queensland police regularly used telephone interception during joint investigations with other agencies.

    "I don't think we should be giving Queenslanders the impression that (the police's) inability to have phone tapping impedes them to a great extent because, in fact, telephone tapping is occurring in this state," she said.

    However, Ms Spence said the state government agreed that police and the CMC needed those powers independently of interstate agencies.

    She said asking for the public interest monitor to be included in the process, as a final check and balance for the community, was fair.

    "At the moment, the public interest monitor is present at the Supreme Court when the police ask for these sorts of powers in relation to listening devices," Ms Spence said.

    "So I don't think we're being unreasonable in asking the federal government to allow Queensland to have the PMI as part of the process when police go to court to ask for telephone tapping powers."
