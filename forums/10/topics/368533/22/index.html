  • SehenSieMich
    20 Jul 09, 01:39

    First and foremost, I believe each and every single individual is entitled to their own opinions, regardless whether they are reasonable or not.

    I think there has been a lot of generalizing and stereotyping in this thread. Then again, stereotypical behavior has always been prevailent in our society today. It is really quite narrow-minded and it puts forward alot of negativity.

    Undoubtedly, there are a fraction of army chaps who, no doubt eager to book out, rush home on public transports without considering whether their sweat and stink will affect the general public. However, there are also army boys who take a shower and put on a clean st of uniform before booking out. Have we overlooked these bunch of people?

    I don't know about you people, but I sweat a lot myself. Singapore has a very humid climate and people are bound to sweat when standing under the sun or sometimes just by walking around. Moreever, these army boys are wearing their No. 4, which is not the most comfortable or thinnest piece of clothing. I personally did not have the chance to wear the new pixellated uniform as I was not issued but the old one is thick and hot as heck! Add on a field pack filled with stuff collected over the week and you get 1 miserable suffering army boy who is just eager to go home, take a nice shower, and ejoy his weekend.

    Of course I do agree that ALL soldiers have the responsibility to uphold their image as defenders of this country, whether they like it or not. It tells a alot about their discipline and responsibility, which I strongly believe should be instilled in them.

    With regards to the post by 'Parn', I feel that it is unfair to compare. I cite a few examples: The United States Army is arguably the most elite amongst the world's military. Yet, there is much to be said about their professionalism and conduct. I'm sure you would have heard of the rape case in Japan, where the accused is a US soldier stationed there. I'm sure you would have heard of the Guantanamo Bay tortures carried out by United States soldiers. All these examples are very well-publicised and with a few simple clicks over the internet, you will surely find more examples of unprofessional conduct of first-world soldiers.

    Yet, the most infamous and publicised public misconduct of a SAF soldier is none other than CPL Dave Teo.

    Does BO and stink really affects the SAF's image as DEFENDERS of our country? Do we really want each and every one of our soldiers to book out in long-sleeved shirts, pants, and ties? The SAF has 1 motto and 1 motto only - and that is to DETER. The SAF is not in the business of training men to be prim and proper. The SAF is not in the business of teaching men how not to sweat when dressed in their No. 4 and carrying their fieldpacks. 

    Perhaps we all need to sit back and reflect. What is truly the intent of serving national service? Do the general public understand and acknowledge the intent of the government drafting young men to serve national service? Have you ever stepped into the shoes of these young men and try to understand what they're going through?

    I understand that the bros here that have or are doing their national service will get defensive when issues like these appear. It is not something we like, nor is it something that we can control at times. If the weather is hot, we are bound to sweat. Our uniforms are not Nike or Adidas sports tees with Dri-Fit technology or whatsoever. Our uniforms are designed primarily to save our own asses in times of war. Our uniforms are designed to enable us to hide from enemy's eyes so that we can execute our tasks without having to dodge enemy fire. Our uniform is what that is stopped us from getting an early ticket to the grave.

    It is always easier to complain about this and that and those. It is always easier to view things from our own perspective instead of others. I would be irritated as hell as I were to squeeze in a MRT cabin with a bunch of stinky, sweaty recruits who have just booked out after a 6 days field camp from Tekong. I would be irritated as hell if I were to sit beside a commando who has just completed his 72km route march. However, inside me, I know that these young men are just doing their job, doing what country asks of them, tired as hell, and just looing forward to go home and see their loved ones after a tough week in camp.

    I urge all of you to take a step back and think about both sides of the story. Either you do it or not, it's your personal choice. It is also your personal judgment whether to condemn these army boys or understand what they're going through.

    I am open to criticism if any of you has any against this post :)
