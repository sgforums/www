(Reporting by Melanie Lee; Editing by Jan Dahinten and David Fox) - SINGAPORE, March 27 - A Singapore man is suing the government for around S$3 million in damages for mistakenly caning him three more strokes than he was sentenced to, court documents showed on Thursday.

    Dickson Tan, 21, who was jailed for nine months and sentenced to five strokes of the cane last year for helping an illegal moneylender, was beaten eight times instead as a result of an administrative error.

    Caning on the buttocks is an additional punishment for male criminals in Singapore for offences ranging from vandalism to illegal possession of drugs and rape.

    It is administered by select prison officers who use a water-soaked ratan cane to beat the offender, who is strapped to a triangular trellis.

    According to court documents filed by the plaintiff on Wednesday, Tan attempted to point out the error before his caning on March 29 last year, but was told that the "records in the file could not be wrong".

    Tan is suing the government for negligence and is hoping to claim between S$1.7 million and S$3 million in total damages, his lawyer Joseph Chen told Reuters.

    He said Tan had received S$8,000 in the form of an "interim payment", a non-liability payment meant to help indigent plaintiffs.

    "This was a failure in the administration of justice," Chen said.

    Singapore's attorney-general chambers and the ministry for home affairs could not be reached for comment despite repeated attempts on Thursday evening.

    Tan, who is unemployed, told Reuters that he decided to sue the government after three unsuccessful rounds of mediation. Tan suffered from depression and post traumatic stress disorder after the beating, a psychiatric report showed.

    "I want just compensation, they must be responsible," he said.

    This is the second time the Singapore government has been sued for meting out the wrong number of strokes. In 1991, it was sued by a man who was caned 48 times instead of his sentenced 24 strokes, media reports said.

    He was given undisclosed compensation without the case going to court.

    The Southeast Asian city-state's Law Minister S. Jayakumar told lawmakers in July last year that this "kind of clerical mistake is an extremely rare occurrence". (Reporting by Melanie Lee; Editing by Jan Dahinten and David Fox)
