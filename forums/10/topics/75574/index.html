    I think quite a lot of applicants - fresh graduates in particular, are probably attracted to the profession because of combinative factors such as :

    1) The prestige of the vocation as a professional,

    2) The relatively high pay,

    3) The entitlement of 12 weeks of school holidays (which really is a big deal),

    4) A commonly misguided notion that a teacher's work will end exactly at 1pm during morning shift or 6.20pm for those in afternoon shift, and

    5) The stable and assured employment concern addressed by the vocation.

    While the intakes might seem high in numbers, it is also very common to see young teachers, especially those untrained and hence unbonded, to terminate their initial one-year contract early.

    The variety of reasons may range in their forms by the hundredth, but among the most common, it is the inability to adapt and handle well the demanding stress and attention that is required of the work.

    A typical classroom teacher begins a day from 7.15.

    From this time onwards, he/she will be involved in classroom interaction with the students until the usual 1.00pm, with half an hour to one hour's break in between.

    After the official duties, teachers are officially free to go off.

    However, tons of work such as preparation of lessons in media, paper, worksheet formats; the marking and correction of assignment matters,; the handling of discipline issues and parental contact; and last but not least the various other classroom / student related school administrative matters will still await them to handle.

    And this does not take into account that i) teachers may be mobilized to teach everything from English, Maths, Science, Social Studies, PE, Art, Music for classes in different levels, as well to be reposnsible for CCA clubs or sports team.

    In addition, there is also the minimum twice a week remedial classes whereby teachers are expected to revisit their lessons again for students who may be slower in the learning process.

    Apart from the above, be expected to be involved in the various subcommittees such as National Day, festivals celebration, sports day events and many other as well.

    The pay of course is very attractive to outside eyes who will not see the actual amount of work involved.

    But please be reminded that in return for that attractive amount of pay, a potential teacher applicant should also have the appropriate apptitude and expectation to be a hands-on all rounder.

    Education and Teaching....

    These are not merely isolated cases of calling or career.

    They are all intergrated as one.

    The prestige of the vocation as a professional
    Hmm...you're sure this vocation is considered prestigious? When I used to teach in Singapore, I always ask this question of my pupils, "How many of you want to be a teacher when you grow up?" I will invariably get one or two hands out of the class of 40. Their reason? "I get to scold children!"

    All is not lost, however. Once you get that rapport with the parents, and they can see your love and commitment to your calling, you will quickly gain the respect. BUT that is on a personal basis. I don't think there is a respect for teachers in general.

    The entitlement of 12 weeks of school holidays (which really is a big deal)
    Entitlement? Read your employment terms carefully, my dear. It is not an entitlement. The ministry is at liberty to call you back any time during the vacations for any length of time. It is just that they have not exercised that right.

    When you are a P6 teacher, there is a good chance that your Mar and Sep holidays are gone. Two to three weeks of your June holidays are likely to be gone as well (2 for supplementary classes, 1 for meetings and such). And your December holidays? One week for staff meeting, leaving you with 5 weeks. Still pretty fantastic, though, until you realise that the December period is a peak period, and travel and such are very expensive. And wait till you become an HOD...my wife really hated it when I was HOD...and was so happy when I resigned.

    Ah...but my heart and passion is still in education (can't escape). My wife understood this, and so here I am, in Shanghai teaching in the international school. And taking up HOD position soon again. Oh well...

    But please don't feel down because of what I said. Ours is truly a calling, and not many can take it and do it well. We need to be somewhat sadistic (in doling work) and yet masochistic (in grading work!). We need to be an authorian and yet be a shoulder to cry on. We need to direct and yet to let go to let them discover for themselves. We need to talk to the class and yet (at the same time!) talk to an individual.

    How many can do that?!?!?!? If it's not a calling, what is?
