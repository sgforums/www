    SINGAPORE : Four immigrant families received a warm welcome with a durian party on Sunday morning - all part of efforts to make new citizens feel welcome and help them integrate into the Singapore way of life.
    Durians and foreign talent - these prickly subjects were the focus of attention at the party at Rivervale.

    Four-hundred D-24 durians were specially catered for the families from China, India, Bangladesh and Malaysia who now call Singapore home.

    Said Michael Palmer, MP for Pasir-Ris-Punggol GRC, "One of the best ways of bringing in new citizens and new PRs into the community is to not only send them a letter or have tea with them but actually to bring them into the Singapore culture. And one of the great pastimes is eating, and durians are an all-time favourite among Singaporeans."

    One couple from Andhra Pradesh, India, were one of many game enough to try the king of fruits.

    Receiving their citizenship last year, the couple said they were happy here.

    Said Reddy U Potturi, a physiotherapist, "My first experience in Singapore was only a bit with language for the first few months. After that I could adapt to Singapore English, and after that everything went on very smooth."

    His wife, Menna Potturi, said, "I keep watching Channel NewsAsia so I can get news about Singapore and how people live over here so that I could get used to this place."

    A Bangladeshi couple, who run a computer business, intend to hire four more workers soon, and they welcome the Prime Minister's call for more foreign talent in Singapore.

    Said entrepreneur Helal Uddin, "As long as there are many foreigners there will be a lot of competition in the work place and better talents in Singapore, and they will contribute to Singapore and better futures."

    This point was echoed by Foreign Minister George Yeo at a separate charity event.

    Said Mr Yeo, "In many parts of the economy we do need foreigners to help plug in the gaps and the whole purpose is to benefit Singapore and Singaporeans. If we don't provide a sufficient pool of workers for investors, they will go somewhere else and this will affect our economy."

    With more parties like this lined up, the winner of the next durian-opening competition could be among the new Singaporeans. - CNA /ct
