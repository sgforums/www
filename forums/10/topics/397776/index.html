    Sylvia Lim blames PAP for “stamping out” opposition representation in Parliament

    Temasek Review

    NCMP Sylvia Lim has lashed out at the PAP for “stamping out” opposition representation in Parliament which led to the present predicament it finds itself in today.

    The PAP has lately introduced a series of constitutional amendments to increase the number of NCMPs in Parliament from six to nine.

    Ms Lim argued that while the changes were implemented to reflect the aspirations of Singaporeans to have more opposition voices in Parliament, it is a result of the PAP’s “success” in preventing the opposition from winning seats in the elections via the GRC system.

    The opposition has never won a GRC since the scheme was introduced in 1988. The closest it came to was in Eunos GRC in the same year when the Workers’ Party team garnered 49.7 percent of the votes.

    Ms Lim also noted a speech made by Senior Minister Goh Chok Tong in 2006 in which he admitted that GRCs has helped PAP recruit fresh candidates as “without some assurance of a good chance of winning at least their first election, many able and successful young Singaporeans may not risk their careers to join politics.”

    “The root causes of our current problems are the abuse of the GRC system and gerrymandering. These have curtailed the expression of the people’s desires at the elections and instead promoted the ruling party’s own agenda. The PAP has created the problem which it is now trying to solve. But we should instead tackle the root causes for a more lasting and sustainable political future for Singapore,” Ms Lim added.

    Though a third of the electorate voted for the opposition during the 2006 general election, they are represented by only two out of eighty four seats in Parliament.

    While the constitutional amendments may increase the number of opposition MPs in Parliament, they will not have dent the PAP’s political hegemony so long it continues to have more than the two-thirds majority needed to introduce bills and legislation as it sees fit.

    With no strong opposition in Parliament to check on the ruling party, Singaporeans will have no choice but to put up with the status quo for many years ahead.
