    From an extract of a Government material, the Office of the Elected Presidency was described as follows:-

    In January 1991, the Constitution was amended to allow for the election of a President by the citizens of Singapore. The elected President will hold office for a fixed term of six years. The creation of the elected presidency is a major constitutional and political change in Singapore's history.

    Under the revision,     the President is empowered to veto government budgets and appointments to public office. He can also examine the Government's exercise of its powers under the Internal Security Act and religious harmony laws, and in investigations into cases of corruption. The President must, however, consult the Council of Presidential Advisers before he takes a decision on some of these matters

    This highlighted sentence - in red - clearly shows the authority and duty of the Office of the Elected President over that of the Government as intended and legislated into Law by the Ruling Political Party.

    Unfortunately, the irony of having the Office of the Elected Presidency is that it is made impotent by the mere fact that the Elected President has to consult his Council of Presidential Advisers before making any decisions.

    The Council of Presidential Advisers comprises six members, of whom two are appointed by the President at his discretion, two are the Prime Minister's nominees, one is the Chief Justice's nominee and the sixth, the nominee of the Chairman of the Public Service Commission.

    With both appointments to the Chief Justice and Chairman of the Public Service Commission being selected and recommended by the Prime Minister, can their selection of their candidate be independent from the Prime Minister's influence ?

    With the Advisory Council largely in the hands of the Ruling Political Party that form the Government of the day - can the President have any space to perform the duties that is expected of him to check any abuses and corruption by his Government ?

    This is the typical charade that the Ruling Political Party has constantly shown to preserve its own controlling position over the Political Life of Singapore.
