    23 Aug 06, 14:23
    Originally posted by sourketchup:
    We need them. Like it or not. I think no one can dispute that.

    But what worries many of us are the advantages that the FTs have over us as I have listed above.
    As pointed out by MS, we need FTs at all levels, from the scientists needed to grow our biomedical industry to the mechanics and production workers who are able to provide satisfactory services at lower prices than their Singaporean counterparts. That raises the competitiveness of Singaporean companies and grows the economy.

    Of course there are winners and losers. The winners are the Singaporean employers, the FT and the nation as a whole. The losers are low- and semi-skilled Singaporeans who will see their wages depressed as a result.

    Every developed nation will face a similar challenge, from Singaporeans who face PRC citizens to British workers who lose their jobs to Polish immigrant workers.

    As to where to draw the balance, I believe in utilitarianism...what will bring about the greatest good for the greatest number of people. Without FTs, our SMEs will not be as competitive and will have to move operations to cheaper economies in the region. Singapore will suffer a double whammy: losing both the jobs and the tax revenue, not to mention the entrepreneurs who move their businesses overseas.

    The recent move to increase the number of doctors and lawyers with second medical and law schools may hurt the wages of existing lawyers and doctors. We already have cheap foreign doctors in our health service. But that is a price we have to pay to contain healthcare costs and grow the economy.

    Singaporeans have to look at the greater good. Ultimately, if the nation loses competitiveness and businesses re-locate elesewhere, we all lose.

    I think it is interesting and instructive to look at how other developed countries are tackling this problem.

    Japan--No immigrants please. NO. No. No. They are now facing the problem of a depleting population.

    USA--traditionally welcoming of immigrants. But not to forget, they have plenty of land, of opportunities. Making the pie bigger for everyone is really true for that country. However, emotions against immigrants still run high now and then. Just goes to show that even in the best of "melting-pots", sentiments of keeping "outsiders" out still exist.

    UK--taking in Polish and Eastern Europeans next. Took in lots of Indians and blacks in the past. Not going into whether it is the right or wrong policy, but riots have erupted in the past. Even now, there are many conflicts.

    France--took in lots of Algerians. Zidane is Algerian. But they still have problems assimilating them.

    Taking in foreigners is a complex and emotive issue, but i'd just like to point out that like it or not, there will be deep feelings against it. I prefer not to have them, but recognise that our country cannot do without them and it is the right policy by the government to let them in.
