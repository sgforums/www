    Survey: Big returns await family-friendly companies

    Study of 11 firms finds that every $1 invested in work-life programmes return $1.68 and staff are happier and more loyal

    By M. Nirmala

    THE stork is kept busy in companies with family-friendly policies.

    At SP Consulting (International), nine of its 13 staff are married and, between them, have 20 children.

    A dozen of these babies were born after their parents joined the 12-year-old company, a happy development that prompted managing director Lim Meng Wee to quip: 'We have more than replaced ourselves!'

    Trusting his employees to deliver results on set targets, he allows them to work from home or come in late.

    The company was one of 11 surveyed in a study by the Singapore National Employers Federation (SNEF), which found that companies with family-friendly policies reaped many intangible and tangible results.

    Workers' morale went up and so did productivity and financial bottom lines, according to the study released yesterday.

    Companies that were surveyed included Merck Sharp and Dohme, NTUC Income and Yokogawa Electric Asia.

    The study found that for every $1 the companies invested in work-life programmes, their average gain was $1.68.

    This figure was derived after the companies worked out how their revenue went up and costs were saved when pro-family policies were introduced.

    On the cost front, for example, the companies looked at the expense of filling a vacant position, which could come up to half the annual salary of the post, compared to being flexible and retaining the worker.

    Their employees also appreciated their moves.

    Three-quarters of those polled said family-friendly practices have made their lives as parents and workers easier to manage.

    Almost two-thirds said pro-family policies persuaded them to continue working for the organisation.

    For example, NTUC Income fixed its attrition rate many years ago by allowing not just married women to work flexible hours but its singles as well.

    'Some singles can't wake up early if they are studying at night or partying,' said Ms Cynthia Chin, Income's human resource manager.

    These employees can start work later at 9am or 9.30am, instead of at 8.30am like the rest.

    Based on the survey findings, SNEF, together with NTUC, the Manpower Ministry and the Ministry of Community Development and Sports (MCDS), has drawn up a road map for employers who want to be enlightened, family-friendly bosses.

    Included are guidelines on how flexible work arrangements can be implemented.

    Other suggestions include creating opportunities for children to visit their parents' offices, providing space at the workplace for new mothers to pump their milk, or even offering laundry pick-up services.

    SNEF's executive director Koh Juan Kiat said the ideas will encourage employers to be more focused on how they can help their workers without fearing that targets will not be met.

    'It can be a win-win situation,' he said.

    Convinced that companies can be persuaded to adopt the guidelines, the SNEF will use the survey results to persuade companies that flexi-work need not compromise the company's bottom line but can, in fact, boost its productivity.

    The survey results were presented at a work-life practices seminar at Orchard Hotel. Speaking at the event, Community Development Minister Yaacob Ibrahim urged firms to heed the example of those polled and be reassured that providing a better work-life balance can enhance their competitive edge.

    'The sooner we dive into this, the faster we will see results.
