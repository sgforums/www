    S'pore's Lee Kuan Yew: Party will win next 2 votes

    Singapore's elder statesman Lee Kuan Yew said Wednesday that he expects his People's Action Party to win the next two elections to extend its grip on power by as much as 10 more years.

    "I don't see any problem in the next election or probably in the next one after that," Lee, 85, said at a dinner to mark the fifth anniversary of a college named after him.

    "But if we don't find a good team in the election after that, and the opposition gets a good team together, we are at risk."

    Lee, who founded the PAP and was Singapore's first prime minister from 1959 to 1990, remains a powerful figure in the Cabinet and in the party. The PAP is the only party to have ruled Singapore.

    The current government was elected in 2006. The next elections are set for 2011.

    Lee, who now has the title of mentor minister, said even if the opposition took power by a "freak result," it won't be allowed to fire top police, army and other officials without the approval of the president, who is appointed by a committee of government officials.

    Lee, who is the father of Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong, said an opposition government would by law also need the president's approval to tap international reserves, indicating it would not be given.

    Otherwise "all our hard-earned savings could go in five years," he said. "I spent 15 years thinking up these safeguards."

    Top Singapore court affirms ruling against Review magazine

    Agence France-Presse, 8 October 2009

    Singapore’s highest court Wednesday ruled that a soon-to-be-defunct regional magazine and its editor had defamed the country’s founder Lee Kuan Yew and his son Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong.

    The three-member Court of Appeal rejected an attempt by the publishers of the Far Eastern Economic Review (FEER) and its editor, Hugo Restall, to have their conviction by a High Court judge in September 2008 overturned.

    Damages are to be awarded to the Lees at a later date. Decisions by the Court of Appeal are final.

    FEER is to be shut down in December, one of a growing number of newspapers and magazines which have fallen victim to the Internet age.

    The Court of Appeal said “we agree with and affirm the judge’s ruling” on the defamation case filed by the Lees in response to a 2006 Review article “and dismiss both appeals with costs.”

    Peter Low, the lawyer representing FEER in the case, told AFP there were no fresh instructions from his client following the ruling.

    Dow Jones and Co., which owns the magazine, declined to comment on the outcome.

    Singaporean leaders have won hundreds of thousands of dollars in damages from critics and foreign publications. They say the lawsuits are necessary to protect their reputations from unfounded attacks.

    Lee Kuan Yew, 86, now a senior cabinet adviser, served as prime minister from 1959 to 1990. Lee Hsien Loong, 57, took over in 2004 from Goh Chok Tong.

    The Lees sued Restall and Hong Kong-based Review Publishing, alleging defamation in a 2006 article based on an interview with Chee Soon Juan, an opposition party leader.

    The article — entitled “Singapore’s ‘Martyr,’ Chee Soon Juan” — described the Singapore Democratic Party secretary general’s battle against the ruling People’s Action Party and its leaders.

    In the article, Restall also touched on the success of Singapore officials in libel suits against critics.

    Last month Dow Jones and Co. announced that it would close FEER, which from 1946 chronicled Asia’s post-war recovery, in December.

    The publication’s heyday was in the 1980s when it was known for the depth and quality of its reporting and for standing up to authoritarian governments and big business.

    “Unfortunately, despite several attempts at invigorating the brand, the Review’s continued losses in advertising revenue and readers are now unsustainable,” said Dow Jones and Co., part of the News Corp. media empire controlled by tycoon Rupert Murdoch. – AFP

    S'pore's Lee Kuan Yew: Party will win next 2 votes

    Singapore's elder statesman Lee Kuan Yew said Wednesday that he expects his People's Action Party to win the next two elections to extend its grip on power by as much as 10 more years.

    "I don't see any problem in the next election or probably in the next one after that," Lee, 85, said at a dinner to mark the fifth anniversary of a college named after him.

    "But if we don't find a good team in the election after that, and the opposition gets a good team together, we are at risk."

    Lee, who founded the PAP and was Singapore's first prime minister from 1959 to 1990, remains a powerful figure in the Cabinet and in the party. The PAP is the only party to have ruled Singapore.
