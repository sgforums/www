    I did not read all the posting on this topic but feel that there are certain points not fully developed on the issue of laws and punishment.

    Isn't it clear that there was a principle from the early days of civilization that punishment must befit the crimes?

    So, I think the issue here is: should there be death penalty to compell people not to traffic drug.

    Parka repeatedly said that Laws are around to protect the public and this is the very basic prinicple of law.

    But when she said next that the death sentence is written for that purpose, to protect the public, we are still begging the question. Do we understand the question or issue here - should there be capital punishment for drug trafficking?

    Parka next said that if death penalty doesn't act as a deterent, the death penalty should still be there to protect the public. In this case, it's not seen as a preventive measure anymore, but more of a curing measure.

    My view on this latest reason for upholding death penalty : We need to protect the innocent against the insidiousness - addictiveness of drug - but at what price to humanity. So this issue still will centre on whether drug traffickers in possession of a certain amount like 20 grams should be hung.

    The additional point put up by those who are in favour of hanging the drug trafficker in possession of 20 grams or more of pure-form drug is that the law is there and everyone has the option not to commit crimes given the knowledge of the consequences.

    The onus is up to the person to defend himself and give a reasonable argument on why he committed the offence.

    The above-mentioned are the basics to be argued by all.

    In my opinion, if civilisation has over the years tried to temper with harsh punishment like death penalty on presumed evil or dangerous art or conduct, why not consider whether in the case of drug trafficking, there is a need to do so.

    Justice is another important component in this argument. Will justice be served if we should in the obsession to keep society drug-free over-do the punishment.

    20 grams are set by men whom we elected to parliament. For convenience, this amount was set. Dangers and addictiveness of drugs in various forms including the higher degree of risk for certain drugs etc were man-made.

    Most of our laws and not just the drug laws require men to interpret various aspects of danger. Falling windows are a danger too. Chewing gums are anti-social and anti-image too.

    Where is the end. All the issues involved will have to be discussed to see if interpretation by men in parliament might be obsessive or over-zealous as well as the bad conduct or evil of wrong doers.

    For a start, I think I would like to acknowledge that drugs like Heroin are killer drugs. They are extremely dangerous. But hanging a man should not be a decision handed down too lightly. Let there be re-examination of all laws made by men to the effect that certain acts are highly dangerous and got to be dealt with harshly.

    If we can stop most drug trafficking by hanging all men caught in possession of above 20 grams of heroin without using the big "C" approach so much the better for humanity.

    Humanity has reached a point where people are considered imperfect and those driven to desperation are most imperfect. If he is not the most cruel wrong-doers that need to be nabbed, let the laws turn to the most cruel wrong-doers with greater outreach to catch them instead of presumptuous punishments alone that such traffickers are the most evil.

    Where there is a way to be more human, I think as denizens of this little globe should. Gods will bless all for such thinking thoughts.

    I think there will be no point in discussing this topic f we are to base our arguments on "laws have its good or bad", "drug traffickers cause great harms to society and need to be executed to protect the innocent" or "it depends on perceptions of the harms or risks" etc.

    To keep to reasonable discussion, let us examine whether death penalty meted out to those in possession of 20 grams of hard drug is excessive or notoriously too drastic.

    If we are to recall how this death penalty came to be imposed, we might see the perspective and from there gain a better understanding whether such penalty is excessive.

    When Singapore imposed death sentence on drug trafficker of such a small quantum of drug possession, it was facing grave problems of widespread addictions leading many social problems as posted by many. As a result of such death penalty, we might have solved our drug problems or subdued them at the expense of those who unfortunately were found in possessions of 20 grams for whatever reasons or circumstances.

    Not many people are interested to know about the personal circumstances of those people who were hang. But not too many countries are following our heavy-handed punishment system. If so, prima facie, there is a case to examine whether our particular insistence on death to drug pushers might be overboard.

    It will be interesting to see how many countries or percentages of countries have followed our death penalty prescription for drug trafficking. If they had done so, have they succeeded in weeding out the addiction or trafficking. If they had after introducing death penalty, then there may be some argument in favour of death penalty but alternatives like education can still be looked into.

    To illustrate the possibility of alternative, reference was made incidentally to god and bible. This has drawn out some pros and cons as many people have different views about god's attitude to punishment.

    To my mind, god reserves death penalty to the worst crime like murder not not necessarily drug possession. Examples showing god had preferred dishing out death penalty may be unrelated to drug case we are discussing.

    So again, I agree illustrating god's attitude to compassion may not be rational and objective for discussions.

    The only point to my mind worth mentioning about god perhaps is that god did not seem to be in great anxiety to impose death penalty for crimes other than wilful murders. Bibles or Korans I believe advocate compassion for human mistakes or lack of rationality. After all to err is human and to forgive divine.

    Is an amount of 20 gram too small an amount to equate to wilfulness in murdering someone. Should our punishment system befit the crimes based on wilfulness as well as actus. Are runners or desperate people who made mistakes due to extremely unfortunate circumstances worth granting them an opportunity to turn over a new leaf as they may be driven by desperation or circumstances to turn to crimes but not the perpetrators who often are someone else.

    The real culprits are often the masterminds who are often rich and powerful individuals wilfully committing major crimes. Try to deter this group of perpetrators in cooperation with international agencies and hunt down such people instead of fixing the penalty of death on proxies.

    If crimes cannot be solved easily because the profits are high or dangers are grave, does it follow that punishment should increase correspondingly then? If the answer is no then should the 20 gram threshold amount be reduced to say 5 - 10 grams?

    If the answer to this logical question is yes then we will be basing punishment on veangence as pointed by pikamaster. Is this what we want of our society - veangence and more veangence on various grounds.

    Therefore, I feel that there is a case that death penalty to deter drug possessor of 20 grams may be too harsh. Those who insist that society is at risk may not be advancing logical arguments to support the status quo of death penalty.

    The least that should be done is to conduct further study to see if death is too harsh a penalty for possessor and on top of this, it might not lead to solution of this international problem.

    It may be a case of chicken and egg to assert that the only way to go is to make it extremely deterrent as drug trafficking is indeed too dangerous. It may be a case of drug being so heavily punished that drug masterminds would be justified to push up the street values higher and higer giving lucrative incentives thereby due to heavy penalty to breed more trafficking. Perhaps, there is a case to further study and review along this practical line instead of just insisting on status quo punishments.

    54. As explicitly formulated, the precept "You shall not kill" is strongly negative: it indicates the extreme limit which can never be exceeded. Implicitly, however, it encourages a positive attitude of absolute respect for life; it leads to the promotion of life and to progress along the way of a love which gives, receives and serves. The people of the Covenant, although slowly and with some contradictions, progressively matured in this way of thinking, and thus prepared for the great proclamation of Jesus that the commandment to love one's neighbour is like the commandment to love God; "on these two commandments depend all the law and the prophets" (cf. Mt 22:36-40). Saint Paul emphasizes that "the commandment ... you shall not kill ... and any other commandment, are summed up in this phrase: ?You shall love your neighbour as yourself' " (Rom 13:9; cf. Gal 5:14). Taken up and brought to fulfilment in the New Law, the commandment "You shall not kill" stands as an indispensable condition for being able "to enter life" (cf. Mt 19:16-19). In this same perspective, the words of the Apostle John have a categorical ring: "Anyone who hates his brother is a murderer, and you know that no murderer has eternal life abiding in him" (1 Jn 3:15).

    From the beginning, the living Tradition of the Church-as shown by the Didache, the most ancient non-biblical Christian writing-categorically repeated the commandment "You shall not kill": "There are two ways, a way of life and a way of death; there is a great difference between them... In accordance with the precept of the teaching: you shall not kill ... you shall not put a child to death by abortion nor kill it once it is born ...     As time passed, the Church's Tradition has always consistently taught the absolute and unchanging value of the commandment "You shall not kill".     55. This should not cause surprise: to kill a human being, in whom the image of God is present, is a particularly serious sin. Only God is the master of life! Yet from the beginning, faced with the many and often tragic cases which occur in the life of individuals and society, Christian reflection has sought a fuller and deeper understanding of what God's commandment prohibits and prescribes. 43 There are in fact situations in which values proposed by God's Law seem to involve a genuine paradox. This happens for example in the case of legitimate defence, in which the right to protect one's own life and the duty not to harm someone else's life are difficult to reconcile in practice. Certainly, the intrinsic value of life and the duty to love oneself no less than others are the basis of a true right to self-defence. The demanding commandment of love of neighbour, set forth in the Old Testament and confirmed by Jesus, itself presupposes love of oneself as the basis of comparison: "You shall love your neighbour as yourself " (Mk 12:31). Consequently, no one can renounce the right to self-defence out of lack of love for life or for self. This can only be done in virtue of a heroic love which deepens and transfigures the love of self into a radical self-offering, according to the spirit of the Gospel Beatitudes (cf. Mt 5:38-40). The sublime example of this self-offering is the Lord Jesus himself.

    Moreover, "legitimate defence can be not only a right but a grave duty for someone responsible for another's life, the common good of the family or of the State".44 Unfortunately it happens that the need to render the aggressor incapable of causing harm sometimes involves taking his life. In this case, the fatal outcome is attributable to the aggressor whose action brought it about, even though he may not be morally responsible because of a lack of the use of reason. 45

    56. This is the context in which to place the problem of the death penalty. On this matter there is a growing tendency, both in the Church and in civil society, to demand that it be applied in a very limited way or even that it be abolished completely. The problem must be viewed in the context of a system of penal justice ever more in line with human dignity and thus, in the end, with God's plan for man and society. The primary purpose of the punishment which society inflicts is "to redress the disorder caused by the offence".46 Public authority must redress the violation of personal and social rights by imposing on the offender an adequate punishment for the crime, as a condition for the offender to regain the exercise of his or her freedom. In this way authority also fulfils the purpose of defending public order and ensuring people's safety, while at the same time offering the offender an incentive and help to change his or her behaviour and be rehabilitated. 47

    It is clear that, for these purposes to be achieved, the nature and extent of the punishment must be carefully evaluated and decided upon, and ought not go to the extreme of executing the offender except in cases of absolute necessity: in other words, when it would not be possible otherwise to defend society. Today however, as a result of steady improvements in the organization of the penal system, such cases are very rare, if not practically non-existent.

    In any event, the principle set forth in the new Catechism of the Catholic Church remains valid: "If bloodless means are sufficient to defend human lives against an aggressor and to protect public order and the safety of persons, public authority must limit itself to such means, because they better correspond to the concrete conditions of the common good and are more in conformity to the dignity of the human person".48
