    Several points:

    1. The reason the Israeli army uses M16s rather than Galils is because they receive a lot of foreign aid from the US that can ONLY be spent on American weaponry. Therefore they would rather buy M16s rather than waste their own precious money on Galils. Think of it as the American gov't "rewarding" their arms manufacturers (who then give executive positions to Congressmen when they retire).

    2. The requirements of the Israeli army are very different from ours from a tactical standpoint. Ranges in the desert are much longer than in the fighting the SAF expects to encounter. That is why the Israelis employ so many snipers (.50 cal snipers some more). Even when they are forced into urban combat, the Israelis don't FIBUA like the SAF does. Their doctrine seems to be to use engineers and armor to just tear down the whole built-up area like in Hebron or Jenin (even when there are civilians!). In fact, the Israelis seem to be equipped for classic armor-based desert warfare, while the SAF is strongly geared toward infantry-based jungle and urban warfare. As a result, the Israelis have almost no use for LADs thanks to the long engagement ranges and lack of close assault doctrine.

    3. The SAR21 has a few good points that you did not bring up:

    A. It does not shit where it eats. The M16 feeds its gas directly back into the mechanism to cycle the bolt, making it one of the dirtiest rifles out there. The SAR21 and G36 both use gas pistons like most modern weapons (and even the old AK47), which makes a lot more sense. This is why M16s and M4s suck -- reliability and maintenance.

    B. The SAR21 (uniquely) does not require any zeroing, or at least very little. To me, this is its strongest feature. It really helps if you can just pick up any rifle and fire it accurately first shot.

    C. It has very low felt recoil according to most experts, though I don't know how the G36 fares here.

    D. It really is smaller than the G36 -- you don't fire the G36 (or any other carbine or folding-stock rifle) with the stock folded if you want any accuracy. Unless you are firing from the hip or a vehicle, you're going to need to open the stock and put it against your shoulder, just like any other rifle. Then the G36 becomes a bit long.

    E. The SAR21 is OUR rifle. This means that we have every right to produce, improve and modify it. It also means that we understand completely how it works. We can actually make a Mk 2 version without asking for permission, or reverse-engineering something like a G36. We can make as many as we want, when we want, along with as many spare parts as we like. This is very important.

    However, I agree that the SAR21 needs some improvement on the ergonomic side. The position of the fire selector is particularly screwed up. And the push-in safety is for things like MGs, not for something like an assault rifle, where you need to switch quickly with one hand. The SAR21 is also a bit heavier than it should have been.

    4. You really don't want to use an M16 with all the junk on it. Each item needs seperate zeroing and targeting devices. This is a pain in the ass, particularly with LADs and other aiming aids. That is also why most armies don't issue their grunts with these (only special forces). It is hard to keep the zeroing of add-ons when you are bashing around on maneuvers. As a previous poster said, they also shift the center of balance of the weapon, making it harder to use and less accurate. The SAR21 has the center of balance right over your hand, which is very nice.

    Much has been made about how you do not need to zero the SAR21, but how exactly has this been achieved? The shooting scope is that it is focused to infinity. As long as the user can see the sight picture, he does not have to worry about the position of his eye relative to the weapon. Thus, the shooting scope can be zeroed at the factory itself to the barrel of the weapon without having to worry about the individual user of the weapon. The same goes for the LAD, which is also zeroed to the barrel.

    So, it is not some magical quality of the SAR21. STK has taken the extra step to make things easier for us. This means that this can be duplicated in other rifles, including the M16, if these uses scopes or reflex sights.

    It also means that for other versions, like the P-rail version, you DO have to zero the sights if it the sights have not previously been zeroed to the barrel. Or if for some reason, the shooting scope loses its zero relative to the barrel, you do have to zero the scope again, back to the barrel.

    Btw, one of the advantages of the KAC modular system is that it allows accessories such as LAD and shooting scopes to be fitted, zeroed in, and then taken off and kept in the haver-sack. These can then be refitted without loss of zero, because of the clamp and rail system employed. It also has the advantage that you can adept your weapon to mission necessity in the field. It also means that you can put what you don't need in the haver-sack, making for a lighter weapon.

    Hey, I don't think anyone here is anti-M16 per se -- it's just that it is important to call a spade a spade and identify both the good and bad points of each weapon under consideration.

    It is true that the M16 is a good weapon. I like it better than the SAR21 in its current form due to ergonomic problems actually. However, the M16 has limitations to its design that cannot be overcome. For example, it would not be impossible to create a KAC system for the SAR21, or to change the position of its controls. The furnishings of a weapon are relatively easy to change. The SAR21 is not the answer either to me though. However, with enough thought, a good weapon that is adaptable to the various needs of troops in the field can be come up with.

    Fundamentally, there are two main differences between the SAR21 and the M16:

    1. Bullpup configuration. This is one of the huge debates in the gun world. Which is better? Bullpups are definitely shorter, but the cost is poor ergonomics. In order to solve the problems with bullpups, they need to become truly ambidextrous (can be fired from either shoulder at any time), and the magazine change must be easier. Truly ambidextrous fire can only be achieved if casings can somehow not eject into the user's face. This can only be done if casings eject forward and downward, rather than left or right (or if you have caseless weapon). This is not trivial to achieve in a compact and light manner, but is important. Fixing magazine change is harder, since fooling around with a magazine well under your chin will always be hard.

    2. Mechanism. The M16 has that fouling problem. The SAR21 is currently unproven (but looks good in terms of reliability and recoil). We'll have to see. However, it seems to me that the SAR21 is easier to adapt (a LMG version with replaceable barrel is available, if I remember correctly). Does the SAR21 have a free-floating barrel?

    If anything, I feel that the day of the assault rifle may be ending. Modern American Interceptor body armor can stop 5.56mm point blank, and even 7.62mm at range with the ceramic plates in. While body armor (currently) does not cover every part of the body and the user still is bruised when hit, it makes me wonder how much longer rapid firing small calibre weapons will remain effective.

    Some form of rapid firing grenade launcher would probably be the way to go for the true infantry weapon of the future. For example, South Africa and Russia both field 6 shot revolver style 40mm grenade launchers. Everybody here probably has heard of the 20mm OICW grenade launcher, but I feel that the programme is rather misguided (simplicity is important in a weapon). A much simpler version would be better. 20mm is almost the same as 12 gauge, so it might be possible to create something like an automatic 12 gauge shotgun that instead fires 20mm impact or "hopper" airburst grenades, with flechette rounds for CQB (12 to a magazine like the Jackhammer automatic shotgun). Ring aerofoil grenades with flat trajectories would allow the weapon to be pretty accurate at range. Carry a PDW as well if you're really paranoid that the enemy will get close. A notional 10mm launcher might be even better. This would actually be a form of explosive ammo, which I believe is forbidden by convention, but you never know...

    Shotgun -- it's clear you've not led a Singaporean infantry section before. By doctrine, the 7 man section is divided into 3 fireteams or "groups", with 2 men each, and the section leader accompanying one of the teams (usually the first "assault" team). Other ad hoc arrangements are possible.

    My personal take on this is that a 7 man team is appropriate. It's not just a matter of manpower. In close terrain, large elements are difficult to maneuver and coordinate. A 2 man group is about right for both FIBUA and jungle fighting, in my experience. This leads naturally to a 7 man section with a section commander.

    Why not larger teams? Contrary to what people think, larger teams do not mean more firepower. You still need to deploy your men with appropriate spacing between them, so a 10 man section has just as many "men per metre" as a 7 man section. Firepower is a measure of the equipment carried by the soldier and his skill at using it.

    The size of your combat formations is a training or command and control issue, rather than a firepower issue. The pros and cons of larger vs smaller combat formations, IIRC:

    1. Larger formations have room for redundancy. One of the posters mentioned how combat and administrative losses might make our 7 man sections inoperative in combat. This is true, and a weakness of our formations. If one man in a 3 man fireteam is killed, it's bad. But if one man in a 2 man fireteam is killed, the team has been rendered ineffective.

    2. Larger sections can be more diverse and flexible in terms of equipment. That seems to be the main thing being focussed on in this discussion. It's hard to carry 4 or 5 types of weapons to deal with a wider variety of situations if there are just a few men in your section. However, I feel that this is overstretching the role of an infantry section. It's much easier to evade in jungle or urban terrain, so it isn't as necessary to be able to take out all kinds of threats you might run into. Also, carrying all that stuff, reduces the strength of the section in its primary roles. Jack of all trades, master of none.

    3. Larger formations need more space to deploy. If your men are used to fight in 10 man sections, then they will need around 30 metres to deploy "3-up". Find me a 30 metre wide space in jungle. They can of course deploy differently, but they aren't trained or used to doing that. They will have a tendency to "cluster fuck". This small example is only the tip of the iceberg. The size of your section has to be appropriate to the terrain.

    4. Larger formations are harder to control. In jungle, you can hardly see 5 metres in any direction standing up. How are you going to effectively command 9 other men deployed across 30 metres while prone? (Where are my men? Can they hear me? Who is the one shouting goddammit? Where is the fucking enemy?) It's not easy. It is not uncommon for the 2 men in a fireteam to lose each other in a maneuver in jungle. Imagine 10 men trying to maintain coherency. Then imagine trying to command three of these in a platoon. In an ambush, the front will be getting massacred, and the back won't even know what the hell that noise in front is. In contrast, in desert, you can see clearly for hundreds of metres in all directions, so command and control is a lot easier.

    In Vietnam, US Army and Marine Corps commanders often split their 10 man sections into smaller 2 man fireteams for these reasons. I feel that the situation as it stands now is OK. Regarding administrative and combat losses, redistribution of men between sections in a company can usually make up for this, since there are only 3 kinds of man in an infantry section (LAW, M203, SAW), though it is still a weakness.
