    Fire and movement, my friend -- bread and butter of infantry around the world. You can see a little bit of it in Saving Private Ryan if you watch carefully. The key is to only come out of cover for less than 3 seconds at a time. This is the time it takes for a soldier to engage you accurately with his weapon. Even a machinegun can only shoot one thing at a time. So one group rushes forward from one set of cover to another within 3 seconds, while the other two groups stay in cover and lay down accurate covering fire. Then when that group is in cover, another group rushes. While you are in cover, roll or crawl if you can, so that you emerge from cover from an unexpected point. If fire is heavy or you are close to the enemy, individual men rush forward in "random" patterns rather than a group at a time to make it even harder for an enemy to engage you.

    That is why accurate fire is devastating. An enemy who can engage you accurately faster than 3 seconds can pin you down completely. This is also why rifle range targets only pop out for a few seconds, so that you get realistic shooting experience.

    If there is no cover, like when you are assaulting a fortified position, there are a few ways to do it. You basically want to pin them down with tremendous firepower and overwhelm them with numbers. Firepower takes many forms, including preparatory artillery and air strikes, close air support, mortar fire or even friendly armor (which also provides cover to hide behind). What you have available to you determines what you do. Since machineguns are organic to the platoon and recoilless rifles to the company, those are trained with most often. You set up at a distance overlooking the objective and pour fire continuously into the enemy's heavy weapons, so that he cannot return fire with them at all. Then you rush him with numbers. This is a lot harder and more dangerous than when there is cover, of course. That's why it's a fortified objective after all.

    Hope this answers your questions about infantry tactics.

  • dkhoo
    26 Oct 02, 02:47
    A good page comparing types of ammo is:

    http://www.fas.org/man/dod-101/sys/land/bullets.htm

    In general, fas.org is pretty good for all kinds of military info, though as usual it is very US biased.

    The executive summary is that FMJ "ball" rounds have a projectile with a dense core surrounded by a metal jacket, producing a roughly spheroid round. These are the only rounds permitted in war. JHP rounds also have a metal jacket, but the front of the jacket is cut open, to produce a hollow point. The jacket tends to spread apart on impact, making the round wider and transferring more kinetic energy from the round to the target. These rounds have a tendency to stop inside the target rather than pass through him, and as a result have more "stopping power" but less armor penetration. The wounds caused are often horrendous, though explosive and fragmenting projectiles are even worse. They are NOT legal to be used in war, but can be used in police work, so anti-terrorist teams carry them.

    Energy equals mass multiplied by the square of velocity. A 7.62mm NATO round has more energy because it is three times as heavy and only slightly slower than the 5.56mm. A 7.62mm NATO round will stop someone in his tracks because of it's larger calibre (the greater the cross-section area, the greater the energy transfer), greater intrinsic energy, and the terminal effect of it's bullet (basically, the way the bullet tumbles in muscle tissue instead of fragmenting into smaller pieces).

    The 7.62mm NATO round is designed for range. It will give a flatter trajectory than the M193 round over a distance of 800m. This "problem" (it's only a problem if you need that kind of range) is solved in the M855 round, which has a flatter trajectory out to 800m as well.

    The price paid for this power and range is greater recoil and a heavier weight per round.

    Of course, in terms of absolutely the greatest stopping power fired from a portable weapon, it'll be a 12.7mm NATO, which will stop an elephant dead in its tracks, drill through an engine block, and penetrate concrete walls... but no sane man is going to drag a Barette M82 through the jungle.
