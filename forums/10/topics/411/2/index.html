    Actually, any rifle that is chambered for the M193 round would be able to fire the M855/SS109 round. This is because the external dimensions of both rounds are identical, since the longer bullet of the M855 round sits deeper in the casing. However, rifles which use the M855 round have to have a tighter rifling twist, in order to stabilize the heaver bullet. (M193 is 55 grains, and M855 is 62 grains if I recall correctly. What are "grains" btw?) Again, if I remember correctly, the M16A1 or M16S1 barrel has a 1 in 12" rifling, and the M16A2 or other rifles using the newer round have a 1 in 7" rifling.

    The optimal rifling twist for the M855 round is actually 1 in 9" but the 1 in 7" rifling was used, so as to stablize heavier tracer rounds that may be used occasionally. The rather tight rifling twist is what I believe causes the ineffectiveness of the bullet that you mentioned.

    What happens with the M193 round is that the 1 in 12" rifling is just within the critical limits required to stablize a bullet of that weight, so when it hits any obstruction such as armour or a person's body, the round destabilizes very quickly, and tends to keyhole (turn on its side). If it hits hard armour, it tends to go splat and generally not pierce the armour but if it hits a soft obstruction such as a human body, the bullet would destabilize and turn on its side, causing the bullet to split apart at its cannelure (the ring thing that the casing is crimped around) and fragment. This causes the horrible wounds that were documented in the Vietnam War, when the M193 round was being used. The bullet would normally blow out huge chunks of flesh and create large wounds that would result in massive bleeding and a quick death, especially if fired at ranges closer than 25m. Also, if the bullet fragmented, the lead fragments from the bullet's core could cause lead poisoning if not removed quickly, and this was generally very hard to do since bullet itself was already rather small, more so for the fragments.

    As for the M855/SS109 round, the tight rifling twist makes the round very hard to destabilize even when it hits obstacles, so it normally just "drills" and punches through the target. This would make it more effective against armour, but rather ineffective against personnel, because it would normally create a very small and neat wound, and normally with very little loss of flesh. And since the bullet doesn't even remain in the body, the wound is both minor and usually heals rather quickly, provided the wound is sterilized and bandaged properly and that the bullet doesn't hit any vital organs. Against personnel with body armour however, I think the train of thought was that the armour would sufficiently destabilize the bullet to the point that it would have the same effect on the body as the M193 after it passed through the armour.

    Came across your discussions. Couldn't resist replying. Let's compare the SAR21, G36 and M16 in terms of length, safety, optics and versatility.

    Length: SAR21 is the shortest, but remember, the G36 has a folding stock, that takes about 20cm out of the length. So, the length is almost the same. No difference. Score: 1-1-0

    Safety: The SAR21 uses a push button safety fitted to the top of the front guard. The G36 and M16 both employs a thumb safet/selector switch. The G36's switch is ambidextrous.

    I've tried the SAR21 before. I cocked the weapon, and had to use my left hand to push the safety on. When I tried to push the safety button off with my right hand index finger with the weapon shouldered, I had some difficulty getting enough leverage. I had to tilt the gun to one side to gain more leverage to push the button in. I prefer the old M16 in this respect. Easier to switch the safety off with a simple swipe of the thumb. The same should go for the G36.

    Pushing the safety in, then moving the index finger to the trigger is two distinct motion. Much easier with a thumb switch.

    Also, to switch the SAR21 to auto, you had to move your left hand from the front guard to behind the magazine to push in the selector button. Again, switching the M16 and G36 to auto is much easier. Another swipe of the thumb.

    Furthermore, it is impossible to switch the SAR21 to safe with just the firing hand. You had to use your left hand. What if your left hand is occupied?

    Score: 0-1-1

    Optics: Much had been made about the SAR21's optics. I've tried the optics before. It is a black circle. When the gun is cocked, a tiny black dot appears in the centre of the circle.

    I actually prefer the M16's iron sights. The contrast between the front sight is better than the black circle. It was also easier to acquire the M16 front sight for snap sighting. Perhaps it is because when I tried the SAR21, I was in a shed, and the target was covered by the shadow of a camo net. Perhaps it is because of my eyesight, but this goes to show the SAR21 optics is not ALWAYS better.

    The G36 uses a red dot reflex sight. I tried an Aimpoint, a Tasco version, an ACOG reflex sight and a Bushnell Holosight before. They are great. It was easy to pick out the red electronic dot against any background, and very easy to acquire for snap sighting. I tried a Bushnell Holosight equipped SAR21-P. It's definitely better.

    Furthermore, the German Army version had a 3.5x shooting scope fitted to ALL their G36, under the red dot reflex sight.

    Score: 0-2-1

    Versatility: One thing I definitely like about the M16 is it's versatility. You can fit a 4x scope by simply putting it on the carrying handle. You can fit an M203 by removing the front guard. Not so simple with the vanilla SAR21.

    With the G36, there is also a hole in the carrying handle to which you can fit other systems. And let's not forget, with the German version, this is a moot point as the 3.5x scope is standard. It also has its own version of a 40mm grenade launcher.

    Much has been said about the laser point incorporated into the front guard. But you can clamp it to the barrels of the M16 and G36 too. So, no real advantage.

    When it comes to true versatility, nothing beats the M4A1 carbine fitted with a KAC Modular Weapon System. This carbine is fitted with P-rails on the top, bottom and both sides of the hand guard. You can fit laser sights, flashlights, grenade launcher, shot-gun, fore-grip, fore-grip/flash-light/laser-pointer combination, together with a whole variety of sights on the p-rail over the receiver of the carbine. Now, how's that for versatility?

    Score: 0-1-2

    So, what is the final score:

    SAR21:1 G36:5 M16:6

    Let's keep our M16's but fit it with the KAC Modular Weapon System. : )
