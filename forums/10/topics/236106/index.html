    Singapore: Biomedical Growth Down To Single Digits Next 2 Yrs

    SINGAPORE -(Dow Jones)- The annual growth of Singapore's biomedical sciences output may slow to "high single digits" in the next two years, compared with 30.2% growth in 2006, the chairman of the country's biosciences promotion agency said on Tuesday.

    "The industry is sustainable in the long run," Phillip Yeo, chairman of the state-run Agency for Science, Technology and Research, said at a briefing.

    Singapore's biomedical sciences industry output totaled S$23 billion in 2006, A-STAR said earlier on Tuesday.

    Biomedical sciences accounted for 10% of Singapore's total manufacturing output in 2006, the agency said.

    The pharmaceutical sector is highly volatile and output can widely vary from year to year depending on manufacturers' product mix and import regulations around the world.

    "The 30% growth is not sustainable," said Beh Swan Gin, executive director of the government's Biomedical Research Council.

    Yeo said there are a few pharmaceutical plants coming onstream, but output from the plants may not be seen this year.

    Singapore is investing heavily in biotechnology as part of its development strategy to lessen its dependence on manufacturing.

    -By John Jannarone, Dow Jones Newswires

    OUTPUT by Singapore's drug factories jumped by over a third last year, pushing overall biomedical production up by 30.2 per cent.
    This robust growth puts biomedical output at a record $23 billion, almost four times production in 2000.

    More than 90 per cent comes from pharmaceuticals, with the rest from the medical technology sector, a field that makes devices such as heart valves.

    Singapore will soon have a new kind of factory on the biomedical scene - cutting-edge plants manufacturing human vaccines and other drugs derived by culturing living cells - a highly advanced field called biologics.

    The fledgling biomedical sector already accounts for one quarter of the gross domestic product (GDP) generated by the manufacturing industry as a whole, making it second only to electronics, which accounts for 29 per cent.

    The figures on the health of the biomedical sciences cluster, which were released yesterday, also showed that it employed 10,571 people last year, up 3.9 per cent from 2005.

    A further 1,800 jobs will be created when new biomedical investments committed last year are fully realised, a process that typically takes three to four years.

    These new commitments will be worth about $2.3 billion in additional GDP a year.

    New plants coming on stream include Singapore's first commercial-scale biologics facilities as well as plants by pharmaceutical giants Abbot and Merck.

    New entrant Abbott's planned $450 million facility, for example, will be its largest nutritional plant.

    International players also continue to set up research programmes here, one of the latest being the Novartis Institute for Tropical Diseases' new malaria research programme.

    The Government target for the biomedical sector to generate $25 billion in output and 15,000 in jobs by 2015 looks within reach, said Mr Yeoh Keat Chuan, executive director at the Economic Development Board for the biomedical sector.

    'The outlook is very positive for biomedical sciences, which means we are likely to achieve our targets early,' he said.

    At the research and development (R&D) level, the number of biomedical research scientists and engineers has grown from 2,055 in 2001 to 4,054 at last count.

    Biomedical research units linked to the Agency for Science, Technology and Research (A*Star) have also chalked up 15 patents, since their first one in 2002, in areas such as virology and bioimaging tools.

    The foundation for biomedical sciences has been set, according to Mr Philip Yeo, outgoing A*Star chairman.

    'For the last five years, we have done basement one, first floor,' he quipped, leaving successor Lim Chuan Poh and his team to build the second and third floors.

    EDB's biomedical investment fund Bio*One Capital - designed to nurture firms contributing to the sector's growth - ploughed $114 million into 16 projects last year. Some products from its portfolio companies are entering the trial stage.

    Bio*One plans to continue supporting firms in biologics as well as those in medical technology and small molecules translational projects.

    What the Government plans to push next is something called 'translational research' or bringing medical innovations from the laboratory to the hospital bed, and finally on to the marketplace.

    (Straits Times, 7 Feb)
