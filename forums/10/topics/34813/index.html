    Investments in selected portfolios from a string of choices is akin to picking the right race horses from a stable of choice breeds.

    Certainly, one would pick from a winning team of reknown stable, trainer and jockey.

    The final risks is a calculated one, given the competition on the track, the track condition, the temperament of the horse and jockey on the challenging day itself.

    Investment portfolios are somewhat the same, we pick Companies based on audited accounts, Profit and Loss and Balance Sheets, Records of Past Financial Years, the Potential for Growth, the State of the Market, the State of the Economic Prospects.

    At least in a race, the results are quite obvious and immediately known.

    In a State managed and funded investment, this becomes a sensitive political issue, especially when the selected "choice horse" do not come home as a winner, but dropped dead mid-way.

    If one poor selection out of ten races can affect one's limited funds, how should one react, when eight out of ten races meets with similar results?

    How many bad investments should one tolerate, and how much should be bled before it is determined that "enough is enough"?

    The Japanese Management Style was once alluded to as being a Prime Example to be emulated, in that the Top Man will accept the responsibility for the failure of the Corporation.

    However, it looks like we follow the Japanese Management Style in areas where it is convenient, and although the Corporate Behavior is expected of other Singapore Management, it is not observed by the Singapore Government and its GLCs.
