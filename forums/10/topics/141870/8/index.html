    Accountability, transparency key in overcoming financial scandals: US charity organisation

    By Channel NewsAsia's US Correspondent Owen Fay

    WASHINGTON : The allegations against Singapore's National Kidney Foundation are far from unusual.

    Worldwide, charitable organisations have often had to cope with suggestions of financial improprieties.

    The United Way of America is a massive charity organisation distributing money across the United States to more than 1,400 organisations.

    Last year alone, United Way raised more than US$4 billion.

    But the company has gone through its own problems.

    Thirteen years ago, the company's president was ousted after he was found to have been running up massive expenses in the course of carrying out his job; accounting irregularities and suspect record-keeping were also uncovered.

    Officials at United Way say they overcame these problems through one very simple strategy.

    Anthony Owens, United Way Foundation, said, "You have to show accountability and you have to be transparent. You have to show the accountability, that the people you have in place are going to be held accountable for their actions and then show the transparency. You have to fully disclose...all information to the public to allow them to generate that trust and credibility factor back in."

    Other companies faced the same kind of setbacks.

    There have been cases where people donated their own money thinking it was going for a specific purpose only to find that it was being diverted elsewhere.

    In the weeks after the September 11 terrorist attacks, Americans donated more than US$500 million to the Red Cross.

    But not long after, it emerged that fully 40 percent of those funds were going to administrative costs and other Red Cross concerns.

    When that came to light, people, not surprisingly, were furious.

    And the head of the American Red Cross had to resign.

    At the United Way they were not surprised

    Mr Owens said, "Anyone can tell you it takes a second to lose credibility and a lifetime to gain it back. But when you're dealing with an issue of charitable organisations and these organisations are depending on the donations and dollars to sustain their operations, that snowball effect has to happen a lot quicker."

    United Way has learned the hard way that in the wake of a financial scandal, transparency is what matters most. - CNA/ms
