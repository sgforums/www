    Article from asia1.com

    It's time for leadership change: Durai
    CEO says he believes in change; earlier, he had said he would not quit
    By Theresa Tan
    July 15, 2005
    The Straits Times
    PUTTING up a brave front after resigning, besieged National Kidney Foundation (NKF) chief T.T. Durai told reporters he 'strongly believed in change'.

    Barely an hour earlier, Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan had announced Mr Durai's resignation as chief executive officer of the NKF at a hastily convened press conference.

    Mr Durai, who initially avoided answering reporters' questions after the press conference, was smiling as he said: 'I have been running NKF for 37 years and I think it's time that a new leadership takes over because I strongly believe in change... a new style.'

    Asked if quitting was a tough decision to make, Mr Durai, 57, said 37 years was a long time and the time had come for a leadership change.

    After dropping his defamation suit against The Straits Times on Tuesday, he maintained that he had done nothing wrong and said he would not resign 'unless people want me to'.

    Overnight, thousands of outraged Singaporeans called for his resignation through online petitions, letters to newspapers and calls to the media.

    As of last night, almost 40,000 people had signed an online petition calling for his removal. By yesterday, more than 6,800 NKF donors had cancelled their regular donations too.

    On Wednesday morning, too, the walls of the NKF headquarters were vandalised with graffiti attacking him.

    Yesterday afternoon, Mr Durai and NKF chairman Richard Yong sought Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan's views on how to go about restoring public trust in the organisation.

    Mr Durai offered to step down and asked the minister if that would 'be helpful'. Mr Khaw told him it would.

    About three hours later at the press conference, a red-eyed and drawn Mr Durai sat looking down most of the time. He looked to Mr Khaw, seated beside him, almost every time reporters put a question to him.

    The minister did most of the talking during the press conference, which was telecast live over Channel News Asia. After the hour-long session, he left immediately, flanked by Mr Yong and vice-chairman Alwyn Lim.

    A former lawyer, Mr Durai started as an NKF volunteer when he was an undergraduate in the 1960s. After 21 years as a volunteer, he took on the top post in 1992.

    In the past decade, the NKF grew to become Singapore's largest charity, with an income of about $100 million in 2003 and about 1,000 employees.

    It also expanded from helping kidney patients, to providing health screening for the masses and starting a fund for cancer patients this year.

    After yesterday's press conference, Mr Durai refused to answer reporters' questions until someone asked if he had anything to say to the public.

    He replied: 'I want to thank the donors of the NKF for helping me in the past 37 years to build a great institution which has achieved iconic status in the world.

    'I pray that donors continue to support the NKF henceforth.'

    I watched the press conference and his response to reporters outside the office. I couldn't help but notice a certain smugness in him while he answered questions.

    Similarly, when quizzed on the difficult choice of stepping down and the reasons why, he chose to focus on his achievements over the last 37 years and that he believed in change. Hence the impression he gave was that he felt the reasons why he was stepping down was becoz he believed in fresh faces to take over his 'glittering record', not becoz he was forced out by public outrage.

    Clearly TT Durai still thinks he has done nothing wrong, whether ethically or morally. The arrogance in his smile seemed to underline his defiance to public opinion. Not one shred of humility was offered nor any form of apology from either him or the board regarding the whole fiasco.

    Makes me wonder whether he deserved any respect in the first place.
