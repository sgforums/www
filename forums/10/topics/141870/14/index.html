    Sept 20, 2005
    No cancer fund? That's a backward step for NKF

    I am an ardent donor of the National Kidney Foundation (although I am not a big contributor) and am very proud of the status it has achieved over the years.

    I can say I supported its programmes and objective to save and help the unfortunate.

    When I first received a letter from then CEO T.T. Durai asking me to support him in setting up a new fund for less fortunate children, I did not hesitate and contributed what I could to the fund.

    I was proud that the institution was embarking on a life-saving programme from kidney patients to disabled children. I strongly believe that it is the way to go for an institution that has a successful programme for kidney patients to extend its experience and know-how to help others.

    It is just like a corporation expanding its area of expertise to other products but in this case it is a non-profit organisation.

    Last year, it stated its intention to help cancer patients. I supported this new fund without hesitation. Since then, I had been contributing whatever I could to the funds.

    The recent news to separate them into two different funds upsets me. Whatever that happened in the NKF saga about transparency are being looked into and rectified by its new board and CEO.

    Should not the new team just move forward in the direction set by the previous team and not move backward?

    Donors need to be educated. Tell them about the objectives of NKF and the direction it is moving in. There is no need to change its direction just because some donors are not happy.

    I'd rather have donors who support the vision and direction of the institution than please those who cannot appreciate the goodwill. Going back to become just an institution for kidney patients is a step backward. Why have so many charities when many of them have problems raising funds and helping their beneficiaries?

    The NKF Children Medical Fund and NKF Cancer Fund will now have to employ their own administration, management and fundraising staff.

    Will this not eat into the charity dollars? Does Singapore have that many qualified volunteers to sit on the boards? Is it not better to belong to a single body which can effectively raise funds by using the same staff and administrative manpower?

    Will it not be cost effective to have the existing staff to multi-task?

    How will the cancer patients feel now? I remember the smiles on the faces of cancer patients when they were first told of NKF's intention to help them.

    How disappointed they will be now? They will feel that they have been abandoned. How about the disabled children? I just can't bear to see the disappointment on their faces.

    I believe the decision of the new team will also not go down well with the kidney patients who had been supportive of the NKF's decision then.

    We do charity work for humanity's sake. Because of this, we strive to better ourselves by helping the unfortunate, go forward whenever we can and help all those who are in need.

    The NKF, as an institution, had moved forward in its mission to help the unfortunate. Let's move forward despite the obstacles we face. I believe the staff of NKF and its donors will not stumble but carry on the mission.

    I plead with the new NKF board and CEO to reconsider and move forward with the vision of helping the less fortunate individuals.

    I wish all at NKF good health and will continue to do my part in contributing to its funds.

    William Tan
