    It is easy to allow anectodal cases to tar everything. It is not true that children in general (over in Singapore) are spoiled or over-protected.

    I remembered one parent whom I called in over his son's grades. He gave me a cane and told me in Mandarin, "Teacher, I give you the cane and the authority. If he doesn't listen, I allow you to cane him!" I had to give it back to him. I told him that I will cane my own son but I do not cane my pupils. Somehow I got the feeling he doesn't get what I mean, because he repeated the same line again. Unfortunately, the child was still not studying.

    Another pupil of mine was called up by me for not doing his silent reading. I got him to stand in front of his class and do his silent reading. The angry mother confronted me the next day for causing 'psychological damage' (she spoke in Mandarin) to her child. I told her plainly that I had no reason to touch her son if he did nothing wrong. Off she went to the principal. Thank God I had a supportive principal. She simply told her, "Mdm, I can give you the hotline for MOE and the e-mail address. Why don't you tell them that my Discipline Master has disciplined your child?" Well...we did manage to get to talk on calmer terms about her child, and from what I knew, I was spoken of kindly by her when she's with the other parents.

    Yet another pupil of mine...well...he's incredible. He joins no CCAs. He doesn't participate much in PE. He doesn't interact much with his class. When he was in Primary 5, his mother refused to allow him to attend the P5 camp, paid for by the school. When he got a chance to go to an event much coveted by Primary 5 pupils - the National Day Parade rehearsal - his mother wrote in to disallow his attendance. When his form teacher spoke to him, he told her what his mother said, "What if the terraces collapse? What will happen to you? It's much safer at home!" The last I got a chance to spoke to her (after he's gone to Secondary school), she told me in worried tones how he has to stay behind in school for his band CCA (CCA is compulsory in Secondary School). I have to assure her that this is part of his growing up, and is good for him. I wondered how much of his independence he has to reclaim as he goes into his teenage years.

    But for each of these cases, I can also come up with truly independent pupils who did charity work, gave tuition to their siblings, led a 'rebellion' against the class teacher (yikes! I had to be called in to mediate) and so on. I can recall pupils who can climb trees and wallow in mud pools. It just happens that the extreme cases are the ones we remember most!
