    SINGAPORE: PM, MM sue Feer for defamation,
    Prime Minister Lee and Minister Mentor Lee file suit against Hong Kong-based magazine, Far Eastern Economic Review, for article on opposing politician Chee Soon Juan,Straits Times,Thursday, September 14, 2006,By Aaron Low
    fr --/www.asiamedia.ucla.edu/article-southeastasia.asp?parentid=52911

    Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong and Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew have filed a defamation suit here against the publisher and the editor of the Far Eastern Economic Review (Feer).

    The suit, filed in the High Court on Aug 22 against Review Publishing and Mr Hugo Restall, stemmed from an article on opposition politician Chee Soon Juan.

    Mr Restall interviewed the Singapore Democratic Party chief for the article, titled "Singapore's 'Martyr', Chee Soon Juan", which the Hong Kong-based magazine published in July.

    The article remains freely available on Feer's website through its archive service.
    (Lion's note ,still availiable as at 15.09.2006,0903H)

    It has also been picked up and circulated through e-mail messages, and posted on several blogs and online forums.

    It is understood that lawyers for the Lees served writs of summons on Review Publishing and Mr Restall in their Hong Kong offices on Sept 4.

    Both have until Sept 25 to appear in the High Court here to respond to the suit.

    The lawyers first issued letters of demand from PM Lee and MM Lee to the publisher and Mr Restall on July 13.

    They demanded that Feer remove the allegedly defamatory article from its website, issue a public apology and pay costs and damages to the Lees by July 24.

    Review Publishing offered to publish the lawyers' letter instead, and suggested that the magazine carry an interview with MM Lee.

    Drew and Napier, acting for the Lees, rejected this and said the magazine was attempting to profit from their defamation by carrying an interview.

    They then proceeded to file a defamation suit against both parties in the High Court on Aug 22, and asked Feer to appoint lawyers to receive the writs of summons.

    Feer, which has no representative here, initially said it would appoint lawyers, but did not do so.

    On Aug 25, the Lees' lawyers applied to the High Court for permission to serve the writs on the publisher and Mr Restall in Hong Kong.

    In the article, Mr Restall wrote of the political situation here and the Government's managing of the reserves, among other things.

    He asked if Singapore deserved its reputation for a 'squeaky-clean government', and cited revelations from the National Kidney Foundation scandal.

    The scandal raised questions about the Government's supervision of the charity, he wrote.

    He also referred to the Government's control of public funds, and cited transparency as well as how funds were used, including at election time.

    Mr Restall and Review Publishing could not be contacted for their comments last night.

    Feer, which says it has a print circulation here of about 3,000, is a declared foreign newspaper.

    Last month, it was informed by the Government that this means it has to comply with certain conditions, including appointing a representative here to accept service of any notice or legal process, and to submit a security deposit of $200,000.

    It had up to Sept 11 to meet those requirements.
