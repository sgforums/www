    I toyed around with the idea of politics at university, but I took a calculated risk.     By that time, I had decided that politics was not an activity that I wanted to be involved in for a number of reasons. First, the personalities that I had to deal with were ruled more by their hearts and gonads than they were by their heads - that made working with them difficult, if not impossible. Second, there is only one hard and fast rule of politics: you're damned if you do and you're damned if you don't. My time is much better spent on things other than dealing with this factor. Finally, I suppose that this is the influence of the Commandos, but I take invisibility to be my most powerful weapon. During this president's term, I used my influence with the student union to get the academic staff to revise some of their policies, in particular instituting a "three strikes and you're out rule" that applied to students who disrupted lectures. The academic staff were terrified of student union action if they threw students out of lecture theatres, and so I brokered a very quiet agreement between the union and the staff to respect the three strikes rule. If a student was thrown out of the class after three warnings, the union would not back the student's complaints. After brokering that deal, I had my head of department pretty much at my beck and call.     I read through Lee's article again, and I can see how the polemic tone of the article could raise adverse reactions. Being one of those who believes in Ernest Hemingway's claim that "Auto racing, bullfighting and mountain climbing are the only true sports - the rest are just games", I was annoyed at Lee's treatment of racing.     In my opinion, the key statement here is "The former kind of risk-taking behaviour is what we want to encourage. It is not so much that there is anything wrong with bungee jumping or motor sports (although the poor standard of Lee's writing does seem to point things in that direction), but that the propensity to take risks for its own sake may not only be inapplicable, it may be hazardous.

    One of my favourite movie quotes comes from Ronin: "Lady, I never walk into a place that I don't know how to walk out of". It encapsulates the spirit of calculated risk-taking: never allow yourself to get into a dead end. Whenever I supervise an Honours student's thesis (this is the first year since 1998 that I have not taken a student on), I question their research design before they even begin to try to propose it. I take them through every step of the process from beginning to end and ask them what they expect to happen at each stage. After that I ask them what they would do if the expected result did not eventuate. Every single time, my students get stumped by the second question, because they fail to come up with contingency plans and develop options for themselves in the event that something goes wrong. Some students have listened and learned to plan accordingly; these are the ones who eventually understood the reason for having to produce a thesis. A few did not listen; they all passed, but their results could have been much better.

    What stood out about this latter group of students was that they were firmly stuck on the idea that their thesis must be earth-shattering. My response to them was invariably that such a goal is fine, as long as it does not come at the cost of the basic obligation to produce a piece of research that is as close to technically perfect as possible.     This is not to say that I discouraged my students from doing anything special with their research; I simply ensured that they were aware of the basic requirements to be fulfilled before they got on to the fancy stuff. The last thesis I supervised is a prime case of this.     The difference between this last student and the others is that she understood and accepted, albeit reluctantly, the fundamental requirement of calculated risk-taking: before you go out and do anything extraordinary, make sure that all of the ordinary bases are covered.

    Writing a thesis is not a matter of making a split-second decision; it is, however, no less a matter of making a timely decision, because a false move could take months to fix. One of the lecturers who sends her students my way has specifically said that she does so because of my reputation for forward planning, by which I ensure that costly mistakes do not occur down the track, in the course of bending every available rule to push the studentsÂ’ performance envelopes.

    This highlights the nature of calculated risk-taking as a happy medium that needs to be struck. On one end of the scale we have the mentality that goes, "Oh, no, we must not deviate from the ordinary; let's stick with what has worked in the past". On the other end, we have, "This is boring - let's do something completely different". The danger of the first approach is that at the rate business, and indeed life in general, progress these days, standing still guarantees that you will go backwards; on the other hand, throwing out the baby with the bathwater by invalidating things that genuinely work is equally unwise.

    To tie all of this back to Lee's point, I would not necessarily decide to exclude somebody from my team if he bungee jumps. However, if this individual was so addicted to the rush of bungee jumping that he went out of his way in other arenas to get the same rush, I would have grave concerns about how he would affect my team's work. I certainly would not want some cowboy coming along and derailing a carefully laid strategy simply for the thrill of it.

    In the course of writing this post, I have realized (to some embarrassment) that we have in fact glossed over one of the basic tenets of this discussion. Going back to Remaking Singapore, are we talking about risk-taking, or are we in fact talking about lateral thinking, of which risk-taking is a part?

    Captain Roger Courtney, RN, said to recruits of the Folboat Section of the Special Boat Squadron in 1942, Â“Are you TOUGH? If so, get out. I need f***ers with intelligence.Â”. Being gung-ho is not all it takes to conquer new frontiers. It is only part of the equation under the aegis of lateral thinking, so that workable solutions may be devised for problems that arise. To generate a novel thought, one must have the willingness to risk deviating from the tried and tested; keep in mind, however, that this willingness is subservient to the goal of generating the optimal solution. Coming up with a new approach for the sake of novelty may lead one to miss the point of the entire exercise, which would make it a wasted effort and would jeopardize everything that had hitherto been carefully established.

    Well Atobe, the reason why I keep things under control is that it allows me the freedom to run a few things on the ragged edge! Apologies, by the way, for taking so long to post Â– it has been a busy week.

    In general, I certainly do not dismiss members of the opposition in disgust, but I do not have enough faith in what I see at present. As I mentioned before, I treasure my invisibility because scrutiny interferes with my goals. I would have to take a very carefully calculated risk if I were to back any member of the opposition. Such an individual would have to be savvy enough to understand the rules of the game, intelligent enough to use those rules to advantage and canny enough to maintain my invisibility. I can see no such person in Singapore's political scene. For the moment, the situation is not set for such an endeavour.

    Speaking of endeavours, you are right in saying that almost every action we perform carries a certain amount of risk, and my philosophy has always been to minimise, if not eliminate that risk, in order to ensure success. I have often been found out on a limb, literally and figuratively, trying to achieve things that many others wrote off as being impossible or too complex. Certainly in my younger years, I had my fair share of failures, often with spectacular results. However, underlying all of those adventures was the sense that the formula was intrinsically right, even though some variables were off the mark. Along the way in secondary school, I developed for myself what I fondly call the Boeing school of life: you visit the site of the accident and pick through the wreckage, working out what went wrong and how to prevent it from going wrong again. After that, never revisit the crash site.

    I cannot speak on the behalf of others about what drives others to persist despite repeated failures, but I can clearly remember what set me on that path. I was in Primary 2 when a test result showed my IQ to be 142. These days, of course, I know that the figure has little real meaning, but it was a rather heady experience for an eight-year-old, and from that point on, nobody could presume to tell me anything that did not entirely make sense to me. To some extent, the obnoxious eight-year-old lives on - I refuse to believe that something is impossible until I find it out for myself.

    The next milestone was the day of my passing-out parade. That evening, I sat in my room staring at the beret in my lap and a thought occurred to me: up to that point, I had been running about trying to prove something, but I had neglected to consider what I was trying to prove and who I was trying to prove it to. From that point, I decided that I had to be my own compass, and working out how that was to be achieved has been my biggest challenge to date.

    I am fortunate in that I have experienced the influence of a number of individuals who had met this challenge before I did. I watched and learned from them, and right now I know enough about it to use those lessons for myself, but I have as yet not learned enough to pass those lessons on. Perhaps it is one of those things that one has to see rather than hear.

    There is a certain freedom in being able to define one's goals for oneself, but it comes at a price. Nobody is there to answer your questions and you become your harshest judge, one that you cannot walk away from, ignore or berate into submission.

    A case in point comes from this week. There is a project that I am working on at the moment, and for the life of me I could not understand my boss' vision of how it has to be done. My boss can be quite an abrasive individual (as Americans are wont to be), but I have already told him that he has to try much harder if he wants to upset me. What did annoy me, and what kept annoying me until I got the answer, was that I did not have a clear enough understanding of what was required of me, to the point that I could almost instinctively take the correct course of action. Along the way, I think I got my boss to tear some of his hair out, because I kept getting it wrong. I could have simply gone with exactly what he prescribed, but that was not satisfying Â– I knew what worked, but I did not know how it worked Â– so I kept working at defining the underlying principles until I got it right. I have been in this sort of situation plenty of times before, and until the problem was solved, I was continuously harangued by that harshest of judges Â– myself.

    Should we inhibit children from taking risks so that they can learn? Certainly not. That said, I think a modicum of resistance should be applied, so that at very least these children can be prepared for they naysayers they will inevitably encounter in the wider world. Given the general tendency for people to react to environmental influences, perhaps the Â“psychoticÂ” (in the non-medical sense ) risk-takers could be from environments on either end of the inhibition scale.
