    19 Mar 04, 02:20
    Under the Singapore Constitution, presidential approval is required for the passage of all legislation, after it has cleared Parliament. Many other important, non-legislative consitutional powers, in theory, flow from the president. For example, he has the sole power to appoint judges, and I think he should have the power to dissolve Parliament as well.

    That's the theory, at least. In practice, of course, the situation is vastly different. As a previous poster pointed out, the President can only make important appointments of officials on the recommendation of the Prime Minister - the President can't just appoint anyone he likes. Also, in practice the Singapore President has never exercised his power to dissolve Parliament except where the PM has requested it prior to a general election, nor has he ever exercised his right to refuse to approve legislation passed by Parliament. This is in contrast to Presidents in other countries, who often make full use of their legal authority to check abuses of power by their respective governments, e.g. in Italy late last year, where President Ciampi refused to approve a new law that would have allowed the corrupt PM, Silvio Berlusconi, to protect the business interests of a media company he owned.
