    Article from Asiaone.com

    Singapore's style of government delivers the goods

    In the Review article, 'Singapore changing for the better' (ST, Jan 25), Liu Long Chang writes: 'Singapore has a powerful ruling party. It practises a form of democratic autocracy that accommodates popular will to vigorously implement its policies and get effective results.' He mentions this in light of the various challenges that Singapore had experienced and the Government's ability to effectively deal with them.
    Detractors of 'democratic autocracy' regard it as a form of pseudo-democracy. This implies that the ideals of democracy - namely, the promotion of civil and political liberties, limited government, free and fair competitive elections - are not implemented by the regime even though it claims to be democratic. While the aforementioned ideals are characteristics of liberal democracies, let us remind ourselves that liberal democracy is just another form of democracy.

    That means democracy is an essentially contested concept, one that is subjected to personal interpretation. The Singapore Government, according to the article, believes in an autocratic form of democracy which is deemed necessary to stave off challenges that threatened to destabilise the status quo.

    Such a perspective would make Thomas Hobbes proud. According to Hobbes (1651), absolutist government is the only alternative to anarchy and disorder and that citizens have an unqualified obligation to the state. To limit the power of government is to risk a descent into 'a state of nature'. Such arguments can be found in his book, Leviathan (1651).

    Given that Singapore is such a small country with big neighbours and limited resources, then perhaps the existence of a Leviathan-type government is more beneficial than a liberal democracy. Politics requires one to be practical. Idealism serves no purpose if it does not yield effective results. I maintain the argument that Singapore's form of democracy serves to establish an efficient and effective government that is able to deliver the goods. Rather than aspiring for limited government, let's think about its feasibility in terms of efficiency, especially so in contingent times.

    Perhaps we could benefit from Plato's wisdom when it comes to democracy. Plato used the analogy of the ship (Republic, 488 AD) to describe the inefficiency of democracy - everybody on board wants to take control of the ship, regardless of whether they are competent.

    In this state of affairs, Plato argued that only a carefully designed rule by highly educated experts would be equal to the task - a rule based on a carefully designed dictatorship of reason.

    Reza Shifdar Buang
