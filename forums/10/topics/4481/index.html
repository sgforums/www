  • Terence
    20 Jan 00, 08:51
    yes, education is important today because of the evolution of professions or ways to carve out a living.

    in the past, the reason our forefathers were able to do without much education and still attain wealth was because of mercantilism. trade and business did not require a specific education per se. observation, apprentiship and a reasonable amount of intelligence sufficed. even today, the engagement of mercantilism on a small scale does not require formal education. (think hawkers, retail traders etc.)

    the advancement in various fields, i.e. finance, social sciences, medicine, science etc. require specific education in order to participate in them. even business or multi-national companies - a more advance version of mercantilism - requires formal education because of the complexities that global trading involves. being exposed to these fields means developing the competence to contribute to them. as raw mercantilism fades, intelligence alone will not account for much without specialised knowledge.

    also life-enhancing knowledge like computer skills, advanced literacy and knowledge of global trends - all of which were not THAT important during our forefathers' time - require education.

    All in all, I think that as time goes on, we see the automation of all the more common and repetitive tasks. The value of humans in such areas is lowered drastically as inventions (including computers and processes) can do the work more efficiently. Human resources become necessary only for the "frontlines", where the changes are occuring.

    I would like to raise an issue at this point. As we know, higher education is still a luxury in many societies, and what happens most of the time is the need to provide the uneducated masses with a job keeps wages low, and stunts technological improvment in the progress. There has been no move to make educators rich, specifically, but I have the feeling that societies can jumpstart themselves out of the cycle if academics and economics can somehow be welded more tightly. It is somehow related to the "copy problem", how information is the root cause of progress because it does not obey conservation laws, but yet stunting progress by putting restrictions on duplication is the only way to make people innovate.
