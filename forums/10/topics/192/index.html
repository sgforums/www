    Blanket generalisations of what is and what is not a human rights violation(be it by the UN, US, Amnesty International or any individual), without taking into account of the unique conditions that exist in different countries, is not only wrong, but obnoxiously arrogant.
    Conscription was scrapped in America, mostly due to the Vietnam War, where American (male) citizens were forced to fight and die in a war they didn't believe in or understand.

    I think you're missing the point here; I was merely trying to draw a comparison between the situation in America when conscription was still in practice, and the situation in Singapore. The similarity present is that people in general do not like conscription since it violates their "right of choice", and provokes the same emotions. I did not at all mention that conscription in Singapore should be scrapped, as it was in America, just because it is a violation of human rights.

    No where did I obnoxiously and arrogantly try to blanket-generalize what is and is not a human rights violation. A human right violation, is a human violation in any country, and in any unique condition that may exist in said countries. A human right violation, may however be more excusable and understandable in the different countries, if the unique situations that exist in them are taken into account.

    For example, conscription in Singapore, though a violation of human rights, is excusable and understandable when viewed in a local context where the population is just too small to support a regular (non-conscription) armed force. Same way, the lack of freedom of speech in China, though a violation of human rights, is excusable and understandable when viewed in China's context, where the population is way too large to effectively control or allow dissent and rebellion, or other forms of anarchy.

    In either example stated above, a violation of human rights is -still- a violation of human rights, just that these violations are excusable and understandable in the unique conditions that exist in both countries.

    As a more direct comparison, giving someone a death sentence does violate his right to life. But when taking in consideration that person killed 10 other people when unprovoked and with malicious intent, this violation of human rights becomes more excusable and understandable.

    I think you have misinterpreted what I have said in my earlier post and perhaps you should re-read what I said.

    Blanket generalizations of what is and what is not a blanket generalization of human rights violation, without taking into account the unique situation that may exist in the different contexts of different arguments, is not only wrong, but obnoxiously arrogant.
