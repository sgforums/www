    Some components of democracy must encompass 1) the rule of law which forms a fundamental principle of the constitution. The absolute supremacy or predominance of regular law as opposed to the influence of arbitrary power and excludes the existence of prerogatives or even wide discretionary authority on the part of the government. i.e. no man is punishable except for a distinct breach of the law established in a legal manner before the courts.

    Equality before the law or the equal subjection of all classes to the ordinary law of the land administered by the law courts. It thus excludes the idea of any exemption of officials or others from the duty of obidence to the law which governs other citizens. ie. no man is above the law whatever his rank or position.

    2) An entrenched (enshrined into the constitution) Bill of Rights, it will place new limits on the powers of the government. Restrain the abuse of power by the Executive branch of government and Parliament itself.

    A Bill of Rights involves giving more power to the courts. Where any Act of Parliament, common law rule or official action is contrary to the Bill of Rights, it can be declared invalid by the courts.

    A Bill of Rights must guarantee the protection of fundamental values and freedoms.

    It will thus provide a set of minimum standards to which public decision making must conform to (ie. transparency) In that sense a Bill of Rights is a mechanism by which governments are made more accountable by being held to a set of standards.

    3) Freedom of speech and expression. The liberty of the press is one of the securities against corrupt and tyrannical governments. The media can be argued to have constitutional responsibilities in regard to providing information to the public about what is happening to the political system. Public debate is vital to democractic politics much of which takes place in the media.

    Legislation and statutes that are specific allows no room for the exercise of discretionary powers. Conversely, legislation and statutes that are ambiguous, drafted in execessively sweeping language, confers ministers and officials the right to exercise the use of administrative discretionary powers. Issues raised are the legality of this discretionary power and its degree intended by the law makers.

    The restrictions to be put upon discretionary power is a matter of degree. Faced with the fact that Parliament freely confers discretionary powers with little regard to the dangers of its abuse, the courts must attempt to strike a balance between the needs of fair and efficient administration and the need to protect the citizen against arbitrary government. Courts must develop general doctrines for keeping executive power within proper guidelines.

    Every system of law must have its own standards of judging questions of abuse of discretionary power. The system must be objective and non political expressing indeed a particular judicial attitude but one that can be applied impartially to any legislation irrespective of its political content. The fact that the exercise of discretionary power involves a question of legality and degree, it often reflects the particular philosophy of a government.
