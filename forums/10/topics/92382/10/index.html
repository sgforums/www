    REPLY:"According to your judgment, the viability of bipartisan system of democracy may be affected by size of population. Yet, viable size is subjective or relative.     I have never heard of Tidal Wave. My participation on sgforums is recent, I have only started properly posting since December. As to whether autocracies are sustainable, I point towards Taiwan & China as past & present examples respectively. They can be sustainable, and no doubt I think the PAP will remain in power for at least several decades after LKY's time. Both examples have seen smooth peaceful transition from one leader to the next. If an autocracy governs well, and is able to decide/plan its successors, then it is sustainable, the labels of what you call it mean little.

    REPLY:"As admitted, in the above, Singapore's particular autocratic system of governance has led to "restrictiveness of the political system and PAP's crunching of the political opposition parties. It follows that since it is clearly not a democracy, being all out to crunch democratic institutions like bipartisanism. If it is not a democracy, why are we discussing it as if it is on par with democracy? We can honestly say that it is a supposedly democratic system which has been modified by the sheer personality of its founding father."
    Singapore practises a pseudo-democracy, I never compare the island with Western democracies. It does not mean non-democracies are doomed to fail.

    REPLY:"If you disagree that there is any system that depends on mass participation and knowledge application by the people, then I presume that you are maintaining that Singapore's particular meritocracy is the way to go where the brightest are indeed effective and had succeeded in running the whole system.
    I keep hearing "mass participation and knowledge application", which till now I find quite rhetorical. You will have to explicitly exemplify what you mean. All it looks like to me is you want the people to be more involved in politics. As for Singapore's meritocracy, I believe the gist is correct. The sourcing of talent to lead the nation, but the definitions of talent are limited somewhat. There are indeed two types of succession. One that is nurtured, and one that is fought over.

    If so, may I ask: have our brightest and the best brains helped Singapore solve the its many problems - high costs, discriminations against the non-A_grades in schools and lack of entrepreneurship, a fact admitted by ministers and economic review committee during the Asian Financial cirisis and the last two recessions.
    I don't think Singapore has high costs, particularly compared to other developed countries. Singapore's problem is it's a middle-man trading port. If the costs are high (which is respect to other ASEAN economies), then the obvious strategy is to widen the margin, ie ensure that you add value to your exports. As for discrimination against non-A schools, I find this incorrect. All schools in Singapore as far as I know are well-funded to provide the student with sufficient access/facilities to compete fairly. The exception is independent schools, but they are not in the government's administration (thus the government can't be accused of discrimination) and capitalism in education is practised world-wide. What is a problem however, is the typical mentality of the average Singaporean & Hongkie. People look very much upon "branded" schools and "local" schools, psychologically differentiating & judging students by where they study. That is a Chinese mentality, not a government bias.

    It would appear that Singapore has deteriorated in its economic competitiveness due to lack of vibrancy of its domestic-sector economy and a serious lack of entrepreneurs needed to sustain further growth. Talents have not quite succeeded at both macro-economic and micro-economic governance.

    On the other hand, academic talents have limited choice and produced high-cost and consequently loss of economic competitiveness in its domestic- sector economy.
    I agree that Singapore's economic competitiveness has deteriorated, but not due to the lack of entrepreneurs. 'Competetiveness' in its economic essence means the ability to compete with other nations. Countries such as Malaysia are rapidly catching up & pushing huge incentives for foreign investment. Yet you scorn the over-emphasis in attracting foreign investments that sacrifice local enterprise. Singapore's competitiveness lies in its ability to attract foreign investment, not the competition of its exports (which are low-cost based!). Hence there is a contadictory trade-off in your stance. The lack of strength in the domestic sector however, can be correctly attributed to the lack of entrepreneurs. However, I don't think Singapore's economy is sufficiently large for new businesses to find much scope to exploit in the market. Partly this is due to the GLCs' domination, but also because of the lack of depth & scope in the domestic market itself. Singaporeans in general aren't risk-takers, there is little individuality that I can find. Most Singaporeans I see are conforming & like-minded thinkers. There is not one Singaporean that I consider outside the box, aside from those that have been exposed to living overseas.

    The talents have produced a tax-plus-cost_recovery governance. Based on data released by the media in 2003, it became quite clear that it is the masses that produce all the budget and off-budget surpluses amounting to billions to allow the government to claim success.
    Correct. One thing that I am critical of is the taxing of the population to feed the government's investment losses. Yet in Western democracies even, this is common practice. There could be alternatives such as bond issues, but I am not aware if Singapore provides such financial assets.

    An average household had paid HDB about S$200,000 to accumulate into the nation's off-budget surplus of S$14 billions over the last 20 years. Lands were acquired using tax-payers funds, transferred to the State Land and re-sold to the HDB to charge the citizens at market price.
    Indeed the government's fundings are however ploughed back into society. No where in the world have I seen the collective excellence of public transport, education, grants, social amenities, libraries & state housing. The paranoia however means the government is emphatic over extra reserves for safe-keeping. Yet it has tried not to let the reserves idle away, but to invest. I think it is a risky stategy to invest heavily if you're a government. Investments should be stable & the risk should be carefully managed.

    The double or triple taxes on vehicle at import, COE and ERP are costing the citizen S$600,000 over his working life thereby depriving him of retirements.
    As far as I know, not many people drive cars. Those that can are likely to be the top 30% of the salary bracket. If they can't retire, the rest of the population I presume are long dead? It's too expensive to have a car in Singapore, given the quality of public transport and the limited small number of places to actually get to. It does however reduce air pollution, the costliness may be an excuse for more government revenue. But it has a green policy that environmentalists agree with. I am in the middle stance on this issue.

    Bipartisanism has been elbowed out by the GRC system. Had it been existed, we might have a better than tax-and-recover system leading to demise of domestic sector economy. There would be competitions and better solutions to our many problems.
    Perfectly true. The re-bordering of the electorial breakdown is a sham. Any tinkering with the election process is obviously to the PAP's benefit. They cannot justify it, and the reasons of 'minority participation' are farcical. It points to the fact that the government claims Singaporeans to be race-motivated in their voting (racists) and is a loophole of meritocracy to deliberately ensure the safety of its own candidates. The minority-participation ruling is undemocratic, unmeritocratic, & its usefulness to the PAP clearly goes beyond the supposed objective.

    As pointed out by Ngiam Tong Dow in his article to the Straits Times sometime ago, the Spartain system of governance has resulted in unquestioned auto-piloting of such autocratic policies until disaster strike.
    That is true. If the system itself does not have the mechanisms to check the government, there is no guarantee that the current PAP may deteriorate in future. You are not relying upon the mechanisms of the political system but more upon the pragmatism & dynamicism of the party...Yet these people will be replaced over time, and somewhere there may be a hitck in the party's clockwork. An accountable system will lessen the likelihood of such damage. However, some argue that it could also prove to be the Pandora's Box. Both are valid predictions. But I believe the government's kiasu-istic mindset when it comes to political reform will not consider the long-term interests of a dynamic political system over a dynamic political party.

    A knowledge-based economy requires the total support of all endeavours in every field through motivation and recognition of worth or everyone civil servants, students, ordinary workers and businessmen.
    That sounds much like capitalism, which I believe is practised in Singapore.

    Although entrepreneurs cannot be created as they are usually born or matured naturally, it can be stifled by lack of motivation or environmental support. Broad-based education policy for example will help to prepare school goers to be strongly based in knowledge and skills application while young based on wider syllables. When the young is encouraged and motivated in practical applications in this manner, they will be better equipped to contribute to society in application of practical knowledge in all fields beyong textbook knowlede and everyone will find their level of worth to strive for his or her own excellence.
    Yes. A wider syllabus is a must. All Singaporeans think alike, because their scope, skill set, & learning environment are exactly the same. It creates a uniformed playing ground for students of all classes, but at the same time it creates uniform-minded people.

    I think non-A students must be encouraged to succeed, because grades do not necessarily reflect their true potential. Creative Technology's founder is a prime example, few government scholars could likely match his success.

    Needless to say, certain errors of judgment like the MT-CL1 university admission have been admitted. MOM has just reversed the past persistent Pro-Grade education which has caused a big brain drain of many of our educated talents. Our pro-foreign investment and tax-and-recover policies have discouraged entrepreneurs and start-ups in our own land.
    As I am not Singaporean and have not lived there for 4 years now, I am not aware of the admission process. New enterprises should be encouraged and given incubation periods for them to strengthen. What must not happen though, is protectionism. Even Singapore's GLCs prove by their overseas performances that protectionism is highly detrimental when it needs to compete with global firms.

    REPLY:"You may not believe the current selection process is entirely IQ-related, but I would put it this way: IQ or academic grades in terms of number of "As" has been the main measuring yardstick in streaming students from primary to secondary and from secondary to pre-university and university. Certain Junior colleges and NUS/NTU have been the feeders to top-civil service selections and government scholarships.
    The yardstick of academic grades is the yardstick for all the world's top institutions all over the world. It's application has proved the correct yardstick for Singaporeans, who make up one of the largest foreign student populations in places Harvard, MIT & Oxbridge. The only exception are business schools, which truly look at "knowledge application". However, you need at least 5 years experience before you can prove your worth, and you still need top grades in scholastic tests.

    Such social engineering had not solved our problems as although public administration does need a certain number of brightest career officers, this whole pro-grade and pro-scholar mentality has produced only redundant highly educated unemployed leaving a vaccuum in our private sector.
    On the one hand, you are highly critical of the government's definitions & selection of talent. Yet on the other, you also blame this policy for draining the private sector of bright minds. This is a contradiction, for it means the government does have a sound idea about recruiting competent individuals.

    REPLY: "You are right in saying that as our scholars highly proclaimed as talents, should not just ride on external market indices or favorable conditions. They must out-perform the mediocre. At best times, they can take advantage of market trends and take a good ride and at worse times, they must know how to maximize use of available or under-used resources to equip all businesses of readiness to eek out a living and rebound as quickly as possible. However, if you are to read the ministers' speeches during the recessions, they would rather not talk about their solutions but keep harping only on external unfavorable conditions."
    Yet for the government to out-perform the mediocre would only promote its overbearing weight in the domestic economy & act as a catalyst for further revenue-generation from the population. As I have believed from the start, Singapore's governing system has indeed provided efficiency, stability and a very wealthy government. However, there are negatives on the other side of the weighing scale, which I think can be found in all governing systems (especially Western-style democracy).

    Singapore's autocracy has resulted in stagnation in creativity, not just in the private sector, but also in government. Every successful dynamic team that I've seen combines a wealth of different skill sets in each individual. They can be grouped together to work out productive value-creation with their alternatives views & experiences. In Singapore though, everyone, even including the scholars, is just another 'number' that is shaped to fit into the government's idea of an ideal system. Whilst the PAP is a high performer, its potential & dynamicism is definitely limited. Much as the government may believe likewise, its value-creation to Singapore has latently resulted in much value-destruction.
