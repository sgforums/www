    robertteh, I am puzzled by the subject of your thread: "definition of true democracy".

    Is there a "true democracy"?

    1) Democracy is a concept of politics created over many years of philoshophical thinking, conceptualisation, implementation, evaluation and, improvisation and/or correction. It is no wonder that democracy has varying models in different nations. It is modelled to fit different circomstances. It is the same for other forms of governance. There is no one form of government where all nations practising it conform to the exact same model. Since each nation has its own needs, it will modify its government to fit itself. So, whose interpretation of democracy do you consider the "truth"?

    2) Since democracy and its interpretation differs amongst its practisers, where can we find its true form? Bear in mind that something that is "true" is a fact, undeniable and undisputable. Do you actually claim, then, that your definition of democracy is an undeniable and undisputable fact? What, may i ask, is the basis of your claim?

    Your thread pertains to governance in Singapore, which leads to the next question: Should we be seeking a "true" form of democracy?

    3) The goal of the science of politics is the creation of a system which will be best able to run a nation. Democracy was a genre created to describe a certain number of systems which have similar characteristics. It is a system which has produced a number of notable successes globally. That is undisputable. But, strictly speaking, our goal is not democracy. Our goal is whatever form of government is ideal in our land. So should we focus our efforts on fitting our way of government into a strictly and rigidly defined concept?

    4) This means that democracy is similar to a scientific theory. In science, theorists produce an approximation to the truth and produce evidence to support it. The evidence is in the form of experiments which is as close as possible to perfect conditions. However, the assumptions made during the experiments prevent the experiment from being the exact representation of perfect conditions, only the nearest. Scientists then go on to provide further evidence in support of the theory or to debunk it. Democracy may have been successful in certain circumstances, but we cannot claim it to be The one and only way. We can, at most, pronounce it ideal under the conditions which it has been tested. Isnt this so?
