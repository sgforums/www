    PROPOSED OVERHAUL OF CURRENT CONSTITUTION,

    The current constitution (Singapore) is too general and vague. There is no specific definition or protection of citizens' rights and integrity of one-man-one-vote election process. In the hand of rogue government, it can be easily twisted or tweaked to suit the narrow interest of whoever the political parties in power.

    Is it desirable to allow differing interpretations of the rights of citizens or power of the government and differring branches of government?

    The answer is obvious: people and government is bigger than the parties and parties should be elected as government to represent people's collective views and interests.

    Therefore upholding of citizens rights and checking of the performances of government should be the desirable ends of the constitution.

    By virtue of the above-stated final goals and objectives of the constitution, it should be a document that spell out the basic rights of citizens, defining the duties and roles of government and their responsibilities to safeguard social, cultural and economic vibrancy particularly in the following areas:-

    (1) To safeguard the One Man One Vote principle so that only the best individuals are elected into political office and there will be complete accountable and transparent check on power of executives by the people.

    (2) To ensure multi-racial representation, to provide that 20% of seats of parliaments will go to the racial groups for allocation to those receiving the highest percentage of votes counted in the one-man-one-vote election return whenever the number of candidates elected fall below any racial representation ratio passed and reviewed by parliament every 10 years.

    (3) To prohibit use of block- or group-representation voting system to avoid impediment or shackling of the one-man-one-vote universal electoral system.

    (4) To safeguard the Basic rights of all citizens irespective of race, culture, religion of tenets including freedoms of speech, political beliefs, right to stand for election or right to make criticisms of policies or practices of government in power.

    (5) To refer all situations of conflicts of interests among the government executives, legislature, judiary and civil service to independent panel with representations from a cross section of people so as to Establish Rule of law, justice, fairness and equity in conduct of laws without fear or favor to the government in power.

    (6) To Establish a low-cost independent panel open to observers from UN agencies to oversee election process and political participation by citizens.

    (7) To Establish powers and procedures for Investigation and adjudication by an idependent sub-court of reported conflict of interests

    (Cool To safeguard the citizens against prosecution for defamation by the government in power for speeches made in the course of election campaigning or criticising policies or actions of government in power.

    (9) To fundamentally safeguard freedoms of speech, choice and association as conditions essential to encourage further growth of social, cultural and economic vibrancy, entrepreneurship and creativity of citizens.

    It is recommended that adequate safeguards be provided so that citizens would be the masters of their destiny which they could participate fully in all aspects of nation building. It should uphold citizens' right to participate in all aspects of nation building and protect them against any inquitable prosecution by any govenrment in power.

    Such a constitution will be proactive and open up the processes for citizens to take part in social and cultural and economic activities to contribute to creativity, entrepreneurship or participations in election, giving feedbacks or gerrymandering of election processes.

    Singapore's democracy is still young and it has not fully developed and matured into one which will motivate and encourage all to participate in nation building. It will be too prematured to rely on some bad experiences with democracies in less successful south east asian democracies to stop its further evolvement. The final objective of ensuring the constitution is to open up participation in all aspects of nation building to citizens to bring about entrepreneurship and creativity accountability and transparency in the governing of the country irrespective of belief, cult, race, religion or political views or inclinations.

    Hi, Paperchicken,

    A true deomcracy comprising the 7 essential ingredients of fundamental rights of citizens, rule of law and accountability and transparency is not idealistic.

    It is attainable and will bring immense benefits to Singapore next stage of progress for the following reasons:-

    (1) Singapore is a young or relatively young country. It has only experienced one part of progress as a nation for the first two to three decades of its nationhood so far.

    (2) It has not experienced as yet the unleashing of creative energy of masses made possible under truly democratic system as the Americans have experienced.

    (3) It is too early to pass judgment whether its present control-based top-down system with a group of talents or presumed talents leading every aspect of national life from the front is indeed the best and sustainable system.

    (4) Given the control-based or guided system, there already existed many problems - high costs, elitism, loss of economic competitiveness to mention just a few.

    (5) There is no immediate prospect of reversing the present high costs without involving the masses in participating in creating entrepreneurship and economic competitiveness. There is much reluctance to toe the control line or policy on the part of youths or masses and many have migrated.

    (6) A critical re-examination of the present control-based policies will be essential to cater to rising expectations and aspirations of youths and people as a whole.

    (7) Leadership, confucianism and Asian values have been tried but as proven by the Asian Financial Crisis, such values are abstract tenets and assumptions unlikely to create better governance but may even give rise to more abuses.

    A guided or controlled democracy will not produce desirable long term benefits needed by a more educated society. Instead, it will lead to alienation with people generally switched off from participating in national affairs or wellbeing.

    A truly democratic system of the ingredients as posted will help to assure the whole population of their support as what people want is tangible participation, freedoms and accountability and transparency not presentation or justifications.

    6 Sep 04, 19:07
    robertteh's 7-point definition of a 'true democracy', while inspirational, also smacks of idealism (and possibly naivety). The American political model certainly does not possess all of those virtues. In fact, I would go so far to add that those prerequisites are set so high that pragmatically, no country can ever satisfy them adequately.

    What is a 'true democracy' anyway? This question has been debated for centuries by the likes of Bentham, Mills and Rousseau. In fact no liberal democracy in the West can be said to be perfectly democratic...hence the term 'polyarchy'.

    The US has had two centuries to develop its political model while European nations have had even longer (more than four centuries) as pointed out in postings under 'American Electioneering'. It's interesing to note that American democracy granted the vote to only white males who owned property initially, before this was given to women in 1920, and black Americans in 1964. And if one thinks of how the previous American election between Gore and Bush was played out (problems with voting paper i.e chads, the manner of counting votes, the influence of media, the large numbers of disenfranchised legal voters, the status of American votes from overseas etc) or the Guantanamo Bay controversies, or the abuse of Iraqi prisoners, or the passing of the Patriot Act etc,...many questions must occur to those who have always placed great faith in the infallibility of American democracy.

    How many former American colonies or countries which adopted the American political model have succeeded spectacularly politically *and* economically anyway? Latin America? South America? Closer to home, the Philippines makes an interesting case study.....even after the end of the Marcos era, the Philippines continues to be embroiled in political corruption, people power coups, political dominance by prominent land-owning families, personality politics (especially movie stars and celebrities), the influence of the Catholic church in politics etc etc. Economically, it is near collapse under the Arroyo administration with poverty and unemployment everywhere.

    When one turns to European (especially British and French) colonies, how many can be said to have succeeded too? Of the more than 40 former colonies, only Singapore and Hong Kong stand out as economic successes. This is not due to *only* colonial legacies like the bureaucracy, rule of law and governance which our former British masters left us (and indeed left with former African colonies and India as well), but also and more importantly, the hard work of those who were granted independence. Remember that Singapore was a very poor country in the 1960s, before joining the Malayan Federation, and after being expelled from it.

    The PAP for all the criticisms of it being authoritarian, has done a good (dare I say excellent) job of ensuring political stability goes hand in hand with economic growth since 1965. As for Hong Kong, what can I say except that it was only returned to China in 1997, and that the British had a change of heart and realised the virtues of democracy only a few years prior to the handover (and after more than 150 years of colonial rule!) Despite the benefits democracy confers, can you blame the Chinese for feeling betrayed at this unilateral change of (British) heart?

    I certainly wouldn't disagree that Singapore has lots of areas to see to where political liberalisation is concerned. (With a younger, and more educated and global population , it is only natural that expectations differ from the older generations.) This is not to say that the incumbent government is doing badly - indeed, there have been concrete steps in liberalization (however gradual) for a long time, especially now. One only needs to compare the state of affairs 15 years ago and at present. Political evolution cannot be rushed- it has to happen at its own pace...the Europeans (includng the Brits) had 400 years, the US had 200 years, the Australians had more than 100 years, surely it's not too much for Singapore to be given more time, given that the country only celebrated its 39th birthday recently? One only has to look to Russia or former Eastern European states to see how things can go terribly wrong if this is not handled properly. Indeed, it is crucial Singapore be allowed to developed its own political model, without trying to copy the West. The West developed their own political models as a result of unique historical conditions which cannot be replicated elsewhere. Even the politically apathetic can see that the US and the UK have evolved differently where political governance is concerned.

    Is Singapore really politically repressed like North Korea or some Middle Eastern country? The answer is obviously no. We enjoy one of the highest standards of living in the world, people are free to move about freely, and despite common complaints about the lack of media freedom or political discussion, we have access to 5000 periodicals, the internet, cable news networks, and even public forums like these. People who insist on denying these realities are either self-deluded or have no intention accepting rational and logical debate. Why? Perhaps their private frustrations and anger over certain govt policies have blinded them to the big picture.

    One cannot possibly vote the opposition in, just for the sake of voting in an opposition. Similarly, one cannot read everything the Western media says, and treat it as sacred. The opposition have to prove themselves (and indeed the current slate of new political leaders in WP appear to be trying hard to demonstrate this). Again, there is no need to elaborate at length the fracas between Mr Chee SJ and Mr Chiam ST over the SDP in the 1990s, or the various newspaper headlines that Mr Chee have made since.

    Until a viable and strong opposition develops, the rational Singaporean will opt for the best available choice, especially a party which has a proven track record politically and economically.
