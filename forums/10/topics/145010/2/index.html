    An interesting point of note that Sookhdeo has not properly elucidated is that Islam does not actually have a formal comand and control system. The Catholics have their Pope, the Buddhists have their Dalai Lama, but Islam does not have an Imam who would be the final authority on what's what.

    So what is the implication of this fact? Sir Gerald Templer, High Commissioner in Malaya at the time of the Emergency, said that the solution to the problem "lies not in pouring more British troops into the jungle but in the hearts and minds of the Malayan people". It's a lot more difficult to win the hearts and minds of Muslim people if you do not have any one person or body that they would listen to - they can either choose to listen to a moderate Imam, or they can choose to buy into the rantings of a radical Imam, since both hold equal standing within the structure of Islam.

    As Sookhdeo noted, the imperative is to turn the whole world to Islam. Any alternative system would be viewed by these radicals as an attempt to Levi's, McDonalds's and Hollywood good Quran-following Muslims to death.

    On the other hand, the individualistic structure of Islam is a double-edged sword. If Islam is open to interpretation with no checks and measures presented by a central authority, what's to say that we can't convince non-radical muslims to take the moderate path?

    Along those lines, this approach may have to apply at different levels.

    At the aggressive end, radicals tend to take part in terrorist activities upon the promise of the glory of martyrdom. In the current American approach to nabbing these terrorists, they're delivering such martyrdon in crowing whenever they catch one terrorist. The more effective solution would be to make the little bastards simply disappear. How can the radical Imams proclaim someone as a martyr when the poor sod could well have been run over by a pork-laden garbage truck and stuck into the mortuary as a Mohammad Doe?

    On the softer end of the spectrum, persuasion could be used. For example, according to the 9/11 commission report, the attacks were funded through hawalas, because OBL's funds were frozen. The question is, how many of these hawaladars were actually conscious of the fact that their activities were being used to fund the attacks? I would wager that the vast majority of them would have carried out the transaction on the good faith that a labourer was simply using the cheapest and surest means of remitting money to his wife and eight kids. What if Western banks could offer a viable alternative to put hawaladas out of business? After all, the banks make the majority of their profit from loans interest and investments, rather than surcharges levied on individual customers.

    I disagree with Sookhdeo's assertion that there is no such thing as moderate Islam. The nature of the religion is so decentralised that no interpretation of Islam can be ruled out as a myth. We need to take advantage of that decentralised system and use that clever invention called marketing to best support the message of the moderate Imams and make the moderate path most palatable to the followers of the religion on an individual basis, which is exactly what the religion operates on in the first place.
