    Well actually I think teaching Chinese in English might not be such a bad idea, given that alot of students nowadays already have a very weak foundation before they start Chinese lessons. It's like ang mohs learning Chinese. Or Chinese learning Japanese or French. It's all learnt using English as a medium.

    Compare the pros and cons... Not using English MIGHT worsen the level of the Chinese language as the years go by, because of more and more weak students. They find that being taught Chinese by using Mandarin is tough, so some might not be able to cope, hence finding it too difficult and eventually giving up.

    With the use of English, students may find it easier to understand what they are being taught. At least when learning is made easier, it's more digestable and ensures a higher literacy rate in the Chinese language, because they learn, whereas in the former case they refuse to. When a certain foundation is built already, it wouldn't be too late for the mastery of the language and the reasons behind how the words are structured or formed etc.

    Just my five cents' worth

    Actually the problem boils down to the fact that there are not many good and attractive children's books in Chinese. Go to kinokuniya and Borders, compare the quality of the English story books and Chinese story books. I went to kino and found that many attractively packaged Chinese books are translated from the English version. No wonder these children do not want to read Chinese.

    Futhermore, Chinese is taught in school in an uninteresting manner. The way of teaching English in Primary school has been revamped many times, now students get exposure to Big Books, have meaningful activities etc. But when it comes down to Chinese, the students are forced to learn 'ting xie', 'zao ju', 'mo xie'. ...yucks...I really hated 'mo xie' when i was in primary school. The whole idea of memorising a passage made no sense to me. I used to read and understand a passage and paraphrase it and rewrite the whole thing without losing its meaning....but my teacher gave me zero....she said that although my sentences were grammatically correct and the entire meaning of the passage was intact, i was not following instructions.

    I really got to liking Chinese only after I graduated from my secondary school, when I got used to reading 'fan ti zi'. It gave me exposure to novels, and I was fanatic over jinyong, liangyusheng, qiong yao and yishu. I was lucky that I need not attend Chinese lessons in JC, as I was exempted, being a first language student in secondary school. I still sat for the 'A' levels, and spent one week before looking at novels and studying how the authors expressed descriptions of places, people, feelings etc.....and I scored an A1.

    No matter what Chinese should not be taught in English, the revamp in the lessons should be to make them more meaningful for students. There should be more teachers, writers, publishers out there to actively promote good and attractive chinese publications, especially for children. What happened to those interesting Chinese Children magazines I had when I was young, magazines like 'youer tian di', 'shao nian yue kan'.....
