    UNTIL last Friday, it was probably Singapore's most hush-hush sex club.
    Membership was by recommendation only, and information about it was spread via the Internet, accessible only with special passwords.

    Then someone blew the whistle.

    Two weeks ago, the police raided the Chinatown cyber-brothel which had promised its clients the fulfilment of their sexual fantasies.

    A few hours after the raid, the brothel's website was taken down.

    The site disappeared as quickly and as quietly as when it was first set up in February.

    Police spokesman Sandra Yip said no massage licence or other permits had been granted to those who ran the place.

    The brothel had begun operations discreetly.

    Unlike others, there was not the usual red lantern outside its premises. No large address number plate. No signboard.

    It looked like any other Chinatown shophouse.

    And it took extreme measures to ensure secrecy. For instance, only those who signed up as 'club members' got the password to enter its website. (See report on facing page).

    Despite this, it still attracted around 100 members.

    What was the draw?

    The tagline on its website claimed it was an exclusive members-only 'Gentlemen's Club'.

    The bigger lure was sex with women dressed as air stewardesses, corporate executives, secretaries - even Sailor Moon, the popular Japanese Anime character.

    The name of this so-called club was 'Dreams Come True'.

    But the fantasy world was busted after a reader of The New Paper on Sunday e-mailed us about the joint.

    Most of the customers were believed to be white-collar workers.

    But was it really as exclusive as it claimed? The operators were happy to recruit just about any surfer who frequented online sex websites.

    Based on members' discussions on the website, the women appeared to be foreigners who worked illegally while here on social visit passes.

    We learnt that the first group of people the pimps targeted were active members of a public Internet sex forum. It is known to men who frequently visit prostitutes.

    When they sign up they are allowed to read 'reviews' of the girls by other members. They could also detail their preference for certain costumes.

    Such peer group interaction was another aspect of this underground activity.

    Clients had sex with foreigners who might have sex-related diseases or who might have been tested HIV positive. No one knew and no one asked.

    The added danger was that the unlicensed brothel did not conduct any health checks.

    Each session cost $140.

    With information obtained from the brothel's website, our undercover correspondent was able to find the place. It was a less than five-minute walk from Chinatown MRT station.

    The brothel was on the top floor of a three-storey shophouse, which also houses restaurants, law firms and other offices.

    Open from 11am to 11pm, it was accessible by a flight of stairs.

    Although the brothel claimed to offer massage services, it did not seem to have proper massage rooms.

    Instead, there were about four small cubicles, with mattresses or raised massage beds in them.

    Each cubicle was named after one of the four seasons, and the respective Chinese character for spring, summer, autumn or winter was displayed outside.

    Separated by partitions, the cubicles were dimly-lit.

    During the two-hour late-afternoon police raid, two men were seen leaving the brothel after the police arrived.

    The first to leave was a man in his late 20s.

    Alone, the bespectacled man with short gelled hair wore a dark, long-sleeved shirt and business pants.

    A few minutes later, an older man stepped out.

    He appeared to be in his late 50s and was clad in a polo T-shirt and long pants.

    Then the girls appeared.

    Sleaze comes to the heartlands
    Saturday June 24, 10:17 AM

    Two weeks ago, senior supervisor Gary spotted a handwritten poster outside a beauty salon in his Housing Development Board estate.

    It was offering a one-hour massage session for $30.

    "I was feeling tired from work and thought I could do with a massage. It seemed a good deal," said Gary, 38. Instead, the father of four went inside and was offered sex by the mainland Chinese masseuse.

    Of late, scores of such parlours have started sprouting up in heartland estates such as Ang Mo Kio, Bedok and Toa Payoh. It appears that many are clearly in violation of the law.

    A massage establishment does not require a licence if it offers only curative treatment in full view of other customers. Otherwise, it needs a licence to operate and its masseuses have to go for mandatory health screenings.

    The clincher: Such massage parlours are not allowed in areas including HDB estates, or near schools, hospitals and places of worship.

    But a check with the Accounting & Corporate Regulatory Authority showed that in the past 12 months alone, 509 establishments registered themselves to offer massage and foot reflexology services, many of which were in HDB residential areas.

    They presented a harmless front and appeared to offer tui na, or traditional Chinese massage, for as little as $30. Others advertised themselves as places that provided eyebrow plucking, acupuncture and facial cleansing.

    On the face of it, these services would not require a police licence, which would costs $480 a year.

    But inside, not only were customers taken inside private cubicles, they were explicitly offered sexual services.

    Mr Peter Sng, president of the Spa Association Singapore, was surprised to learn that more than 500 establishments had registered themselves over a single year to offer massage-related services.

    "It's incredible that there are so many of them. The association is very concerned with the growing number of massage establishments springing up in the neighbourhoods," said Mr Sng. "We encourage the authorities set up more stringent checks as well as looking into ways to regularise these businesses."

    There should be checks to stop HDB estates from turning into another Joo Chiat, with masseuses prowling void decks and coffeeshops, he added.

    "(The massage parlours) may grow into a cluster. When a cluster is formed, competition is greater and may take on a more visible form as seen in Joo Chiat," he said.

    A Today visit to Ang Mo Kio found nascent clusters already forming.

    At Street 43, there were six massage parlours facing each other in an open-space car park, each with their entrances at the back of the four-storey shophouse block. Customers made their way up the stairs to the second floor, where business is carried out.

    On Street 52, there were five joints within a 50m radius.

    While many of these may be legitimate businesses, at least one parlour at each of the two locations was willing to offer sexual services.

    Last year, six massage parlours had their licences revoked, all for offences committed under the Women's Charter, said police spokeswoman Assistant Superintendent Siow Cheng Cheng.

    But many of these establishments may not even have licences, since licensed massage parlours do not operate in HDB residential areas.

    Last Sunday, a masseuse was killed at one such establishment in Ang Mo Kio Avenue 10.
