    Mah Bow Tan on how he learnt to drive a bus to learn workers’ “trust”

    April 30th, 2010 TEMASEK REVIEW

    National Development Minister Mah Bow Tan, who had come under fire of late from Singaporeans for his failure to control sky-rocketing HDB flat prices, now tries to woo the man in the street by revealing his “common roots” during his younger days.

    Mr Mah was a boss at the Singapore Bus Service in the late 1970s. Speaking during this year’s May Day dinner, Mr Mah revealed that he once learnt to drive a bus to understand the difficulties drivers face in reverse-parking a bus.

    He did not elaborate more on whether his “experience” served any useful purpose to the workers or SBS.

    In order to win trust from the workers, he also learnt how to “swear” in Hokkien though he did not mention the expletives he had picked up.

    Mr Mah claimed that “the deep level of mutual trust and understanding among the tripartite partners was the ‘most fundamental and abiding’ reason for the recovery from last year’s recession.”

    “If anyone thinks that was easy, just look at the situation in many other countries, where government, employers and unions were locked in conflict, unable to take difficult decisions because they simply did not trust one another,” he was quoted as saying in the Straits Times.

    The “tripartite partners” refer to the government, employers and workers. Mr Mah forgot to add that there are no independent trade unions in Singapore who truly represent the interests of ordinary Singapore workers.

    The largest trade union NTUC is a pseudo-PAP organization which is always led by a PAP minister. It has limited power or influence to fight for higher wages and better working conditions on behalf of the workers.

    For example, many Singaporeans were forced to accept a wage cut during last year’s recession, but when the economy improves this year, their wages have yet to increase despite repeated exhortations by NTUC on employers to do so.

    Fortunately for Mr Mah, he is expected to receive a hefty 8.8 percent pay rise this year on top of his $2 million dollar annual salary, which is more than four times that of U.S. President Barack Obama.
