    What we should look for in our biomed investment are the potential for job creation and commercial gains.

    There's no point pumping millions of investment to fuel the research interests of local scientific elites or FTs. Breakthrough in pure research don't create jobs or make money. That's why Brenner is not an industrialist, though he is much respected in his field.

    Dr Lee has a good point about the strategy of identifying niche areas ie. Singapore should differentiate its R&D from other countries'. All else being equal, concentrating our resources on niche areas increase the chance for our success. Then we should choose the niche which has market potential.

    Philip Yeo's strategy is less likely to result in a breakthrough because our investment is spread over many areas. Plus every other country is working on the same popular fields of stem cells and cancer. His strategy will result in a diversity of talents working in Singapore which makes the R&D scene more interesting, not will not get us to commercial success sooner.

    I'd suggest that Singapore invest in food & nutrition R&D. Products like vitamins, diet pills, cosmetics have more immediate commercial value and are lower tech and more appropriate for our level of R&D capability. Even less sexy products like reduced-harm cigarettes have huge and immediate market potential. Forget about Nobel-prize winning cancer cures for now.
