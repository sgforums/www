    Our doctors are just facing what the rest of the population has been facing for a long time, namely, that anyone can come to Singapore and work.

    For donkey years, our male population has been moaning that foreigners are treated similar to them, despite our own Singaporeans having sacrificed the best two and a half years (or two years) of their lives to the duty of training and defending the country. This has led to the oft-heard comment that being a Malaysian (or Chinese or Indian) in Singapore is better than being a Singaporean!

    There is another flip side to the coin to this, not just about it being unfair to our own doctors, who paid throught their noses to study in NUS Medicine, and got bonded in the bargain. These Indian doctors are surely not be attending to our rich and affluent, but rather to the poor and needy. So the waiting times may be reduced, but they will be attended to by doctors who do not understand their own languages. As all know, medicine is as much about healing as about comforting. Deny it as one might, the difference in language is surely a hindrance to it. Put it another way, would our rich and powerful be willing to be doctored by one of these "bangla" doctors? How many times have we heard our old complaing that these Indian doctors in the polyclinics have no idea what concerns are being voiced? How many of our parents, uncles or aunts would prefer to be seen by them?

    Surely the solution should be to increase the intake such that there would be enough doctors to cater to our own needs in the long term, rather than permanently using these Indian doctors to make up the shortfall. Are they really cheaper? Can someone come up with hard facts that they WILL be paid lower salaries ALWAYS, or rather, only lower salaries in the first year or first two years. If they are going to be paid a similar salary to our own doctors eventually, why not just take in more Singaporean medical students? Why are we paying the same salary to foreign doctors, when they are not as desirable as compared to Singaporean doctors, and most would eventually take the money back to India?

    As to Mushroom's contention that most would still move to the private sector even if we are to take in more medical students, I don't think so. If we produce enough doctors, the private sector would be so flooded with doctors that there would be enough ALSO for the public sector.

    As it is, many aspiring Singaporeans are forced to go overseas to pursue their dreams of practising medicine, resulting in millions of dollars going to foreign medical colleges. Singaporeans who graduate overseas would also find it more easy to continue working and finally settling down overseas, resulting in a double whammy of losing both money (for their fees) and their talents (when they migrate). As to overproducing doctors, that should not even be an issue. Isn't the government pursuing the policy of promoting bioscience? What better people to become researchers and clinical scientists if not doctors? Armed with a medical degree (and the knowledge), they can move on to become even more effective administrators, scientists, researchers, lecturers etc. in the bioscientific field.

    Anyway, this move of bringing in Indian doctors smacks of the same old knee-jerk, short-term reaction to a present problem, instead of implementing a long-term course of action that may take more effort. We can bring them in for the short-term, but there should be plans to educate enought Singaporeans to man our hospitals in the long run.
