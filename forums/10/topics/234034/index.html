    THE door has been opened wider to foreign doctors, as Singapore moves towards a new goal of having one doctor per patient in public hospitals.

    The public sector currently employs 3,156 doctors - which translates to one doctor for every two patients at any given time.

    The Health Ministry is relaxing registration rules for foreign doctors by reducing their probation periods, and recognising qualifications from more foreign medical schools to boost the number of doctors here.

    Singapore currently has 7,611 registered doctors, including 2,286 who were trained in foreign institutions. Of these foreign-trained practitioners, 976 are Singaporeans and the rest are foreigners. The relaxed rules will also benefit Singaporeans who qualify at newly recognised schools.

    Previously, all foreign specialists had to work under supervision in an approved institution - usually a public hospital - for three years, while non-specialists had to be supervised for six years, before receiving full registration.

    From this year, the probation period for foreign doctors has been shortened to two years for specialists, and four years for non-specialists.

    Those who have had at least five years' relevant experience after becoming a qualified specialist or family physician are now allowed to practise immediately in the private sector.

    More institutions, including three top Indian medical schools, will be added to the list of recognised institutions here next month. Since 2003, the number of schools on the list has soared from 20 to 120.

    The aim is to emulate renowned teaching and research institutions overseas, such as Sweden's Karolinska Institutet, where the doctor to patient ratio stands at one to one.

    With more doctors, each can have more time to spend with each patient as well as on teaching juniors and conducting more research. This would improve patient satisfaction, and also job satisfaction for doctors, said MOH's director of medical services, Professor K. Satku.

    The need for doctors will become more pressing as the number of patients is expected to rise, with a growing and ageing population, and more foreign patients.

    Speaking to students at the Singapore Management University on the globalisation of healthcare on Friday night, Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan said: 'When more (foreign patients) come, and we do not plan our capacity, whether it's more hospital beds or doctors, prices are bound to go up... our job is to anticipate this and move the supply curve so that prices will come down to a more competitive level.'

    His strategy is to increase the number of doctors trained here as well as overseas.

    The National University of Singapore (NUS) has increased its intake of medical students, and classes at the new Duke-NUS Graduate Medical School will start this August. So from 2012, Singapore will produce about 300 doctors every year.

    Mr Khaw also hopes to further increase Duke-NUS' intake by another 50 students in future.

    But this will not be enough as doctors retire or quit. Last year, 212 doctors left the profession.

    Foreigners are needed to fill the gap but they are in short supply worldwide.

    One big recruitment pool may be the Asian doctors in Europe who might consider a return to Asia now that European Union rules are kicking in to favour EU citizens over foreign residents.

    'This is an opportunity for Singapore to draw them in and expand our health-care,' Mr Khaw said.

    (Straits Times 26 Jan)
