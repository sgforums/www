    A RAG-AND-BONE man decided he didn't want illegal gamblers in his neighbourhood.

    So he chided them and threatened to call the police.

    Instead of leaving, the ruffians beat him up, breaking his bones.

    Mr Tan Lak Tee, 43, was attacked twice and his injuries include fractures in his arm, index finger and one of his ribs.

    The attack happened two months ago but Mr Tan, who is single, still feels the pain of his injuries.

    The rag-and-bone man, who mainly deals in scrap metal, claims he has lost weight and his business has been affected. He used to make up to $500 a day before the attack, but now he says he cannot lift anything heavy and his income has fallen.

    The incident happened at 7pm on 15 Jun when Mr Tan was cycling home and saw some men around a table opposite his rented house near Geylang Lorong24.

    Among the gamblers were Tan Chee Kiong, 38, and his two accomplices. The two Tans are not related.

    Said MrTan: 'I was curious and went to have a look. I saw that the men were gambling.

    'One of the men saw me and told me to leave. I then asked the man why I could not watch as the road did not belong to them.

    'When I said that I wanted them to pack up and leave, another gambler uttered vulgarities at me. I told him that I was going to call the police.'

    Mr Tan said he and the gambler then got into an argument.

    'Then, a group of people started attacking me,' he said.

    Mr Tan said that, at first, they were unarmed, but later they got some metal rods and cangkuls to attack him.

    'I took a metal rod from my bicycle and used the rod and my bare hands to fight them off,' said MrTan.

    'They then chased me to my house and tried to get in, but I quickly closed the door and rushed into my room.

    'Soon there was blood on the floor in my room,' Mr Tan said.

    Mr Tan said the attack left him bleeding from his head and his upper body and there was flesh hanging off his left index finger.

    It was Tan who took a metal rod from a dustbin nearby and hit MrTan's right hand before he rushed into his house.

    When Mr Tan thought the coast was clear, he walked out.

    STILL WAITING FOR HIM

    But Tan and the other men were waiting for him, and attacked him again.

    'I fought them off, but they kept on hitting me. I managed to get back into my house and locked the door and gate.

    'The men then ran away,' said MrTan.

    Around 8pm, he called the police and told them about the attack.

    He was then taken by ambulance to Changi General Hospital.

    Later that day, a Malaysian, who witnessed the attack, saw Tan sitting in the back lane of Geylang Lorong22. He called the police and pointed out the suspect.

    Tan was arrested.

    Tan admitted to attacking Mr Tan and implicated his two accomplices.

    But both men had left Singapore and are now on the run. Police gazettes have been issued for their arrest.

    Mr Tan's injuries were extensive. His left index finger, left forearm and right rib were broken, and he had several cuts, including a deep gash on his forehead.

    'The doctors had to put metal screws in my finger and arm,' he said.

    'I was hospitalised for three days and now cannot bend my left index finger or lift heavy things any more.

    'The doctors said I was fortunate that my broken rib did not hit my lung or kidney or I would have died.

    'I still take antibiotics and painkillers, and have to take a laxative for my constipation as my fractured rib makes passing motion painful.'

    Mr Tan's elder sister, who did not want to be named for fear of reprisals, said she was supposed to visit her brother the day he was attacked.

    But, as it was raining that day, she decided to visit him the next day instead.

    'When I arrived at his home at 9am, I saw a trail of blood leading from the road to the front door,' she said.

    'When I opened the door, my brother was gone and there was a pool of blood on the floor of his room.

    'At first, I thought his pet dogs had been fighting, but when I could not find my brother, I called the police.

    'They told me my brother had been in a fight and was in hospital.

    'I put plastic bags over my legs and took nearly an hour to clean all the blood away,' she said.

    At the Subordinate Courts on 10Aug, Tan pleaded guilty to using violence while being a member of an unlawful assembly, and rioting with a deadly weapon.

    District Judge Hamidah Ibrahim sentenced him to 21/2 years' jail and sixstrokes of the cane.

    For rioting with a deadly weapon or any object that could have been used to attack and cause death, Tan could have been jailed for up to seven years and caned.

    He was jailed for four months for theft last year, and for nine months for theft in dwelling in 2005.

    Tan was also jailed for two months in 1992 for drug offences.

    Gang violence by girls on the rise

    They dress, fight and commit crimes very much like male

    GIRL gangs are making a comeback, with an attitude and aggression that bear an unsettling resemblance to boy gangs.

     

    They dress like the boys, they fight like boys, even the crimes they commit are what the boy gangs are notorious for: rioting, robbing and extorting money, all offences which carry jail sentences.

    One of the five detectives interviewed for this report, offered this snapshot of today's girl thugs: "In the past, girls broke the law in such areas as unlawful assembly, fights over men, and in staring incidents at public places such as discos.

    "Now, they are doing what boy gangs do: breaking into cars to steal, threatening people and demanding money from schoolmates and neighbours."

    Official figures show a disturbing rise in teenage girls committing extortions, robberies and other crimes.

    In the first six months of this year, girls were involved in: 16 robberies, up from five last year; 25 thefts, up from 21; 236 shoplifting cases, up from 188, and 15 extortion cases, up from 10. In 1996, there were no reports of girls involved in extortion cases.

    A total of 310 girls were arrested in the first six months of this year, compared with 273 in the first half of last year.

    The detectives said that the pace picked up last September. They now make two to three arrests a week. Before, one a week was about average. Most of these girl gangsters are between 13 and 17 years old, although the police have come across girls as young as 10.

    Police officers have sometimes mistaken them for boys because of their "short hair, men's shirt, trousers and Doc Martens shoes".

    And when they fight, it is with a vengeance.

    "Before, the girls would scratch and pull each other's hair. Now, they punch, kick and scratch their victims with sharp objects such as small knives. In some cases, they attack with stiletto-heeled shoes, leaving a bloody mess."

    Compared with their forerunners, the Butterfly and Rose gangs of the early Sixties, the gangs today give themselves "cutesy" names like: Xiao Ding Dang (Little Doraemon, a Japanese cartoon character) and Xiao Tian Tian (Little Candy, another Japanese cartoon character).

    They operate in gangs with between 15 and 20 members. Membership comes easy too: Initiation rituals are not required. There's no need to make offerings to Guan Kong (the Chinese God of War), for example. They just wear the same shoes, coloured wrist bands or sport similar tattoos.

    Girl gangs often pick on neighbours or schoolmates.

    Said a detective: "They would extort money from other girls, after accusing them of staring or spreading rumours about them."

    Counsellors who have dealt with these girls say the lack of parental control and attention is the main cause of this rising tide of thuggery.

    Most of the girls are from homes with a history of family abuse or where the parents have marital problems.

    Their parents tend to shrug off the misdeeds, and often ask the police to let the girls off because "they're just kids", the detectives said.

    Even after the girls are convicted and sent to homes or placed on probation, many parents refuse to see that their child is developing into a full-fledged criminal.

    Counsellor Lindy Ong, 35, who has set up a support group in a church for parents with problem teens, said: "Some just leave their kids with us and expect us to perform miracles. They don't even turn up for counselling sessions."

    In a research bulletin issued by the Subordinate Courts, a court official said: "If the young person's undesirable values and erroneous methods of solving her problems are not corrected early, there will be dire consequences."
