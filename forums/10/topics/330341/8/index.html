    IT'S an issue that divides the protectors and those who wish to be protected.

    To the police, the law is clear. If an offence is non-seizable, they can't make an arrest. Their hands are tied by the law.

    But for the civilian, it is confusing, frustrating, and at times, infuriating.

    His logic, governed by emotions, is simple: If a person gets beaten up, his assailant should be detained for questioning, arrested, investigated and possibly prosecuted.

    The New Paper reported that Mr Kelvin Lee was punched in the face by a stranger last Wednesday. The blow knocked out four of his teeth and left him with a bloodied mouth.

    Yet, his alleged attacker was not arrested initially because the police had classified the case under Section 323 (voluntarily causing hurt) of the Penal Code.

    This is a non-seizable offence, meaning no immediate arrest.

    Non-seizable offences require a magistrate's authorisation before the police can make an arrest or exercise full powers of investigation.

    But if the police assess that the case is of significant public concern, the investigating officer (IO) will apply for an order to investigate, especially if the victim is young, infirm, elderly or disabled.

    The police have since told Mr Lee that the police would be investigating the case and that he need not lodge a magistrate's complaint.

    But in any reasonable person's book, Mr Lee's injuries may be seen as grievous hurt.

    This was the gist of the letters received.

    Voluntarily causing grievous hurt comes under Section 325, a seizable offence, which empowers the police to arrest the culprit.

    So why did the police not classify Mr Lee's case under Section 325?

    Lawyer Nakoorsha Abdul Kadir points to the categories of injuries listed under the section.

    In the Penal Code, a person who causes 'hurt' is defined as someone who causes 'bodily pain, disease or infirmity to any person'.

    But only the following injuries are classified as grievous hurt: castration, permanent damage to either eye, ear or limb/joint, permanent disfiguration of the head or face, or fracture/dislocation of a bone.

    Grievous hurt is also said to have taken place if the victim's life was put in danger, or if he, as a result of the assault, was in severe pain or unable to go about his daily activities for 20 days.

    Mr Nakoorsha said: 'Technically, (Mr Lee's injuries) may not fall into any of the categories.

    'But the police can always upgrade his case to a seizable one (if they later find the assault was more serious).'

    Former police officer Luke Lee, who is now a criminal lawyer , said the police may have their own 'internal guidelines' for Section 323 cases.

    'When I was with the police, we used them to determine how the case should be treated and how far the investigations should go.'

    ALLEGATION

    He declined to disclose the guidelines.

    'But the case should be investigated anyway as there was an allegation of a racist remark,' he said.

    The victim had said his alleged attacker accused him of making a racist remark, which he denied.

    Another former cop, Mr Lionel De Souza, felt the police had 'failed to exercise initiative and prudence'.

    He said he would have arrested

    Mr Lee's alleged assailant.

    '(Mr Lee) was reported to be bleeding from the mouth. To a reasonable man, his injury could be serious,' the private investigator noted.

    'In such a situation, I would, as the IO, classify the case as voluntarily causing grievous hurt, for the purpose of investigation.'

    But, he said that after questioning the assailant and formally charging him under Section 325, he would release him on police bail till Mr Lee's detailed medical report is out.

    'If the report shows his injuries don't fall under any of the categories of grievous hurt, I'd then amend the charge to voluntarily causing hurt.'

    There is another dimension to the issue: The possibility of the police abusing their power if they're allowed to make arrests in all cases.

    It's a matter of exercising discretion, Mr De Souza argued.

    'Police investigators are vested with investigation powers under the Criminal Procedure Code, and they should exercise due diligence and discretion when investigating cases similar to Mr Lee's,' he said.

    The police also do not want people to make indiscriminate reports or to use it as a malicious tool.

    Mr Nakoorsha noted: 'There's a good reason why the police can't just arrest anyone. If (the law allows them to), it will be abused.'

    And he feels victims of assault should not underestimate the power of a magistrate's complaint.

    It's a simple process, he said - go to the court, fill up a form and submit the necessary documents. The magistrate will decide on the same day if the case warrants further investigation.

    'It's a fast and efficient process, and it's free. As long as you show you have grounds for a case, the magistrate will direct the police to investigate.'

    Last year, 3,659 magistrate's complaints were filed, according to a report in The Straits Times last month. Of these, 32 per cent concerned alleged assaults, making it the most common grievance brought before a magistrate.

    If a criminal charge can't stick, the victim can take the culprit to court in a civil suit.

    But that involves a lot more work - and money. The victim has to hire a lawyer, get witnesses and go through other legal procedures. And the burden of proof is on him, Mr Nakoorsha said.

    But while there's legal recourse for victims, the bigger question is: Will the perception that the police lack bite or are reluctant to make an arrest embolden the violent?

    This becomes more pertinent in the light of recent violence reported in schools and in nightspots.

    Would school bullies now get the signal that it's okay to bash the weak?
