    I have studied the WP Manifesto and would like clarifications and a debate on it to help me decide who to vote in the coming elections. This thread is on the healthcare issue (http://www.wp.sg/party/manifesto/m2006_chp06.pdf) only, an area that I am more familar with. Perhaps others can debate other issues in the manifesto:

    Beliefs
    The statements are generally acceptable and indeed attractive to most people. It's the implementation that may be difficult.

    WP believes that it is more cost-effective to pool the risks of the population to meet healthcare needs. That is true but I would caution that with an aging population, that risk will only escalate. The government talks about delaying the inevitable with a healthy lifestyle. What the Minister of Health does not know (or prefers not to reveal) is that it may not make much difference to the healthcare costs.

    We will all die from some disease or other - I have never seen a death certificate that lists the cause of death as due to old age. We all die from diseases associated with old age, like heart failure, cancer etc...Healthy lifestyle or not, the time will come when we will need healthcare. In fact, as technology improves, we are dying from diseases for which treatment is increasingly expensive. My grandfather died from simple pneumonia which would have been treated with antibiotics (few hundred dollars), my grandmother died from a bleeding ulcer (surgery costs 1-2 thousand dollars) but chances are that I will die from cancer, treatment of which will costs tens to hundreds of thousands of dollars.

    The WP states that all should be provided with basic healthcare. But how basic is basic? When my grandfather had TB in the days before streptomycin, the only treatment is to rest the lungs by creating a pneumothorax because antibiotics were too expensive. Early breast cancer was treated by surgery and standard chemotherapy, but now there are new drugs that will improve the cure rate from 60% to 80%, at the cost of tens of thousands. Is that drug basic? HIV drugs do not cure at all but improve the length and quality of life. Is that basic?

    The fact is, our expectations are rising way beyond our means to pay for them. What is basic healthcare for an Ethiopian with an ischaemic foot (an amputation to treat the lack of blood supply due to disease) is not acceptable for the Singaporean who wants vascular surgery and reconstruction. Can we afford to offer all Singaporeans the WP's basic healthcare? Do we need to define it in dollars and cents?

    The NHS in UK offers free basic heatlhcare at delivery. HIV drugs and many expensive drugs not available to Singaporeans are provided free here. But the people are demanding more. They want newer, more effective drugs like Herceptin and Velcade for various kinds of cancer but the costs can bankrupt the entire system.

    The WP must be clear in their definition of basic healthcare and the costing. How much more will they expect the government to fund and where will the money come from?

    Proposals
    Medishield and deductibles: WP wants to increase the limitation on Medishield claims per year (currently 50k) but does not specify how much. They want the government to fund most of the healthcare costs but does not specify how much more.

    Does the WP know how much more it will cost the government to implement its policies? Suppose the WP were to take power tomorrow, how much more will it pump into the healthcare budget and where is the money coming from?

    Health insurance: WP wants a comprehensive public health insurance scheme but does not specify who is going to pay for it. (I think they mean Singaporeans themselves). I have purchased fairly comprehensive health insurance policies for my parents and they cost me about 1.5k per person per year. How many Singaporeans can afford it?

    WP also talked about a compulsory basic hospitalisation insurance scheme but it is not clear how it sits with the comprehensive public health insurance scheme. Presumably the former is optional and paid by Singaporeans and the latter is compulsory. But why should I have both? If I have paid for a comprehensive package for myself already, why should I pay more into the basic healthcare policy?

    WP wants universal basic healthcare based on costs of B2 wards. Do they mean the cost to the hospital or cost to the patient? As it is, the government subsidizes a large part of the B2 charges. The actual cost of the B2 wards is much higher than what you see on the fee schedule in our public hospital. Do they know that?

    How basic is basic healthcare? Currently HIV drugs, expensive cancer drugs like Gleevac, Rituximab, Velcade, Herceptin, Irresa etc are not standard drugs and are not available to B2 patients unless they pay for them. WP wants HIV drugs to be included but what about the others? How much is the limit on payouts for the universal basic healthcare policy?

    WP wants the premium to be based on the 90th percentile of medisave contribution, but what sort of claim limit will a universal healthcare insurance policy yield? Can that amount pay for the basic healthcare package?

    WP also wants a cap on the maximum deductibles based on 2 years' medisave contribution on a salary of $2000 and Medifund to pay for those who cannot afford to pay. Does WP know how much will be the shortfall based on a claim limit of say, 50k a year as per current practice? How much more will the government have to fund to meet the shortfall?

    Medisave for treatments not classified as basic hospitalisation insurance
    WP wants to allow Medisave to be used for specialist and other treatments not considered as basic healthcare. Is this a backdoor exit in case the basic healthcare insurance policy works out to be too expensive for the government to fund? Because the WP has not defined what is basic healthcare, when push comes to shove, what if a WP government simply declares that basic healthcare means treatment not exceeding $2000 per year per patient? Everything else must come from the patient through the medisave. With hospital costs running up to tens of thousands, how far will that go?

    We need to know exactly what basic healthcare insurance covers.

    Subsidy for polyclinics and means testing
    WP wants to continue subsidy for polyclinics but wants to introduce means testing as to the quantum of subsidy. The question with means testing has been how to implement it. By income tax returns? Then all the rich tai tais who do not work but spend their time at dim sum will get subsidies. By housing types? Then the well-off single Yuppies will get subsidies whilst the struggling family in a 5-room flat who cannot sell their apartment except at a huge loss will not get healthcare subsidy.

    WP manifesto- Healthcare: Containing healthcare costs
    On this issue WP advocates the setting up of a National Basic Health Care Council to decide on the question I have raised, as to what should be included in the basic hospitalisation insurance scheme. That is not very helpful since voters have no idea if what the WP is advocating is something better or worse than what is currently covered by the Medishield schemes. Deciding what should be included is much easier if we know how much money a WP government will pump into healthcare. If I know your budget, I can tell you what the menu will be.

    WP wants to study how advancement in medical technology can reduce rather than increase healthcare costs. As a doctor involved in medical research, I am not hopeful that advances in technology will reduce healthcare costs. Pharmaceutical companies are not charities. They fund research so that they can sell newer drugs that will increase their profits. A*STAR is pushing biomedical research so that Singapore can earn more money from advances in medical technology, not reduce spending. In recent years Singapore has earned quite a bit from the growth of the pharmaceutical sector. The people who have paid for our increased earnings have obviously spent more in healthcare. Advances in medicine have served to improve the length and quality of life, but at a cost. Far from reducing healthcare costs, embracing the latest that medical research has to offer will tend to increase healthcare costs. There are exceptions, but they are few.

    I think we can better reduce healthcare costs by administrative measures. There should be greater cooperation between the two healthcare clusters to share equipment and expertise, garner the advantage of their combined purchasing power to seek the best deal from pharmaceutical companies rather than functioning as separate entities.

    The WP suggests the use of IT to reduce administrative and manpower costs. That has happened already under the current government. Medical records are now electronic and made more easily accessible to doctors all over the island. This has increased efficiency but I am not sure if it has reduced administrative and manpower costs significantly. It is true that we will fewer clerks to retrieve case notes if they are all available electronically but I don't think that will make a dent in manpower costs compared to the capital and running costs of the IT systems.

    The WP has advocated outsourcing of health care delivery to reduce costs. Cleaning and non-core functions of the hospitals are already outsourced to a large extent. Instead of 50-year old amahs, we now have 19 year old Bangladeshi health attendants who can work harder and carry heavier loads. What about the retrenched amahs though? The Minister of Health is also looking into outsourcing some XR reporting to Bangalore and will probably happen.

    What other areas will the WP outsource next? Singaporean surgeons are among the most expensive in the world....can we outsource operations to Malaysia? Allow Medisave deductions for patients who have their surgery done in Malaysia or Thailand, as long as they are cheaper than those in Singapore? (However, patients will have to go to the Malaysian or Thai courts for redress if anything goes wrong). It would also be cheaper to locate our labs in JB to take advantage of the cheaper rental and infrastructural costs. It will not increase the turnaround time that much since reports can be accessed electronically over the internet.

    The last WP proposal under this section is already a MOH policy: to make public the costing of medical treatment by all hospitals. I might add another: would the government make public the salaries of hospital doctors? Those of us in the profession are already aware of it. Surgeons and eye surgeons in particular are the highest paid although there is no lack of doctors wanting to train in these disciplines. With annual pay exceeding 1 million a year for many top surgeons, who would want to be a psychiatrist, geriatrician, radiologist or pathologist? Should we go towards a system like the NHS in UK, where all specialists in public service are paid a similar amount, with higher pay for seniority and performance? The system in Singapore fails to ensure talented doctors go into all fields of medicine.
