    Certainly someone here hasn't heard about what a regressive tax is all about, and how GST is technically a regressive tax.

    Now, just to enlighten you a little, typing 'define: regressive tax' in Google yields the following definition:

    '(Regressive tax) is a tax in which the poor pay a larger percentage of income than the rich. Progressive Tax is the exact opposite of regressive tax.'

    Why is GST categorised as a regressive tax? Simple. When it comes to expenditure on basic necessities such as foodstuffs and water, comsumption patterns do not change much with rising income. Both rich men and poor men still have to eat rice. By increasing an across-the-board tax on everything including basic necessities, what you're doing is making the poor pay more for their necessities, i.e. taking out a larger percentage of their income.

    The beef that we have with the increase in GST has nothing to do about the rich having to pay more for their luxury lifestyle. Rather, it has everything to do about the poor having to pay more for their meagre lifestyle. Unless you tell me that all poor men would break their bank accounts to eat caviar and drink chardonnay at every meal, you are in effect increasing the burden on them.

    If they introduce a progressive tax that will increase taxes on luxury items, I might still be able to believe them, but to increase a regressive tax? Oh please... I'm not stupid enough to believe that that will work.

    I will agree that there will be government handouts to the poor, but any handouts will be based on statistical data based on average consumption patterns. And as with any statistics, the law of averages tells us that there will always be people who will not be adequately represented by the data.

    If a net monthly household income of $1000 is taken as the upper limit of lower-income families, then a household with an income of $1000 gets the handouts, while another household with an income of $1100 but with added burden of medical bills and what not doesn't get it.

    In the end, to sum it up, this is what the increase in GST will do to the various income groups:

    Low-income: Some will benefit if their consumption patterns are below the average and the handouts more than offset the rise in GST. Others with consumption patterns that go above the average will suffer a net loss.

    Middle-income: No benefits from handouts, and with the largest burden of all since they have lower incomes than the high-income group.

    High-income: No benefits from handouts, with a smaller burden than the middle-income group.

    I hereby invite you to challenge my economic analysis.
