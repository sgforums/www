    BUSINESS PITFALLS
    Local rivalry hinders Sino-Singapore industrial park joint venture
    FOO CHOY PENG
    SCMP, Thursday, April 9, 1998
    http://special.scmp.com/cbr/cbr199804/ZZZONQPZRKC_p2.html

    Speaking the same language does not necessarily mean talking the same language. That is what Singapore investors have discovered - to their dismay - in Suzhou, the historic city 80 kilometres west of Shanghai.
    Four years ago, Singapore sealed a joint-venture deal with the Chinese central government to turn 70 square kilometres of land in Suzhou into a model industrial park which eventually would provide work for 360,000 people and housing for 600,000.

    Thus was born the China-Singapore Suzhou Industrial Park (CSSIP), to be built over 15 to 20 years at a cost of about US$20 billion to the developers - a Singapore consortium holding a 65 per cent equity stake and Chinese partners with 35 per cent.

    On the surface, there was much to be upbeat over the joint-venture park. Singapore, with about 30 years' experience running industrial parks, had something to impart to the Chinese.

    That the deal was signed by Vice-Premier Li Lanqing and Lee Kuan Yew - the father of modern Singapore - suggested there was nothing the country would hold back to ensure the success of the venture, hailed as the flagship of Sino-Singapore co-operation.

    Singapore, in turn, would secure a foothold in the rapidly growing mainland economy.

    The complexities and frustrations of operating in the unruly market that was China were well known, but many thought these could be reduced because Singaporeans speak the same language and the governments, at the highest level, shared a commitment to the project.

    However, as the Singaporeans have discovered, speaking the same language does not make it any easier to carry out the deal.

    The CSSIP might be a deal between Singapore and the central Government, but because it is sited in Suzhou, officials of that city exert more influence over it than Beijing.

    That should not have been a problem, except that Suzhou had about three years before set up its own park, Suzhou New District.

    Singapore sources said it chose to set up in Suzhou, knowing there was another park being developed, because at the time the joint-venture park was targeted at hi-tech and capital-intensive foreign investors, whereas Suzhou's own park was meant essentially to house industries evacuated from the city centre.

    "The two parks were supposed to cater to different investment segments - one for the medium to high end, the other for the low end," an analyst said.

    However, friction began to build after the CSSIP authorities complained that the supposed division of labour between the two developments had evaporated, with Suzhou's park competing with it for the same investors.

    To the Singaporeans, the Suzhou city government was reneging on the fundamental promise that attracted them in the first place - an understanding that the park was a priority for Suzhou. Instead, they found their joint venture played down by local officials in favour of Suzhou's own park.

    Differences over how the park should be managed were bound to occur, given the size and scale of investment. Singaporeans tended to be more straight-talking and business-friendly, but were often at a loss in dealing with the red tape and developing the guanxi necessary to oil the engines of business.

    For their part, the Chinese tended to be highly bureaucratic and slow to respond to suggestions.

    These were differences of working styles and methods and could have been settled with more patience and sincerity on both sides.

    Matters came to a head when a Suzhou vice-mayor, on a business trip to Bonn last year, advised German investors to ignore the joint-venture park and head for the city-backed location because it had the full backing of the city government and all its resources.

    He also said the Sino-Singapore park was not supported by President Jiang Zemin who, in fact, gave it priority over other Singapore projects nationwide.

    The vice-mayor's words got back to Mr Lee, who visited China in December to seek clarification from Beijing on its position towards the joint-venture park. ..........................................
