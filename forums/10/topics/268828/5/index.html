    It is getting to be more worrying the manner in which our CPF is being managed, considering the non-transparency of the system that has been implemented by the Government aka the Ruling Party.

    If the concern is with the ability of the Aged to be independent with sufficient savings from CPF - the Government has failed to make CPF work with its many about turn changes in its policies as it meanders through the last thirty years.

    If each Singaporean worker has a CPF Account that receives $200 from its own wage deduction, and another $200 from its Employer, this will result in a monthly INDIVIDUAL contribution of $400, or $4,800 per annum.

    If there are 1,000,000 in the Singapore Workforce, this annual sum received by CPF will be $4,800.000,000 per annum.

    Across the working life of the next 30 years, the CPF Board will have received a shocking total sum of $144,000,000,000.

    This total at the end of the 30th year has not even considered the annual compounded interest that is payable by CPF on the accounts held.

    This total has not considered any wage increases as some in the workforce move up the wage scale.

    This total sum is only considering the initial 1,000,000 workers from the 1950s period, and has not considered the additional contributions from the new annual entries into the workforce.

    With a CPF system that has to support a larger population of those born in the 1950s, it will require an ANNUAL INCREASE to the numbers entering the workforce to contribute more than the amounts that the 1950s generation will have to withdraw.

    It will require a larger annual CPF contributing population to form the large base of a Pyramid, and the retiree population withdrawing from the CPF forming the space at the smaller apex of the pyramid.

    Unfortunately, the effective outcome of the Population Control Program during the 1960s campaign of 'STOP At Two' has resulted in an annual shrinking of the birthrates and a slow depletion of the population that do not reproduce itself.

    This must result in an inverted pyramid when the larger numbers from the 1950s baby boomers are now retiring, and it is being supported by a smaller and shrinking workforce that ccontribute insufficiently into the CPF to support the larger amount being withdrawn by the 1950s retirees.

    All that we have seen is denial from the Government that there are no funds to make the payouts, but a slew of policy changes seems to hint otherwise - when policy changes indicate a continued delay to the withdrawing age from the inital 55 years to the present 65 years; and we have also seen the many programs that deplete our main CPF acounts by deduction for Medicare, or transfers to MediSave.

    These deductions to Medicare and MediSave will only provide a minimal coverage to each Singaporean, and will hardly cover the total sum of any major medical treatment that has reach astronomical sums even at Government managed hospitals.

    There seems to be a reluctace and resistance to snowball all the individual deductions and transfers into a Super National Insurance Scheme that will provide a Super Coverage Net for ALL Singaporeans, that will be of sufficient amount to protect Singaporeans healthcare without need for any CASH outlays.

    Such a scheme works as the amounts not utilised by healthier Singaporeans can be used by those less healthy and in greater need for help.

    If charity and self-help is expected from Singaporeans, it is surprising that the Government being in the seat of main control and major influence will not take the Leadership Role to make this obvious happen, but will indulge in tinkering the CPF system to achieve some minor goals with smaller effective results.

    The CPF system belong to Singaporeans, and if it must be used to help one's offspring to gain a better education, it is also an investment by the parents on their children who will be in a better position to look after the parents in old age.

    If there are fears that conflict will arise in the abandoment of the parents, there are legislations already in place to ensure that this do not happen; unless the Government is claiming that the policy is a failure.

    The CPF has been manipulated to its death, and it looks like Singaporeans will have to bear with another round of arrogance from the Government in not consulting us for decisions already made.
