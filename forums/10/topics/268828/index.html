    13% of students who borrowed from parents' CPF accounts have not paid back money

    SINGAPORE: Manpower Minister Dr Ng Eng Hen said Tuesday that 13 percent of students who had borrowed from their parents' CPF accounts for tertiary education have not been able to pay back the money despite the Board's enforcement efforts.

    To better prepare Singapore for the needs of the ageing population, Dr Ng said the Ministry should not extend the use of CPF for educational purposes.

    He told Parliament there were other existing avenues through which students could obtain financial assistance.

    He stressed that only 40 percent of active CPF members turning 55 are able to meet their CPF minimum sum.

    Hence, Singapore should help members set aside more CPF savings in the future, instead of allowing withdrawals for more purposes. - CNA/ir

    Firstly, the fact that most people in Singapore take their "education path" for granted and "expect" the government to do everything for them stems from the culture instigated by this regime on its own, isn't it? Then again, it's an inherent feature in most "nanny states". After all, are you seriously expecting an overly-domesticated cat to possess the survival instincts of its counterparts surviving in the wild?

    Back to your analogy. Are you aware Federal Grants are available to just about every American college student (and I literally mean every college - from private to public schools)? In addition, numerous private colleges and state governments actually provide further financial aid (which could come in the form of grants, loans or work-arrangement schemes) to facilitate the student's educational financial liabilities. To illustrate such policies in practice and the significance of them in helping students out, just look at how many of the country's top institutions (which charge in the range of USD40000-USD45000 annually - my figures could be a little off as it's been sometime since I left college but it should be roughly in that range) are actually only able to afford a need-blind admissions policy to Americans, but not to International Students simply because the dearth of Federal Grant/Aid available to foreign students would render it impossible for such colleges to provide sufficient financial aid to help these students through. (As far as I know, Harvard, Yale and MIT are the only colleges that has a need-blinds admission policy for International Students due to their excessively large endowment funds)

    On a sidenote, many American college students spend a year or two doing internships or taking part in charitable/worthwhile causes before they begin college do so out of interest and to boost their CV (which is essential for those planning to attend graduate schools and a massive source for reference letters and endorsement) as much as for the money.

    In Singapore, tuition grants are only granted to the local institutions which are effectively state-funded (the local universities and polytechnics) - if you were attending a private school, you wouldn't even get any form of state grants to help you tide things over. In essence, you're left to fend for yourself.

    In essence, there's hardly any parallel between the 2 systems.
