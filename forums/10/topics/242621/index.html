    SINGAPORE'S PSA International has won the right to build and operate a major container port at the Panama Canal, the world's third busiest waterway after the Malacca Strait and the Suez Canal.
    The move represents a big growth opportunity for PSA - the world's No.2 port operator - as about 5 per cent of all global shipping passes along the 92-year-old canal.

    And the canal is set to become even more important after a US$5.25-billion (S$8.3-billion) plan to widen the waterway to accommodate bigger ships was recently given the green light.

    PSA has not disclosed the cost of the facility, which will be at the canal's Pacific Ocean entrance, but it is estimated by industry watchers to cost between US$100 million and US$200 million.

    While this is not PSA's biggest overseas investment, it presents a big opportunity to further tap into the fast-growing trade between China and America's East Coast.

    PSA operates a number of terminals in China, but the Panama port will be its first in the Americas.

    The Panama Canal is a massive short-cut letting ships sail between the Atlantic and Pacific oceans without making the long, treacherous trip around the bottom of South America.

    About 40 per cent of all containers shipped from North Asia to the US East Coast pass through the famous waterway.

    The new terminal takes PSA's global network to 25 ports in 14 countries.

    Like Hong Kong rival Hutchison Whampoa - the world's No.1 port operator - PSA has been investing overseas for growth and now moves more containers abroad than in Singapore, the world's busiest container port.

    PSA will face familiar competition at the Panama terminal as Hutchison operates terminals at both ends of the waterway. PSA is set to start operations by 2009.

    PSA's concession from the Panamanian government comes two years after Panama and Singapore signed a free trade deal.

    'I am pleased that we have taken this important step to develop a major port facility on the Pacific coast, in tandem with the widening and expansion of the Panama Canal, which is currently underway,' said PSA chief executive Eddie Teh.

    'Our mutual collaboration will serve to add impetus to the flow of world trade between Asia, the Americas, Europe and beyond.'

    The facility will be located at a former US naval base at Rodman.

    The first phase of the three- berth project will involve PSA building a 330m-long berth on a 12-ha plot that will handle both container and roll-on-roll-off vessels.

    Equipped with three quay cranes, the berth will be able to handle 450,000 standard containers a year.

    A water depth of 14m also means that it will be able to handle large ships that will sail through the canal when it is widened. Many modern vessels are now too big to pass through the waterway.

    The Panamanian government said the PSA terminal will double the country's port capacity, according to news reports on Monday.

    It is unclear what impact PSA's Rodman project will have on Panama's earlier plans to develop a giant US$600 million container port at the Pacific end of the canal. Since last year, the country has been keen for PSA to participate in the project.

    Straits Times, 14 March
