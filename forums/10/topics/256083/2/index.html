    Actually, leading a country consists of 3 primary functions :

    1. The Executive (PM, pr President, in our case MM as well it seems.)
    2. The legislation (Parliament, incl the cabinet)
    3. The Judicary (Judges/courts, Police Force)

    These 3 bodies have to be independent of each other.

    1. The executive makes decisions, makes plans for the future of the nation.

    2. The legislation passes bills, represents the people.

    3. The Judicary ensures that no one oversteps their boundaries, or abuses power, preserves the constution, police force enforces the the legislation passed by Parliament.

    So the checks and balances for formed by the relationship and interdepence of these three bodies.

    Legislation answers to the executive, the executive is kept in check by the judicary and the judicary is answerable to the legislation(the people).

    Its goes full circle, so no one is right at the top with abosolute ruling power. All three bodies have to work together.

    But in our unique nation, all three bodies are answerable to the PAP leadership. Giving the executive abosolute power, and hence, the system of checks and balance breaks down.

    Actually, that's not how it should work in any civilised democracy (it doesn't merit comparison with this regime which functions on the basis of the laws of the jungle). Quite simply, there would be a conflict of interest if any branch of Government had to answer to another.

    Taking 2 models of democracy which you'd probably be familiar with, you might wish to consider the US and Great Britain.

    In the US, the Executive and Legislature are elected seperately and do not answer to each other (although they often work together to reach a compromise should both branches of Government fall in the hands of different parties as no bills can be passed without the joint approval of the Executive and Legislature). For the Executive, the people elect the President and Vice-President (the Legislature and Judiciary cannot overrule the results of elections for Executive and vice versa). The Legislature comprise of elected representatives to both the Senate and House of Representatives which form Congress. The Judiciary, however, comprises of 9 justices nominated by the Executive (ie.the President) and approved by the Legislature through Senate. Although the Judiciary comprises of members appointed by the Executive and approved by the Legislature, the Judiciary does not account to the Executive or Legislature in any way for its actions are not bounded by either.

    In Great Britain, their brand of parliamentary democracy - the same brand which the fascists of this regime base themselves on (in theory, at least) as do most former British colonies - is different from that of the US. The Legislature, comprising the House of Commons and House of Lords (most policies are passed in the House of Commons where the MPs are elected and the House of Lords have very limited powers), functions in such a manner that the leader of the party with the most seats in parliament will become the Prime Minister (even if the largest party does not have enough seats to form its own government, a coalition government will then be form with the Prime Minister being the leader of the party with the most seats). However, the power of the Executive is actually vested in the Monarch (ie.Queen Elizabeth II at this point in time) and exercised through the Monarch's ministers. Note that the Prime Minister actually exercises his power, via the Monarch, through the Executive and not the Legislature - the PM's role in the Legislature(ie.House of Commons) is no different from the other 646 elected MP's as far as power goes. The power of the Judiciary lies in the hands of the UK's courts of law and function as an independent entity from both the Legislature and Executive.

    In essence, both models yield examples whereby no branch of Government is actually "inferior" or accountable to another - and that's how things should be for any civilised democracy to function in the manner stipulated by its Constitution.
