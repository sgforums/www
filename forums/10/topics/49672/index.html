    Tycoon's ex-China bride pens her tale
    Giving former husband a pseudonym, she writes about marriage, divorce and legal battles, but book may not be sold here

    By Neo Hui Min

    THE Beijing woman who started talk of the 'China bride syndrome' here after an acrimonious divorce case with her wealthy Singaporean husband, has written a book on her experiences in Singapore.

    Entitled Wo He Hao Men Da Guan Si, or Taking On A Tycoon In Court, the book is not yet in book shops, but a weekly Shanghai newspaper, Shanghai Yizhou, has printed extracts.

    'I would say that almost 100 per cent of the story is true. I have been bursting to tell my side of the story and now I've done so.' - Madam Shi Fang, in her book Wo He Hao Men Da Guan Si (Taking On A Tycoon In Court)
    It is the tale of how she met her former husband, her relationship with his family, how her marriage fell apart and her three-year legal battle.

    Now 37, Madam Shi Fang gives her husband the pseudonym Mr Ye Shun'an in the book, and does not name his family. But she claims that most of the account is based on her life here.

    She told The Straits Times in Mandarin by phone: 'I would say that almost 100 per cent of the story is true.

    'I have been bursting to tell my side of the story and now I've done so in this book.'

    A former tour guide, Madam Shi wed Mr Koh Pee Huat, son of shipping tycoon Koh Nai Tor, in 1990 in Beijing.

    After 1 1/2 years, she filed for divorce, claiming a share of their home, shares in the Kohs' company, and maintenance of $4,133 a month.

    COURT BATTLES

    MR KOH Pee Huat married Madam Shi Fang in 1990. About three years later, she obtained a divorce from him on the grounds of desertion.

    The High Court ordered Mr Koh to pay her a monthly maintenance of $2,300 and a lump sum of $46,700.

    But she asked the court to increase her maintenance to more than $4,000 and order that she receive a half-share of their Jalan Pelepah home in Pasir Panjang and a share in the family business.

    She lost the appeal.

    In 1995, she took Mr Koh to court for fabricating false evidence in his affidavits, in their disputes over maintenance. He was convicted and sentenced to six months in jail for falsifying evidence. This was reduced to one month after he appealed.

    But she did not get more maintenance from him. Instead, she was granted a settlement of $250,000 once she vacated the Jalan Pelepah house and withdrew all claims to it. She appealed for more but lost the case in 1996.

    In 1998, she was back in court. This time, she was tried for allegedly making a false declaration in her divorce affidavit against Mr Koh. She was acquitted.

    The court awarded her much less. She lost her appeal.

    The case led to the Chinese-speaking community here labelling her a Xiaolongnu, or 'little dragon girl', a derogatory term used in recent times to describe women from China who compete with Singaporean women for their husbands and boyfriends - and their wallets.

    In the published extracts, Madam Shi writes of Singaporeans' prejudice against Chinese nationals.

    There is a scene of her overhearing Singaporeans saying: 'Chinamen are so scary. When they have rice, they take not one but two portions.'

    Her retort is that 'Singaporeans think that eating little is high-class behaviour'.

    And in a description of a fight with 'Mr Ye', which arose because he had accused her of keeping a necklace they had bought for a friend, she says that he retorted: 'Have you had enough? Who asked you to be a PRC?'

    PRC refers to People's Republic of China, the official name of mainland China.

    Up till today, Madam Shi feels she has been wronged.

    'I feel that media reports were very biased against me. I was portrayed as an airhead and nobody asked me for my opinion,' she said.

    Friends and journalists egged her on to tell her side of the story in a book.

    Since returning to Beijing in 1998, she has been busy writing, and completed the book only last year.

    The articulate woman now lives in Shanghai and has spent the past two months at her publisher's office editing bits that could land her in legal trouble.

    Does she fear a lawsuit?

    'Absolutely not, because everything is factual. I can prove it,' she said.

    As she is reluctant to edit out her take on Singapore's judicial system, the book may not be distributed here.

    Mrs Anamah Tan, the lawyer who represented Mr Koh in the divorce case, said that if defamatory, the family could sue her even though the book was not distributed here.

    Mr Koh Pee Huat could not be contacted for comment.

    Meanwhile, Madam Shi is looking forward to publishing an English version.

    After that?

    'I really don't know yet. I've been so busy lately, I'm just hoping to have enough time to get a haircut,' she chuckled.
