    I think there's no homogenous Singaporean stance on homosexuality at all.  It will continue to be a subject of discourse since there will be different portions of society that will rightfully have their own opinion on the matter.

    What is important to realise is that, there is no "overnight" change over norms of society.  Norms, practices and views can change but it takes a lot of time.  Attempting to expedite it often result in excessive conflict and in some case, violence.  

    This current generation of mainstream Singapore society is still conservative and do not agree with homosexuality.  Given the "deep rootedness" of heterosexual norms, homosexual norms are not likely to reach the "critical mass" of adoption in the near future.  Attempting to push new norms faster than what society is ready to accept is potentially dangerous and disruptive.  I believe this AWARE saga has highlighted this as well in the form of "death threats" that manifested from this dramatic conflict.

    I believe as of yet, no country/society in the world has adopted homosexuality as a mainstream norm over heterosexuality.  It seems that heterosexuality is here to stay as an age old accepted norm.  

    True, there are countries that are more tolerant if not accepting towards minority homosexuality norms.  These countries are liberal countries that enshrine the individual's right to choose their own lifestyle and speak freely about it; liberal values that create an atmosphere that does not promote conflict between hetero and homosexuals.  

    However, even as liberal as these countries can get, there is only "acceptance" for homosexuals and its norms, they do not promote homosexuality as a mainstream sexual culture.  

    Homosexual and Heterosexual proponents need to learn to live with each other in a non conflictual manner.  Sometimes, the minority needs to recognise that as a minority, they cannot push too hard on certain issues that would inflame the majority.  Majorities also have to learn to be gracious to minorities, giving them space to grow and a contribute to the society as a whole.  If Singaporeans can adopt the same gracious and considerate mindset that we have gained from living in disproportionately multi-racial society, we can also adopt that same mindset to live peacefully a Singaporean multi-sexual-orientation society.
