    Nov 27, 2006
    S'pore Power, as people's asset, should consider lowering its profit margin

    I refer to Yow Wai Mun's letter "Singapore Power's tariffs don't reflect market forces" (ST, Nov 7) and Singapore Power Limited's (SPL) reply 'Singapore Power results boosted by international ops' (ST, Oct 27) to my letter 'Why delay opening up electricity retail market ?' (ST, Oct 16).

    SPL's reply states that 'the improvement in the results of the group from 2004 to 2006 is due mainly to our international operations...The number of accounts in arrears exceeding $500 for three months and longer is about 3,600 and the number of Pay-As-You Use (PAYU) meters installed todate is about 12,500'.

    Does 'due mainly to our international operations' mean that more than 50 per cent of its profits were due to international operations?

    As it is the subsidiary of SPL, SP Services (SPS) is the sole provider of electricity to consumers in Singapore. I would like to ask what was SPS's profits and profit growth from 2004 to 2006.

    If the majority of SPL's 53 per cent profit growth last year was due to international operations, I suggest that it considers reducing the profit margin of its local operations.

    SPL's assets were built up over the years by being the monopolistic supplier of electricity and these were derived from the earnings and profits that came from the monies paid by Singaporeans.

    SPL is the asset of the people of Singapore, and it is not privatised as a listed company yet. So none of its profits are being returned to Singaporeans by way of dividends.

    Its overseas expansion would not have been possible without the revenues generated by the electricity bills of Singaporeans over the decades.

    In the past, statistics on arrears were given for those of more than three months, and the highest figure ever reported was about 10,000 ("Pay-as-you-use meters to help poor households", ST, April 22, 2005).

    Now, by changing the benchmark for the statistic to 'arrears exceeding $500 for three months or longer', the number has dropped dramatically to about 3,600 in just over a year. Does it mean that almost 71 per cent of PAYU households have paid off their arrears?

    Why not go back to the old statistical measure for arrears, and just tell us how many are in arrears for more than three months, in addition to those exceeding $500?

    Leong Sze Hian
