    On March 2, 2006 the USA and India singed an agreement in New Delhi for the cooperation of the two countries on the issues of the nuclear reactors, nuclear fuel and expertise. The agreement has met with mixed reactions in both countries. Even the US congress has tabled the possibility of seeking conditions to the nuclear deal (1).

    The 2nd of March agreement of the US with India is undoubtedly a serious blow to the regime of the Non-Proliferation of the Nuclear Weapons (NPT). The concerned agreement is clearly against the spirit of the NPT. This move has sent a wrong message to countries that plan to develop nuclear weapons. The wrong message is that: you do not need to join the NPT and go through the International Atomic Energy Agency's inspections and observe the rules of Additional Protocol of the NPT. You just go ahead and follow your nuclear programs out of the framework of the NPT and disregard the calls of the international institutions (like IAEA) for making your nuclear program transparent and eventually when you achieved the nuclear bomb capability (and the capability to deliver them on the potential targets), the big powers will accept you in the nuclear club and give you some of advantages that they have reserved for themselves.

    The agreement for nuclear cooperation of US and India also sends a wrong message to the International community and especially the countries close to the USA. The wrong message is that: we (the US) are pushing Iran and the countries like that to observe the NPT and you should do the same, but sometimes we do not observe what we say ourselves. We are different from you and NPT is for you, not for us. This double standard will damage the US position towards the NPT and give others more grounds to criticize the US policy in this field.

    Now the question pops in mind: India has a well-developed nuclear energy program from the military point of view. The US is concerned about the NPT and the importance of stopping the spread of the nuclear weapons now more than ever. So, why this agreement is concluded between the two sides at this juncture?

    The answer lies in several elements. Some of these are more important than others:

    1- The expansion of globalization. Economic inter-dependence between the two countries is increasing. The Indians and Americans need and are ready to use the market of the other side. The Indians are thirsty for access to the latest technologies in the fields like computer sciences and the Americans are counting on the large market of the middle class Indians. "India has more than 300 million middle class consumers, more than the entire population of the United States". (2)

    2- Outsourcing policies of the USA are expanding. These days it is very usual that you call in the USA the office of HP, for example, to order a print head and someone in the New Delhi takes your order and arranges for the delivery. Some of the US industries are outsourcing their whole operations to the countries like India. With the great manpower in India, this country is an ideal place for the outsourcing. At the same time, a great number of the skilled people from India travel to the USA for working. So the outsourcing is two sided here. "Over 65000 Americans live in India, attracted by its growing economy and richness of its culture." (3)

    3- The possibility of persuading India to put its nuclear program under international supervision. According to the new agreement of the US and India almost 65% of the Indian nuclear program will be put under the international safeguards. "8 out of India's 22 power reactors will remain outside international controls, along with a new breeder reactor." (4)

    4- Persuading India to continue its policies in the field of non-proliferation. Unlike Pakistan (and its semi-official underground network of selling all kinds of nuclear technology and equipment), India has a good reputation in the world in this field. However, there are strong voices in India that call for export of the nuclear know-how in order to gain more financial resource for India. Therefore some sources claim that the US-India agreement of the 2nd of March 2006 in fact ? is strengthening the NPT." (5)

    5- India needs to import uranium (at least until it changes the nuclear systems in India) and the US can answer to those needs. India faces shortage of uranium for the moment.

    6- India has millions of Muslims (even more than Pakistan) but it is a Hindu state. The US and India have mutual feeling of threat from the religious extremists, especially the Muslin ones. Therefore, the US hopes to get the assistance of India against extremist Islamic moves. The recent vote of the Indian representation in the IAEA against Islamic Republic of Iran (in the case of the nuclear crisis of Iran) showed the importance of these concern. India put the proposed gas pipeline of Iran-Pakistan-India (which is very important for India due to its energy needs in future) in jeopardy, at the cost of voting against Iran.

    7- Expansion of the relations between India and Israel. These relations have several implications. Except than contacts with Israel per se, the concerned relations affect the Indian relations with the USA, the Arab countries, the Islamic Republic of Iran, Islamic extremity groups and institutions.

    8- India wants the US support in its struggle to gain a seat among the permanent members of the United Nations Security Council. India has been trying for this goal (along with Germany, Japan, and Brazil) for several years. India has even proposed to change the place of the United Nations Headquarters from New York to New Delhi. (The US may welcome this idea if gets disappointed from the UNO).

    9- India in considered a major democratic country in the area that democracy is not established in many parts of it. The USA, according to its policies, seek to strengthen the security by expanding relations with India

    10- New relations of the US-India as a balance against China.
