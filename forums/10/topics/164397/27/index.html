    Australians divided over hanging
    Email Print Normal font Large font December 1, 2005 - 8:30AM

    Australians are evenly divided about whether fellow citizen Nguyen Tuong Van should be executed in Singapore for his drug crime, a poll shows.

    A Morgan Poll conducted last night found 47 per cent of Australians believed the Melbourne man should go to the gallows.

    But 46 per cent said the death penalty should not be carried out and seven per cent were undecided.

    Nguyen, 25, will be executed in Singapore's Changi prison at 6am local time (9am AEDT) tomorrow.

    He was sentenced to death after he was caught at Changi airport in December 2002 with almost 400 grams of heroin in his possession.

    The poll also found that only 27 per cent of Australians believed the penalty for murder should be death.

    The pollster said this was the lowest rating ever recorded and was down 26 per cent since August 1995.

    Some 66 per cent of respondents said imprisonment should be the penalty and seven per cent could not say.

    Where the penalty for murder is imprisonment, 49 per cent of Australians said imprisonment should be for life (down 11 per cent since 1995), 48 per cent said the judge should fix the number of years, and three per cent were undecided

    Email Print Normal font Large font By Dewi Cooke

    Lawyers for condemned Melbourne man Nguyen Tuong Van will make one last attempt to argue his case to the Singapore Government today, despite conceding his hopes of clemency are "almost non-existent".

    Lex Lasry, QC, who has led the fight to save the 25-year-old from the gallows, said he was not confident the government would grant them an audience.

    "We've just sent a request through that they be prepared to talk to us sometime today, it went through the High Commissioner...they haven't actually said that they will see us," he told theage.com.au.

    Mr Lasry said the legal team were clinging to the hope that the Singapore authorities would at the last minute change their mind but accepted "the prospect of that is very, very low, almost non-existent".

    Mr Lasry expressed his admiration for his client. He said Nguyen was in good shape emotionally and had been spending many hours in prayer.
    "I saw him yesterday morning and he was good, he was strong, he was composed, concerned for his family and the people around him," he said.

    Visiting hours at the prison will close around 5pm today. From that point on, Nguyen will be alone.

    Mr Lasry and his colleague, Julian McMahon, will visit Van this morning, as will his friends Kelly Ng and Brownwyn Lew.

    The two young women have campaigned tirelessly to save their friend's life and Mr Lasry said the emotional stress leading up to their final goodbye had started to show.

    "They were emotional, obviously, at the end of the day yesterday but I think they are enjoying the visits. So they're coping but it's difficult for them, I mean they're young and they're obviously vulnerable to that sort of emotional stress involved."

    Mother will touch Nguyen's hands
    Email Print Normal font Large font December 1, 2005 - 4:20PM

    Condemned Australian drug trafficker Nguyen Tuong Van and his mother will be allowed to hold hands, but not to hug, before he is put to death.

    Singaporean officials today said they would grant Kim Nguyen an exemption from a total ban on physical contact with death row inmates.

    Kim Nguyen had asked to be able to hug her son one final time before he is executed at dawn tomorrow morning.

    But in a statement, the Singapore government said it would allow only limited physical contact between the condemned man and his mother and brother Khoa.

    "Mr Nguyen will be allowed to hold hands with his mother and brother," said the statement from Singapore's ministry of foreign affairs.

    It said it had agreed to the request for contact following a personal appeal by Prime Minister John Howard to his Singaporean counterpart, Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong, at the recent Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting in Malta.

    The statement said that like many jurisdictions that authorise capital punishment, Singapore did not allow "contact" visits between prisoners and family members.

    "Such encounters can be traumatic and are likely to destabilise the prisoners and their family members," it said.

    M. Ravi, a human rights lawyer who has worked on death penalty cases and campaigned to save Nguyen, said he could not fathom Singapore's decision.

    "To say you can't hug is abominable. I am lost for words," he told AAP in Singapore.

    "How can you just restrict it to the hand and not have bodily contact?"

    Pressure on Singapore to allow a contact visit before Nguyen's execution had gathered strength in recent days, with Foreign Minister Alexander Downer lobbying the city-state's government.

    Nguyen's lawyer Lex Lasry QC had said it would be inhumane if Singapore did not allow Kim Nguyen to hug her son one last time.

    "It's inconceivable that there could be any valid reason why that wouldn't happen," he said today, ahead of Singapore's announcement.

    Kim Nguyen, distress etched on her face and a handkerchief clutched tightly in her hand, arrived at Changi prison this afternoon for a final visit with her son.

    She got to the prison gates at 12.35pm local time (3.35pm AEDT), looking drained and tired.

    Very slowly, she walked the 10 metres from her car to the gates, aided by staff from the Australian High Commission in Singapore.

    - AAP

    Melbourne lawyer launches private prosecution

    December 1, 2005 - 4:17PM

    Prominent Melbourne lawyer and civil libertarian Brian Walters, SC, has begun a private prosecution against condemned drug trafficker Nguyen Tuong Van in a desperate attempt to prevent his execution in Singapore tomorrow.

    Mr Walters is seeking an urgent reply from Federal Justice Minister Chris Ellison over the summons, which he believes could allow the Federal Government to seek Nguyen's extradition from Singapore to Australia.

    "We've requested the Justice Minister to seek his extradition back to Australia," Mr Walters said. "I believe that if he does so then Singapore is then treaty-bound to comply with the request. I believe that it would place the responsibility on Singapore to comply with its treaty or to, in defiance of the treaty, carry out its sentence."

    Mr Walters has charged Nguyen with two offences under Commonwealth law Â— one count of attempting to import heroin into Australia and one count of conspiring to import the drug into Australia Â— and one count of conspiring to traffic heroin under Victorian law.

    "I think there's a realistic legal possibility that this could work," Mr Walters said. "The political realities are that Singapore is very reluctant to bend in relation to this but Singapore ought to be asked the question and I don't think we should die wondering."

    The summons issued for Nguyen requires him to attend Melbourne Magistrates Court on February 2 next year.

    Federation of Community Legal Centres executive director Pauline Spencer said a private prosecution was rarely used but was a mechanism available to any member of the public.

    "They are just issued at the counter. They are filed as documents, nothing is proven and there is no prima facie case," Ms Spencer said.

    "The government has been saying that without charges an extradition cannot happen.

    "Now this has been delivered to the Justice Minister Chris Ellison he can move to seek extradition."

    Mr Walters said there was enough time for extradition proceedings to be started and therefore to stay Nguyen's execution tomorrow.
