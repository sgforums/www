    AdvertisementJohn Howard has ruled out lobbying Commonwealth nations for support to stop the execution of Melbourne man Nguyen Tuong Van in Singapore.

    The Prime Minister, who is in the Maltese capital Valletta for the Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting, said he did not believe putting pressure on other Commonwealth nations "would be the least bit appropriate".

    "This is an issue between Australia and Singapore and I think it would be unhelpful and not likely in any way to change the mind of the Singaporean Government."

    His comments came as Opposition Leader Kim Beazley called on Foreign Minister Alexander Downer to present a united front with his Labor counterpart Kevin Rudd to plead for clemency for Nguyen.

    Mr Beazley said a bipartisan delegation should travel to Singapore to make a final plea for Nguyen's life.

    Singapore is a member of CHOGM, but Mr Howard said lobbying other nations would harden its resolve to carry out the death penalty.

    "Sadly I do not believe the Singaporean Government will change its mind," Mr Howard said.

    "And I think therefore it's not really, in a way, being very sensitive to the family to pretend."

    He said he had already argued the case at length with the Singaporean Government.

    United front

    Mr Beazley said this morning he would write to Mr Howard asking him to send Foreign Minister Alexander Downer and Opposition foreign affairs spokesman Kevin Rudd to Singapore in a last-ditch bid for clemency for Nguyen.

    The 25-year-old convicted drug courier is due to face the gallows on December 2.

    Mr Beazley said Australia should also press ahead with a case in the International Court of Justice, despite the Government's legal advice such a case would not succeed.

    "I think in that sense we have to try everything," he told the Nine Network.
    "An international court case or at least the manoeuvres towards one would again show the Singaporean authorities how serious we are on this."

    Mr Beazley said Singapore should not be bullied into changing its decision.
    "What we're about is saying 'you're on the wrong track'," he said. "This is not the right way to go with this young man.

    "For starters, you shouldn't hang people but this young man has something to contribute in our search for the Mr Bigs and we want to put behind bars the people who are really behind this drug trafficking."

    Airline pressure

    Prominent Liberal backbencher Bruce Baird says the Government should take 25-year-old Nguyen's execution into consideration during negotiations with Singapore Airlines over a share of the lucrative Australia-US air route

    Regional NGOs meet in Singapore to battle drug abuse in Asia
    By Dominique Loh, Channel NewsAsia

    SINGAPORE : Some 120 delegates from the ASEAN countries are in Singapore to exchange ideas and experiences in the battle against drug abuse.

    Home Affairs Senior Parliamentary Secretary Mohamad Maidin says that is the way to go, as the war on drugs cannot be fought individually.

    Regional governments have to work hand-in-hand to formulate effective prevention measures.

    It is a brainstorming session that could help ASEAN countries fight drug abuse.

    Each delegation brings different experiences of their fight against drug abuse.

    The Malaysian delegates say they are seeing abusers use inhalants like glue and then progress to other drugs.

    Their outreach program focuses on schools and particularly the family.

    "At the parental level, they can tell when their children are taking drugs, Also, a message is to take care of your children, look after them and see what are their activities, who their friends are, where they are in the day and at night," said Kamilia Ibrahim, secretary general of the National Association for the Prevention of Drugs, Malaysia.

    Over the last ten years, Singapore waged a campaign against heroin and more recently synthetic drugs.

    Tough penalty measures and a comprehensive aftercare system helped to keep numbers down.

    With less than 1,000 abusers last year, Singapore now has one of the lowest prevalence of drug abuse in Asia and Europe.

    Said Poh Geok Ek, president of the Singapore Anti-Narcotics Association: "YouÂ’ll find that certain drug problems in some countries spread very quickly to other countries. And certain programs that work can also be applicable to other countries. Eventually, we will learn how the drug problem is spread, how it can be fought and prevented. This is important so we don't have to reinvent the heel."

    Delegates will not be confined to just exchanging views, they will be making visits to Singapore's prison system, drug rehab centres and a halfway house.
