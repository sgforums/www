    Bracks opposes Nguyen silence
    Email Print Normal font Large font November 29, 2005 - 11:57AM

    Victorian Premier Steve Bracks says he does not support plans for a minute's silence to mark Australian drug courier Nguyen Tuong Van's execution in Singapore on Friday morning.

    The proposal has sparked opposition from the RSL and ex-servicemen, who have argued such observations are reserved for commemorative services honouring those who died while serving their country, normally in conflict.

    It comes as Singapore's hangman says there is still a chance his government may grant Nguyen an 11th hour reprieve from the gallows.

    The Australian Government has played down its latest attempts to have Nguyen's execution stopped, while former High Court judge and governer general Sir William Deane says the execution would be a breach of standards of international law.

    Mr Bracks said he would go about his business normally on Friday, and the focus of oppositon should be on the death penalty.

    "I believe there should be vigorous representations made to the Singapore government and the authorities to oppose the death penalty even now, between now and Friday, but I don't think that would be advanced enormously by cancelling, for example, a cricket match," Mr Bracks said.

    "But I would urge the prime minister to do everything he can and to make further representations to even have the foreign minister (Alexander Downer) visit, if possible before Friday, and to make those direct representations."

    His comments follow those of RSL National President Bill Crews yesterday, who said a minute's silence was reserved for commemorative services honouring those who died while serving their country, normally in conflict.

    Hope for "11th-hour" reprieve

    Three days out from Nguyen's planned execution, Singapore's chief executioner Darshan Singh said there was a chance the Singapore government might reduce his sentence to life imprisonment at the last moment.

    Mr Singh, who has hanged more than 800 prisoners over 45 years, told Channel Nine: "They may say at the 11th hour, they may give him a life sentence. It's still possible".

    Attorney-General Philip Ruddock is seeking urgent advice from Solicitor-General David Bennett about whether an appeal could be launched under Singaporean law, specifically the provisions in the city state's constitution which relate to clemency pleas.

    But Mr Ruddock did not appear to hold much hope of success for the move.

    "We've always been looking at every possible avenue to deal with . . . what we believe are the extenuating circumstances in this particular case why a mandatory death penalty should not be exacted," he told ABC radio.

    "Mr Nguyen's legal advisers have said that they believe they have covered all of the possible issues and we've certainly communicated with them all of the advice that we've received to explore every option, and we'll continue to do so."

    Supporters flock to church

    The prayers for Nguyen have continued at St Ignatius Catholic Church in Richmond, where Nguyen and his twin brother Khoa went to kindergarten and primary school.

    Parish priest Fr Peter Norden said the church would open at 6am on Friday, with a short service at 8.30am followed by a 10-minute silence leading up to 9am.

    "Just a time for quiet, a time for prayer and then the big bells of St Ignatius, which often wake people up in the morning anyhow, will sound at nine o'clock to symbolise the 25 years (of Nguyen's life)."

    He said people had been drawn to Nguyen's case because he and his family represented the type of people "who aren't powerful", that commonly end up on death row.

    "Van Nguyen is born in a refugee camp in Thailand after his mother fled Vietnam as a refugee," he said.

    "This is the sort of person that is executed in most situations around the world in the US and other countries, and I think that is why people have sort of seen the futility of it."

    THE impending execution of Nguyen Tuong Van has created a wave of misplaced empathy within the popular Australian imagination.     John Williams, Thornbury

    Another letter from The Age:

    And my poor fool is hang'd: no, no, no life?
    Why should dog, a horse, a rat have life
    And thou no breath at all? Thou'lt come no more,
    Never, never, never, never, never.

    FOUR hundred years after they were written, Shakespeare's words (King Lear, V, iii) still resonate in 21st-century Australia.

    Since hanging is the most common method of suicide in Australia, there must be many people experiencing vicarious trauma in the current crisis of Nguyen Tuong Van's impending sentence. Those of us who have witnessed the aftermath of a hanging know how deeply it assaults our psyches and are aware of what a barbarous and brutal method of death it is.

    When I read the article "The precision of ritual in the gallows' shadow" ( The Age, 24/11), I wept. The article triggered my memory of finding a loved one hanged many years ago. Unlike Nguyen, my partner took his own life when suffering serious depression. The sight of his corpse so traumatised me that the memory of his face remained blocked for a long time.

    The amnesiac veil that protected me from that violation was ripped away some years later, when I held a severely injured but still struggling animal as it was being put down by a vet. The animal's violent struggle was frozen on its face after death. For me, that evoked the memory of the grotesque appearance of my beloved partner's face when I found him.

    The psychological damage that image inflicted on me does not ever fully go away. It may recede for a time but is easily re-activated by events such as Nguyen's looming execution. The grim reality of this method of death is life-changing for those who witness it.

    So, as well as the politics of capital punishment, it is the actual manner of death by hanging that I find offensive. This utterly depraved practice should have no place in a civilised society. Such a death in a state-sanctioned, cold-blooded and mandatory sentence is an obscenity.

    Perhaps it should be mandatory for those who sentenced Nguyen Tuong Van to watch his execution. And may that experience haunt them for the rest of their lives, if that is what it takes to remove this abomination from the statutes of all nations forever.
