    Premier defends 'barbaric' slur
    Email Print Normal font Large font November 25, 2005 - 2:59PM

    Premier Steve Bracks has been forced to defend the strong language used by Attorney-General Rob Hulls in his comments on Singapore's impending execution of convicted drug courier Nguyen Tuong Van.

    Before leaving to meet with his Singapore counterpart yesterday, Mr Hulls described the coming execution of Nguyen as a "barbaric act".

    "Rob Hulls did the exact right thing I would expect an attorney-general to do," Mr Bracks said today.

    "We want to make it clear that we have a policy which opposes the death penalty and I think that was done effectively and well."

    Earlier today Nguyen's Australian-based lawyer Lex Lasry QC has warned former prime minister Gough Whitlam that his harsh criticism of Singapore as a "Chinese rogue port city" could harm Nguyen's case.

    Polite diplomacy

    In contrast, the Singapore Government-leaning Straits Times newspaper has praised John Howard's "polite" diplomacy on the issue, but warned that more Australians could face the gallows.

    In an editorial headlined "Execution of a drug runner", the paper held out no hope for Nguyen, who is scheduled to be hanged next Friday.

    "The reality is more Australians can expect to face the death penalty here because too many choose to dice with death," it predicted.

    The daily said while it "shared the anguish" of Nguyen's family, Australians need to understand why the Melbourne man deserved to die for his crime.

    The newspaper is Singapore's main English-language daily and, like all the domestic press, has close links to the governing People's Action Party.

    Mr Lasry said the input from Mr Whitlam and Mr Hawke was welcome.
    "But pejorative language probably will not help. No doubt Mr Whitlam feels frustrated by the case, as we all do," he said.

    Advice on challenge

    Acting Prime Minister Mark Vaile says the Government expects to receive advice later today on a legal challenge to the execution, but was not hopeful of success.

    One of the lawyers providing advice to the Government, Sydney University's Professor Don Rothwell, said today avenues were still open for taking the case to the ICJ.

    Mr Vaile said Australia would not use trade sanctions to put pressure on the Singaporean Government, but was still discussing the possibility of an ICJ challenge.

    "We are getting advice on that and we hopefully will have that back to us today," he told the Nine Network.

    "But we don't believe that offers an option to pursue in this matter."

    Prime Minister John Howard said yesterday the Singapore Government would go ahead with the execution and "I do not believe what is now being said is going to make any difference".

    Mr Whitlam yesterday criticised Singapore as he called for Prime Minister John Howard to raise Nguyen's case at the Commonwealth Heads of Government Meeting in Malta

    Mr Howard has said he will not use CHOGM to pressure Singapore, but Mr Whitlam told The Age newspaper it was the right platform.

    "If CHOGM is any use then it should be raised there, because it concerns many other countries, some larger, some smaller than the rogue Chinese port city," Mr Whitlam said.

    He was "never surprised" at Singapore's neglect of human rights, he said.
    Mr Hawke declined to reveal the contents of a letter he sent to Mr Lee, saying it was "a private communication".

    In Singapore, The Straits Times described the looming execution has been "churning up a wash of angst from across the seas".

    It recommended that "Australians should take an objective look at the crime, not only the punishment".

    "The drugs trade is a destroyer of lives and society, vividly so in this region. Australians who have an imperfect understanding of this can only learn to educate themselves."

    The editorial praised Howard's "polite" diplomacy, but it also recited the city-state Government's position that it is up to Singapore to choose its criminal justice system.

    "As much as Singaporeans make no judgment on the value of jurisprudence which has served the Australian Commonwealth well, Australians show their breeding by learning to accept what the Singapore situation requires."

    - AAP

    Nguyen affair inspires hasty sidestepping
    Email Print Normal font Large font November 25, 2005
    Page 1 of 2

    Who would want to be an Australian - especially an Australian in strife abroad? What with Messrs Howard, Ruddock and Downer in charge of the shop back home, prospects for any meaningful humanity are dim to non-existent.

    In particular there's the mealy-mouthed response to Nguyen Tuong Van, who is on death row in Singapore. His plight has been met with elaborate displays of hand-wringing from the three Australian humanitarians, yet nowhere in the stream of well-worn official federal condolences has there been sustained outrage at the barbaric, disproportionate penalty being imposed on one of their fellow citizens.

    Just listen to the Foreign Affairs Minister, Alexander Downer, on the ABC's Lateline on Wednesday night: "Lex Lasry [Nguyen's lawyer] has been going around and finding lawyers with different ideas and we've been putting them to our lawyers, one after the other, and I've sat at my own desk and scratched my head and tried to think up other different approaches. But you know, we are desperate. I mean, we're in a situation now where it seems to us there's nothing much more we can do."

    Then there was the Prime Minister's interdiction on Tuesday: "The only people who can stop the execution are the Government of Singapore. There is nothing illegal in what they have done [sentenced Nguyen to death]."

    It's pretty unhelpful when young Alex is at his desk scratching his head for fresh legal ideas and up the passageway the PM is saying it's all perfectly proper.

    Downer's mantra is we don't want to do anything to "hold out false hope to people". John Howard's constant message is that he feels "desperately sorry" for the condemned man's mother.

    Both have given little Nancy Reagan lectures about the evils of drugs and the cruel finger of fate that awaits smugglers of forbidden substances in Asian countries.

    That's all very well, but utterly half-baked. What about a full-blooded statement along the lines that the Australian Government thinks the execution of one of its citizens is brutish and uncivilised, that it is intolerable and that Singapore would be regarded with the greatest displeasure by Australia if it should proceed. You look at the reams of government comment about this case and nowhere can a clear, unequivocal statement be found.

    As far as an effort in the International Court of Justice is concerned, the Commonwealth has simply thrown up its hands. Even if it is found that the court ultimately cannot save Nguyen, you'd think a sincere government here would give it a determined try.

    The Attorney-General, Philip Ruddock, has already arrived at a decision: "Singapore is not bound to submit to the ICJ in relation to determinations in this matter."

    Howard also has pre-emptively declared the court has no jurisdiction. How can he be so certain in the face of strong countervailing legal opinions without even knocking on the door?

    It has been a disgraceful piece of humbug from the Government but we should not be surprised. After all, it was only in August 2003 that Howard was calling for a "debate" in Australia on the death penalty. Of course, he did not personally support capital punishment "for pragmatic reasons" but he said a lot of people were in favour of it. A lot of those people are supporters of the Coalition, according to the most recent opinion poll on the topic.

    All of that got a good ventilation when Amrozi was sentenced to death by Indonesia for his part in the first Bali bombings.

    Feelings naturally were running high, with 56 per cent of those polled wanting the death penalty for terrorists in Australia. More broadly, Howard then floated a few thoughts about capital punishment: "I respect the fact that a lot of people are in favour of the death penalty, a lot of people who are close to me are in favour of the death penalty."

    In an interview in August 2003 Howard said: "I know a lot of Australians who believe that the death penalty is appropriate and they are not barbaric, they're not insensitive, they're not vindictive, they're not vengeful. They're people who believe that if you take another's life deliberately then justice requires that your life be taken." But of course that's not vengeful, vindictive, etc.

    The opinion poll at the time showed 40 per cent were "strongly" in favour of the death penalty, per se. Of those, 64 per cent were Coalition supporters.

    It seems as though throughout the Nguyen case a fair amount of dog whistling has been going on.
