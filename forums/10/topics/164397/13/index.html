    Email Print Normal font Large font November 23, 2005 - 4:12PM

    AdvertisementThe Federal Government has rejected a suggestion convicted drug smuggler Nguyen Tuong Van's life could be saved because of a loophole in the law.

    Barrister Tim Robertson suggested in a letter to Justice Minister Chris Ellison yesterday that Australia could charge Nguyen with conspiracy to import heroin, which is an Australian offence, and then seek his surrender under mutual extradition arrangements with Singapore.

    Singapore would then have to surrender Nguyen because he was not serving a prison sentence, but was on death row, Mr Robertson said.

    But Senator Ellison said through a spokesman today he had considered the suggestion from Mr Robertson, but it was not legally possible.

    "The Attorney-General's Department came back with advice that for two reasons it wasn't possible," the spokesman said.

    "The first is that the principle of double jeopardy strictly applies to extradition matters.

    "Australia cannot seek the extradition of a person for prosecution of the same matter.

    "If he was to be extradited it would have to be if there was another matter he was involved in.

    "The Australian Federal Police has advised that they have no information that he was involved in any other matter for which Australia could seek his extradition for prosecution."

    The spokesman said the Government also had also been advised that the death penalty was a custodial sentence.

    The 25-year-old Melbourne man is to be hanged on December 2 after he was convicted of drug smuggling for carrying 396 grams of heroin in transit at Singapore's Changi airport in 2002.

    Foreign Minister Alexander Downer today said government lawyers were investigating any legal ideas raised and this afternoon he would consult with another lawyer who believed Nguyen could be spared through international treaties.

    SYDNEY: Australian youngsters aged between 12 and 15 are more likely to use marijuana than smoke cigarettes, according to a government report which also revealed an increase in ecstasy use among youth.

    Some 16 percent of teenagers aged 14-19 were regular cigarette smokers seven years ago, compared with 10 percent in 2004.

    Cannabis use over the same time period for the 14-19 age group fell from 50 percent having tried the drug in 1998 to 25 percent in 2004, the report said.

    While illicit drug use in general has fallen, teenagers were more likely to be experimenting with party-drug ecstasy, the report found.

    Less than five percent of respondents aged 14-19 used the drug in 1998, compared with more than 6 percent in the latest study.

    A brief history of death by decree

    By Connie Levett
    November 23, 2005
    AdvertisementAdvertisement

    The death penalty was used in Singapore during colonial times and stayed on the books after independence in 1965, with Lee Kuan Yew the founding prime minister. The penalty was mandatory for murder and offences against the president, and was discretionary for a number of other offences.

    In 1975 the Misuse of Drugs Act was amended to make the death penalty mandatory for people caught trafficking a certain amount of drugs (the amount varies depending on the drug). For heroin it is more than 15 grams. Nguyen was convicted of trafficking 396 grams.

    The Arms Offences Act was also later amended to provide a mandatory death sentence for a range of firearms offences.

    The mandatory death penalty for drug offences was introduced because "in the 1960s and 1970s the drug problem in Singapore assumed alarming proportions", according to the judgement in Nguyen's final appeal.

    By comparison, Iranian law provides a mandatory death sentence where the trafficking only involves more than 10 grams of heroin.
