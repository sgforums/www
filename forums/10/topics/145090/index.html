  • BillyBong
    4 Aug 05, 18:29
    We are a long way from matching the rigidity of environmental friendliness in other developed countries, especially in the industrial sector, let alone the recycling bins we place all over SG.

    At the moment, it's all lip movement and hot air. We talk a lot but have not focused into proper and feasible everyday practicalities that will entice the local singaporean to respond favourably. All our ISO 14000, 15000 are words on paper. Many shipping companies blatantly dump their chemical wastes in the sea, factory environments are horrendous with oil seeping into drains and contaminating our lines etc.

    Safety and green environment has not reached a level of awareness needed to educate the average singaporean. They simply cannot believe in threats like global warming and airborne pollution, having never experienced one.

    The difficulty is in convincing this majority which form the nucleus of the working population about the inherent and immediate dangers of damaging the environment. Once this is overcome, the rest is simply laying out a roadmap.

    Take obesity for example. Everyone talked about it but no one did anything concrete. In the last 5 years, the message has finally gotten thru. Now everyone talks about BMI and fitness in schools. Pressure on MOE to ensure kids stay trim and fit is ever increasing.

    Likewise, the environment. Education is the first step. Everything else is doomed to fail.

    Yup I totally agree!

    Recycling bins can scarcely be found in Singapore. There used to be some at Watten Park estate. I'm not sure if they're still there now though. As far as I know, recycling bins are found in schools, and BP stations. Aside from that, recycling bins are a rare sight.

    Compared to other developed countries such as Australia, we, as a nation are definitely guilty of being not being environmentally conscious. In Australia, plastic bags at the supermarkets are given out sparingly. Shoppers are encouraged to buy the reusable bags (which go for 99 cents). The bags are, in fact, quite fashionably made, such that it isn't a stigma to be seen carrying one of those bags. Common garbage bins are separated into those meant for 'cans', 'bottles', and 'paper' material.

    It's high time Singapore did such things too, instead of just shoving down 'Reuse, reduce, recycle' down student's throats when there are seemingly few avenues to encourage such behaviour.

    The problem is economics...the government must financially support recycling efforts to make it attractive. Thirty years ago, I remember we recycle almost everything....bottles, newspaper, old clothes, cardboard etc...we were most enthusiastic because we got a few cents per bottle from the karang-guni man, who came by regularly.
