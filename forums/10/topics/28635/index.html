    The digging of the Kra canal would not be a problem for us at all.

    The reason is simple -- the traffic through the Malacca Straits is much higher than the Kra canal can possibly handle. The canal would only take a small amount of business from us. The Suez and Panama canals are mostly economic failures because the capacity of a canal is not very high, unless you spend terabucks to dig a really wide one. Add to this the enormous cost of running a canal and it is not even clear that Thailand would even benefit from it.

    This is the real reason why no one has actually dug this canal -- there is a significant danger that it will be a money loser. The only reason why they are making noises about it now is that the digging of the canal would instantly create a few hundred thousand jobs, which would be a real boost for the ruling coalition now that unemployment in Thailand is so high. They are also trying to justify the immediate and continuing expense of the canal by saying that they will set up an export processing free trade zone around the canal, which will hopefully turn into an industrial powerhouse. This is nice in theory, but Thailand is lacking in the other factors that would cause such a zone to grow (skilled cheap labor, political stability, infrastructure, etc)

    As it is, Singapore does not handle ALL of the traffic through the Malacca Straits and we never did. For example, we are unable to handle the largest class of oil tankers, so they just all sail right past us and dock at ports that can (such as Klang if I am not wrong). This traffic is a big pie and there is more than enough for everyone to get a piece. As long as we are competitive, we will still attract business.

    About the moves of a few companies to PTP -- it's bad but not as bad as most people think. As it is, we are pushed to beyond maximum capacity, so even after they left, we did not lose that much money. Truth be told, PSA is so popular that we can afford to turn some people away. And Evergreen is already shifting some of its traffic back because it is beginning to realize PTP is cheap -- in every sense of the word

    OK, it is probably unfair to say that the Suez and Panama Canals are failures (the Suez was a success and the Panama Canal eventually became a borderline success, though it bankrupted the original builders). However, a Kra canal is not comparable to these because the distance saved is so small. Most small canals are failures. Search Google for "canal economic failure" and read about the huge number of canal failures over the decades.

    The key to understanding why many canals are economic failures is that it is often more efficient to simply build a port at each end of your planned canal and ship the goods across BY RAIL. There is usually no need for the ship itself to move between the two points, unless it is a naval vessel or specialized ship like an oil tanker. This has become less true with larger and larger merchant vessels though.

    Even for the Suez and Panama Canals, the volume of shipping to ports at the two ends of each canal is much higher than the shipping through the canal itself. In particular, the US itself is an important destination for goods, which was less true when the Panama Canal was built. It is unknown how much of the goods traffic between the bodies of water the canals link these canals actually handle.

    About PTP, yes it is a problem that Maersk and Evergreen left. Of course we need to fight to get them back. No, we should not panic. In the end, PSA is charging what she can afford to charge. Singapore's high price is not just for fun -- it is because we give faster service and better connectivity. While it is true that the premium product usually suffers most in a recession, that does not mean that our strategy is invalid. If anything, we should try for both ends of the market. For example, PSA should buy into PTP if given the chance (as it has done with ports all over the world). In addition, PSA should allow shipping lines a little more autonomy in SIngapore -- which was one of the reasons Evergreen left.

    dkhoo,

    glad you enjoyed the article, amazing what the Straits Times misses out isn't it?

    Agree with your analogy that the Straits of Malacca is overcrowded and the Kra Canal will ease the overcrowding, but my point remains, lets take a very basic example:

    Randomly select a hundred ships that pass by Straits of Malacca today. Say, 70 call into Singapore, 30 sail on by.

    Then factor in the Kra Canal, assume it relieves Straits of Malacca of 50% of traffic, or 50 ships. Best case scenario, the 30 that sail on by Singapore use the Kra, along with 20 ships that would have otherwise come to Singapore, use the Kra. That leaves PSA with 50 ships. Worse case(unlikely), it'll see only 20 of the 70 calling into Singapore.

    Now
    50/70 = 71% or 29% reduction
    20/70 = 28% or 72% reduction

    The real case will likely be somewhere near the best case, but even so, if you look at www.psa.com.sg, Singapore handled 15.52 million TEU last year. A 29% drop in traffic would roughly represent an almost 5 million TEU loss yearly. Can we afford that kind of haemohrrage? To answer that, remember that MSC had a 2 million TEU capacity in SG while Evergreen had only 1 million TEUs. As a country with no resources but a deepwater port that makes transshipment as one of its main source of revenue, I'd say we can't. Not during the best of times, and especially not in times like this. Remember the fact that each lost container doesn't just cause PSA losses?

    Like I've said before, the above example is vastly simplified and makes lots of assumptions, but I think it will suffice in here.

    Of course, its all hypothetical, for the Kra Canal hasn't been, or looks likely to be, built. But it gives the "We'll survive despite the Kra Canal because Singapore is always Numbah One!" camp something to think about. I hope.
