    Board firm against early CPF withdrawal

    About 400 were turned down in the last 2 years. CPF Board says those in financial difficulty can get help from CDCs, MOM

    By Leong Pik Yin

    THE rules are clear, but many still try their luck each year.

    Former oil rig engineer Shahid Hussein, who has not been able to find full-time work for eight years, is among about 400 people who have written to the Central Provident Fund (CPF) Board over the past two years appealing for the early withdrawal of their CPF money - and been turned down.

    UNCHANGED:
    CPF INTEREST RATES
    INTEREST rates for funds held by the Central Provident Fund (CPF) Board will stay unchanged between April 1 and June 30.

    A board statement on Friday said 2.5 per cent will be paid for funds in the Ordinary Account and 4 per cent for deposits in the Medisave, Special and Retirement accounts.

    The rate is higher than the average 0.59 per cent offered by the three big local banks here - DBS, OCBC and United Overseas Bank.

    The CPF Act provides for a minimum CPF interest rate of 2.5 per cent per annum.

    The concessionary interest rate for Housing Board mortgage loans, which is pegged at 0.1 percentage point above the CPF interest rate for the Ordinary Account, will therefore remain unchanged at 2.6 per cent per annum during the same period.

    The rates, reviewed quarterly, will be revised again on July 1.

    The 51-year-old gets by, earning $40 a day when he works as a security guard but the employment is ad hoc.

    The board was not moved by his request to withdraw the $200,000 in his CPF account four years early, despite his circumstances.

    'It's been hard for me to get another job because of my age,' said Mr Shahid, who used to earn $4,200 a month as an engineer, excluding extra bonuses when working offshore on oil rigs.

    'The only reason I've been able to get by is because I'm single and I live in my brother's flat.'

    To add to his woes, he also owes about $5,000 in taxes.

    'I'm totally broke. Why can't I be allowed to take out some of my CPF savings in bad times? It's my hard-earned money.'

    This is the second time Mr Shahid had tried appealing to the CPF Board. He was rejected the first time, a few years ago.

    Last year, he turned to his MPs, Dr Michael Lim and Rear-Admiral Teo Chee Hean, for help. Both wrote to the board on his behalf, to no avail.

    Members are allowed to withdraw their CPF savings only when they reach 55, or under special circumstances, such as physical or mental incapacitation.

    A CPF Board spokesman said that other premature withdrawals are disallowed 'as they go against the primary objective of CPF', which is to help members have enough savings for 'retirement, housing and health-care needs'.

    She added that those who request early CPF withdrawals represent just a 'tiny fraction' of the three million CPF members here.

    While the board understands that some may be facing financial difficulties in this economic climate, the CPF 'must stay focused on these core purposes', she said, pointing out that they could seek financial assistance from community development councils, or get help on employment opportunities from the Manpower Ministry.

    Still, to those like Mr Shahid, saving for the future makes little sense when the present seems so dire.

    'I don't feel I should go around and beg for financial help when I've got $200,000 sitting in my CPF account,' he said.

    MPs, like Dr Teo Ho Pin (Holland Bukit-Panjang GRC), have received their fair share of such pleas for help.

    'I can understand their urge to take out the money. They feel they can't even survive the present, why talk about the future?' said Dr Teo, who received about five such appeals for help last year.

    'But I don't think it's advisable to use up CPF savings. You never know what kind of needs you may have when you're older.

    'I think the key is to find them a job as quickly as possible. Or, if they're unable to work, we can give them financial assistance.'
