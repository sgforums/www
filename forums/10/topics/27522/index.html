    Sniper weapons are specialised guns... they are expensive, not as useful as an assault rifle in close combat (most have low capacity magazines and lack full-auto/burst fire), require good maintenance, are more fragile (Glass can break... as anyone who has used an NVG before knows) and aren't as magic-kill as they appear to be in the movie.

    The long kill-range of sniper weapons mean that they are more affected by wind than shorter ranged weapons. The real sniper doesn't just put crosshairs on the bad guy and fires... He sees movement in the leaves and other wind-sensitive objects to gauge bullet drift and trajectory. He also has to judge the effect of a cold barrel and the relative humidity in the air. Its not easy...

    The sniper needs

    1.) Flawless fieldcraft. (you need to set up a hide with only a sniping partner and stay there for days. Yep... you do your business there, sleep there and eat there.)
    2.) Infinite patience. (targets may not appear, or appear at the wrong time and wrong place after days of waiting.)
    3.) To be very, very fit. (You are in a 2 man team. If you are spotted and engaged by anything you can't take out, your best choice is evasion... run before they get arty on you!)
    4.) Tremendous endurance. (You've been inserted on a hillside to take out a particular division commander. The target will be in sight in 2 hours... but your hide is infested with red ants, and you can't move as there are enemy helicopters around. Guess what you need to do... Endure!)

    Ah... One should never trust movies that much.

    For example, sounds of silenced weapons are almost always wrong (its on purpose so real terrorists won't recognise the actual sound). In Saving Private Ryan, many realism factors were correct... However, the sniper was shown adjusting his scope to counter-snipe the German in the clock-tower... I seriously doubt that a real sniper would mess with his rifle's zero in such a critical situation.
    Also, that sniper appears to be attached to the company the Captain led onto the beach, not to a squad-level unit that the captain ended up leading... although I'm only guessing at that.

    Finally, locating a sniper that has fired is pretty easy with modern tools. A thermal imager can pick out the heat shimmer from the barrel after the shot. Also available are sniper locators, which are field computers designed to judge a sniper's direction from source of noise and distance by measuring the crack/thump-boom (initial firing of the round after the crack/thump of it passing by or hitting someone. Longer the difference between the two sounds, the greater the range of the sniper.) Besides, one can tell the direction of the sniper by examining the body (if within reach and having fallen into cover) and wound. Remember... the sniper isn't moving yet... making him very vulnerable to arty and tank suppression/carpet fire.

    And contrary to popular belief, silenced sniper rifles are either rare or have limited range/killing power/ammunition availability.
