    February 19, 2004
    $2 fee to buy to buy from tuckshop School's cashless system forces pupils to use ez-link in canteen,bookshop

    By Chua Kong Ho

    Would you fork out $24 a year in fees so that you could use an ez-link card to pay for your food at the canteen?

    Perhaps.

    But students at Naval Base Primary School do not have a choice.

    They have to use ez-link cards to pay for their canteen transactions and purchases at the bookshop because their school has gone - cashless.

    The students can opt out of the scheme.

    But the principal's ban on canteen vendors accepting cash means students would then be unable to buy food.

    IT vendor Chowiz Pte Ltd, which developed and manages the Web-based system the canteen vendors use, charges each student $2 a month.

    Naval Base Primary School's principal, Ms Elizabeth Poey, told reporters at the launch of the scheme yesterday that the $2 is deducted from the students' Edusave accounts.

    Both EZ-Link and POSB, which are behind the arrangement, stand to gain from their involvement.

    EZ-Link makes a commission on every transaction made using an ez-link card.

    POSB benefits, as it stands to grow its customer base because, in order to participate in this scheme, students need to have a POSBKids Account

    linked to the ez-link card.

    But the bank, which pioneered mass savings among children here, said that students will gain from this.

    Under the scheme, unspent pocket money can be transferred to the child's POSBKids Account to earn interest.

    This allows children to learn about budgeting and managing their finances from a young age, said POSB managing director Lam Siok Loon.

    Chowiz, POSB and EZ-Link target introducing this scheme at another 40 schools by the end of the year.

    Said Mrs Lam: "Our aim is to open a POSBKids Account for every primary school kid in Singapore.

    POSB currently has more than 300,000 POSBKids Accounts.

    Mr Chan Soo Sen, Minister of State (Education, Community Development and Sports), attending the launch of the scheme yesterday, said: "It is

    heartening to see such collaboration between the private sector and schools as the ultimate beneficiaries are our students."

    Chowiz, too.

    It comes up with an initial outlay of about $40,000 a school to equip it with the necessary Web server, card readers and terminals.

    But it will collect $26,400 from the 1,100 students at Naval Base Primary School, at $2 a student.

    With a target of 40 schools, Chowiz can expect more than $1 million in fees a year.

    Mr Chow Hoo Siong, Chowiz's managing director, said that one in 10 parents initially chose to opt out of the scheme.

    "With any new technology, there will be some who are initially opposed to it, he said, adding that half of those who initially opted out have since rejoined the scheme."

    Mr Chow also said that Chowiz has approached tertiary institutions with the same proposal to go cashless but without success.

    "Tertiary students are older and much

    harder to convince (of the value of the services), he said.

    EZ-Link senior vice-president Nicholas Lee said that he did not expect the schools to be a "primary revenue driver for the loss-making company".

    "This is more about adding value to the ez-link card and to get the schoolchildren used to paying for purchases with their ez-link card, he said.

    EZ-Link, a wholly owned subsidiary of the Land Transport Authority, incurred public wrath when it chose to sell its ez-link cards to members of the public for $5 instead of collecting a refundable deposit.

    It has tried to develop non-transit uses for its contactless cards but has met with little success.

    EZ-Link earns a commission each time someone uses its cards to pay for a purchase.

    Mr Lee declined to reveal how much commission EZ-Link earns per transaction,but it is believed to be around 1.4 per cent of the transaction value.

    He also told Streats yesterday that EZ-Link was planning to interest companies in using its cards as staff passes, which would have functions ranging from building access to keeping track of attendance.

    February 20, 2004
    School absorbs $2 fee for cashless system
    Students still have to pay $2 a month if they opt for online savings portion of the overall programme

    By Chua Kong Ho

    NAVAL Base Primary School is absorbing the $2 monthly fee that allows pupils to use their ez-link cards at the canteen and for other purposes.

    IT firm Chowiz Pte Ltd, which developed and manages the suite of school applications using the ez-link card, clarified yesterday that the costs of subscription are borne not by the students but by the school.

    Ms Emily Lim, business development manager at Chowiz, said its eLifestyle suite of applications allows students to perform functions such as buying food at the canteen using their ez-link card, paying for incidentals such as photocopy costs, logging in their attendance and temperature readings, using their ez-link cards.

    The schoolÂ’s canteen vendors and bookshop operator do not accept cash.

    Students, however, have to pay for the newly launched online savings programme in collaboration with POSB.

    This scheme is an optional module of the overall cashless system.

    The fee is also $2 a month for each student and can be paid for through oneÂ’s Edusave Account because it is considered an enrichment programme, said Ms Lim.

    Naval Base Primary launched the e-savings programme on Wednesday, the first school in Singapore to do so.

    Under the programme, students can select modules from an online curriculum to learn how to budget and manage their finances. They can also transfer unspent pocket money to a POSBKids savings account to earn interest.

    Addressing concerns that some needy students may not be able to afford the $2 monthly fee for the e-savings programme, Ms Lim gave the assurance that Chowiz will waive the $2 fee for pupils on financial assistance schemes.

    "We want to bring the benefits of technology to every student," she said.

    She also stressed that the e-savings programme was an optional one. Those who do not wish to take the programme can still use their ez-link cards to perform other functions under the basic suite, such as making purchases at the canteen or bookshop.

    ChowizÂ’s clarification comes as a relief for many parents, who were worried that they would be burdened with even more fees when more schools adopt the programme. The company aims to introduce the e-savings plan at 40 schools by the end of this year.

    But even though parents or students do not have to pay for their schools to go cashless, many still want the option of using cash at schools.

    Mr Jamsari Ahmad, 38, a systems manager and father of a four-year-old, suggested that students should be given a choice of using cash or their ez-link cards. He pointed out that incentives could be used to encourage pupils to adopt the contactless card payment, instead of using a blanket ban.

    He cited the example of commuters paying lower fares for using their ez-link cards on buses and the MRT than when they pay in cash.

    Some parents also expressed concern that the young will not know how to handle real money as schools go increasingly "cashless".

    Said Mr Jamsari: "How will my child know how to distinguish between a 10-cent coin and a 50-cent coin if he doesnÂ’t handle cash in school?"

    Others questioned the wisdom of paying $24 a year to participate in an online savings scheme. They pointed that the interest accrued may not be sufficient to cover the cost of joining the scheme.

    Said Mr Ho Chee Meng, 36, an account manager: "At the current level of interest rates offered in Singapore, the children will have to save thousands of dollars to offset the yearly fee of $24 (for the e-savings programme)."

    Chowiz said that the fee should be seen as paying for the curriculum content and not just for the savings transfer alone.

    There are those who support Naval Base PrimaryÂ’s decision to launch the e-savings programme.

    Mr Michael Choy said: "It is important for us to understand that the school has taken a futuristic approach to helping their students adopt the right mindset to 21st century financial transactions, the age of the cashless society."

    He added: "We should look beyond the administrative fees to see how what is done today in the school would benefit the students tomorrow."
