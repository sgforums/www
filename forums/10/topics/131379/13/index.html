    Originally posted by pikamaster:
    there must be some mechanism that causes a limited number of appeals to be present; the question is how is this achieved?
    All capital punishment cases go to appeal upon conviction automatically. I think your question is why the number of successful appeals is so low. The reason, I think, is the nature of the legal process. You can only appeal on point of law and if the High Court judge has ruled correctly based on the law, you will lose the appeal. As I have mentioned before, the problem is that during the investigation and trial, the prosecution has all the advantage. They can detain the suspect for long periods, interrogate them without the presence of the defence lawyer and have access to all the forensic experts. By the time you go to trial, the defence team has very little to support its case whilst the prosecution has built up a formidable case against the accused. This is made worse by the fact that our courts are so efficient, cases go to hearing so quickly that the busy defence lawyer often does not have enough time to do a good job at preparation.

    You must understand that in an adversarial system, the judge can only decide based on which side gives a more convincing argument of the case. If the prosecution has all the advantage, chances are the prosecution will win the argument. To be fair, if the prosecution finds out in the course of their investigation that the accused is innocent, they will drop the case before going to court.

    4) You said, "...so it is wrong theoretically for the majority to bully the minority into conforming to their view..." But when we are dealing with an issue like this, when the question is whether we should retain the death sentence or not, there can be only one answer, 'yes' or 'no'. If society is divided in its opinion but with the majority insisting that it should stay, can be it right to ignore its wishes? Surely in a democratic system, the way to resolve such a dispute is to put it to the vote and that majority rules.

    One way to know how the population feels about this issue is a referendum, which our government is always against (for reasons I do not agree with). Another way is to support opposition parties who campaign against capital punishments in the General Election. We may never be sure how Singaporeans will vote on this, short of a referendum; but judging from coffee-shop talk and the media reports after the Huang Na case, I don't think you will have much public support.

    5) Your other question, ".. what makes you 100% sure that a guilty person once released, will commit the same crime again?"

    I don't think that is the reason for retaining the death sentence. It is not fear that the criminal will re-offend that we put someone to death. I think the main justification for capital punishment is the belief that there must be retribution for wrongs done. Kind of karma: whatsoever you sow, you shall reap....an eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth. It is what drives the father of a murdered daughter to insist on witnessing the hanging of the murderer with his own eyes. Without that, he cannot find closure and society believes that he should not be denied that satisfaction. In some societies and cultures, if the State does not perform this duty adequately, the relatives of a victim will feel a need to avenge the wrongs done by vigilante action.

    Finally, does it mean that if Singaporeans are not sufficiently educated about an issue, we should decide what is right for them, instead of allowing them to decide for themselves? That sounds very much like the paternalistic approach of our government.
