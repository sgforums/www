    There are more than one reason for capital punishment.

    It is a deterrent to some extent but no punishment will ever be able to completely deter the criminal. In this regard I do agree that the death sentence only takes out one criminal from circulation but does not necessarily stop another from committing the same crime. It is difficult to know how many are deterred because of capital punishment because you never know what would have been the incidence if the law were not in place. Comparison with other cities is the best guide we have but no two cities or cultures are exactly alike.

    There is not much mention so far about the need for victims (or the State as the broader representative) for vengeance. Since Hammurabi gave his famous law code, many societies have felt a need to see retribution for wrongs done. "An eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth" may be odious to radical leftists but it is for the society to decide if that principle is past its time. So whilst Europe and some US states have abolished capital punishments, other US states strongly believe it should remain. I believe that Singaporean society at the moment wants the death sentence to stay. Each time a hidious crime like the Huang Na case occurs, we read from the media of the populace baying for blood. The government is responding to public sentiments on such matters.

    What I disagree with is the process. In the Huang Na case, the accused was not allowed to see his lawyer during his interrogation despite repeated applications by his lawyer. The reason given was that the crime was heinous but I fail to see what that has to do with the proper administration of justice. Especially when we do have capital punishment, an accused person should have every safeguard against a wrongful conviction, which is quite possible in our adversarial system. However, there was no hue and cry over this although it had been widely reported. In many western jurisdictions, his evidence taken without the presence of a lawyer would have been inadmissible. Why is that so? Perhaps because Singaporeans are so inflamed by the crime that they are willing to look away....

    So forget about abolishing the death sentence because the population still supports it. We should reform our police investigation and legal process, if we can....

    Pikamaster,

    Well you should have phrased your questions clearly right from the start then.

    Legal fees vary from case to case and I am not sure if your question relates to the prosecution or the defense. A defendant like OJ Simpson obviously paid more for his homicide case compared to a homeless charged with murdering a prostitute. The expenses incurred by the UK government for investigating and prosecuting a homicide is around a million pounds, according to figures released on BBC recently.

    As to the other questions, I agree with your apprehension. As I said, I disagree with the process of convicting and executing a prisoner on death row. We do need to reform the current process:

    1. The principle of 'innocent till proven guilty' is enshrined in our legal system which we have inherted from the British but even that is being challenged in these days of terrorist attacks. You know, of course, that we have the ISA and UK has similar legislation in Northern Ireland which allows for imprisonment without trial. Back to Singapore, no one is executed till proven guilty but yes, I agree there is a sublime pressure to rush through the trial and appeal process. That is my concern too.

    2. That problem is true everywhere. The world is not fair, I am afraid. If another black person were charged with the same crime under the same circumstances like OJ Simpson, would he be able to hire the same defense team and would he be able to get away? I doubt it.

    3. I agree totally. That is what I said previously. We need to reform our legal process to provide greater safeguards against a miscarriage of justice.

    4. I don't know what you mean. Nobody 'regulates' the success of appeals. An appeal to the Court of appeal is automatic in capital punishment cases and has to be based on point of law. If the defense lawyer cannot give a good reason for the appeal, it fails. I do not believe there is political interference with the decision of the court in individual cases. And please don't put words in my mouth. I observed the low rate of success at the appeal court but certainly I have NEVER alleged that it is due to some manipulation by the government.

    I don't think you can fault the Appeal Court either. The problem is that our adversarial system is rather biased towards the prosecution in that all the forensic experts in the country appear for the State. They are not allowed to share their evidence with the defense until much later when the trial has started. As in the Huang Na case, the lawyer cannot be present at the interrogation and is not even allowed to see his client until the police has extracted every piece of incriminating evidence. By the time the case goes to trial, the accused has already admitted his guilt on so many counts that all a lawyer can do often is just plead on mitigating circumstances. The defense lawyer has the odds against him and it is difficult to make a convincing argument at the trial and subsequent appeal. So the Appeal Court rightly dismisses the case so often.

    Economics is not the reason for having the death sentence, although it certainly features in the debate. As I see it, the reason it is not the time to abolish capital punishment is mainly because our population supports it, especially after a particularly heinous crime like the dismembered body case. Instead I think we need to reform our legal process to provide more safeguards. With more safeguards against a wrongful conviction, however, it may mean that we might let the occasional guilty go free because no legal process is perfect.

    At the end of the day we need to strike this balance: would you rather let a guilty person go free or an innocent person be wrongly convicted? That is a question Singaporeans must answer for themselves and not by any foreign organizations.
