    .
    In a nationally-televised press conference yesterday, Mr Murakami admitted that the source of his information was none other than Takafumi Horie, the 33-year-old chief executive of Livedoor who used to be the poster boy of new Japanese business until he was charged with breaking securities laws.
    .
    Mr Murakami said Horie had told him of his plans to take over NBS in late 2004. His fund then started buying NBS shares, which surged once Livedoor launched its takeover bid in February 2005.
    .
    Mr Murakami made a neat profit by selling the shares his fund had accumulated to Livedoor and other market investors.
    .
    Trying to defend himself yesterday, Mr Murakami claimed: "I didn't think Livedoor had the wherewithal to actually go through with it. I didn't buy (NBS) shares because of my conversation, but it's true that I heard what I heard."
    .
    Livedoor eventually withdrew its bid, but Mr Murakami said that he continued discussing business matters, including NBS, with Horie.
    .
    "We saw each other at the gym, so of course we chatted," he said.
    .
    Both he and Horie had become cult figures for their attempts to shake up Japan's somewhat conservative business environment, where the interests of the management sometimes took precedence over those of the shareholders.
    .
    A defiant Mr Murakami, who famously made his first equity investment at the age of nine and has been using his stakes in various companies to ask them tough questions, said that Japan was hostile to those who challenged the system.
    .
    "Don't you think this country tends to kick out people who challenge?" he asked. "I may not be qualified to say this since I have done something illegal, but I hope that Japan will one day become a country that accepts people who challenge."
    .
    Mr Murakami added that he would resign from his fund, now headquartered in Singapore, but that it would continue to operate without him.
    .
    Asked to comment on this, a spokesperson for the Monetary Authority of Singapore said that MAC Asset Management was an exempt fund manager which undertook fund management activity in Singapore as well as an exempt financial adviser.
    .
    "In Singapore, if an exempt fund manager or exempt financial adviser or any of its representatives are found not to be fit and proper, MAS can take appropriate regulatory action," the spokesperson added.
    .
    Officers at MAC Asset Managment's Singapore office refused to comment.
    .
    Mr Murakami, meanwhile, said that only a third of the fund's money has been invested, with the rest sitting on its books as cash.

    It is not a matter of refusing to upgrade one's skill at the age of 40 PLUS - but a matter of how one can tap a person's experience, skill and talent to allow that person the shortest learning curve.

    Should we count a Bank Manager's - or Doctor's - past experience to be irrelevant, and dump all his past training and accumulated experience to have him learn an entirely new trade ?

    Why do we insist on these specialists aborting their rich past, and start Life in a fresh shell ?

    If these experienced individual have no financial strength nor organisation contacts, why do we leave individuals to their own ends ?

    Instead of making useless calls and recriminations, why could we not set up a national organisation to absorb all such talents, and deploy them into our neighboring Third World Countries to advance their society ?

    This can help our retrenched professionals to regain their self-worth, expose them into new territories and new opportunities, advance our own National Prestige and diplomacy, and nurture new business opportunities for all sides.
