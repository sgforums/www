    Govt can keep land it got for $4.2m: Court

    Developer had sought return of land acquired in 1983 and now worth an estimated $100m

    (SINGAPORE) The government does not have to return a $100 million plot of land to a property developer for the $4.2 million it paid the company to acquire the site in 1983, the Court of Appeal has ruled.

    Teng Fuh Holdings claimed it had suffered a great injustice because nothing had been done with the land since the government acquired it some 24 years ago.

    Teng Fuh also said the land was zoned industrial when it was acquired but re-zoned residential in the 1993 Kallang Development Guide Plan, showing bad faith.

    The developer said it did not know about the re-zoning until 2004 when it first appealed for the land to be returned.

    The land, acquired for general redevelopment, is at 20-22 Geylang Road. It is now estimated to be worth about $100 million.

    Teng Fuh was awarded $4.2 million compensation based on the market value of the land in 1973 - 10 years before the actual transaction. Teng Fuh has remained a tenant, paying rent of more than $21,000 a month. It is estimated to have paid more than $5.6 million in rent over the years.

    The Appeal Court dismissed Teng Fuh's application on Friday, saying it was out of time. The application should have been made six months from the time it was declared in 1983 that the land was to be acquired for a public purpose, the court said.

    Teng Fuh also did not account for the delay as it had 'the interest, the knowledge and the means to have acquired the information' to make its application 'long before' doing so in September 2005, the court said.

    The re-zoning of the land under the 1993 guide plan was public information and accessible to anyone who wanted to know, it said. As Teng Fuh is 'actively engaged in property development', it 'should be aware' that land use zoning is 'liable to change' when 'land usage needs and planning policies change'.

    The court said that had the application been made in time, Teng Fuh's action would have 'merited serious consideration'.

    The court noted that the Urban Redevelopment Authority had not explained why the land was not redeveloped or why it was re-zoned. The collector of land revenue could have done more to explain what the land was intended for when it was acquired, when this was intended to take place, whether there were any subsequent changes to the plans, and if so, what the current plans are.

    This prolonged inaction, if not explained, could constitute an arguable case of reasonable suspicion that the land was not needed for general redevelopment when acquired in 1983, the court said.

    Teng Fuh was represented by Tan Kay Kheng and Aw Charmian of Wong Partnership.
