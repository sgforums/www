Are we world class as claimed by our leaders?

  • robertteh
    3 Aug 05, 23:14
    If we cannot produce competitive world-class products with names like Toyota, Hyundai, Samsung, Tatong, or Nokia but keep insisting we have world-class this or that we will get the end feel very empty.

    It is very clear that to build a really world-class country we cannot just make claims about leadership or talents or about our world-class this or that, we should be entrepreneurs offering many alternative solutions that presumptuous leadership or all kinds of look-good forms. We need to build our substance. However now our political culture is very lopsided in favor of those who are called elites. Any ones who are not elites will be deemed a threat and sidelined.

    For example, we have a non-elite Sim Wong Hoo who has made it but he will be frown on as a kind of smart alec not in the class of academic elites as our leaders.

    No matter how entrepreneurial and how successfully he has created world products with practical applications and consistent management philosophy like "Creating Huge Excessive Advantages Tactics", he and his types will not be regarded as equals to the elites.

    The hugely competitive Taiwanese Computer entrepreneurs have done the same with CHEAT by creating compatible industrial standards for the Taiwanese PCs which finally broke the stranglehold of the invincible IBM standards. Singapore lacks practical technology applicators and entrepreneurs who can think out of the box like these Taiwanese PC Entrepreneurs. The present leaders are too concerned with image and self-importance and we are left behind technologically because of such pride or face and we are not paying attention in applying technologies from schools being overly concerned with paper chase or look-good examination results.

    I believe that in order to be worth being called leaders and talents or elites we must change our current political philosophy to include all with practical abilities and cut out all the rhetorics emphasising more on practical abilities and results.

    The immediate task should be to build a new team to take Singapore higher to meet the next challenges ahead in the real world we need to cut out all the pretentious look-good approaches in managing the economy and get more action types of people to start up new technology.

    There is a very sorry negative political culture here deeply ingrained in looking good while avoiding accountability and liability for mistakes. Sim's "No U-Turn Sydrome" will help the present leaders to be more accountable and transparent and be willing to accept responsibility for mistakes without all the U-turns.
