    The origin of surname Zhu (same as that of the Ming Dynasty's founding emperor) came from the Cao clan which claimed to be descendants of the legendary emperor Zhuanxu. History has it that King Wu (Zhou Wuwang) had bestowed the states of Cao and Zhu (with the ear radical) to two factions of the clan in the Zhou Dynasty. During the Warring States period, the weak state of Zhu was crushed by a much stronger state of Chu and the surviving clan members changed their surname to Zhu (without the ear radical).

    The third surname on the list, Sun is one of the most popular surnames throughout the entire history of China. One source comes from King Wu's (Zhou Wuwang) half-brother in the Zhou Dynasty whose descendant adopted the style name (zi) of his grandfather -- Sun as his surname. Another comes from the descendants of honest official Bi Gan who was also a prince by lineage in the Shang Dynasty. Bi was killed by King Zhou (Shang Zhouwang) and his clan members, fearing persecution, fled and were scattered throughout the different states. To conceal their identities from enemies while reminding themselves of their common royal lineage, they all agreed to change their surnames to Sun (character meaning grandchild).

    There are multiple origins for most surnames, and Bi Gan's son was but one for the surname Lin. According to Chinese genealogical records, there are two other main origins, one from King Ping's (Zhou Pingwang) second son Lin Kai born of a concubine in the Eastern Zhou Dynasty, another from a minority Xianbei tribe whose surnames were summarily changed during one nation-wide surname-changing campaign initiated by King Xiaowen (Xiaowen Di) in the Northern Wei Dynasty.

    During the Warring States period, Lin Gao from Bi Gan's lineage served as the Premier for the state of Zhao. He had nine sons who served the Zhao king faithfully, and the king bestowed upon Lin's sons the title of "The Nine Dragons". That's about the closest I could find about the Lin's in the Qin Dynasty. It could be a princely title at that time, but I am not able to verify that information from my sources.

    Originally posted by suntze:
    Do you have any info why northerners usually refer to themselves as Han ren and the southerners, ie Cantonese and Hokkiens, as Tang ren?
    No problem. Glad to help a fellow enthusiast in Chinese history.

    For the uninitiated, the reason why the Chinese race usually refer to themselves as descendants of "Han" or "Tang" was because the Han and Tang Dynasties were two of the most glorious periods in Chinese history when the country of the Central Plains or China was strong, powerful, abundant, and vibrant with culture.

    "Nan Tang Bei Han"... If my memory served me right, this distinction originated during the dying years of the Tang Dynasty when the northern region of China was actually divided among several autonomous tribe nations like Liao, Xixia and Jin. (Readers of Jin Yong's pugilistic novels should be familiar with these tribe nations.)

    One remarkable characteristic of the Chinese culture was that the roots were so deep and the values were so strong that the culture was able to exercise considerable amount of influence on the foreign tribal nations that occupied the land. These nations took to the Han language, rites and government structure naturally during their occupation of the northern regions, and the citizens there referred to themselves as "Han ren (citizens)".

    On the other hand, most citizens claiming Han roots (though actually the Han bloodline had already been pretty much corrupted by that time) moved with the capital to the south, and called themselves "Tang ren (citizens)" to differentiate themselves from the Han citizens of the tribe nations up north. Hence the distinction was born, but it has slowly been blurred as the centuries rolled by through the subsequent dynasties.

    An interesting point of noteworthy mention is that many southern citizens, especially those in the Guangdong and Fujian provinces, still retain the tradition of calling themselves "Tang ren". At the end of the Qing Dynasty when many of these citizens emigrated to foreign countries and established communal enclaves there, they named these Chinatown enclaves as "Tang ren jie (street)" or "Tang cheng (city)".

    Huang (representing yellow), another popular surname in Chinese history, shared similar origins with the surname Qin in the Ying clan. As mentioned for surname Qin, the Ying clan's origin could be traced to a noble who served the legendary emperor Shun. A faction of the Ying clan founded the state of Huang when King Wu (Zhou Wuwang) ascended the throne in the early years of the Zhou Dynasty. When the king of Huang offended the powerful state of Chu by refusing to offer tributes, Chu attacked and annexed Huang, and the surviving clan members subsequently adopted the surname of Huang.
