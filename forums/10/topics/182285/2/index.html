    In English law, any disobedience or disrespect to the authority or privileges of a legislative body, or interference with the administration of a court of justice constitutes contempt of court. We cannot allow any litigant who is unhappy with the judgment of the court to cast aspersions upon the court, for that would shake the very foundation of civil society.

    Sir Eardley-Wilmot, the Lord Chief Justice of common pleas, is widely regarded to have laid down the law on this issue:

    "To be impartial, and to be universally thought so, are both absolutely necessary for the giving justice, that free, open and uninterrupted current which it has for many ages found all over this kingdom, and which so eminently distinguishes and exalts it above all nations upon the earth. Again, the constitution has provided very apt and proper remedies for correcting and rectifying the involuntary mistakes of judges, and for punishing and removing them for any perversion of justice. But if their authority is to be trampled on by pamphleteers and news-writers, and the people are to be told that the power given to the judges for their protection is prostituted to their destruction, the court may retain its power some little time, but I ant sure it will eventually lose all its authority.

    The object of the discipline enforced by the court by proceedings for contempt of court is not now, if it ever was, to vindicate the personal dignity of the judges or to protect them from insult as individuals, but to vindicate the dignity and authority of the court itself and to prevent acts tending to obstruct the due course of justice..."

    There are 2 types of judges:

    Subordinate Courts Judges (Magistrates, District Court, Coroner's Court, Family Court, Juvenile Court)
    These judges are appointed by the President upon the recommendation of the Chief Justice. They may be promoted within the ranks of the Subordinate Courts (Magistrate -> District Judge -> Senior District Judge) and may be removed at any time, just like any other civil servant.

    Supreme Court Justices (High Court, Court of Appeal)
    These people are appointed by the President upon the recommendation of the Prime Minister. They CANNOT be removed from office before their retirement age. In order to remove a sitting Supreme Court Justice, the government will have to pass an Act in Parliament and must fully justify the reasons for the removal to the members of parliament.
