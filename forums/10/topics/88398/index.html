Backups will allow business as usual in case of attack

    WASHINGTON - If terrorists were to strike major US financial centres, the business world would be jarred, but likely not crippled, say industry officials.

    The Sept 11, 2001 attacks caused the longest closure of the stock markets since the 1930s, but financial institutions have since installed sophisticated backup computer systems and far more elaborate business continuity plans.

    A new assault would disrupt trading only briefly, industry leaders believed.

    'In the past, Wall Street has had backup plans in theory, but they were somewhat dusty,' said Ms Bethany Sherman, spokesman for Nasdaq Stock Market, the largest electronic US stock exchange.

    Today, 'all of our major systems are redundant, and in some cases we have three backups'.

    For example, New York-based Nasdaq has a data centre in Connecticut and another backup centre in Maryland.

    The exchange has boosted its computer power enough to handle the trades for every stock listed on all US exchanges, Ms Sherman said.

    Even if the electricity failed all along the East Coast, the data centres have several backup generators and enough diesel fuel to keep systems humming for more than a week,     she said.

    Industry officials had been double-checking their business-continuity plans since Sunday, when Homeland Security Secretary Tom Ridge announced 'new and unusually specific information' about possible Al-Qaeda plans to use car and truck bombs to attack financial institutions.

    On Tuesday, Mr Ridge met New York Mayor Michael Bloomberg, Governor George Pataki and financial executives in Manhattan.

    He praised the industry for its 'numerous drills and exercises to test backup systems'.

    Industry executives said an attack should not disrupt services to customers.

    'We feel confident that if there were a (terrorist attack) at any particular location, our business continuation plan would take over and it would be business as usual,' Prudential spokesman Laurita Warren said.

    Mr John Hall, a spokesman for the American Bankers Association, said bank customers had little to fear from a terrorist attack because all banks were required by regulators to have disaster plans and backups systems in place.

    'Banks have a proven record of disaster recovery,' he said. They routinely faced threats from natural disasters, such as fires, earthquakes and hurricanes, so they were well prepared to recover from a terrorist act.

    Financial institutions also were stepping up physical security measures. In Washington, traffic near the the World Bank and International Monetary Fund backed up for several blocks in the morning because of street closures and checkpoints where guards used detectors on vehicles entering carparks. -- LAT-WP
