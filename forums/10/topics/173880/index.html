    Fret not, estate duty is most moral of taxes
    Jan 11, 2006
    http://straitstimes.asia1.com.sg/forum/story/0,5562,364195,00.html?

    THE Forum has recently been filled with letters arguing the case against estate duty and often questioning its very morality.
    It is time to air the counterargument. Many feel that estate duty is, in fact, the most moral of taxes. The individual himself is not affected by the tax, by definition - he is dead when it is levied. Thus its impact during the wealth-development phase of an individual's life is less distorting than other forms of taxation.
    The development of wealthy dynastic elites carries with it the risk that meritocracy will degenerate into oligarchy as the elites define and control the measures of merit.
    In modern society this is educational attainment, and the potential problem of the best schools becoming dominated by a narrower, wealthier segment of society than is desirable is an issue already well recognised and debated in Singapore.
    Singapore actually has a reasonable balance between the normal desire of people to pass wealth to their descendants and the claims of society over its future shape and structure. This is particularly so in the context of a top marginal tax rate of 22 per cent.
    Most Asian cultures place a far greater importance on securing wealth for future generations of the family than most Western cultures but it is always worth bearing in mind the words of Andrew Carnegie, one of the leading US 'Robber Barons' of the late 19th century: 'The man who dies wealthy dies disgraced.'
    Michael Coleman
