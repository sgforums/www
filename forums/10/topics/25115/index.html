    More bucks for the bang

    Is South-east Asia moving towards an arms race? BHAGYASHREE GAREKAR examines
    the recent patterns of arms acquisitions in the region for answers

    ARMS deals in South-East Asia are making headlines once again as the region
    surfaces from the 1997 economic crisis that shrivelled defence funding.

    As the ink dries on multi-million dollar equipment deals, the buyers describe
    them as long-overdue military modernisation exercises. No, it is an arms race,
    say others, fearing that the new procurements would escalate tension in the
    region.

    In Indonesia, Thailand and the Philippines - where the budgets for arms
    spending are still tight - the weapon wishlists keep growing longer. But these
    countries do not have the bucks for the bang.

    But on the other hand, both Malaysia and Singapore have placed sizeable orders
    for sophisticated equipment.

    For Malaysia, even weightier deals are still in the works. In its largest
    defence procurement exercise in decades, it ordered more than RM2 billion (S$958
    million) worth of military equipment this month.

    Among them are air defence systems from Russia and Britain, Polish combat
    tanks, Pakistani anti-tank rockets, mobile military bridges and assault rifles.

    It is also eyeing new fighter aircraft - possibly the US FA 18 Super Hornets or
    Russian Su-30 - and French Scorpene submarines.

    In the trail of the deals which created ripples across the region, Malaysian
    Defence Minister Datuk Seri Najib Tun Razak explained: 'Malaysia is only trying
    to catch up with the world because our spending was deferred during the crisis.'

    International defence analyst Christopher Foss of Jane's Defence agrees with
    this assessment.

    'Much of the arms purchases are clearly a case of replacing obsolete technology
    and old vehicles and aircraft,' he told Sunday Review recently.

    However, the US-based global intelligence provider Strategic Forecasting
    (Stratfor) said the big ticket items in the deals revealed strategic motives
    beyond modernisation.

    It said the crisis-induced five-year lull in spending does not wholly explain
    the 'sheer scope and scale' of the recent purchases.

    They 'suggest preparations for countering both insurgencies and full state
    threats'.

    In similar vein, Dr Andrew Tan, security analyst at Singapore's Institute of
    Defence and Strategic Studies (IDSS), told Sunday Review: 'It's certainly not
    mere maintenance of status quo. The deals are not just for replacement of
    equipment but acquisition of new capabilities.

    'Malaysia did not have a main battle tank or an SU-30 or a submarine which they
    now plan to acquire.'

    But he pointed out that inferences that Kuala Lumpur's procurement is meant to
    match Singapore's recent and planned buys - submarines, helicopters, frigates
    and fighter planes - are premature.

    For one, most weapons being acquired were along expected lines, he said. Tanks
    have long been on its list and as for submarines, Malaysians have been training
    in submarine operations for over 10 years.

    'The plan to buy a second squadron of MIG-29s has been on hold for five years
    and if they're now looking at SU-30s instead, it's probably because they are
    better value for money,' Dr Tan added, remarking that the end of the Cold War
    and the shake-up in the global arms business had brought down prices.

    In addition, he said: 'Malaysia does have legitimate defence needs. They have a
    sprawling country to defend and they have territorial disputes with Thailand,
    Indonesia, Brunei and the Philippines.

    Of course, Singapore does matter, he commented, saying: 'Malaysia does not want
    to be left behind and there's also the prestige factor. But it is not as though
    Malaysia perceives a dire security threat from Singapore. This is not an arms
    race.'

    An arms race, he said, would presume that the two nations are mutually
    antagonistic and an arms purchase by one was matched quickly by the other.

    That is not true of the Malaysia-Singapore relationship, he said, which
    consists mostly of normal social, political and economic interaction.

    So what would account for Malaysia's undeniably big arms drive?

    Datuk Seri Najib said the purchases were targeted at defending the country in
    aerial combat, which he called the single-biggest threat in modern warfare.

    But the acquisition of Russian, European and Pakistani anti-aircraft systems
    are not connected to Singapore, according to Mr M. G. Mahmud, a Kuala
    Lumpur-based defence analyst with the Asian Defence Journal.

    He told Sunday Review: 'Malaysia's purchase of anti-aircraft missiles is not
    directly related as such to Singapore. We have never really had an effective
    anti-aircraft system.'

    Emphasising that neighbours should not be concerned, Datuk Seri Najib said
    Malaysia spends only 2.5 per cent of the gross domestic product (GDP) on
    defence, while Singapore spends between 5 and 6 per cent.

    But in the eyes of a Malaysian social activist, Singapore has been made a bogey
    to justify arms purchases.

    The Malaysian media had been playing up the issue of Singapore's reclamation
    works in the Johor Straits and its self-sufficiency in water, noted Mr Kua Kia
    Soong of human rights group Suaram.

    'It is very clear that this Singapore bogey is there to justify the arms deal,'
    he told the BBC recently.

    In Stratfor's estimation, however, 'rivalries with Singapore, which itself is
    heavily armed with modern military equipment, are unlikely to escalate into
    conventional war'.

    Another possible reason for the surge in arms purchases is Malaysia's desire to
    be the leading Asean country.

    'Malaysian leaders want to expand the country's regional influence and a strong
    military would add weight to Malaysia's economic strength and political
    independence,' Stratfor noted.

    It said some of Malaysia's purchase plans appear out of place given that the
    main security threats are posed by immigrant labourers and religious, ethnic or
    political violence.

    Signs are that the country is instead preparing for 'long-term contingencies',
    it said.

    These could be in the form of unrest at its border with Thailand's
    Muslim-majority southern provinces, it said. Or worse, a possible disintegration
    of Indonesia. Malaysian defence planners think that if Jakarta loses control
    over Borneo in particular, Malaysia must be prepared to act pre-emptively.

    It should also be able to fend off aggressors or refugees from other areas,
    especially Sumatra.

    On its part, Indonesia has stayed unconcerned over Malaysia's weapons
    expansion.

    'Indonesia doesn't see Malaysia as a threat. If they had, then they would have
    expanded their weapons purchases before the financial crisis when both Malaysia
    and Singapore launched a major expansion,' analyst Bob Lowry told Sunday Review.

    At less than 1 per cent of the GDP, the country has one of the lowest military
    budgets in Asia at US$1 billion (S$1.8 billion), although analysts in Jakarta
    said that about 70 per cent of actual spending of US$3 billion comes from the
    military's network of legal and illegal businesses.

    The funds crunch has delayed replacement of equipment. 'They are down to their
    bare bones, they have no money to spend on new equipment. They are flat out
    maintaining capability,' said another analyst.

    Because of the US defence embargo since early 2000, the plan was for deals with
    Russia and China, which offer cheaper equipment. But even this proved
    unaffordable.

    The strain is showing. For the lack of functioning transport planes, troops
    cannot be deployed quickly to conflict areas. In Aceh, where at least 30,000
    troops are deployed in counter-insurgency operations, soldiers complain they
    lack boots and personal equipment.

    Unlike Indonesia, Thailand has viewed Malaysia's arms purchases with some
    concern but does not see the region in an arms race.

    'Not yet, but it is close to one. Right now, countries are acquiring new
    capabilities like electronic warfare, intelligence equipment to extend their
    operational capability beyond their own borders,' said Dr Panitan Wattanayagorn,
    a leading defence expert at Chulalongkorn University.

    Thailand would like to match its neighbours but faces funds constraint - the
    military budget is about 1.4 per cent of the GDP compared to 4.5 per cent a
    decade ago.

    Major-General Palangkoon Klaharn, the defence ministry's deputy spokesman,
    said: 'When Malaysia and Myanmar bought Russian MIG aircraft recently, we did
    not follow.

    'We must accept that Asean neighbours have minor problems on and off, like
    Thailand and Malaysia have over border issues. But war is not the option.'

    Dr Panitan said: 'In the short term, Thailand's security threats come from the
    Myanmar border, where drug and weapon smuggling is on the rise. It is also
    concerned about the security of its sea lanes. In the long term, it is the
    potential threat posed by emerging superpowers like China, India and Japan.'

    But there are no plans for a major order of military hardware in the next five
    years, Army commander General Surayudh Chulanont has said.

    Instead, it would emphasise maintenance and modification of existing weaponry.
    To cut costs, the army is modifying 40 personnel carriers into armoured carriers
    using local contractors.

    Last week, it accepted delivery of three Blackhawk helicopters bought using
    last year's budget to replace its ageing Bell units.

    Before the 1997 crisis, the navy had been beefing up with new frigates and an
    aircraft carrier but its recent request for a submarine was rejected by the
    government.

    But perhaps the most hard-up are the Philippines armed forces, which are known
    to be the most ill-equipped in the region.

    For a time, the Philippine Air Force's aircraft were so decrepit that they were
    described as 'flying coffins or widow-makers'. Naval vessels and army equipment
    break down frequently.

    'When we drew up the estimates for the modernisation programme in 1995, the
    peso-dollar exchange rate was only 26 to a dollar. Now, it's 51-52 to a dollar.
    Our estimates not only have to be revised; we need more money to upgrade our
    fighting capability,' remarked a military official.

    But money is hard to come by with the defence budget of 46.2 billion pesos
    (S$1.6 billion) capped to about 4 per cent of the GDP and a separate
    modernisation programme of 5 billion pesos for 2002.

    The promise of equipment and at least US$100 million in aid from the US has yet
    to materialise.

    The Asia-Pacific region, along with the Middle East, is the world's premier
    arms market and the latest rounds of acquisitions can be viewed as
    modernisation. But that is not as innocuous as it sounds.

    'Modernisation means it's not the maintenance of status quo, so mutual
    suspicions can get reinforced,' said Dr Tan of IDSS.

    'And these need to be allayed by bilateral and multilateral confidence-building
    measures and diplomatic initiatives.'

    WITH REPORTS FROM: Edward Tang in Bangkok, Leslie Lau in Kuala Lumpur, Luz
    Baguioro in Manila and Marianne Kearney in Jakarta

    Beating drums of S'pore-Malaysia war 'is insanity'
    by Brendan Pereira

    KUALA LUMPUR - Singapore will continue to provoke Malaysia and create the
    impression that it is strong militarily and ready to wage war, but Malaysians
    must not behave hysterically, a newspaper said yesterday.

    A senior editor of the Mingguan Malaysia, writing in the column that uses the
    pen name Awang Sulung, noted that following the uproar over the reclamation of
    land off Pulau Tekong, noises are being made about going to war with Singapore.
    This time, it is a small group of Malaysians who are beating the drums of war,
    he noted.

    'These people who do not have a complete understanding of the problems at hand
    want the government to stop the supply of water and take confrontational
    action,' he said.

    The columnist said that it was an open secret that if Malaysia cut off the
    water supply to the Republic, Singapore would use its air power to attack Johor,
    try and wrest control of the water-treatment plants and occupy the state.

    'Many Malaysians talk about this. They also like to talk about how strong
    Singapore's military is. In reality, we are in a position where we can face
    Singapore head-on.'

    But there would be little benefit in going to war - both sides will end up as
    losers. Also, he did not believe that Singaporeans would support going to war.

    'Kuan Yew has given them too good a life. Worldly pleasures are everything for
    them. Are they willing to lose everything in a war?'

    The Malay-language newspaper which often mirrors official thinking also
    reproduced an open letter by Awang Sulung to Senior Minister Lee Kuan Yew.

    It was published in September last year after Mr Lee's visit to Kuala Lumpur.

    Among the many pieces of advice given was this: Like Israel, Singapore feels
    that it is squeezed by neighbours who have the potential of becoming enemies.
    Like Israel, Singapore feels the need to be strong militarily and to be able to
    protect itself.

    Just a reminder, said the letter, Israelis still live in fear and blood
    continues to flow even though it is supported by the lone superpower.

    He ended by saying that only insane Singapore leaders would wage war against
    Malaysia, just as only insane Malaysian politicians would turn the taps off on
    Singapore.

    'Let us pray that our countries will not fall into the hands of such insane
    leaders,' he said.

    The talk of war also figured prominently in a column in the New Sunday Times by
    Mr John Teo, executive director of the East Asian Business Council, and a
    contributor to the English language daily.

    He referred to a comment attributed to SM Lee about going to war if access to
    water was cut off and wrote: 'That Singapore should even rhetorically air the
    threat of war against its larger neighbour - even if its tries to justify it on
    the grounds of self-preservation - exposes how arrogantly it still tends to
    misread Malaysian sensibilities nearly four decades after separation.'

    He blamed the Republic's 'un-neighbourliness' as the root cause of irritation
    with other countries in the region. Post-1997 Singapore has made a conscious
    effort to cut its reliance on South-east Asia for its economic prosperity.

    'The timing of such a policy was unfortunate. Singapore probably compounded the
    problem by giving the impression... that while it was happy to be part of the
    region's success during the good times, it wanted out when SEA plunged into
    crisis.'
