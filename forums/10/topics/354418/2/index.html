    Obama criticizes executive compensation practices

     

    President calls for mechanism to hold CEOs accountable for performance  

     

    By Alistair Barr, MarketWatch

     

    Last update: 3:48 p.m. EDT April 2, 2009
    Comments: 33

     

    SAN FRANCISCO (MarketWatch) - President Barack Obama criticized executive compensation in the U.S. on Thursday and called for new ways to hold top managers accountable for the performance of the companies they run.

     

    "The way that too many corporations have operated for too long is that you have a CEO who basically selects his board," Obama said during a press conference at a meeting of the Group of 20 nations in London. "The board, in a fairly cozy relationship oftentimes with the executive, hires an executive compensation firm, which surprisingly tends to think that it's necessary to retain the best talent to pay people $20 million or $30 million a year."

     

    Such "habits and practices" haven't served shareholders well because they've distorted the decision-making processes of CEOs, Obama added.

     

    The G20 has come up with a "set of principals and best practices" to help shareholders judge executive compensation, he added.

     

    "You can still have outsized rewards and success for successful business people, but it'll be based on not short-term performance, not three-month performance, not your ability to flip quick profits off products like derivatives that don't turn out to be particularly productive to the company, but based on sustained effective growth," Obama explained.

     

    "It doesn't mean that we want the state [to] dictate the salaries. We don't," he said. "But we want to make sure that there's mechanisms in place that holds people accountable and produces results."

     

    Alistair Barr is a reporter for MarketWatch in San Francisco.

     

    Lesson to draw from the statement is that Singaporeans should be suspicious of the "ideology of high pay" that is propounded by the beneficiaries of such ideology.
