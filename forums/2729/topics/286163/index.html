    SEAB will try to fit your grades into normal distribution curve.
    If a lot of people do very well, the curve will be heavily skewed to the right with mean > 80% and low standard deviation, then the expected A1 grade will be more than 90%.

    For example,
    Assuming the paper is very easy.
    Mean of the score is 85%, Standard deviation = 2
    Assuming that SEAB allocates 10% of cohort to get A1, The A1 cut-off point will be 87.5%.
    Assuming SEAB allocates another 5% of cohort to get A2, The A2 grade will be 87%Assuming SEAB allocates the next 15% of cohort to get B3, the grade will be 86%Assuming SEAB allocates the next 10% of cohort to get B4, the grade will be 85.5%Assuming SEAB allocates the next 20% of cohort to get C, the grade will be 84.5%Assuming SEAB allocates the next 40 % of cohort to get grade D and below, the grade will be <84.5%.
    So fail grade is < 84.5%

    From the above illustration, only 5% difference will cause you to fail.

    So, you must do very well when the paper is very easy. Careless mistake will cause you a B grade.

    The normal distribution curve above is very 'normal', i.e. that graph is constructed under the assumption that the paper is relatively hard that mean is 50%
