    i come from a pretty dysfunctional family too and can indentify with all that you've posted. you're not alone in this -- many singaporeans are forced to confront their tormented childhoods, and i've seen people emerging as stronger individuals each time round. on the other hand, i've also known people suffering from bad moodswings, depression, and a phobia for marriage stemming from such bad childhood experiences. being brought up in such a family may not be such a bad thing if you do know how to react to it.

    my dad was like yours -- a male chauvinistic pig. our characters clashed since i was young, and it was partly my fault -- i was a rebellious, wild and proud kid. he was a proud man -- too proud to give me a hug or tell me he loved me. he was exceptionally staunch in his beliefs and i would not waver in mine too.

    but i couldn't live with the way he treated my mum. shouts, yellings pierced the golden silence that we had enjoyed for the past few minutes. it became worse as i grew older. the shouts turned into heated arguments and the arguments turned into beatings. things reached a climax when my mum threatened to commit suicide. i hated my mum for putting up with all this, i thought she was a real dumbo. at that point of time, i was persuading my mum to divorce my dad for the good of the family. however, with the passing of time, i realised that she's actually one of the bravest people in the world i've ever known. i really admire your mum for the courage so evident in her. sometimes you would wish she just left him, but it takes alot of longsuffering, patience and moral courage, if i might add, to stick to your dad even when the going gets tough. you ought to be proud of her. faithfulness is a thing of the past in today's dog-eat-dog world.

    don't hate your dad. i mean, we've all hurt others in one way or another so try to refrain from pointing a finger at him k. for all you know, he may not be aware that his actions have the effect of hurting everyone, including you. you could try to initiate a heart-to-heart conversation with him for two reasons. one, its the perfect medium for you to unleash your bottled feelings. and two, it has the potential to remedy the situation. he may be a bad husband, but that does not make him a lousy father. once he understands how you feel, he may wish to make some restitutions in order to heal your emotional wounds.

    i know how you feel, i struggled with loving my dad too. always bear in mind that you are the one at the losing end hating. you end up with emotional scars deep inside you and your train of thoughts goes haywire. so stop hating him.

    as for your mum... you can try to lighten her burden by helping her with the housework or taking care of your siblings if you have any. encourage her to take up other enrichment activities like cooking lessons, taiji or a sightseeing tour etc. this will broaden her social circle and being surrounded by friends helps to alleviate the pain. encourage and praise her whenever you can, her self-esteemed and dignity must have been severely damaged by the affair. for my mum, she took things better after she became a christian and went to church. she got a wider perspective of life, and she's a happier person.

    always remember that you are the one that can make a difference in this bleak situation. the most important thing is not to allow your parents' problems to enter your life. feel free to drop me a pm if you need encouragement in your struggles. cheers.
