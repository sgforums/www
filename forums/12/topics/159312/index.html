    Honestly, are you really paying attention in class? Even that is not enough, the only way one can learn is to keep questioning. And I don't mean questioning the teachers... Question yourself... ask yourself whether you know why it is this and this and not that. Don't be afraid to make mistakes in your assignments, because that's the best way to assess yourself.

    And if you still don't understand the logic behind the theories.... go ahead and question the teacher... until you really understand... and not because you are embarassed and you don't wish to trouble your teachers.

    I've relied on this and obtained more than satisfactory results for all my subjects that are based on logic (Maths and Science). For Social Studies, you will need a very sharp mind to ace the papers. From my teacher's opinions, the one's who get A1 standards for social studies are really crazy people. They see a source and they know what it's purpose and the tone of the source. They can even evaluate it's reliability just by one read through. For the normal people, list content and get your Bs.

    Language wise, it's more practise then anything.

    Tuition is not necessary. Because most people I know who take tuition, they also can't be bothered to give half their attention to lessons... what's the point of tuition? You are only wasting money. If only you feel that you need to reinforce on what you already know... then tuition might be a viable option for revision... and not building your foundations. And some people become cocky just because they have tuition.

    If you want tuition in languages, engage someone who teaches the language in a school. They have strategies and methods to teach effectively.

    You should not be looking for untrained teachers, especially for languages. Definately not some university undergrad.

    Generally, I would simply read more, to improve the language. It will help, after some time - don't expect immediate improvements overnight - this is unlike maths or sciences, where you can be familiar with the theories and rules in a short while.

    No point burning mindnight oil, seriously. You cannot do this for ANY subject. Lack of concentration and tiredness will result in unwanted mistakes - a certainty.

    I give Mathematics tuition (both A and E Maths) - which is very different from giving English tuition.

    I gave up teaching English, because I wasn't able to handle the grading of compositions and comprehension summaries. Only a school teacher can effectively do that.

    For maths, it is a case of right is right, wrong is wrong, all answers are straightforward. In english, you know that anything is but straightforward.

    For Social Studies, there is a need to know content. So long as you can ensure that you know what you have to know, you are safe. There are some strategies that you can take to ensure that you know more.

    Remembering is NOT knowing. I normally tell my students: Knowing is more powerful than remembering. Know your facts, not remember them. This is true esp. for History or Geog or SS, which are content subjects.

    I do feel, that if you are not seeing any results from your "hard work", it may be a case of wrong study methods. Reading the text and notes several times may not mean that the content gets into your head.

    Perhaps a trained school teacher can help you with these learning strategies, and these strategies do not apply only to English.

    Sometimes parents think that getting tutors is giving the student personal attention, and children ALWAYS learn better when they are one to one. I DISAGREE. For some of my students, it is a clear case of paying the tutor (me) to ensure that the child SPENDS TIME on his work, because if there is no tutor, the child would just do anything else BUT touch his books.

    Untimately, the best students are conscientious (aware) of what they want to achieve, and are aware SPECIFICALLY and CLEARLY of WHAT THEY HAVE TO DO to achieve their aim, and have the discipline to DO IT. It is a good idea to LIST OUT the things that the student will do to improve their subject on a piece of paper, and generally stick to it.

    Improvement CAN be speedier with tuition, but is not guaranteed, even if the tutor happens to be the best around. It takes two hands to clap. Student must be conscientious, tutor must be competent. Both must cooperate. If the teacher has to force the student to do certain worksheets or exercises, that is useless tuition.

    Hope this helps, cheers!

    Must be referring to me

    FYI, I was an undergraduate tutor not too long ago. In fact, only recently!

    My point was - hire an undergraduate ONLY if he or she is COMPETENT in teaching that particular subject, and is familiar with the syllabus. Quality of the tutor is important. This usually comes from word of mouth.

    From my experience and from what I heard, undergraduates are better at teaching subjects such as maths and sciences, where much of the emphasis is application of theories and rules. I think, you don't need NIE training to teach such subjects effectively. The tutor must know his/her stuff thoroughly.

    However, most are not able to handle or teach effectively languages, particularly so if there has been no prior NIE training. There is more to teaching a language than ensuring the correctness of grammar or general sentence usage.

    Like I said, I tried, but found myself not competent in teaching O level English. I KNEW that I was not doing what a normal school teacher would have done. I wasn't able to gauge or benchmark just how effective I was, or how ineffective I was. The grades were simply not improving. After 1 year, I told the parent that they should find a school teacher instead. They might have to pay more, but certainly there is greater assurance that grades will improve (no guarantee of course).

    However, I knew that I was tutoring Maths quite well, going by the jump in grades. I applied the method of how my school teacher taught maths when I was in school last time, and it worked well!

    To a CERTAIN extent (I am not generalising here), undergraduates are teaching something they are not competent in, only because of the financial incentive. Worse, they do not even know that they are not competent, as they are not subject to comparisons with other tutors to gauge their effectiveness. By the time the exams come, and the grades do not jump up, maybe 1/2 a year is already wasted, not to mention the money. No "quailty control" here.

    Of course, not all are black sheep. But seriously, most undergraduates are never trained tutors in the first place. Some parents understand this, and insist on school teachers right from the start. Quality wise, school teachers win hands down. But this is not to say that undergraduate tutors are all losers, some have proven to be winners as well.

    Just be discerning in hiring a tutor!

    Good luck!
