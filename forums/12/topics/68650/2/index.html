    It's good to see that many forum members here have the heart to offer their perspectives on the situation to try and help - it helps restore my faith in human nature. However, there are a couple of things that we need to consider here.

    First, the presenting problem, i.e. what the person says is the problem at the start, if often not the real issue. It could simply be a manifestation of the real problem. Time and care therefore need to be taken to determine what the nature of the problem is. If we try to get right to solutions at the start, we may inadvertently interrupt and cut short the process of identifying the problem.

    I know it sounds like a whole lot of wanky piffle, but it really is the case.

    Second, from my limited experience in dealing with depression and its variants, the first thing I learned the hard way is that sometimes there is no such thing as why. I've learned to stop asking, "Why are you depressed?" - if there is a reason, it will come out eventually. Instead, I focus upon the person's experience at that point of time, and just create an environment in which they can talk about how they are feeling, letting them clarify the specifics of the situation.

    Again, it sounds like a wank, but that's generally how it's done. That's exactly why I decided not to become a clinical psychologist and go over to the dark side instead - organisational psychology!
