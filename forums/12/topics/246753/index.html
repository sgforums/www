    Actually, equal opportunity employment is a pretty hard principle to enforce even in first-world democratic countries with top-notch labour standards simply because discriminations can arise through all sort of means, covered up by a vast array of excuses.

    That said, I think dork3d has a point in suggesting that one should request for all essential terms to be stated in a document most people are expected to sign prior to employment - that's just about the only thing you've to fall back on should any disputes arise and you need to pursue matters through legitimate avenues to redress your grievances. This is really important and not something you should be shy/have reservations about when it comes to asking for it - in fact, you shouldn't sign anything until you're sure you've read through all the terms and make sure everything on it is agreeable to you...

    I'm afraid that's the norm. You are usually not allowed to bring the document home, although you'll be given a copy - at least an abridged one, if not the full copy - of it to keep once you've signed it for your reference. In fact, that's not restricted to employment documents - it's the same for people accepting scholarships too.

    That's why I suggest you should not feel compelled to rush through the terms and sign it even if you feel pressurised. Take the entire day if that's how long you need to go over every term and clause - that's your prerogative and your right. At the end of the day, if you don't look after your interest, no one will.
