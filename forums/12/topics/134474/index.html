    had a sibling who also had cancer, still undergoing treatment in fact.

    initial response is of denial/disbelief. even though the patient may show outward signs of acceptance, one may not have completely accepted the fact. as did the counsellor mentioned, "your head may have accepted the fact, but not your heart." i think this is important to take note. dont assume that the patient has accepted the condition.

    personally, the most important thing is for you and your siblings to be on a positive note. it doesnt help if the general mood in the home is downbeat.

    be present when your mom goes for an appointment. ask the doctor what treatment is to be expected. things to watch out for, to expect during treatment. in fact, ask anything that you want to ask. search for information on the internet if need be. ask what are clinical trials. why did the doctor choose treatment A instead of B. etc

    talk about it to close friends. it may be okay initially. however some treatment go on for months. it woudnt be easy nor advisable to carry that around for prolonged periods. it usually feels better after you talked about it to someone you trust.

    bills will be an issue. it is not going to be pretty. for public hospitals, if possible transfer down to ward C. a CT scan costs about 1k, after subsidy, medi shield, etc one pays about 90.

    i dont see how having a specialist would help. there are only so many treatment methods available, some on clinial trial basis and more expensive. ultimately it is how one's body responds to the treatment that is important.

    some posters mentioned distracting her from the cancer. the fact is that it wouldnt be easy. it is unlikely that you can distract her from her having cancer, especially during treatment. perhaps i would put it differently, treat it just like any other day. what you could do is to lighten the mood. laughter is effective. talk about your day at school/work. talk about a scene on tv. you dont even need to talk to her, talk among yourselves. whatever.

    throughout my brother was in a generally upbeat mood. so i have no prior experience with patients that are in denial for long periods or in low moods.

    realise that most of the work you do would be acting as the crutch, the patient has to do most of the walking.
