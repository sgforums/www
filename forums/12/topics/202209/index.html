    sassysapphire86, this issue somehow strikes a very deep chord with me and I hope the insight I could offer will help somewhat.

    Just thought I'd give a brief account of myself for you to draw any parallels that might be applicable in your case. In my case, she was someone I knew in my teens(in fact, since we were both 14) and I guess a reason I never committed myself to her - in spite of the numerous hints she dropped along the way - then(make that throughout our teenage years) was the question that shrouded our future : literally in every sense of the word from whether she was what I wanted for a soulmate to more practical issues like what would happen a decade from then(I mean not too many teens could actually pan out a career/life they see themselves having 10-20 years down the road, could they?) and whether we could find a consensus as to where we would like to settle down(we both did not see ourselves staying in Singapore for good, although we could never seem to agree - or maybe we hadn't really given that a serious thought then - where we'd like to call home) if we ever got together, among a host of other issues. In truth, I never had doubts she'd excel in her academics and life(and as I found out after we got together much later, neither did she harbour any doubts about my academic potential) even at 14, so academic concerns were never much of an issue here. Nevertheless, we did become very good friends, but we were never - not officially by mutual consent, anyway - romantically involved until we were in college.

    That said, when we eventually got together, it was still sort of a long distance relationship(well, maybe not as far apart as yours in question, but I'm pretty sure you've got a rough idea of the distance between Philadelphia and California - more specifically, between UPenn and Stanford - considering you've been studying in the US), although I must stress in my case the fact that both parties wanted to try and make this work made a hell of a difference - the relationship's still going strong now. More importantly, in retrospect(and this is a perspective I hadn't thought of in my teens), I'm glad we never actually got together in our teens as - for one reason or another - I think chances of a relationship working out would have been much slimmer and had that happened, we would probably have lost each other a very good and capable friend too. Perhaps, to put it succinctly, had we made a commitment when we weren't really ready for the relationship, we wouldn't have given this a fair chance of working out. (Feel free to PM me should you need an opinion on how to keep a long-distance relationship going)
