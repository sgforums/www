    Video games addiction     is use of computer and video games to make life bearable. Instances have been reported in which users play compulsively, isolating themselves from family and friends or from other forms of social contact, and focus almost entirely on in-game achievements rather than other life events, and exhibit lack of imagination and mood swings. There is no formal diagnosis of video game addiction in current medical or psychological literature. Inclusion of it as a psychological disorder has been proposed and rejected for the next version of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM).

    Tips on Moderating Gaming and Internet Use

     

    Internet addiction is a term applied to those who spend excessive amount of time online at the expense of and to the detriment of other aspects of their lives.

     

    Examples of such obsessive behaviours include:
    • Spending too much time on chat rooms which replace real-life relationships
    • Compulsive gambling online, or involvement with online auctions
    • Obsessive computer game playing, including multi-players games
    • Addiction to cyber sex or pornography on the Internet.

     

    Addiction to Games
    When time spent playing games on the computer begins to harm a pupil’s social relationships, or disrupts school life, the pupil may be addicted. Like other addictions, the pupil will spend more time playing games to feel good. Time away from the computer games will result in moodiness or withdrawal.

     

    Some Symptoms of Computer Games Addiction
    • Spending most of non-school hours on the computer games
    • Not keeping up with schoolwork
    • Lying about computer game use
    • Choosing to use the computer to play games, rather than meeting friends
    • Dropping out of other social groups (clubs or sports)
    • Irritability when not playing games on the computer

     

    Proposed Actions to Prevent Addiction (Games)
    Below are some strategies that you can share with pupils. Teach the pupil to:
    1. Maintain a balance between playing games and involvement in other activities. Pupils can map out the different activities of the day and ensure that gaming does not take up the majority of their time.
    2. Take breaks from gaming. Teacher can advise pupils to put in a 'reality break' to see what else is going on in the house; try to focus attention away from the fantasy world of the games for a few minutes. Keep to a strict number of hours playing online games per week.
    3. Make gaming a social occasion. Invite friends over to play games. Choose games that allow peers to play.
    4. Play educational games instead. Switch over to playing educational games on computers. There are computer games that provide exciting and educational opportunities and are more worthwhile than non-educational computer games such as shooting games.
    5. Seek alternatives. Pupils get a sense of power from playing and mastering computer games. Pupils could look for interactive activities that give them power through participation and learning. Physical games like basketball and activities like cycling should be encouraged.
