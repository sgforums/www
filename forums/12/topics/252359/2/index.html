    A Barefoot Boy's Incredible Journey

    Excerpted from Unstoppable, by Cynthia Kersey

    He possessed a five-day supply of food, a Bible and Pilgrim's Progress, a small axe for protection, and a blanket. With these, Legson Kayira eagerly set out on the journey of his life. He was going to walk from his tribal village in Nyasaland [Malawi], north across the wilderness of East Africa to Cairo, where he would board a ship to America to get a college education.

    It was October 1958. Legson was sixteen or seventeen, his mother wasn't sure. His parents were illiterate and didn't know exactly where America was or how far. But they reluctantly gave their blessing to his journey. Legson wanted to serve mankind, to make a difference in the world. To realize his goal, he needed education. He knew the best place to get it was in America.

    Forget that Legson didn't have a penny to his name or a way to pay for his ship fare. Forget that he had no idea what college he would attend or if he would
    even be accepted. Forget that Cairo was 3,000 miles away, and in between were hundreds of tribes that spoke more than fifty strange languages, none of which Legson knew. Forget all that. Legson did. He put everything out of his mind except his dream.

    He hadn't always been so determined. Like many of his friends in the village, it was easy for Legson to believe that studying was a waste of time for a poor boy from the town of Karonga in Nyasaland. Then, in books provided by missionaries, he discovered Abraham Lincoln and Booker T. Washington. Their stories inspired him to envision more for his life. So he conceived the idea for his walk.

    After five full days of trekking across the rugged African terrain, Legson had covered only 25 miles. He was already out of food, his water was running out, and he had no money. To travel the distance of 2,975 additional miles seemed impossible. Yet to turn back was to give up, to resign himself to a life of poverty and ignorance. I will not stop until I reach America, he promised himself. Or until I die trying.

    Sometimes he walked with strangers. Most of the time he walked alone. He entered each new village cautiously, not knowing whether the natives were hostile or friendly. Sometimes he found work and shelter. Many nights he slept under the stars. He foraged for wild fruits and berries and other edible plants. He became thin and weak. A fever struck him and he fell gravely ill. Kind strangers treated him with herbal medicines and offered him a place to rest and convalesce. Weary and demoralized, Legson considered turning back. Instead, Legson turned to his two books, reading the familiar words that renewed his faith in himself and in his goal. He continued on.

    On January 19, 1960, fifteen months after he began his perilous journey, he had crossed nearly a thousand miles to Kampala, the capital of Uganda. He remained in Kampala for six months, working at odd jobs and spending every spare moment in the library. In that library he came across an illustrated directory of American colleges. One illustration in particular caught his eye. It was of a stately, yet friendly looking institution, surrounded by majestic mountains that reminded him of the magnificent peaks back home in Nyasaland.

    Skagit Valley College in Mount Vernon, Washington, became the first concrete image in Legson's seemingly impossible quest. He wrote immediately to the school's dean explaining his situation and asking for a scholarship. Fearing he might not be accepted at Skagit, Legson decided to write to as many colleges as his meager budget would allow. It wasn't necessary. The dean at Skagit was so impressed with Legson's determination he not only granted him admission but also offered him a scholarship and a job that would pay his room and board.

    Another piece of Legson's dream had fallen into placeÂ—yet still more obstacles blocked his path. Legson needed a passport and a visa, but to get a passport, he had to provide the government with a verified birth date. Worse yet, to get a visa he needed round-trip fare to the United States. Again, he picked up pen and paper and wrote to the missionaries who had taught him since childhood. They helped to push the passport through government channels. However, Legson still lacked the airfare required for a visa. Undeterred, Legson continued his journey to Cairo believing he would somehow get the money he needed. He was so confident he spent the last of his savings on a pair of shoes so he wouldn't have to walk through the door of Skagit Valley College barefoot.

    Months passed, and word of his courageous journey began to spread. By the time he reached Khartoum, penniless and exhausted, the story of Legson Kayira had spanned the ocean between the African continent and Mount Vernon, Washington. The students of Skagit Valley College, with the help of local citizens, sent $650 to cover Legson's fare to America.

    In December 1960, more than two years after his journey began, Legson Kayira arrived at Skagit Valley College, carrying his two treasured books. But Legson Kayira didn't stop once he graduated. He became a professor of political science at Cambridge University in England and a widely respected author. He rose above his humble beginnings and forged his own destiny. He made a difference in the world. "I learned I was not, as most Africans believed, the victim of my circumstances but the master of them."Â—Legson Kayira.

    Hello Everyone, This article was extracted out from a 2005 Calendar that I had received as a gift. Two Japanese men were exchanging their views pertaining to Life on Earth. Joe (Zulkifli Mahmood) THE WAY WE AS ADULTS LIVE SENDS AN IMPORTANT MESSAGE TO CHILDREN A: I heard that, when you saw the Earth from outer space for the first time in your first mission, you felt that the Earth was a solitary beacon of life in an ocean of complete darkness. B: When you look at the Earth while orbiting at an altitude of 300 kilometres (186 miles), making one round in 90 minutes, you know that there are pandas, whales, and your friends down there. You know it but you can't see it. On the other hand, if you step out of the spacecraft, it's a world of death. You keenly realize that science and technology are keeping you alive up there. You asked yourself if people's belief that the atmosphere protects the Earth is true. There happens to be air, vegetation, and ocean now, and you happen to be here. And you rarely become conscious of death as long as you are there. But there is no assurance. Maybe there is assurance until tomorrow or 100 years from now. When I was in outer space, I felt that everything is uncertain. In that sense, the existence of animals and plants is something you have to appreciate. A: I found lion tracks on the African savanna where you can look out over horizons 360 degrees around you. When I saw those tracks, I started shaking so badly I had to hold my knees. It was not fear of lions. It was rather from my inner self, feeling the uncertainty of my own existence just like you said. Most people who live on the Earth now barely have a chance to experience fear that makes them hold their knees. If they all felt it, the world would never have wars. B: There is an incomparably greater existence, so you feel the pettiness of your own existence. A: Yes, I was convinced there must be something. That's why I included "Natural Order" in the title of the book of the photographs I shot in the African savanna. B: Now we often hear people say humans are violating and destroying the environment and that the Earth will be ruined by humans. But when I went around the Earth on the Space Shuttle, the existence of humans is totally invisible. Of course, large cities like Tokyo and New York look gray. But when you compare them to the whole, the gray areas are still much smaller. Rather, the sense of existence of forests and deserts is much greater. However, when I saw scratches in the Amazon's jungles that were otherwise all covered in green, I realized that they were traces of slash-and-burn farming. I actually went to the Amazon after I returned to the Earth. Farmers slash and burn the jungles to a width of about 300 metres (980 feet), and there are enormous oil pipelines cutting across the jungles. So I experienced two points of view. The destruction is still small when you look at it from outer space, but it is immense when you look at it on the ground. A: It is difficult to look at things from such different points of view. B: It's not logic. What we need is experience to realize the fragility of our existence - something that makes you tremble, like you said. A: For example, you go out of town with your family, and your kids find a bug and get interested in it. If you tell them not to touch the bug and drag them away from the bug, your kid's sensitivity will be ruined. I think that sort of thing happens a lot now. B: To show children how we adults live - that's an important message to children. A: When my daughter was still a little kid, I took her on extended shooting sessions in Africa and Australia. She said she felt that a place where she could see the faces of her family was home to her no matter where she was, regardless of what culture she was in and what national borders she was surrounded by. B: When you stay in outer space on the Space Shuttle, it's a totally artificial world. The air you breathe is made of liquid oxygen. The water you drink is made by mixing hydrogen and oxygen. Everything is artificial, and nothing is natural. You lead such a life. Then you return to the ground, and, as soon as the hatch is open, air flows in gently. And that air is somehow wet. You smell earth in it. You feel there is life here. You've come back to the Earth. It's your home. You feel relieved. A: Wherever you open the hatch after landing, that's your home. B: Then you are handed a glass of water, and that's so delicious. It's completely different from the artificial water you were drinking in outer space. As soon as you taste it, you feel the Earth is our common home in the end. I think that only on Earth can one feel at ease and at home. A: That's indeed true. We owe our existence to this wonderful planet. Tokyo, June 26, 2004.

    LETTING GO

    To let go: Doesn't mean to stop caring, it means I can't do it myself

    To let go: Is not to cut myself off, it is the realization that I can't control another

    To let go: Is not to enable, but to allow learning from natural consequences

    To let go: Is to admit powerlessness, which means the outcome is not in my hands

    To let go: Is not to try to change or blame another, I can only change myself

    To let go: Is not to care for, but to care about

    To let go: Is not to fix, but be supportive

    To let go: Is not to judge, but allow another to be human

    To let go: Is not to be in the middle arranging outcomes, but to allow others to effect their own outcomes

    To let go: Is not to be protective, it is to permit another to face reality

    To let go: Is not to deny, but to accept Is not to nag, scold, or argue, but to search out my own shortcomings and to correct them

    To let go: Is not to adjust everything to my desires, but to take each day as it comes and cherish the moment

    To let go: Is not to criticize and regulate someone, but to try and become what I dream I can be

    To let go: Is not to regret the past, but to live for the future

    To let go: Is to fear less and love more.

    These are instructions on how to let go. Perhaps it is letting go of a rebellious child or a burden of sorrow, losing a loved one or learning to live with a hearty ache which we cannot let go. Study it, pray over it and you will find that letting go of your burden will release a peace within you which will allow your spirit to soar - to be free, to completely give "it" to GodÂ… and let the work be done within you where the need is anyway.

    BE A LAKE

    The old Master instructed the unhappy young man to put a handful of salt ina glass of water and then to drink it.

    "How does it taste?" the Masterasked.

    "Awful," spat the apprentice.

    The Master chuckled and then asked the young man to take another handful of salt and put it in the lake. The two walked in silence to the nearby lake and when the apprentice swirled his handful of salt into the lake, the old man said, "Now drink from the lake."

    As the water dripped down the young man's chin, the Master asked, "How does it taste?"

    "Good!" remarked the apprentice.

    "Do you taste the salt?" asked the Master.

    "No," said the young man.

    The Master sat beside this troubled young man, took his hands, and said,"The pain of life is pure salt; no more, no less. The amount of pain in life remains the same, exactly the same. But the amount we taste the 'pain'depends on the container we put it into. So when you are in pain, the only thing you can do is to enlarge your sense of things ..... Stop being a glass. Become a lake!"
