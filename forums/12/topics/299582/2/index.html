    In a hypothetically perfect scenario, your confession would work out and this, in turn, leads to a blissful marriage.

    Now, I can't add much more to the points not already discussed here. With the greatest respect, you just might not see the bigger picture at your age. For a start, many friends you make are just there for a phase in your life - just because you have been close with this group for the last couple of years does not in any way indicate you'd still keep in contact with them when you're 70.

    What some have suggested in terms of you confessing would at least give you a chance (however futile is another matter and may not necessarily be an accurate reflection of his thoughts about you anyway since you don't really know what he thinks) is logical in the sense of looking at the bigger picture. Now, picture the ideal scenario and contrast that with the other possibility of a rejection that leads to awkwardness in the group and you effectively have to do an thorough evaluation as to whether a potentially risky action in the short-term is worth more than a long-term bliss. At your age, peer acceptance may mean an awful lot to you and it may be the single biggest factor in determining your course of action.

    Perhaps, just let me shed some light on the long-term ramifications which you may wish to consider besides thinking exclusively in the short-haul. Let's assume you didn't confess and you actually stop socialising with the same group (include that guy in your assessment) 20 years from now. Would you actually look back and wonder what might have been had you been more proactive? For many, even a formal rejection is a proper closure (after all, there is a reason why most job and college applications yield an outcome, right? ) to move on and is something for them to look back on in the distant future that they had at least tried and not live to look back on things that might have been. In the event that, through some other circumstances, you actually find out your destiny might actually have been very different had you been more proactive, would you be able to live with that and be at ease with yourself? In that regard, you would then be deciding on whether a lifetime's regret is actually worth a momentary awkwardness (which may not even materialise).

    The point here is do you like him enough to actually want to settle on the knowledge that you will never know what he really thinks just so that you do not feel awkward in the company of a group of friends which may not necessarily be everlasting? If it's just an infatuation, then it might not be that viable and prudent for you to express your interest in him. However, if you're convinced it's love you're talking about that would last a long time, you might just wish to reconsider the fact that the risk you're taking by not confessing could actually be worse than if you were to confess - especially if you looked at the bigger picture in the long term.

    Good luck whatever you decide to do...
