    Actually, that IS an interesting question that tvdog raised.

    Theoretically speaking, the objective of zeroing is to set the intercept of two lines at the intended point of impact. The first of these lines is the trajectory of the projectile. The second line traces the path from the targetted spot to the tip of the foresight to the centre of the rear sight to the shooter's eye.

    It would make sense that for iron sights, since the layout and size of the face varies from person to person (in particular the distance between the cheek and the eye), zeroing is an individual thing, and the foresight height has to accommodate for how high the shooter's eye sits, and the rearsight for the amount of tilt of the shooter's head, in order for the second line to be adjusted correctly.

    Now how that gels with something like a SAR-21's scope is a different matter altogether - regardless of what angle you take to look through the sight, the crosshairs shoudl still stay on the same spot, and only the field of view should be affected by not looking directly through the sight. Once the sight is zeroed, would the crosshairs then not point at the right spot regardless of who is looking through it? Any differences in accuracy should be caused by the shooter's manner of handling the weapon.

    Thought I'd chime in here for a few brief comments.

    If the rifle is "zeroed" through the iron sights using a particular sight picture, then that rifle should shoot to the same POI with subsequent users if they adopt the same sight picture that was used to zero the rifle. The iron sights and barrel are attached to the upper receiver and are fixed in relation to each other, so it does not matter how the shooter's head is situated with respect to the rifle, as long the dominant (aiming) eye is in the correct position to acquire the same sight picture each and every time.

    In practice, however, not everyone might use the same sight picture. For example, one shooter might prefer to have the entire bullseye sit atop the front sight post, while another might prefer that the top of the front sight post horizontally bisect the exact center of the bullseye. When zeroing a firearm, the shooter's preferred sight picture has to be accommodated.

    Other factors that might work against achieving a repeatable zero in a particular firearm might include:

    Variances in the ammunition. Manufacturing variances in the mass production of ammunition (coupled with the occasional lapse in quality control) might result in inconsistent powder charges, projectile weights and seating depths. The resulting variations in muzzle velocity would cause the POI to wander from the POA. Granted, the 5.56mm cartridge is a very flat-shooting round, and differences in muzzle velocity might not change the POI significantly at 100 yards, but at 200 yards and beyond, the changes in POI do become noticeable.

    Military-issue and military-surplus ammunition aren't exactly known for holding tight manufacturing tolerances. I've measured variances of up to 400 fps with milsurp M193 (55 gr.) ammunition (and slightly less with M855 [62 gr.] rounds) out of the barrel of the same AR. That's why AR shooters who want to achieve a repeatable zero to maximize accuracy, handload their own ammunition.



    Weather conditions. Wind, temperature and humidity do, to varying degrees, affect the ballistics of the projectile. A gusty crosswind would wreak havoc with your POI, especially at extended shooting distances. An explanation of how temperature and humidity affect ballistics can be found here.

    Fortunately (or unfortunately, depending on your point of view), it's always hot and humid in Singapore, so the effects of temperature and humidity can almost be regarded as a constant.
