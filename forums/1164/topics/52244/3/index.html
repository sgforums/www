    Japan's navy has requested initial funding for the first of what will be the largest fighting ships--eight 13,500-metric-ton-class helicopter carriers.

    Funding to begin building the first ship is being sought in the fiscal 2004 budget. Each is expected to cost $1 billion and carry four Sikorsky/Mitsubishi SH-60K subhunters.

    Regional tensions, particularly the ballistic missile threat posed by North Korea's Taepo-Dong program, have prompted Japan to rethink much of its post-World War II pacifism. The Japanese navy's largest fighting ships are four 7,250-metric-ton Aegis cruisers. (The navy does have three 13,000-metric-ton transport ships.)

    However, for the past 30 years navy planners have been seeking a light carrier for helicopters and vertical takeoff/landing (VTOL) aircraft, such as a Boeing AV-8B. But their pleas have been rebuffed by the Defense Agency out of regional political concerns over Japan "rearming."

    But the North Korean aggression is wearing away such resistance. The country has developed its first military reconnaissance satellite fleet and is considering a missile defense system.

    The plan is to assign each of the navy's four fleets two of the helicopter carriers. Currently, those fleets are equipped with a 5,000-ton flagship that can carry three helicopters.

    Besides the situation's political ramifications, the navy will face some budget opposition because the cost is so high relative to other programs. The plan does not include introduction of a VTOL aircraft.
