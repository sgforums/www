    Officers are trained to accept painful orders and ensure that it is carried out (without question and without hesitation). That is drummed into them during OCS and for the first two years of service or when they reach captain. Because an officer is only as good as his ability to receive and give orders.

    What drives the men are the NCOs. The backbone of the armed forces. Ask any scout, pioneer or commando. Seldom do these elements operate as a platoon (the smallest size where you'd expect to see an officer). There basic leadership is practiced hands on, from the ground up. You don't need an "Air level" to become an NCO. However good NCOs seldom, if ever become, good officers (extremely few exceptions).

    Israel has a different method of identifying leaders during their NS. All Israelis enter BMT as equals. They pass out and go into different units for their vocation training. After a year in service, the good ones are identified and sent to the NCO course. The very best are sent to officer cadet school. Selection is based not only on education, but on soldiering skills and performance as well. When they passed out as NCOs or officers, they return to their units or join new intakes. Males will serve 3 years, females 2 years. In short they prove themselves as soldiers first, before being NCOs or officers.

    If I may bring an extra variable into this topic discussion: the SAF scholar.

    For the most part, I think all OCS-trained officers are fine, or at least the ones I've worked with as part of an operational unit. There's always an initial easing-in period as they find their niches, trying to a right balance between "pulling rank" per se (aka chickensh!t in US parlance) on one extreme & Pasir Laba-style can-do collective team spirit on the other. [It's another story with the couple of 2LTs from each batch who tend to wear their ego like their rank.]

    For the rest of the NSF stint, they tend to play 2nd-fiddle to the regular OC/CSM's or equivalent, but IMO this system works cuz they settle in very well upon taking full control come reservist time (with their maturity as well as the overall unit's).

    Well, from my experience anyway & from the ancedotes I hear.

    More to the point: I did my NS during a time when the "2nd-generation " of SAF scholars, i.e. those in the '80s, was fast-tracked into their training/appointments & I can honestly say they provided for a fluctuating quality of leaders then. Without naming names, I served under 3 of them (one a CO) & Lee Hsien Loong's pioneer influence was still lingering throughout the formation. I'm sure they'll all brilliant students & worthy OCS cadets, but the sense of entitlement one of them displayed was a huge turn-off.

    On the one hand, we Hokkien pengs knew they were high-fliers who's essentially climbing on our backs & that they'll definately be promoted no matter how they led us. On the other, it can be a very alienating experience having to follow & tolerate a megalomaniac's "leadership" - even our 2LTs & experienced sergeant major were pissed off with him.

    I dunno if the selection process got better since the '90s, but here's hoping they've weeded out the conceit trait since then.
