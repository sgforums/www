    Malaysia test-fires new multiple rocket system

    Army chief says the expansion of nation's modern weapons arsenal is not an attempt to establish an offensive military capability

    LUMUT (Perak) - Malaysia has test-fired its newly acquired multiple rocket launching system, which has been touted as a new addition to its deterrent defence capability, at a cost of RM17 million (S$7.8 million).

    The Malaysian army fired a total of 48 live rockets towards a designated sea area about 80 km from the launching site in Tanjung Hantu near Lumut on Thursday.

    The event was held in conjunction with the 70th anniversary of the army which was celebrated on March 1.

    The test was done to ascertain the handling by the local personnel of the system newly-acquired from Avibras Aerospacial, based in Brazil.

    The demonstration involved the firing of 24, 300-mm SS-60 rockets and 24, 300-mm SS-80 rockets into the sea.

    The 300-mm SS-60 rockets have a range of 60 km, while the 300-mm SS-80 rockets have a range of up to 89 km.

    Known as the ASTROS II or Artillery Saturation Rocket System, the multiple rocket launching system was acquired by Malaysia in October last year to enhance its surface-to-surface combat missions.

    Speaking to reporters after the test, army chief Wira Mohamed Shahrom Nordin said Malaysia's expansion of its modern weapons system should not be interpreted as an attempt to establish pre-emptive or offensive military capability.

    He said the purchase of new weapons systems was part of the armed forces move to equip themselves with 'deterrent defence capabilities' in the face of mounting threats and uncertainties in global security.

    'Of course, our recent purchases have been more artillery weaponry for ground and air defence to increase our military capabilities, but due to recent political developments in the region, the move has been generally misunderstood.'

    The army chief said the armed forces would continue to expand their 'deterrent defence capabilities' in the coming years by purchasing powerful weapons such as the IGLA, ANZA and JERNAS air defence missile system and domestic warning radar system.

    General Shahrom said the purchase of more lethal and accurate weapons systems was important to ensure the army - the backbone of the country's defence - and other units of the armed forces are well equipped in every combat mission in the future.

    He said the army was preparing an infrastructure development framework to organise the distribution of the acquired and soon-to-be-acquired weapons systems to each unit under its wing.

    The event was also attended by the Sultan of Kelantan, Sultan Ismail Petra, who is the Colonel-in-Chief of the Royal Artillery Regiment and Raja Perempuan Kelantan Tengku Anis.--New Straits Times, TheStar/Asia News Network
