    Even though the IJN was relatively unscathed by the 1st World War, the Washington Naval Disarmament Treaty of 1920 did placed the Japanese at a disadvantage with regards to capital ship and other surface ship construction. The IJN managed to circumvent the handicap by exploiting a clause which allows for the conversion of existing hulls into carriers when the potential for naval aviation was not as fully understood. The result was the creation of the 1st Air Fleet, probably the most powerful carrier strike force at the outbreak of war. The conditions for the growth of the IJN and its rivals - the IJN and the USN/RN were in my opinion relatively even when all aspects are considered. The IJN expansion compared to that of the RN and USN was not due to the 1st War World but was rather due to the application of sufficient political will as exerted by the military in the civilian government of the 1920s and early 1930s as well as the military regimes of the mid 1930s onwards. This political will was ineffectual in the case of the RN and USN with an example being the strong isolationistic sentiments of the US Goverments in the inter war period. A disproportionately large part of the Japanese budgets were diverted to the military and to finance this, increase taxation, inflation, economic imbalances and a relative deterioation in civilian standards of living were the downside of this expansion. It can be concluded that the expansion of the IJN and IJA was thus unsustainable over the long term.

    As for my opinions on the Kriegsmarine, I apologise if my statements were not clear enough. What I mean was that had Germany allocated more resources to U Boat in Plan Z and had more U Boats ready on the eve of the Second World War as a result of such allocation, then the larger submarine force might just be sufficient to strangle Britain. I understand that Hitler ordered the switch in priority from surface construction to U Boat in late 1939 but then perharps in retrospect, the change in policy was a bit too late to have a decisive impact at the initlal point of the submarine campaign when the British were at their most vulnerable to such a campaign. Yes it is true that the Treaty of Versaille specifically prohibited the Germans from constructing submarines but as with other branches of the German armed forces, the Treaty was frequently corvetly broken. German U Boat construction expertise was maintained during the interwar years prior to the abrogation of the Treaty by their covert involvements in the submarine projects of friendly countries notably Finland and Russia. A latent industrial base was also maintained for future U Boat programs during this period of time. Hence it is not surprising that Germany began the open buildup of its U Boat arm, progress was rapid. I think the other battleship launched was the Tripitz.

    Hello 05

    Before I comment on your points, I just want to say that our discussions and disagreements are nothing personal and let us not get too heated up by the disagreements. This may sound a bit frivolous but I have seen discussions degenerated in this forum into shouting matches because of strong disagreement

    Our main point of disagreement was that the IJN pre world war 2 conditions were more agreeable for its expansion vis a vis its regional rivals - the RN and the USN. I share your view that on the outbreak of the Pacific War, the IJN was superior to its rival. I merely disagree with your view that the reason for their greater expansion was due to the fact that they were unscathed by the WW1 vis a vis the RN. I still consider that the presence of political will and its determination to channel resources into military build up was the main factor that led to their greater expansion. Let's keep our disagreement in focus Yes it is true that the IJN only had to focus on the Pacific Ocean as their threatre of operation. However it should be noted that RN had more or less abandonedits pretense of being able to exert dominance in the Pacific at the turn of the 19th century. When Fisher became the 1st Lord of the Admiralty, he instituted a program of closing down the peripheral squadrons and concentrating the RN in home waters and maintaining minimal strenght in the Mediterranean only - the gun boats and patrol boats in other locations can be ignored in our force balance. Simultaneously, the Anglo - Japanese Treaty of Friendship was signed in 1904 which effectively handed over the security of the British Asian colonies to the Japanese. This situation was not rectified in the inter war years due to various factors such as financial weakness, political apathy etc. I have not read the book " Standards of Power" as you have recommended but if proposed another viewpoint, I would surely love to read it. As for the USN, it recognised after WW1 that the IJN would emerged its most likely adversary and hence the force structure and strategy were already geared towards a war with Japan. Examples of this include the formation of a carrier arm with most of the carriers being focused in the Pacific, the famous War Plan Orange drafted in the late 20s and early 30s which gave the USN a major role in the Pacific War and a minimal role in a European conflict. Even if there was not no implicit understanding the the main theatre of operation for the USN was in the Pacific, the vast industrial strength of the US would allow it to develope a sizable force in the Pacific as envisioned in the Two Ocean Navy Act of 1940.

    As for the Kriegsmarine, we should not confuse the product of policy with the cause of policy. The surface components of the Kriegsmarine were not constructed under Plan Z for an invasion of Norway. Rather it was fortunate that the surface components were present to allow for the execution of the plan. Still I have the fullest admiration for the Kriegsmarine in the execution of the initial phase of the invasion - a multi pronged invasion executed in the greatest secrecy and in the face of an absence of sea command contradict military orthodoxy and its success was a magnificent feat of arms.
    I also agree with you that given the constraints on U Boat construction due to Plan Z, the Kriegsmarine did its best in preparing the U Boat fleet for war. I also have my reservation that Plan Z would give the Kriegsmarine superiority over the RN had it being allowed to carry on untill 1946. The indication of the extent of German expansion would have been alarmed the Britain and a naval armed race would surely have been triggered with the outcome going either way. German naval expansion can only be directed at the British and since the loss of sea command would mean imperiling the survival of Britain, it is highly unlikely that the British would not respond in kind. History would again repeats itself and we will see the reenactment of the dreadnought race between Britain and Germany in the decade prior to the outbreak of WW1

    Hello Doenitz

    I read your post in which you mentioned that a guerre de course strategy has serious limitation. I am no suggesting that a guerre de course strategy is a replacement for a maritme strategy that aimed at command of the sea but rather in view of Germany position, a guerre de course would have to be the temporary expedient untill Germany complete the consolidation of its continental hegemonic position. Even Mahan may be wrong about a lot of things, I still subscribed to his view that maritime supremacy should be the aim of the navies of every nation with imperial ambitions or even for modern nation states with no such pretensions like Singapore.
    Moreover its adversary Britain was also particularly vulnerable to a commerce strangulation campaign by virtue of its postion. Upon consolidation of its continental hegemony, the Germans can then marshal the resources of its conquests to implement a maritime strategy of sea command to defeat the British should the guerre de course pursued earlier failed to do so. The original guerre de course campaign was waged by the French against the British in the 1700 - 1800 in recognition of French naval weakness and by giving up maritime supremacy, the French left their overseas colonies isolated and allowed the British with it command of the sea to reduce these colonies and led to the lost of most of the French possesions in North America and the Indian Ocean. Maritime supremacy and its geographical position also allowed Britain to pursue a strategy of attrition against the French in concert with continental allies in the so-called continental strategy of Britain that lasted untill the 1st World War. For the Kriegsmarine, the strategy of guerre de course was defeated in both world wars by various factors such as convoying and losing the technological race and the abandonement of maritiem command allowed the Allies time and space to bring the full might of the Americans in the European conflict. However all these should not distract people from my argument that in Germany context prior to the WW2, a guerre de course strategy was still probably the best mid term strategy to pursue as argued in my earlier postings.
