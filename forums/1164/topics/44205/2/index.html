    The British did recognised that the Japanese would be a threat to their Asian colonies in the interwar years and I agree with you that the construction of the Singapore Naval Base was in recognition of that threat. However the construction of the base does not neccessarily means that the British feel that they have the capability to defend their asiatic colonies. The construction of the base was slow in the 1920s and only picked up in the 1930s under recognition of Japanese expansionistic tendencies especially in the 1933 Manchurian Incident. The fact the British refused to base their a fleet here and instead intend to dispatch a fleet here was illuminative of British strategic thoughts. In view of the political situation of the 1930s, the British began to appreciate that the possible prospect of a renewed war with a rearmed Germany and a resurgent Japan. There was implict recognition that a strong fleet would only be sent to Singapore should there not be a requirement for their basing in the Home Islands in a European conflict. If the British had not abandoned their global defense concept of the 19th and early 20th century, a fleet would probably be assigned on permanent station in Singapore togather with the base. What good would be a base without a fleet? In the end, Force K was dispatched to Singapore and in retrospect it was difficult to envisioned what the British hope to achieve against a Japanese invasion fleet covered by a task force involving at least 2 Fleet carriers. I bring up this point in support of my argument that the RN has switched its focus to the security of its home waters as mentioned in my earlier posts. The building up of its Eastern Fleet from 1943 onwards should be viewed in light that the European naval threat has subsided sufficiently for the build up of this fleet. After all, what good are battleships and fleet air craft carriers in the anti submarine war? An interesting side note to this was that in 1940/41 before Pearl Harbor, the British mooted to the Americans that they should shift their Pacific Fleet to Singapore but fortunately for the Americans, they turned down the offer.

    It should be noted that the USN shifted its Pacific Fleet from the West Coast to Pearl Harbor in the mid 1930s in recognition of the Japanese threat and a view of the ORBAT would indicate that the majority of the principal surface combatants such as battleships and aircraft carriers were on rotation to the Pacific Fleet for a significantly longer period than they were to the Atlantic Fleet.

    As for the Kriegsmarine, it is not surprising that the Kriegsmarine never did received the amount of funding it received. It is rare for a country to maintain both a strong navy and a strong army at the same time and you or Doenitz did mentioned that Germany recognised that she is first and foremost a landpower. I do not think Plan Z was meant merely for the protection of her home waters. Germany natural homewaters would be the Baltics and in the confined waters of the Skagerrak, perharps a different fleet structure instead of one with ocean going U Boats, aircraft carriers and battle ships would be more appropriate. However if you view the North Sea as Germany homewaters, the perharps your argument would be justified in view of the Germans experience of the British WW1 blockade. The developement of the the shipboard centrimetric band radar, shipboard high frequency radio direction finding, escort carriers and long range ASW aircrafts all spelt the death knell of the U Boat arm.     hello 05

    I stand corrected on the issue of the independent sailing by fast merchant ships . I do not understand your sentence If the U Boats had longer range, they could be convoy raiders like the Bismarck I thought the U Boats were already convoy raiders? As for the issue of using them in the Med, I think that would be an unwise tactical decision. Why would you want to divert your forces from the main theatre of operation - The Atlantic? Between the India - Britain convoys and the US-Britain convoys, I think the value of the latter would be much greater than the former. Moreover, only a few of the German U Boats diverted to the Med were able to emerge safely from the Med - The transit of the Straits of Gibraltar was especially hazardous. The U Boats diverted to the Med were hence effectively locked in the Med. I grant you the fact that the ASW conditions in the Med were terrible - High acoustic background and poor sound transmission etc. The British submarines based in Malta and Gibraltar did have an excellent campaign though but there was no requirement for them to be in the Atlantic. Using the U Boats to take out the Home Fleet would have been a misapplication of resources. As attractive as idea of taking out combatants were, the Atlantic Campaign was ultimately about the economic strangulation of Britain and hence taking out a tanker would be much better than taking out a cruiser. The risks were greater and the possible returns lower. To support my argument on this, I would point to the case of the Japanese submarine policy. The I Boats were designed with fleet action in mind and they were not unleashed on an organised commerce hunting campaign. The returns of the I Boats on warships were rather mediocre but at because of this decision, the Allies convoy in the Pacific were pretty much unmolested to the extent that by 1944, the Allies merchant vessels were travelling the Pacific unescorted and unconvoyed. I have 2 major critique of the U Boats operation. The 1st one was the inflexibility of the command and control system. Upon sighting of the convoy, direction of the convoy battle were shifted to U Boat HQ in Kerneval. The rationale was that the HQ would have a more holistic picture and hence better able to direct their operations to maximise impact. The downside of this was that the HQ have no inkling of the actual tactical conditions and the commanders were not free to pursue better options. Coordinating the wolf packs also generated large amount of signal traffic and with the advent of shipboard HFDF and the breaking of Engima, it allowed for the tracking of the U Boats and contributed to their destruction. A better model of tactical control would be the American method whereby the subs operated in packs of 3 or 4 with a local senior commander in full tactical control and minimal HQ interference. The 2nd main criticism was the very poor interservice cooperation between the Kriegsmarine and Luftwaffe. Insignificant aircraft were allocated to the U Boat campaign and even these were allocated grudgingly. Coordination of the air support and U Boat operation was minimal as well. This deprived the U Boats of intelligence and tactical support.
