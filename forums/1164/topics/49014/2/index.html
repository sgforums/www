    This is plain embarassing. Remembering what 1 Commando was like ten years ago, watching my old unit fall from grace in this manner really stings.

    My loyalty still remains with the Commandos; such things are hard to let go of. However, being realistic, I am not surprised, much less offended, at the smudging of logistics records. For as long as I have known, the ethos of the unit was that maintaining stores held a very low priority in comparison to getting the job done. The implicit assumption was that in completing the mission, plenty of equipment, both ours and the oppositionÂ’s, would be damaged, destroyed or lost. This assumption was carried through to training, and in the interests of focussing upon the mission, our willingness to write off equipment was much higher than what would be typically found in other SAF units. Certainly our logistics people earned their pittance.

    Call it arrogance, callousness or just plain sloppiness, but we never paid much attention to the niceties of military life. We were often cited for not saluting other unitsÂ’ officers, employing guerrilla tactics in BTEC exercises and, in one case, painting in our own parking space at SAFTI. As Henry David Thoreau would have it, "Simplicity, simplicity, simplicity! I say, let your affairs be as two or three, and not a hundred or a thousand instead of a million count half a dozen, and keep your accounts on your thumb-nail". We were there to do a job and do it damned well, and as long as that was achieved, we were, and I do believe we remain, unabashedly unapologetic for all other transgressions.

    What, if the rumours are true, shames me is that the fitness records were falsified. A full score on the fitness tests was the minimum required for the job, and a problem in this department, in light of the ethos I described above, simply will not do. If the unitÂ’s state of fitness was such that records had to be falsified, losing the Best Combat Unit competition would be a foregone conclusion anyway.

    Another part of our ethos is that we tend to self-regulate, and in terms of fitness our standards would be more demanding than what Mindef sees fit to impose. As much as I would hate to disagree with a fellow Commando, I would point out to DarrenWong that if we need to falsify our records for the sake of meeting Mindef standards, we have well and truly fallen even further below our own standards. Unless things have changed radically since I left, Commandos are self-starters; on a normal day, without need for instructions, most of us would go for runs around AETI while waiting for the Duty Officer to sign everything off. I was far from being the fittest man in my company. However, despite all these years and a relatively sedentary lifestyle, I can still clear a 2.4 kilometre run in less than ten minutes.

    Vis-Ã -vis the claims of Commandos turning their MILES harnesses off during exercises, a major shift in culture must have taken place for such practices to occur. Apart from the usual grumbling about being unable to book out in the evenings, my lot rather looked forward to playing OPFOR and having some real fun. Some of us wanted to try out new tactics; other simply had plenty of aggression to release. Either way, the net effect was that the majority of us went out for the satisfaction of properly trouncing the opposition. This, more so than any regulation, precluded the idea of turning the MILES harness off. Again, it our self-regulatory nature manifesting itself. If the current lot are in fact trying to win for the sake of the record, half the unit motto ought to be shamefully erased.

    Those from my company who stayed on have risen to reasonably senior positions by now; the last time I checked, all of the officers were majors. I can only sincerely hope that they remember the way we did things, and maintain or regain that standard.

    THAT BAN ON COMMANDOS
    3 soldiers fined for rifle sling cover-up
    Elite warriors barred from Best Combat Unit contest after discrepancies uncovered

    By Goh Chin Lian

    DETAILS of what had happened to warrant the unprecedented move to bar the Commandos from vying for the Best Combat Unit title this year emerged in a military court yesterday.

    Three regular servicemen overseeing supplies for the elite force had made up documents to account for some extra rifle slings that were not on its records, ahead of an audit by the Defence Ministry.

    But the auditors uncovered the discrepancies and reported them for investigation.

    This cover-up and other discrepancies over individual fitness test scores dashed 1st Commando Battalion's hopes of clinching the top award that it had won a record 17 times since the contest started in 1969.

    The Singapore Armed Forces had barred the unit to uphold the integrity of the contest, which involves a year-long assessment of its forces on matters ranging from logistics and battle readiness to administrative efficiency.

    No details emerged about the fitness test scores yesterday. A Defence Ministry spokesman said the officers in that case would be 'disciplined in due course'.

    Yesterday's case involved the logistics officer of 1st Commando Battalion, who had come to know of 144 extra rifle slings in its store last October, just before the audit.

    The usual procedure is for the Commando headquarters to record that the slings had been purchased, but the consignment had gone straight to the battalion instead.

    Instead of waiting to sort out the confusion, the logistics officer instructed his quartermaster to draw up a document showing that the slings were on loan from the headquarters to the unit.

    The quartermaster and a warrant officer from the headquarters signed for the loan, knowing it was bogus.

    The three servicemen were each fined $800.

    In passing judgment, Major (NS) Marvin Bay, the president of the court martial, noted, among other things, the servicemen's past record of exemplary conduct and that their actions were not for personal gain.

    But Major Bay, who is also a district judge in the Subordinate Court, said: 'It never pays to do things the easy way rather than the right way.

    'It is after all when we are faithful in small things that we can be trusted in important matters, such as the defence of Singapore.'
