    What the heck, now that I'm in the mood ....

    Seibeisuaykia,
    Singapore's relied more on MOUs when it comes to creating bilateral ties &, even when things have progressed on to signed agreements, we've been careful not to overcommit or overaccomodate to an extent of indirectly antagonizing our neighbors. Especially any perceived attempt at siteing foreign bases locally. I mean, we do provide faciliation offices, maintainence/resupply means, even R&R, for visiting troops, but they're certainly not on the military/economic scale of the British & its Naval Base days at Sembawang (ask your pa or grandpa).

    Much of the ongoing tactfulness is due to historical baggage & geopolitical realities:

    >> Foremost is the survivalist instinct or so-called siege mentality: the initial "poisonous shrimp" analogy (small but indigestable by predators), subsequently displaced by the Total Defence concept (partially hinting at preemptive capacity as effective deterrence).

    >> Background is our constant juggling of the existing multipolar balance of power, both within SE Asia (Malaysia & Indonesia) & also the larger Asia-Pacific area (notably China & Japan). That's why we joined SEATO as part of Malaya, then tried to maximize AMDA's unique arrangements (KL's "non-aligned" stand discouraged British staging posts locally for SEATO purposes) during the Self-Government & then Merger eras.

    >> Attitude is an underlying mistrust of overdependence on other countries, esp. post-'65. Singapore's 1st attempt at joint-military cooperation (to foster bilateral trust with Malaysia) was provided for under the Separation provisions, but it quickly crumbled & hasn't recovered since. The only quasi-military security treaty we have today is the FDPA, initially designed to replace AMDA but, from the local perspective, is realistically "valued" most of all as a neutral platform to build security cooperation/confidence across the Causeway! (Beyond the golf games between senior officers, that is.) There's also an argument to be made that the ANZAC links are also comparatively more "precious" than the US's, since historically they, along with Britain, have deployed individual infantry battalions here until the '70s & '80s (under the FDPA, indirectly to aid our "poisonous shrimp" strategy).

    [I also recall reading somewhere that Singapore has some unease about "too-friendly" US military ties, esp. since they're reluctant to reciprocate by sharing or transferring some of technology & arsenal.]

    In short, we have what Tim Huxley calls a very "opaque" defence strategy: train primarily to deter, except there's no explicit rival enemy (officially anyway); exponentially encourage military cooperation & strategic relationships for shared-partner stability, yet fear being swallowed by bigger entities (even the idea of a ASEAN-level military force); occasionally impatient with the pace of regional joint-service training (with Malaysia particularly perceived as the reluctant partner), while attempting to suppress, or at least downplaying, our "seige mentality". The American presence in this part of the world is of paramount importance to us, but the regional subtext is just too sophisticated for us to ally unreservedly with them, like certain countries do.
