    The first day of the Asia Pacific Conference yesterday saw France and Singapore lift their already close defence relations to a new plane with the signing of an agreement for joint studies in aerospace and radar technology.     UPDATE 1-Eurofighter flights to resume, deliveries ready
    Mon November 3, 2003 01:02 PM ET
    By Jason Neely, European Aerospace & Airlines Correspondent

    LONDON, Nov 3 (Reuters) - Builders of the Eurofighter said on Monday the fighter jets would be cleared to fly again within days, allowing first deliveries to resume less than a month after a surprise braking failure shook the programme.

    "Clearance for the DA (development aircraft) fleet to fly is being finalised this afternoon and they should start flying tomorrow or on Wednesday morning," said Ian Bustin, spokesman for the Eurofighter consortium.

    Some test planes have received approval to fly and he said the next step would be to approve production aircraft destined for the air forces of Germany, Italy, Spain and the UK.

    Approval is required from a joint air safety panel of industry and air force officials.

    "Everything is coming back on stream...it's a matter of days," Bustin said, referring to production flight approval.

    A test plane was forced to land without brakes last month, halting flights and triggering a probe of the incident.

    "What they are saying is that it was an anomaly in a landing gear computer," Bustin said, adding the jet involved had resumed flying.

    "The worst-case scenario was that it was a software failure or a general systems failure, we would then have had a much more difficult audit process," he said.

    The setback came as the four nations building the Eurofighter were beginning the first of 620 deliveries planned.

    Germany and Spain have received their first production versions while planes for the UK and Italy are ready.
    "We have three that are ready to go to the UK as soon as the suspension of flying is lifted and two that are due to go to Italy," Bustin said.

    Eurofighter expects to deliver up to 26 of the aircraft this year, part of a first tranche of 148 jets.

    TRANCHE TWO

    Talks about a second tranche have begun amid speculation that budget constraints in the UK and elsewhere could lead to order cutbacks, something Bustin said would not happen.

    "Tranche Two negotiations are not about the numbers, the numbers for Tranche Two are absolutely set in stone," he said. "Tranche Two is about what capability goes on those aircraft and when you start building them."

    A deal must be struck by early 2004 to avoid costly disruptions in production at the four final assembly sites.

    The jets cost from $45 million to $65 million and high-volume production is key to keeping costs down.

    Upgrades in the second tranche are expected to include better missiles, including the Meteor beyond visual range air-to-air missile built by a European consortium led by MBDA.

The Rafale achieved a 1:1 kill ratio (1 Su-35 destroyed for each Rafale lost). The EF-2000 kill ratio was 4.5:1 while the F-22 achieved a ratio of 10:1.     JDW 15 October 2003

    Singapore shortlist fighter bids
    Craig Hoyle/ JDW Aviation EditorLondon

    Singapore 's Defense Science and Technology Agency shortlisted three bidders on 10 October for the final phase of a competition to equip its air force up to 24 new fighters aircraft from late this decade.

    Hinting at a possible departure from its traditional procurement of strike aircraft produced by US manufacturers, the country has identified two European suppliers and just on US proposal as having the potential to meet its future requirements.

    The competition has been reduced to include US Boeing F-15T, the Rafale offered by French team comprising Dassualt Aviation, Snecma and Thales; and the Typhoon manufactured by the four-nation Eurofighter consortium. Boeing F/A-18E/F Super Hornet; Lockheed Martin F-16 Block 60; and Sukhoi Su-30MK were eliminated from the running.

    The Reupblic of Singapore Air FOrce (RSAF) requirements is for an initial squadron of 8 aircraft, with an additional 12-16 fighters to be procured later. "We expect to take delivery of our first aircraft in 2008-09," said MInistry of National Defense (MINDEF) spokeperson on 10 October. The new type will replace the air force's ageing fleet of A-4SU Skyhawk attack aircraft, around 40 of which remain in squadron service.

    "The final [platform] selection will most probably be made in late 2004 or 2005," said the MINDEF source, noting that "the exact timing will depend on the evalution process". This scehdule shows a marked slow-down from earlier proposals, which sought a contract announcement late this year and first aircraft deliveries during 2006-2007 (JDW 6 March 2003). Exact details of RSAF contract - including final numbers and cost - will become clear following release of invitations to tender around mid-November.

    Boeing's F-15T is a further development of the US Air Force's F-15E and the derivative F-15K, which was selected by the Republic of Korea Air FOrce last year to meet a 40-aircaft requirement. The aircraft is the heaviest under consideration; its maximum takeorr weight of almost 37,000kg is about 14,000kg greater than either European platform. Boeing officials hope to build on F-15's recent success in South Korea, where it saw off competition from both the Rafale and Typhoon designs.

    French industry, meanwhile, hopes to secure its first export success for th eRaflae, which lost out to the F-15K in thefinal phase of Seoul's approximately $4.5 billion F-X fighter competition contest. The Dassualt-lead team is believied to be promoting its Rafale mk2 in Signapore , which features more powerfull engines and enhanced avionics equipment, although Dassualt declined to confirm this a s JDW went to press.

    Eurofighter officials are looking to expand Typhoon's export book, following the singapore seater's recent success in securing an Austrian order for 18 aircraft.

    Singapore competition also includes requirmeents f ro extensive technology transfer and could request the oversea deployment of one squadron of aircraft to assist in training activites. The RSAF already maintains Skyhawks and F-16 detachments in France and US, respectively, and bid leader BAE Systems has offered Singapore the opportunity to operate Typhoons in the UK.

    Although its elimination from the contest will come as a blow to LOckheed Martin, which has been contracted supply the RSAF with 58 F-16 since the late 1980s, the company could benefit in the longer term through Signapore;s interest in th eF-35 Joint Striek Fighter (JSF). Singaproe is security co-operation participants in the Lockheed MArtin-led JSF project, following its $50 million commitment to the programme's system devlopment and demonstration phase earlier this year(JDW 5 March)
