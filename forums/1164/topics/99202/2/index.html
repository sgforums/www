    actually, i am currently serving my national service... i'm on a 10-day leave but most of my friends are busy studying for exams, so i spend most of my day sleeping and watching crap on tv (i haven't watched tv for over a year now, come to think of it).

    ok, first what exactly do we mean by "abuse"? corporal punishment, i.e. slapping, kicking, punching soldiers?? or hazing, i.e. forcing new privates to undergo an "initiation process" (which may or may not include corporal punishment)???

    well to answer your question, officially corporal punishment and hazing is illegal in the korean military... unofficially though, hehe...hazing is still thiriving, and corporal punishment, though not as bad as it used to be, is still around... though the severity of both really differs from unit to unit. some units are very brutal, while others are reputed to be quite slack.

    basically, it's an old tradition in our military, passed down from senior enlisted to junior. eventually, in time, the "juniors" become the "seniors".... and the process repeats itself.

    i won't go into too many details on a public forum, though i will say that the first few weeks after i arrived at my unit were the worst in my life. compared to the hazing i received at my unit, basic training was a breeze. i was pushed to the limits of my mental and physical endurance... each night when i was finally allowed to go to bed i wanted to cry because the hazing was so severe. i actually became sick with fever and vomitting, but some seniors thought i was malingering, so they kept on punishing me until i "realised" that no, actually, i wasn't sick!!!

    these days, i still shudder when i think of the hazing i went through as a private. (if u really wanna know the details i can pm you, i guess.)

    right now, i'm a corporal in the army. for the record, i have never been hit, although when i was in basic training i was often handled physically, like being shoved to the ground, lifted by the collar and thrown, and choked (once! ) by my drill sergeant. but i escaped the worst because i was quiet and kept my head down.

    personally, i don't approve of corporal punishment - nothing too severe at least. it's risky - no one ever died from doing too many pushups, flutterkicks, sleep deprivement, threats, etc. - but someone can be seriously injured if beaten up... i know this one guy who was beaten on an almost daily basis when he was a private - he eventually landed in hospital! (he recovered and left in one piece - no permanent damage, lucky for him.)

    if one really needs to use the @sshole approach, i've observed that just swearing and threating to beat the shit out of an unmotivated soldier while waving an entrenching tool usually works well enough! either that, or make him go into a modified pushup position, except that his head is on the ground and his hands behind his back... he'll be crying for mercy in no time!!

    hmmm, so did the hazing toughen me? i think it did, but mostly mentally. physically, i was actually worse than before - the constant fear, stress, and punishments took a toll on my health; i became very sick (and when i reported that i was feeling "ill", my seniors responded by doubling the intensity of the hazing!!!) but one thing i learned: the human body (and mind) is more resilient than we think... and what doesn't kill you, really does make you stronger! (it's a quote from Nietzche, btw)

    NOTE: the "barraks room" hazing i described here is to be understood as different from the training, which is a completely different animal. i'm not from combat arms so i can't speak with any authority on their training, though from what i hear and observe it really, really sucks to be infantry. i work as an interpreter for a bunch of occifers, so i don't really have any "field training" to speak of besides basic training and the occasional roadmarch, NBC training, basic skills training, range, etc...

    the korean army is already hell to begin with....!!!!!! confined to base for 2 years (there are no "weekend pass", only a few "leaves" per soldier for duration of the 2 years), sh!tty living conditions, even more sh!tty pay (we get allowance of maybe SG$20-40 a month depending on rank), and a very brutal, if neccessary, hazing system.

    i had a friend who volunteeered for Marine Corps infantry; i thought he was insane, though i admired his spunk at the same time. if i was an american, maybe i'd volunteer for combat arms in u.s. military, but not in the korean army.... but that's just me

    Interesting read this thread. However see little evidence of rank and file, reservist or even instructors/trainers with their side of story.

    I am on last year of active cycle (retiring as reservist next April), was BMT instructor, am infantry pioneer section commander.

    BMT, when I was active, may seem like hell without the drama today. We had unwritten rules, no physical contact with trainees, no abuse of family, economic, religious or racial origins. What we did was more towards mental strengthening. Vocal abuse was after a time curtailed because foul language was deemed 'inappropriate'. There was still a lot of noise given the need to condition recruits to 'do' not 'second guess then react' to basic orders like 'cover fire' instead of taking cover.

    Corporal punishment was at platoon/section level. Individual attention was only for special attention cases, known trouble makers and obvious slackers. In my 2 year and close to 320 recruits (trained within my platoon), we only had one case of 'fail BMT'. He was on SAF regular contract and thought he could just slide through BMT.

    Our creed was simple, what we taught had to give each and every recruit basic survival skills in a shooting war. Basic weapon skills, discipline to follow proper orders (regardless how great the inhibitions they may have), mental stamina to carry on beyond their phyiscal limits and a fierce pride in their unit, their buddies and themselves.

    In ITD half the battles were mental, handling Scholar Batches, Obese (average weight at start was 85kg). Physical training was graduated but mental conditioning was from day 1.

    I never agree to hazing as it is determental. A proper sequence of "instruct", "warn" then 'punish' works within a structured framework to train soliders. The army has no time for another dunking tub incident which serves no-one except to make commando trainers feel like 'lords of the tub' with no military value whatsoever. Find it hard to believe that the trainers at 1Cdo still believe that guardsmen can benefit what was originally designed for Ranger/SAS troops.
