    Ever wondered why the Americans hung on to the M-16 series for decades long after its service life?

    Of course this can be directly attributed to 2 failed programs: the ambitious search for a caseless weapon and then the OICW/XM8 failed to produce a successor.

    But it is also because the M-16 is actually a pretty damn good weapon inspite of its self-fouling gas system. It had turned out to be one of the best rifle when it comes to ergonomics. And its clean simple lines had made it easy to attach things to. (So much so that someone once said they look like christmas trees with all the misc attachments.)

    Another proof is the civilian versions. Private arms manufacturer in the US can choose to manufacture any weapon they want for the ciivilian market but the M-16 variants seemed to be the most popular. There must be hundreds of M-16 variants out there, more than any other weapon type in history.

    With the XM-8 now dead, and the known dislike of bullpups by US armed forces, one wonders why they don't just adopt a M-16 with a piston drive. There are already a few versions being made and marketed - among them H&K. I think such a weapon will beat the HK G36, now considered one of the best conventional layout weapon.

    Apart from the M-16, the only other weapon I have handled is an MP-5. Once at a police exhitbition and then also played a lot of combat games with an airsoft in Hong Kong. It is ergonomically very good especially with a standard fixed stock. The only thing I didn't like about it was the lever-type mag release. I prefer the M-16 push-button release. Though the MP-5 also has the push-button together with the lever, the button is impossible to reach without first removing your hand from the pistol-grip. It's probably there as an alternative just in case the lever is broken off or something.

    The M-16 push-button makes for very fast reloading. In an emergency you can just push the button and let the empty mag drop as you reach for a fresh one. I used to do this during CTSS rundown as there is an instructor following to pick up the empties for you

    Ah... Ultimax 100.

    Very acccurate when fired in very short bursts. Tried it several times. But once during live firing I got a double feed that took the 2 instructors more than 10 mins to dislodge the 2 jammed rounds.

    I don't know if the 100-round drum jams often since I've never even touched one before. But several other nations' special forces like Indonesia and Philippines are using it and I have yet to hear any reports on its performance. Most reviews I read on the net seems to sing nothing praises about the weapon, but never specifically mentioned the drum.

    Of course these weapon reviews have to be taken with a big pinch of salt - including those from Jane's.

    Why?

    Because they were all done by journalists of unknown military background. Furthermore, they were written after a day at the range, not under protracted real or even simulated combat situations.

    However, many reports said that it passed US Army trials with flying colours but for various reasons weren't exactly what they were looking for.

    In my opinion, the Ultimax will not see much further upgrading as I'm pretty sure the next SAW from ST will be a belt-feed. The reason I suspect this is because a drum/mag fed weapon is not the money cow CIS thought it will turn out to be.

    I had a discussion re drum vs belt feed on other forums. There were many opinions against drum mag; like you can't reload it by hand and this creates logitiscal problems. But you can't exactly hand link a belt of ammo either.

    Both drum and belt 5.56 ammo had to be separately supplied different from the riflemen.

    But one guy made a very good point against drums: You have to carry the empty drums with you and they are not exactly featherweights.

    Whereas with the belt option, after firing off a belt there is nothing residual for the SAW gunner to carry. You get resupplied with new belts and you're good to go. But with drums, you have to return the empty drums to be reloaded back at base camp.

    But again, belt feeding has the problem of slower combat weapon reloading and exposed ammo can get dirty, get hooked on stuff etc. And most belt-feeding mechanisms add more weapon weight. But definitely less prone to jamming.
