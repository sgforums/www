    WW2 was when a lot of new weapons and concepts were invented and used for the first time in combat. Among them was the MLRS.

    However, many weapon concepts had since been made obsolete now 60 years after WW2. Among them are the battleship, the towed anti-tank gun, the half track etc etc.

    But the MLRS remains in service despite its shortcomings of being less accurate compared to howitzers and mortars. But technology has now made the rocket many times more accurate than its predecessor in WW2.

    The advantage of the rocket over the arty gun is being able to carry a big warhead - up to 300mm inn WW2 - and being able to unleash a massive salvo (if you have enough of them) in a very short time.

    The advantage of 100 rockets all impacting within a minute as opposed to many guns shooting 100 shells over several minutes is significant.

    This kind of massive bombardment had been used by the Russians to successfully halt many enemy attacks of infantry and armour across a broad front. Of course, this advantage is offset by its lack of accuracy and you need a lof of launchers and a big supply of rockets.

    But, apparently in WW2 at least, rockets are much easier to produce and still cheaper than the whole howitzer/shell package. To put it crudely, rockets - whether air launched or land based - were nothing more than flying bombs with a motor and fins attached.

    England, US, Russia and Germany were all big users of rockets in WW2. The Russians were the biggest user, the Germans had the best rockets (I'm not talking about the V-2), the British mounted them on ships and the Americans even improvised launch racks mounted on on Sherman tank turrets.

    Only Japan didn't use musch rockets cause she didn't have the capacity to make them in significant numbers.

    Western countries today are not big fan of MLRS and though they are retained in service, howitzers and mortars are preferred.
