    In what situation can you discharge a firearm in public?

    In what sitaution can you shoot another person in self-defense?
    The laws governing the justified use of deadly force in self-defense vary from state to state, but can usually be distilled into three primary requirements:

    1. The threat has be of sufficient magnitude as to cause you to be in fear for your life.

    2. The threat must have ignored any and all demands by you to not approach any closer.

    3. It is not possible for you to run away from the threat or to take any other reasonable action(s) to de-escalate the situation.

    * they did not obey your request to move away and continue to approach you - but unarmed, can you leagally shoot them? Or do you have to fire a warning shot in the air first? (Is it legal to fire a warning shot?)
    If all they did was approach us and nothing else, then I'd be in legal hot soup if I shot them, especially if we had the option of running away. A warning shot is a definite no-no as that would land you in jail on a charge of reckless endangerment, even if it did succeed in de-escalating the situation.

    * one reaches into his coat but you don't know what he's reaching for? He ignores your request to freeze. Can you legally shoot him straightaway or fire a warning shot? Or do you have to wait till you see what he's got in his hand? It could be a flask of cheap whisky.
    Exactly. He could just be reaching for a flask of liquor. Or a weapon. Shooting him for the mere act of reaching into his pocket would give the prosecuting attorney a field day at my expense.

    However, if he pulls out a gun or knife and points it at me while still demanding money, then all bets are off.

    * one of them pulls out a knife and continues to approach you? Warning shot or shoot straightaway?
    Two in the chest, one in the head, then call the police and deal with the unpleasant legal consequences.

    My friend's car was parked at the extreme end of the ramp, with the negroes between us and the stairwell (the only possible escape route). They continued to approach us in a menacing fashion, demanding cash. You could argue that we could possibly have de-escalated the situation by handing our wallets over to them, but what guarantee would we have that they wouldn't inflict grievous bodlily harm on us after that?

    Precisely. That's why it should only be employed as the very last resort in self-defense.

    It is a double-edged sword because the use of deadly force, even if justified, can potentially land you in a legal nightmare. Your firearm will most likely be confiscated by the police and never returned to you. During the trial, the prosecuting attorney will likely try to paint you as a trigger-happy gun-toting maniac who shouldn't even be allowed to carry a mechanical pencil, let alone a firearm. You'll have to hire a competent criminal lawyer, at a cost of several thousands of dollars, to defend you. And even if the trial clears you, the family of the deceased bad guy will most likely institute a wrongful death civil lawsuit against you, and you'll once again have to retain a lawyer to defend you.

    The instructor in our handgun safety class pointed out that a permit holder who had used his gun in self-defense would, on average, spend upwards of US$20,000 in legal expenses in the aftermath.

    Damn lawyers...

    However, he also pointed out that the overwhelming majority of confrontations involving permit holders drawing their pistols in self-defense, ended without shots being fired (as was the case with my confrontation in the parking ramp).

    So if I'm cornered, no escape route, being approached by 2 men, at what distance can I consider myself in grave danger and open fire in self defense?
    That would depend on the distance the bad guy would have to be at to inflict bodily harm on you. If you're cornered and the bad guy pulled a gun and pointed it at you from a distance, you'd probably be justified in employing deadly force in defending yourself. If he pulled out a knife at that same distance, the court will likely take a dim view of your shooting him when he wasn't within range to do you harm. However, if he continued to advance towards you with the knife drawn and ignored demands from you to back off, I'd say you would be within your purview to use deadly force.
