    I'm not sure I'm following you here.

    When a pistol is operated in double action (DA) mode, pulling the trigger both cocks the hammer/striker and then releases it. A single action (SA) trigger just releases a previously-cocked hammer/striker.

    Compared to an SA trigger pull, a DA trigger pull is significantly heavier and longer as the hammer/striker spring is being compressed while the trigger is drawn back. An SA pull is usually very short and light as it only has to move the sear a very short distance to release the already-cocked hammer.

    The effort required to draw a DA trigger tends to jerk the pistol, negatively impacting accuracy. Pistols shot in the SA mode tend to be more accurate, due to the lighter and shorter trigger pull.

    A pistol with poorly-zeroed sights will not shoot to the point of aim, regardless of whether it is SA or DA.

    Regarding point #2, I'd be curious to know what kind of DA/SA pistol has the ability to do what you've described. I'm not doubting that there might be some pistols that can be cocked in the manner described, but I have personally never come across a DA/SA semi-auto or revolver in which you can cock the hammer or striker (that is, put the pistol into SA mode) by simply pulling and then releasing the trigger.

    In other words, if you initiated a DA pull from the hammer down/striker unloaded position, pulled the trigger back to the point just prior to hammer/striker release, and then eased off on the trigger, the hammer/striker would return to its fully forward position. The one exception to this would be if the pistol was equipped with a "hammer drop safety notch" which would cause the hammer to stop just before contacting the firing pin (a safety feature to prevent a pistol from discharging if the cocked hammer were to accidentally slip off the sear).

    However, if you did in fact use such a pistol in the manner you described during your NPCC stint, then you were effectively shooting the pistol in SA mode. Think about it: the first part of the trigger pulled fully cocked the hammer and allowed you to ease up on the trigger without uncocking the hammer, and when you subsequently pulled the trigger, you were releasing an already-cocked hammer.

    For DA semi-autos, yes. The recoiling slide cocks the hammer/striker and puts the pistol in SA mode for subsequent shots.

    For DA revolvers, no (with the possible exception of the Mateba, which I believe is a revolver that operates like a semi-auto).

    I should mention that some pistols are double action-only (DAO), meaning that there is no provision for pre-cocking the hammer or striker. Consequently, the trigger pulls of DAO pistols are usually very heavy. My daily carry gun, a Czechoslovakian CZ100 chambered in 40S&W, is DAO.

    That's a loaded question! I would say the effective range would be those distances at which you can consistently hit your intended target with reasonable accuracy.     Yep, the world is full of crims not like good old Singapore.

    At a close range, say 3m, would the small .357 do as much damage as the big .357?
    That would depend on the weight and type of the projectile.

    Both calibers use projectiles of approximately the same diameter (0.355" and 0.357" for the 357 Sig and 357 Magnum, respectively), and projectile weights can vary widely. Commercial 357 Sig ammo can be found with projectiles ranging from 125 gr. to 147 gr.; 357 Magnum ammo is available from 110 gr. all the way up to 180 gr. Also, a hollowpoint projectile is apt to do more damage than an FMJ one, if it properly expands in the target medium.

    Commercial 125 gr. 357 Sig ammo is typically loaded to a muzzle velocity of around 1400 fps, with 158 gr. 357 Magnum ammo at approximately 1500 fps.

    (By the way, gr. = grain, and 7000 gr. = 1 pound.)

    I am sure testing using ballistic gelatin has been performed to ascertain the effectiveness (as measured by penetration, expansion and projectile mass retention) of both calibers, but I currently do not have that information. However, at 3 meters, a well-placed shot from either caliber is likely to disable a normal human (i.e., one who isn't high on pain-suppressing drugs).

    Next I would love to own a shotgun - I know no one argues with a shotgun.
    Except that it's rather difficult to conceal a shotgun in public.

    How do you qualify for a license to carry a concealed weapon? Are you in law enforcement? Or are you a CZ Armourer as your signature indicates?
    The procedure for applying for a handgun carry permit varies from state to state, but the requirements are usually quite standardized: you have to attend a state-sanctioned firearms training/safety course and submit an application to the city or county office or LE agency. A criminal background check will be performed, and if it comes back clean, you are a candidate for a handgun permit.

    I say candidate because there is a wrinkle in the process. In some states, handgun permits are given on a "may issue" basis, meaning that you have to show a need for carrying a handgun in public, even if you meet all the other requirements. In these states, the issuing authority (usually the county sheriff) is the sole authority in determing whether the need is valid or not. Most law enforcement agencies in this country are vehemently anti-2A and do not want firearms in the hands of private individuals. Consequently, most handgun carry permit applications in these "may issue" states are denied for the flimsiest of reasons.

    Other states, including Minnesota, have "shall issue" handgun permit laws, meaning that you cannot be denied a permit if you meet all the prerequisites.

    And yes, I am one of the several CZ-certified armorers servicing the Upper Midwest region. I am not in law enforcement, but I do occasionally work on CZ pistols carried by LEOs.

    Some states have "open carry" handgun permit laws, while others require that the handgun be concealed. Even if the carry law makes no specific provision that the handgun be concealed, it is still a good idea not to carry openly in public as people around you may react negatively to seeing a firearm. LE agencies in states with "open carry" handgun laws have gone on record as saying they may not be able to legally prevent you from carrying openly, but they'll nail you on other charges like creating a public disturbance.

    Open carry can also make you a potential target for criminals. Consider this scenario: if an armed criminal walks into a convenience store with the intent of robbing it, and he sees a customer openly carrying a firearm, who do you think the criminal will shoot first?

    It's best to be discreet and carry concealed, even if the state allows open carry.

    As for waving a pistol around and firing randomly into the air, well, you can certainly do that... until the first round from an LEO's gun takes you down.

    Here is a link to the FAQ section of Minnesota's handgun carry law. It is representative of similar laws in other states, and might help dispel any misconceptions you have about carrying handguns here.

    In most states, any law-abiding citizen or PR can legally purchase and own firearms approved for sale in this country, without need of any kind of permit. But if you want to carry a hangun (concealed or openly) in public, you need the relevant permit (the exception being states like Vermont which allow you to carry openly without the need for any kind of permit).
