    Given that, I think what they're attempting to do with PT-91M makes sense

    Put yourself in their shoes ... in the event of a conflict with Singapore and the SAF pours Northwards across the Straits of Johore.

    The TDM is outnumbered in sheer numbers for armoured platforms. This makes an open meeting engagement folly.

    What they do have is strategic depth ... lots of it. They can afford to trade space for time.

    Imagine a signature managed PT-91M sitting in prepared firing postions overwatching likely avenues of advance. Using it's small silhouette, signature management and close cover of vegetation, it is likely to remain "invisible" until it moves or fire.

    Say it conducts a brief ambush engagement, reverse into the cover of the reverse slope and scoots to a prepared fallback position. This is where the improved powerpack, transmission and Diehl tracks start to make sense.

    Given the likely engagement ranges, a hit by a KE APFSDS round from either party (PT-91M/Leo 2) is bound to mean game over for the other. Hence the upgraded FCS start to make sense too.

    If the TDM can string together a zone of such penny packet ambush positions, they can quite effectively stall the momentum of any advance, assuming the SAF remains casualty conservative.

    Heck, imagine if whole advance comes to a temporary stop because the commander of the lead element doesn't like the bloody nose he got and wants to regroup and let the following elements move up ... can you say Astros time? Enough to give the commanders of the lead and following elements a bloody headache.

    This trading of depth buys them time to find other means to resolve the conflict.

    Just one hitch to thier plan though... the PT-91M is best fighting from prepared positions (in the event of rapidly changing battlespace), due to its limited gun elevation and depression. If it needs to employ a fluid defence by using non-prepared positions, it would find itself unable to "hull down" properly compared to western models.

    This limits it's ability to make full use of whatever terrain it has to play a battle. For example, the limited depression of the soviet-style tanks was well known by the Israelis when they often engaged Arab armour in places where they knew their enemy tanks had to fully climb a berm in order to get a proper shot while the Israelis could hunker down behind a berm and get off their shots exposing only the turret.

    Hence this tank would actually be unable to fight from behind a reverse slope, unless it was a prepared position that had taken out the slope.

    But I'm sure the MAF have though about it, and have found ways to make up for it. Dozer blades for their tanks to dig their own positions maybe? But in a fluid battle it might be totally different.
