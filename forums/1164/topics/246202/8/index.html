    AFAIK, the real difference between the Â“generationsÂ” of tank FCS has been the range of the Thermal Sight, and not the gun stabilization system, which has been already perfected since the first Gulf War.

    The stabilization upgrades to the T-72 design were more of playing catch-up to modern standards then actually excelling in them. Once again the limited elevation and depression of the main gun is an issue when it comes to stabilization over rough terrain or when climbing and going down slopes or even bumps on the road- thereÂ’s only so much you can do with a design that was intended to be used en masse with less regard to individual unit ability and more on numbers to overrun the enemy.

    Aiming the gun and keeping it on target was not a problem, but the issue of detecting your enemy before he sees you is another, hence the emphasis on getting increasingly powerful sights.

    On the other hand this might be a moot argument (for us at least), given the armies that have been attempting to go network centric have been putting an emphasis on Â“sensor-weaponÂ” separation. That is, the separation of the platforms who do the detection and targeting from the weapons platforms that do the actual engagement.

    This means that instead of having to manually acquire your targets yourself, this information is streamed to you from external sensors (other units closer to the target, UAVsÂ… etc) via network and your course of action is then decided.

    On the other hand, while the long-range aquistion of the enemy is great in the latest-generations of Thermal Imaging Systems, they are outgrowing the range of their weapons platform (especially on use with tanks). For example, even with the ability of the SAVAN 15 to acquire a target at 5km is impressive, the tank using it (assuming a PT-91) has to close to within 2 km in order to use its rounds effectively. And within that range even a previous generation tank (with a Gulf War 1 era TIS) would have detected them by then (and itÂ’ll be a matter of who shoots first).

    Tactically, the advantage of the latest Thermal Sights has not been that it allows you to engage the enemy first (unlike the first Gulf War where coalition armour could engage Iraqi tanks without even them being detected), but that it allows you to see the enemy first and take appropriate action before he receives the same intelligence (assuming he has a weaker FCS sight).

    HOWEVER, such a penetrator is also more vulnerable to yawing forces and snapping under them, and hence some more modern packages like the armour "wedge" of the Leo2a5-6 were (supposedly) designed to introduce a penetrating long rod to yawing forces, (hopefully) snapping it and making it far less effective.

    Hence the reason why 120-5mm penetrators are reaching their limits. With current materials there's only so strong a dart you can make for the diameter-length ratio. The longer and thinner the better but the projectile also becomes weaker versus other forces. And I think we are approaching the limits of what current APFSDS technology can offer)

    Hence recent efforts to improve the 120 performance involved increasing the length of the gun (L55, same penetrator but with more force, so there's no need to use a slimmer dart) and the like, and less on tweaking the round.
