    For those not aware -- the Bionix ICV was one of four finalists for the Stryker competition. I have even heard reports that it was the runner-up. So don't pray-pray with our vehicles!

    The reasons why the Bionix ICV lost to the LAVIII are quite simple:

    Firstly, it is clear that the US Army leadership wanted wheels, not tracks, due to the higher strategic mobility they provide. Wheeled vehicles burn less fuel per km and travel faster on roads. Therefore, tracked vehicles are normally loaded on wheeled carriers for long-distance moves and they wanted a vehicle that could self-deploy. However, the LAVIII that won the competition gained a lot of weight on the way to becoming the Stryker, mostly in the form of electronics and armor. Since the original vehicle was not designed to take so much weight, the Strykers are bound to break down more than expected, as they are experiencing now.

    The irony is that the Bionix ICV was based on the much heavier Bionix 25 and 40/50 that we use, so even if it were loaded down with the Americans' toys it should still have worked well. The Bionix ICV was also the most heavily armored of the finalists (despite the removal of the Bionix 25's applique), so it would have also needed less add-on armor. A Bionix-based Stryker might have turned out to actually be easier to support than the current Stryker in the end. Sad really.

    Secondly, ST simply did not know how to play the game. Weapons purchases in the US must go through Congress, and all the congressmen want some of the weapon to be built in their state to create jobs. ST tried very hard to arrange it so that the Bionix would be built all over the US, and for former US Army generals to back their bid, but they simply were not as good as the LAV team. This was their first time fighting for such a big contract, playing in the premier league of the arms industry. They just did not have the experience to succeed politically. Too bad.

    Thirdly, the US Army wanted the LAVIII -- the competition was for show. The requirements and rules kept changing throughout the competition when the tracked vehicles handily thrashed the wheeled ones, with the Bionix and MTVL in front. It was really a farce.

    Why the US Army did this is still anybody's guess. Some people say that the US Army CoS at the time (Gen. Shinseki) did not understand armor. He left armor early in his career. He ended up forcing a wheeled vehicle on them they did not want. The Stryker is still disliked by tankees in the US Army, though loved by its own crews. The lack of understanding also shows when you see how he gave everybody in the US Army black berets, which were formerly a status item worn only by armor, then airborne. That was a real slap in the face for these outfits -- imagine if we gave everybody red or khaki berets. There was probably some infighting somewhere during the Stryker programme.
