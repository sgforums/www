    ok, for the above picture..i wanna share 1 way to do panel lines..

    first of all, i am damn lazy..i don't want to go thru the process of washing..
    second, i do not want my panel lines to be too obvious..makes my plane looks cartoonish..so i experimented and found out this method to be the easiest so far..and fulfill my requirements..

    1) spray a layer of clear gloss to the whole body..(since i use lacquer based paint, the gloss doesn't give much effect, but i want to add a layer to seal in the original coat of paint)

    2) use a mechanical pencil..sharpen the tip using pen knife..then slowly take your time to draw out the panel lines..as for the lead, i am using 3B..change according to your preference..

    3) Use eraser and lightly rub over the surface according to the flow of air(ie, stroke from front to back)..this will randomly lighten some of the lines..and also erase those lines you accidentally draw...

    i found out that this method is quite useful..the difference before and after can be seen in the pic above..the left side i havent do yet, the right side, i did the panel lines already..and it's fast..took my total 15 mins to do 1 wing..

    Hi Unidentified, to paint a good set of tank tracks, you have to understand the kind of abuse they go through. Here is what I usually do...

    As Centurion has said, first paint them in Gun Metal, as they are made of steel. Second, wash rust colour onto the nooks and crannies of the tracks, as rust tends to gather on these areas since moisture gets trapped there and theres no contact with the ground during movemen .

    Next, wash (yes wash not dry brush) a layer of soil/mud colour onto the tracks. Washing will ensure that the soil/mud looks like it got onto the nooks and crannies of the track instead of sticking to the surface which is what happens.

    Finally, using silver, dry brush the track guides and the portions of the track links that jut out, because on the real thing these same portions come into contact against hard ground and road and tend to become polished and smooth.

    Tedious? You bet. But from my NS days, thats what I observed on the tracks of tanks/AFVs that have been used.

    You can try pastel chalks that Centurion suggested, but my personal preference is Gunze's acrylic weathering line, which includes colours like rust/mud/sand etc. Because these paints come with pigments inside that when dry, gives the weathered models the 'feel' of the real thing (run your finger along a part painted with Gunze's Rust and it feels like the part has really rusted)

    Originally posted by ShuLL:
    hmm..but wont the tamiya x20a thinner damage the gloss layer underneath?
    x-20 A is acrylic thinner. As safe as splashing water on the paint layer.

    It is safe for both lacquer and enamel based paint.

    As a general thumb of rule,
    You may spray acrylic paint/thinner over anything

    Enamel paint and be sprayed over both acrylic and lacquer.
    Enamal thinner will eat into cured enamal paint and sometimes into acrylic paints. So if you want to do enamal washing on enamal and acrylic surfaces, spray gloss coat first.

    Lacquer is the worst. You cannot spray it over enamal or acrylic surfaces. Straight away strip of the paint.However it is resistant to enamal thinner. So you can do washing without much preperation.

    for that blurred part, get grade 2000 sand paper and send down the frosted part. Den use tooth paste and polish a few rounds to remove the major scratch marks. After that, get tamiya polishing compound to finish up the micro scratch marks. Your canopy will be shinier than new.

    In the future, don't use cement to glue your canopy. Use white glue instead. It can be purchased from any stationary shops. It doesn't bond extremely strong, but it holds the canopy in place. It dries clear and is water soluble. Only thing, don't push on the glass surface. It will drop out easily.
