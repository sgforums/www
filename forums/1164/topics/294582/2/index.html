    Before talking about strategic targets, you have to contextualise the conflict. Different conflicts = different targets.

    In this case, i'll evaluate a balanced conflict between neighbouring powers, meaning that both sides are equally susceptible to whatever strategies are in place.

    Within this context, the first target in a preemptive strike would be the anti-air and retaliatory capabilities. How are you going to take down anything (comm/logistics/hardware) before removing the defences ? And even if you Could take down the infrastructure, what you wouldnt want is a full scale retaliation on your territory.

    One example here could be operation desert storm. Alot of initial coalition targets included SAM/AA sites, SCUD launchers and ground based fighter aircraft. The fact that they successfully obliterated the Iraqi defences/long range retaliation capabilities allowed for their subsequent air superiority.

    So think about this. A country without long range weapons systems, without air strike capability, without air defence capability. So what if they have communications ? By the time they get their ground/naval forces anywhere within striking distance, their infrastructure (including communications) would have been pounded to the ground by enemy air power.

    And what does that leave ? Afew army groups and naval flotillas in the middle of enemy territory with no communications and coordination = many dead soldiers.

    You all should try this game called "Cuban Missile Crisis" or also known as "The Day After."

    Very nice game. Realistic units from the Vietnam war Era, so you'd see a lot of WW2 and Post WW2 units.

    Game play features 4 factions, Anglo American, German Franco, USSR, China. In each faction, are contesting over a region in a strategic map where you will choose where to send your detachments/companies/brigades to attack.

    Targets include scenario objectives that advances you to the next chapter, or airfields (allows you to project air power), oil production & storage facilities (gives and allows storage of oil), parts factories that produces spares and ammunition.

    Upon attacking a target, the game play will change mode into a Command and Conquer type of game play where you command the actual troops on the ground. Those familiar with the blitzkrieg series of games would find it familiar. Line of sight and high ground is very important, and so our self-propelled howitzers to avoid counter-battery fires (AI does this DAMN well). Tanks and infantry are vulnerable without each but are awesome in the attack when supporting each other.

    In all, if you like some sense of realistic limitations in a war game, this is it. You cannot attack limitlessly in a turn, since every engagement will use up oil, spares and ammunition. Without a reserve force to counter enemy penetrations, you'd find yourself losing all your strategic assets as well.

    To sum it up, it emphasizes a lot on the truth of warfare. "Amateurs think tactics, Professionals think Logistics."
