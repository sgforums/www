    What is the role of a Section Assault Weapon?

    Â• static automatic fire in support of assault/defence
    Â• mobile automatic fire during assault
    Â• conduct patrols
    Â• conduct recce
    Â• perform OP
    Â• set up ambush

    In these situations, the Section has to have strong automatic firepower and yet be highly mobile and be able to bug out quickly if things go wrong.

    During WW1, the concept of SAW was born with infantry using mag-fed LMG like the Lewis, Chauchat as light support MGÂ’s and also as mobile automatic fire during the assault phase.

    During WW2, famous LMG like the British Bren, Russian DPM and US BAR were used extensively and successfully in the SAW role. They were all mag-fed automatic weapons designed on the outset as a LMG.

    Interestingly enough, the Germans concentrated on developing belt-fed MG-34 and MG-42 GPMG instead and had no real mag-fed LMG other than those they captured. Of course they later came up with 2 full-auto capable assault rifles the MP-43 and the FG-42.

    The whole world today still cannot agree on what is the best system for a SAW. The only thing that is universal is that the SAW uses the same caliber ammo as the basic assault rifle.

    Roughly, there are 3 general types being used for the role of SAW today:

    Â• Option ONE
    This is an Â“upgunnedÂ” version of the basic assault rifle. Usually the barrel is heavier and longer and a bipod is added. It is also usually converted to fire from the open bolt.

    Examples in use are the British LSW, which is a Â“heavy versionÂ” SA-80. Another is the RPK, Â“heavy versionÂ” of the AK. Both use box magazines of about 30 to 40 rounds. Another is the M-16HBAR.

    The advantage of this system is the lightweight compared to other SAWs and the ease of training as it is basically the same weapon as the basic assault rifle.

    The disadvantage is the small ammo capacity. 30 to 40 rounds on bursts or full-auto is not much firepower. The gunner will be changing mags so often he will be quite ineffective. And especially if the design is a bull-pup like the SA-80 or SAR-21, changing mags while prone on bipod must be quite difficult, I imagine.

    Â• Option TWO
    This is a dedicated belt-feed LMG design that (mostly) has magazine-feed as an option. Examples are plenty with the most famous being the Minimi, Negev etc.

    This concept is different from the Option ONE as this is basically a Â“scaled downÂ” GPMG as opposed to a Â“scaled upÂ” assault rifle.

    Belt feed offers great firepower as you can have a belt of 200 rounds on the weapon on the static support role. For the assault role the belted ammo comes in convenient canvas pouches that prevents snagging or ammo becoming dirty.

    The disadvantage is that in the assault role, the weight here is heavier and reloading a belt under fire can be quite tricky.

    The Â“combo-driveÂ” of mag and belt feed is unbeatable, though.

    14 Feb 05, 21:14
    Â• Option THREE
    This is a dedicated magazine-feed LMG in the tradition of the old greats like the Bren, BAR, DPM, Lewis etc. It is not an adaptation of an assault rifle or belt-feed GPMG etc but designed as a LMG from day one.

    Although the mag-fed LMG was very popular in both world wars, today this is not the case. I only know of one example in use today and thatÂ’s the Ultimax. Nearly every modern LMG design is dual belt/mag feed like the Minimi, Negev or just belt-feed like the CETME Ameli.

    The Ultimax had been sold (in not very big numbers) to many countries like Croatia, Indonesia and Philippines and has a 100-round drum magazine. The Ultimax as we all know is a very lightweight LMG that fires from the open-bolt and is allegedly very accurate.

    The apparent advantage over Option ONE (eg SA-80) is of course the big firepower with the 100-round drum.

    The apparent advantage over Option TWO (eg Minimi) is the lighter weight and maneuverability and ease of mag-feeding.

We often forget that the Ultimax drum alone weighs quite a bit too. I think this is because most of us have never used a drum for excercise.

    So why doesn't SAF issue drums for training? I can assure you, it is not because the drums have to be machine loaded. GPMG belts are machine-loaded as well, but that has never stopped SAF issuing them. No army will trust not having every part of each piece of equipment fully tested by all troops during training. The fact that we don't train with drums tells you a lot.

    I am sure the reason is because the drums are very delicate, prone to jam and posibly still prone to dropping off the weapon. If used by SAF soldiers for training all these faults would show up and would be bad for overseas sales. The only times I have seen the SAW with the drum is during weapon tests by potential foreign buyers at the range or during NDP or other publicity pictures.

    As to your point there being no need for a SAW to have high firepower...

    Well, we didn't invent the concept or the need for a SAW with high auto firepower on the move. So let's not now decide that we don't need high firepower for a SAW.

    As to relying on the GPMG instead... because of its weight and size and the fact that it is normally crew-served by at least 2 people, the GPMG is not suited to firing on the move. Furthermore the GPMG is allocated to one per platoon, and we have 3 to 4 sections per platoon. So how can one GPMG provide sustained covering fire for every section during an assault?

    But the fact that the Ultimax is mag-fed has contributed to its simplicity, cheap price tag and super light weight. A belt-fed gun has all these fiddly parts that drive the belt and feed trays just like a GPMG. On top of that add the mag-feed option and the weapon weight will rise significantly - further reducing its chance of overseas sales.

    But then, having to carry the empty drums with you after expending the ammo is a huge drawback as the Ultimax drums aren't light. Whereas with the belt system, nothing to carry after expending the ammo belt.

    But it is now very obvious to the people at ST that they have missed the boat on big overseas sales of the Ultimax as everyone has follwed the US to adopt the Minimi or another belt-fed LMG. The Ultimax is an orphan being the only mag-fed LMG available.

    The only countries confirmed to have bought the Ultimax are mostly poor third world 5.56mm-user nations like Croatia, Philippines and Indonesia. (A forum friend from Bosnia or some former East-bloc country said their army bought SAR-80 from us.)

    If these countries are using the Combloc 7.62 calibre they would have a lot of other cheap LMG to choose from. But when you look at 5.56mm LMGs, there really aren't that many options that can be considered cheap. But now that South Korea has made their own versions of the Minimi, I can expect it will be comparatively low-priced and take away even more potential Ultimax buyers.

    So I am sure the clever sales and engineering people at ST are cracking their heads on what next. And I am sure that SAF strategists are also not very happy to stick with a weapon that can only reliably fire 30-round magazines.

    Don't get me wrong, I love the SAW, and I would hate to see it go as it is our pride and joy to have such a famous and unique weapon.

    But the strength of the Ultimax - its 100-round mag - is also the source of its problems. So I think if we can come up with a belt-fed SAW it would be the right way to go.

    Or design a better drum for the Ultimax.
