    Stay cool, do your IPPT run on a treadmill
    By Benjamin Nadarajan

    OPERATIONALLY-ready national servicemen (NSmen) can soon choose to run the 2.4km for their Individual Physical Proficiency Test (IPPT) on treadmills in air-conditioned gyms.

    For a trial year beginning Jan 5, the Safra National Service Association clubhouses in Tampines and Toa Payoh will be used as IPPT venues.

    Instead of pounding six times round an outdoor track, NSmen will run on treadmills.

    Other tests - chin-ups, sit-ups, shuttle run and standing broad jumps - will be conducted at the clubhouses' multi-purpose halls.

    However, only those looking to simply pass the fitness test may go the Safra route.

    Those aiming for monetary awards of between $100 and $400 and the silver or gold standard must still go to the fitness-conditioning centres at Maju, Khatib and Bedok camps.

    After a year, Safra will consider holding the tests at its other three clubhouses at Bukit Merah, Yishun and near Clarke Quay, while the Defence Ministry will decide if all NSmen may take their IPPT at the clubhouses.

    Safra president Cedric Foo, also Minister of State for Defence, announced the initiative yesterday at the association's annual general meeting at its Yishun Country Club.

    'While the SAF must continue to demand high physical fitness standards of our NSmen, it would be good if we can make it more convenient for our NSmen to fulfill their NS duties,' he said.

    The tests will alternate daily between the two clubhouses and be available every day except Fridays and public holidays.

    Extra treadmills will be brought in for IPPT runs.

    Each test, free to Safra members, should take about 90 minutes.

    Non-members will pay $2 for using club facilities.

    Banker Jacques Then, 32, feels the music in the gyms will stimulate a person to run faster.

    But retired army officer Chan Tien Meng, 53, said: 'Fitness tests should always be done outdoors where the soldier is exposed to various weather conditions. We can't possibly fight wars in air-conditioned areas, can we?'

    The run, considered by many to be the toughest part of the Singapore Armed Forces' annual Individual Physical Proficiency Test (IPPT), is now done outdoors at stadium running tracks or selected SAF camps.

    NSman Melvin Tan, who is in his 20s, asked: 'Has the SAF gone soft? We can't possibly fight wars in air-conditioned areas, can we?

    Fitness test not a combat training activity

    I REFER to the letter, 'Soldiers should train as they would fight' (ST, Dec 2), by Mr Chia Ying Chng and the article, 'Indoor fitness test runs 'too soft' ' (ST, Dec 3).

    The Individual Physical Proficiency Test (IPPT) is a standard physical-fitness test comprising four static stations and a 2.4km run in T-shirt, shorts and track shoes. It is not conducted under field conditions and is not a combat training activity.

    SAF servicemen are trained and conditioned for combat by undergoing a series of field exercises (in skeleton or full battle gear) and not by how they take their IPPT. The combat training the SAF puts soldiers through is compre-hensive, tough and realistic.

    The 2.4km run on a treadmill in a Safra gym will require the performance standard and effort of an equivalent outdoor 2.4km run. The treadmill will have the gradient adjusted to compensate for the gym environment. This has been calibrated after careful study by the Defence Medical and Environmental Research Institute, working together with the Army's Training Department and Army Medical Staff.

    The equivalent standard on the treadmill has been cross-validated by having servicemen perform both outdoor and treadmill tests. There is therefore no compromise on the IPPT standards.

    The SAF offered NSmen the alternative of taking their 2.4km run in Safra gyms to make it more convenient for them. We recognise that NSmen have to balance demands on their time from their family, career and NS duties. Where we can make it more convenient for them to carry out their NS duties without compromising operational readiness, we will do so.

    COL BERNARD TOH
    Director, Public Affairs
    Ministry of Defence
