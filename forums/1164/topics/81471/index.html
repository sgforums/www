    Yes, you can be posted out of your NS unit if you didn't ORD'd with them. As for petitioning them to take you back, I'm doubtful: your designated replacement must be himself unreliable with attendance (enough for them to want to dump him as well).

    Who called you - unit i/c, unit S1 cell, etc.? I'm surprised you weren't called up to attend a mob briefing beforehand. How come 2 weeks?

    I won't be cavalier with the word "standby". Mob "manning" is mob manning; the other requires you to pack a standard fullpack, for one thing.

    No "confirmation" about activation; they can call you back anytime with the assigned period. If it's just to track reporting time, you'll only have to physically sign in & then go home (no debriefing even). Nope, it won't count as an ICT if you move out for deployment, if that's what you meant.

    No contact by hp with an open-mob. The people activating it doesn't have direct access to the requisite unit rosters, nor the logistics to call everyone up. FYI The latter's actually done by your own unit people, with a silent mob.

    Icemoon is correct with the reporting times. KAH = Key Appointment Holder. Sorry, the phone #'s aren't known to the general public.

    There may be no mob briefing. Usually they send you a letter informing you of the manning, what to bring and pack etc.
    Not in my case. They usually did the general mob briefings at the tailend of my ICTs, but I was also regularly called back - as a recall i/c - to attend half-days briefings in case it's a silent mob. During the latter times, I often see a few unfamiliar reservists - presumably temporarily attached or newly posted to my unit - being pulled aside to get their own general mob briefings. Some of them stood out cuz they were really "old birds", complaining to the SAF regulars that they're too old to be targeted, that they can't understand the English-language regulations, etc.

    You have to prepare a standard fullpack in both cases.
    Actually I've never packed a standard pack; ditto my kakis. We brought in what we usually bring for our ICTs, albeit with prior networked knowledge of whether it's only a sign-in, or draw-arms, or move-out activation. The officers, KAHs & others who reported very early would spread the word by phone (if it's something out of the ordinary). I remember us being caught out only once on the "standby" regimentation, but they didn't formally check the equipment until the M+6 hour was up, so there was some last-minute scrambling to bulk-buy items. Even then, I don't recall any formal scolding on incomplete equipment either. Probably can't say the same for our CO, though.

    I dunno how much the SAF policy has changed with regards to phone contact as a communication tool for callups & mob postings (beyond the legal liability). Where I came from, that not enough per se - you had to be formally informed either via black-&-white or a physical presence, & by an appriopriately-senior figure at that (SSG or WO).

    That's why I asked the thread-starter who contacted him. If it was from a clerk (unit S1 or reservist faciliator), they should've correspondingly sent along a mob letter, registered-post if need be. If it was from a recall i/c - & I've done this before myself as instructed - it's done usually to confirm the phone #'s as contactable, & I also had one guy basically making the same complaints as Raynor's done here. (I dunno whether to believe him, but told him to call the battalion office anyway.)

    It's tempting to treat these cases cynically, as with the "old birds" from my previous post, but I've learnt in time to give them the benefit of the doubt. Many of them are, for want of a better description, "peripheral" soldiers - armourers, MT-line technicans &, when we still had them, cooks - who were SBMT-trained but largely did their NS in their own "world", away from the more-regimented operational units & thus unfamiliar with things like mob manning. That said, some of them do made a smooth transition - I know this ammo storeman who converted into a "kilat" RP - but most remained so-called "misfits" who took cover in the bunks when they're not needed. But their vocational skills are much valued, if you want tasteful meals, or your Land Rover to be repaired, or your hastily-cleaned rifle ~wink~ to be accepted when returned!

    evozero, thanks for the warning but I've read the posting guidelines & think I've been discreet enough - unless this board is irrationally kiasi.

    I think those who had to "standby" got a letter notice beforehand but, reservists being reservists, some of them are half-serious about it. Thankfully, my unit officers/KAHs were cognizant of this, would plan ahead & get the max out of the situation. Translated, that meant designating an area for the standby formation as they reported in, with periodic semi-formal fullpack checks. Those who lacked stuff are asked to borrow them from non-standby people &, as a last measure, resorted to joining a bulk-buying before H-hour.

    The bigger context here is many reservists don't possess a complete set of personal equipment in the 1st place - or anymore. I mean, how many of you still have the "green" vest & "blue" shorts? Jackknife & rifle-cleaning kit (I didn't ORD with either)? A fork (to go with your spoon)? Also, they've gotten used to the QM providing the expendables - toilet roll, powder, etc. - but doesn't know if he'll do the same for the mob standbys.

    I-like-flings(m), the junior sergeant-major conducted the formal equipment check; the inspectors just stood aside & took notes. That's the thing - I get the impression that they were more interested in the overall operational efficiency (reporting times, complete & proper stores, functional transports, etc.). Hope I've worded this right: IMO there's no need to overemphasize on standard fullpacks per se. Yes, it's an important individual responsibility, except it's not a be-all either: at worst you're deducted marks for it, but then it's only one component towards calculating an overall passing grade. I'd even dare say our CO didn't get scolded, if the inspectors had noted how we positively improvised to meet the standby-pack criterion.
