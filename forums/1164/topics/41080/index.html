    Hi All,

    After every conflict, there will be lessons learnt and those progressive armed forces will try to apply such lessons to their own organisations. It could be in the form of doctrine, training, equipment and logistics.

    I can think of some of the lessons that the SAF learnt (my own speculation) from past conflicts.

    During my NS days in late 80s (you can see how old I am), there was a lot of focus on leadership in the section and platoon level. I think this is learnt from the Falklands War in '82 where the British prevailed although they are outnumbered and have similar equipment as the Argentines. Their Section Comd, PCs and OCs showed much bravery and initiative compared to their Argentine's counterparts. The Argentines have the 1st Gen NVGs whereas the British regular infantry are not equiped with any NVGs.

    After the 1st Gulf War, there was focus on Chemical Defense and more focus on IT. Also emphasis on heli mobility (probably learned as a result of the 101st Airborne's left hook in GW I).

    As for GW II, I think the lessons learned are:

    a. Must safeguard rear areas as in a rapid advance, a lot of renegade enemy forces could be left behind. Maybe SAF should purchase those LAV type 6x6 amoured vehicles to safeguard rear areas.

    b. if you have a modern MBT, it will be be invincible against a force without modern ATGWs

    c. Target idenfitication (IFF) very important. Yet at the same time frontline troops must be allowed the initiative to use deadly force without hesitation.

    d. It is useful to have a top notch MBT like the M1 which can be used to flush out targets in FIBUA.

    e. Must probably rewrite our FIBUA manual to include Armour

    f. Apaches are quite accident prone. Maybe should use other less capable attack copters to complement our Apache force.

    Just my 2 cents.

    Hi all, here are some observations that might be useful for SAF:

    1) Infantry section tactics are important. Leap-frogging is better than expending tank rds and Javelin missiles against a house which could have been stormed easily by a section of troops with GPMG support. Use CS gas to lower enemy reaction...

    2) Realistic combat loads are important. Would recommend that SAF soldiers train constantly with simulated load on training exercise (the blue condensed rubber things)

    3) Would need to equip each and every line troop with body armour. Coalition casualties have been thankfully low, thanks to body armour in more than one occasion.

    4) Our GPMGs are good. Its pretty much the same type employed by the Brits and the Americans. But would suggest a bandolier box like what U.K. has. About 100-150 rds would suffice. Its better than having to have the gunner flip the belt over the MG, and unflip it when the need arises...also, have a MG gunner SBO, and equip each gunner with a pistol.

    5) Combat optical gunsights are highly important. At present, we only have it on the SAR-21s. Would recommend having it for SAWs and GPMGs.

    6) Grenadier vests are important. I dunno how grenadiers carry their rds presently, but I think its by using bandoliers. This limits the amount of 40mm grenades a grenadier can carry. Also, it'll be difficult to move with bandoliers flipping around your chest or waist.

    7) NVGs are highly important. Should have every line troop issued with one and train in night exercises with it. Modifying existing helmet so that it can mount the NVG.

    8. Sharpshooters and snipers are important. However, we should give the shaprshooter a dedicated weapon instead of the SAR-21 or M-16S1 with Avimo scope. Would recommend a 7.62 auto-loader rifle like the SR-25.

    Okay, I think that's all I can think of for now. I'll get off the soapbox now...please feel free to comment...

    Some very good suggestions I see here. Tango1, I think you did not serve your NS in the infantry or you'd realize a lot of the things you suggest are already being done. The only thing I do not agree with is the need for sharpshooters. The terrain we are likely to face is very different from the desert, with much shorter ranges. We already have a weapon system that fulfills the need for mid-range (400m-1000m) fire at the platoon level -- the GPMG (though this is volume rather than point fire). I do not think we need something like that at the section level.

    The main issue I have is the seeming vulnerability of the Apache to ground fire and dust. Although it is armored and difficult to shoot down, it seems to be quite easy to render combat ineffective even by 7.62 fire. It now seems that our investment in Apaches is not a silver bullet against Malaysia's PT-91 MBT and ASTROS MLRS. Tactics need to be adapted. The Apaches will need to operate as part of a team, with ground troops or supporting fires to clear out enemy infantry. That would be a little dicey if the Apaches are performing long range strike. So this means we should rely on fixed wing strike aircraft for long range strike and give Apaches a CAS role, operating with the ground troops at the front.

    Rear area protection has also been demonstrated to be very important. Guerilla and commando attacks are an even greater problem in our local terrain. Our development of the armed and armored Bronco ATTC is now shown to be prescient. However, we need to increase our use of this platform and learn how to tactically employ it. The SAF needs to practice rear area protection, with more emphasis given to anti-guerilla and counter-insurgency scenarios in our exercises. Adding wheeled armored vehicles like the Terrex to our inventory for convoy protection might also be a good idea.

    Bypassing cities to strike at vital objectives was one of the main themes of the campaign and is likely to be what the SAF would do in a conflict since we lack the manpower to storm cities. It has been proven to be sound in modern warfare with a few caveats. Encirclement of the city must be complete and thorough, or the defenders can stage strikes from the cities, escape and appear elsewhere, or be reinforced. This is what happened in Basra, which was never encircled from the north. Humanitarian aid to the city must be ensured, even if that means handing supplies to the defenders. Checkpoint manning is a more involved skill than many expected and is extremely important for proper military security, aid provision and media perception of the war. Again, it should be given greater emphasis in SAF training.

    The use of artillery and mortars for probing, baiting and harrassment fire by the militarily weaker force needs to be studied. The Iraqis seemingly managed to bait the Americans into striking civilian targets with mortar fire, or drive them into prepared positions with artillery. We need to learn how to do that too, and how to counter it. Unit leaders need to be disciplined under fire and not allow it to cloud their judgement and do what the enemy wants them to do. This is hard to train for, since you cannot bombard a unit with real artillery in training. The best we can do is mental preparation.

    That's all I can think of for now.
