    Don't dismiss Taiwan defence, PLA told

    General's stern warning reminds People's Liberation Army that complacency led to its only resounding defeat - at Jinmen

    HONG KONG - Don't underrate your enemy! This was the strongest warning ever issued publicly by a senior officer in China's People's Liberation Army (PLA) recently against military adventurism on Taiwan.

    General Liu Yazhou, deputy political commissar of the PLA Air Force, warned that in the event of a military showdown with Taiwan, the PLA's biggest enemy would be its tendency to take the enemy lightly.

    In an article, A Review of the Jinmen War, widely circulated in cyberspace, he said he felt rather disturbed, after attending some of the closed-door sessions on how best to subdue Taiwan, by the fairly common phenomenon of PLA officers underrating the enemy.

    This was the first time so senior a PLA officer had come out publicly to warn against complacency, said to be a common sentiment within the military.

    One example he cited was his question, raised during one of these sessions, on how the PLA could amass sufficient number of troops in the area facing Taiwan without being detected by American satellites from space and Taiwanese radar on the ground.

    He got a trifling reply: 'See, whenever there is a week-long holiday, the railway will be busy moving tourists around.

    'We could make use of one such occasion, asking our troops to dress in civilian clothing and enter Fujian via the railway network.'

    This unprofessional reply, among others, prompted him to write the article.

    The 1949 Jinmen (spelt Kinmen in Taiwan) War was the first complete and disastrous defeat ever suffered by the PLA since its formation in 1927. More than 9,000 troops were landed on the island but never returned.

    Gen Liu, son-in-law of former president Li Xiannian, is the only senior PLA official to have visited Taiwan. On that visit, he made a special trip to Jinmen to study why the PLA suffered its first debacle.

    Jinmen is a group of islands just a few miles off the mainland coast, facing Xiamen city. Historically, the island, together with the Penghu archipelago, has been the stepping stone to Taiwan.

    The first Jinmen War was fought in October 1949 in the last stages of the 1946-49 Civil War. At that time, the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) had swept across the entire mainland, forcing the ruling Kuomintang (KMT) forces to withdraw to Taiwan.

    With repeated victories all over China, the PLA became complacent. In his article, Gen Liu quoted past documents showing that right from the supreme commander Mao Zedong down to the leaders on the ground, none had taken the KMT resistance in Jinmen seriously.

    An example: Given the scarcity of ships at the time, not all the available ones were devoted to ferrying troops and ammunition. Instead, some were used to ship live pigs across so that the soldiers could hold feasts to celebrate victory.

    Gen Liu demonstrated convincingly that complacency brought about the PLA's first disastrous defeat.

    He cautioned that many of the problems that led to the debacle half a century ago still existed today. Yet it was a pity the PLA had never conducted any thorough research into the causes of its defeat.

    In this regard, he considered the PLA less prudent and pragmatic than its American counterparts.

    After losing the Vietnam War, the United States conducted thorough studies into the causes. Such research continues to the present day, three decades afterwards.

    Gen Liu drew important conclusions from the debacle. He pointed out that capturing Jinmen was as important in any future war against Taiwan as it was in the past.

    Clearly, this was a rebuttal of those who claimed Taiwan had lost its military significance now that the PLA possessed jet fighters, medium to long-range missiles and information-warfare capabilities.

    When the Jinmen War was fought, no one in the PLA realised that it would be an amphibious war.

    Most considered it an extension of the ground war. So what defeated the PLA was not the KMT resistance but the sea, according to Gen Liu.

    Likewise, any future war against Taiwan would be an amphibious one in which the PLA would have to face the same enemy - the sea.

    Up till now, the PLA had never won a war at sea even though it was invincible on land, he said, and there was no reason for complacency.

    Gen Liu urged the PLA not to underestimate the resistance, a factor leading to the previous debacle.

    In a future war against Taiwan, given its geo-strategic significance to the US, it would be the whole Western world defending Taiwan.

    China had to factor that in, he said.
