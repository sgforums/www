    Just a few thoughts, only my humble opinion of course.
    Lets first ignore the question of the necessity of a warrior culture but the possibility of a warrior culture in singapore.
    First of all, i believe the gurkhas and the sikh(though my knowledge on the sikhs are superficial) are not true warrior cultures but rather sub-cultures within the society. the societies from which these sub-cultures originated from are neither warlike more excessively aggressive but they do possess a cadre of warriors acting as the depository and also the teachers of a warrior ethos to a group of followers and disciples. They are neither mainstream nor is this ethos a common strain that runs throughout the society.
    Cultures truer to the form of a martial society would be the pre-Theban hegemony Sparta of ancient greece and the institution of samurai class which emerged in the post-heian period.
    These two societies mobilized their economy and efforts towards the maintenance of a warrior class and the people produced from this class were also the social and political elite.
    We know sure as sure that such an occurance would not be possible in singapore, for such a phenomenon would require the very ideologies and societal norms of the country to be martial in nature and singapore's version of a democracy coupled with capitalism and our education system but prevent such an occurance. Can you imagine that a singaporean kid separated from his family at an early age to live in communes where he was to live and breathe the subtle and not so subtle arts of warring. Where any failure to graduate as a trained warrior would condemm him to be cast down into another class of citizenry inferior in rights and status to the warrior class.
    Thus in the definative aspect, a warrior culture is impossible,

    But of course there is a likelyhood of breeding a sub culture of men and women to be warlike in aspect and behaviour is there. The closest we have to a martial-subculture is perhaps the SOF. No, the commandos are not even close(no disrespect to them), at the end of the day, they are still boys want only want to get drunk on the weekends.
    The SOF, i dare say have the training and expertise in soldiery, but not the culture. This culture is as you and i understand it, their lifestyles have to be martially orientated in almost totality. Every waking moment in training and sweating, and the quieter moments in contemplating the perfection of their drills and gunnery. That meals and living conditions are geared towards toughing the body and provides for no distraction. Also these men should feel the need to pass this culture on to their children and when their bodies can no longer take the outdoor rigours they would still remain within the community and serve in any position as required. That there is to exist honour between combatants and there is glory in victory or sacrifice.
    Thus for the creation of such a sub culture would require conscious effort from the government to promote and secure the lifestyle of such a subculture and protect it against the temptations of a normal capitalist consumer lifestyle and also to safeguard the norms and ways of this subculture.
    Of course what i just spelt out is the ideal in my mind. Presently the existance of such a culture could be considered vulgar to mostwho are now attuned to diplomacy and democracy.

    IMHO, there will be no local warrior culture; the most important reason of all being we're not at all militant society and we're not threatened by any obvious military threat. Ask yourselves, what do the majority of Singaporeans value most and what do they aspire to? The best answer I can come up with is economic/financial success. Most Singaporeans don't see soldiering as a means to getting to that sort of success and most would say "Oh yah, that guy couldn't make it in the private sector, so he joined Army lor".

    There are exceptions to the rule such as (the perrenial glamourboys) fighter pilots who end up as (more or less) lucrative commercial pilots and also the various technicians out there who end up valued for their technical expertise in the private sector (with a little luck). Also officers with the aptitude for organisational skills are pretty much valued but when going out to the private sector, they have a mountain to climb since the environment is so much different.

    The Singapore warrior culture will (i believe) only live as a small sub-culture within the military (as evidence by an admiration for our SOF peeps)

    By the way, soldiering for most people doesn't end up anywhere successful in later life. My friend's dad was a private in the Malayan/British army, did patrols in Borneo during the Konfrontasi and even had his friend shot in the eye beside him during an engagement. What's he doing now? He's a driver for a towkay.
