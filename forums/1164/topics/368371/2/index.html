    Villagers Against Chin Peng's Return

    LENGGONG, May 29 (Bernama) -- The call for former Malayan Communist Party (MCP) Secretary-General Chin Peng to be allowed to return to Malaysia has evoked painful nostalgia for residents of a remote village in Hulu Perak, which had once been a target of the communist aggression.

    The government's decision as announced by Prime Minister Datuk Seri Najib Tun Razak to not allow Chin Peng, who is currently living in exile in Hatyai, Thailand to return to Malaysia, even on humanitarian grounds, was received well by villagers as well as an individual who claimed to be a former MCP leader.

    A former MCP unit assistant chief in Kampung Belum, Mohamad Wan Kechik, 84, said Chin Peng should not be allowed to return because of the atrocities the guerilla movement inflicted on the people.

    "Although I was a member of the MCP in the past, I still don't agree on Chin Peng's return because he had caused much hardship to villagers here.

    "It's not that I want to praise him, but he is good to those who listen to him but can be really mean to those who don't," he told reporters when met at Kampung Belum Baru here.

    The British placed more than 150 families from Kampung Belum into Kampung Belum Baru to curb communist aggression.

    Mohamad said although the MCP had officially laid down arms 20 years ago in 1989, it isn't easy for those who went through the ordeal to forget it.

    His friend, Isa Awang Ngah, 82, said the communists controlled the Belum area for three years from 1948 to 1951, and villagers were tortured for not cooperating.

    He said villagers had to surrender any type of food such as rice, padi and corn to the communists to avoid being tortured.

    "The communists were really nice at first, but they were actually mean and could not be trusted. If they give ten sen they will ask back for a ringgit," he said.

    "I do not agree with anyone who would want to bring back Chin Peng and I am not willing to face the communists again. Because of them I had to leave the village," he said.

    Another villager, Sidek Ishak, 71, recounted his unforgettable experience in which he saw a public execution in front of his eyes.

    "If the communists want to kill someone, they will invite the villagers to come and enjoy a feast of meat . After the meal they will conduct the execution in public to instil fear among the villagers," he said.

    The Prime Minister on Wednesday announced that Ching Peng would not be allowed to return to Malaysia because it would cause unhappiness among the families of victims who were killed by the MCP.
