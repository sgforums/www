    31 Jan 04, 08:11
    In the TOW1, the firing unit has a computer which looks for two things: the hot glow from the rocket engine of the missile and the location of the crosshair. It compares the two points and issues corrections to the missile accordingly. For example, if it sees that the glow from the rocket is to the left of the crosshair, it commands the missile to fly to the right. This is "optical tracking". The TOW1 was easily defeated by flares (which look like the glow from the rocket engine, confusing the firing unit), so the TOW2 instead looks for a strobe light on the missile that flashes according to a certain code.

    The wire is very very thin and does not weigh much per km. Most of the time, the firer will be found by the launch signature of the missile rather than the wire. Being tracked by the wire is the least of his worries.

    Wire Guided Missiles requires some degree of human guidance, and can be launched from the ground or from the air (more suitably from helicopters that hover in static position during the flight of the missile).

    The human eye will locate a target using an Opitical Device (near similar to the binoculars being built as part of the Missile Command Module Package).

    After the Wire Guided Missile is launched a spool of 'super thin' wire is released that will follow the missile as it fly towards the target.

    New updated data, from the manned Optical Device, is relayed to update and correct the flight path of the missile towards the target.

    Latest technology allow the use of fibre optical wire as a link between the human and the missile in flight.
