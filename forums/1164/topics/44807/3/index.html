    zigzag,

    The armor triad is typically phrased as Mobility, Protection and Firepower. Mobility encompasses weight, range and speed, among other things. Protection takes physical size and stealth into account. Firepower includes belly load and weapon selection. A balance is needed between all three factors.

    However, while the armor triad is often quoted by tankers, it only covers the technical design of an armored vehicle. There are other equally important factors that are typically the purview of staff officers. These are Cost and Sustainability. Cost does not only refer to purchase cost, but also manning requirements, life cycle costs, maintenance costs and upgradability. Sustainability includes factors like maintainability, reliability and logistical burden.

    There is also a catch-all category sometimes referred to as Human Factors, which includes things like comfort, psychological impact, user-friendliness and training requirement. I find that these tend to step beyond the actual vehicle design to unrelated things, though.

    Of course, there is a lot of overlap between these six main criteria. For example, range and ammo load are also Sustainability problems since they affect the logistical requirements of the vehicle. But this is the normal schema I find used to discuss armored vehicle design.

    I would not place any factor above the others. There needs to be a balance. That is the whole idea. You must have some of all of them.

    That said, I feel that too little emphasis is given to Cost. We must have equipment and doctrines that are suitable for our unique situation. The optimum strategy when you are poor is not simply a scaled down version of the strategy when you are rich. We are not a very large armed force or economy and we need to have some restraint when making equipment purchases (not just armored vehicle purchases, but aircraft, etc.) I am sure that is already being done, but I am still worried. Some recent acquisitions have seemed rather costly to me.
