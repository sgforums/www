That also means that once the weapon is finalized (they are still working on reducing system weight to be actually lighter then the M-16/203 by about a pound), you can get thrice more of it out to the troops then you could with the old system, and you get a fivefold increase in effectiveness as well.     While the technical specifications of the Land Warrior system are truly impressive, they do not address the main problem with warfighting the Americans seem to have: target identification. I would very much like to see how the Land Warrior systems manage to distinguish the following from each other:

    1. An enemy guerilla in civilian clothes.

    2. A civilian with a hunting rifle.

    3. An angry civilian with a hunting rifle who wants to avenge his wife who died yesterday in a JDAM attack.

    4. A special forces soldier from your own side in enemy civilian clothes.

    5. An allied guerilla in civilian clothes (e.g. Kurds or Northern Alliance).

    6. A reporter on an undercover story with the guerillas.

    This has always been the problem with the US Army, since VIetnam, through Somalia, Kosovo, Afghanistan and now in Iraq. Their firepower far outstrips their ability to apply it discriminately. Each time they shoot the wrong guy, be it their own soldiers, allies, civilians or neutrals, they make the situation politically worse. They are still fighting WW2, storming the beaches of Normandy. Just like in the movies, with a good guy and a bad guy. Clean and simple.

Whatever blitzkrieg forward defence strategy we use will be followed by some period of counterinsurgency operations, which we are mostly untrained for. At least our peacekeeping experience in E. That is why infantry carry short ranged assault rifles, so you can actually identify what you are shooting at. Blasting away at blobs in your thermal sights from 2-3 km away may be good in a shooting war, but is really dumb in a lower intensity conflict.
