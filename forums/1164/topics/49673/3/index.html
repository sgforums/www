And of course, the cheapest way to deal with them is guerilla war, to make thermal sensing irrelevant.

    The important thing is not the sensors, but whether the soldiers are trained to fight smart. Do they know how to put up decoys, camoflage themselves and deceive the enemy? Do they know how to counter this? Land Warrior will give you a lot of information -- what do you do with it? The same detection-deception game has been played for thousands of years, since before Sun Tzu. Technology changes nothing but its appearance. The most disciplined and motivated soldiers are still the ones who win. This is true of war in general -- it is a very old game, which merely changes appearances from time to time.

    The Americans have the toys, but are still bad at this game. You do not become a champion footballer by buying good shoes. They bombed Serbia for weeks but hit almost nothing, because the Serbians put up decoys and hid their equipment. The Americans had satellite surveillance and communications, gunships, nightvision and thermal sights, but were still intercepted, surrounded and defeated by handphone-carrying skinnies in Somalia. The Americans had ground radar, air surveillance, first-gen thermal sights and every other form of information and technological dominance in Vietnam and still were completely outmaneuvered. The North Vietnamese followed patrols around for weeks, ambushed them and eventually defeated the whole US Army, Air Force, Navy and Marine Corps.

    You should read a little more military history, ST. Technological advancements have always been useful only if there is also the training and leadership to employ them effectively. They are merely tools, which must be used by disciplined, well-led men to fully realize their potential. The OICW and Land Warrior systems are very cool, but if the Americans spent just a fraction of what they are spending on it on training, and revamped their personnel system, the gains would be far greater.

    You are not wrong, ST. The OICW is a cool piece of tech. But I am tired of the "wunderkrieg" mentality that people who learn about war by watching CNN seem to develop. Weapons are tools. Do not put the cart before the horse by neglecting the men who employ them.

    Back to the M4 vs SAR21 debate. The shorter effective range of the M4 is not necessarily a fault. The Americans really liked them in urban combat in Iraq. The problem is that you lose some flexibility if you have a short-ranged weapon. That is OK for specialty troops, or troops engaged in special missions, but for a general issue personal weapon, flexibility is important.

    Ah, I see the confusion now. I believe that the OICW is the wrong direction to head in weapon design. I also contend that the Land Warrior programme comes at the expense of better training and personnel system reform and is therefore crippling the US Army's warfighting ability. I have made these points before, but I have not emphasized them.

    What do infantrymen want in a personal weapon? I do not speak for all infantrymen, but I think my views are not atypical. I want something simple and reliable. I want something which can achieve fire superiority over the enemy by being lethal, accurate and intimidating. I want something light and easy to use. I want sufficient ammunition and supplies to run the weapon all the time without fail. The OICW fails on all of these points. It is complex and most likely unreliable and difficult to maintain. The grenade launcher is powerful, but fires too slowly to achieve fire superiority and pin down the enemy, while the 10" barrel 5.56mm "carbine" is a joke. It is heavy, and difficult to use. The ammunition is expensive and fragile, and will probably not be issued in great quantity. It also needs batteries.

    It is a fantastic weapon, but it is not a fantastic INFANTRY weapon. It is not built for a hungry private who will be lying in mud for weeks at a time, with little access to logistical resupply, too shagged to think. It ignores the dirty reality of infantry combat. It is an "infantry" weapon designed by someone from Armor or the Airforce (no offense to tankers or zoomies: I would probably suck designing a tank or plane).

    The perfect infantry weapon would be a GPMG which weighs as much as a rifle and is as accurate as a sniper rifle. It would be as rugged and reliable as a MAG or AK. The ammunition would be lighter than 5.56mm, but cut through armor and hit as hard as .45", and it would be issued by the 5000 round box. It would have only two controls and break down into three parts for cleaning, which you would need to do once a year or so. It would be paired with super-light LAWs with advanced thermobaric warheads, which would simply blast apart any cover used by the enemy and incinerate groups in the open -- far more effective, reliable and flexible than airbursting mini-grenades.

    I think you get the idea. Research for infantry weapons should focus on greater reliability, weight efficiency and accuracy, through improved mechanical, materials and explosives technology. The OICW tries to solve all the problems with infantry (low accuracy under combat stress, poor ability to defeat enemies in cover, poor weight efficiency of ammunition) with computerization and complexity, which is incompatible with infantry combat. These problems should be dealt with through better training and technologies that take the infantryman into account.

    While I understand your point that this new technology need not come at the expense of training, the truth is that it does. The OICW is only the last of many attempts to solve training problems with technical solutions. The M16A2 rifle has a 3 round burst instead of full auto because they do not want to take the time to train their men to have proper fire discipline. The M9 pistol is double action for the same reason. This goes all the way back to when the Union issued single-shot rifles during the Civil War instead of repeaters because they were afraid the men would shoot off all their ammo, even though they had the industrial capacity to do either. All these "improvements" resulted in less effective weapons for the men. This whole attitude is endemic to the US Army, and I fear it is going to lead to trouble yet again with the OICW.

    As I have said, we will see. The OICW is very cool technology, but I fear it is not going to be very effective or well loved by its users. It may even prove to be less effective than the M16/M4 for the reasons I stated. No use having a wondergun if it runs out of batteries, or the software crashes, or it jams, or is so heavy you leave it at base, or you only get a few rounds. Weapons are a bit like computer software -- more features does not necessarily mean better. Land Warrior is not necessarily better than the current situation if it is not reliable, rugged, and logistics-light. We will see how it goes.
