    The bulk of the money spent is mostly overseas - for the training of the SAF in the military arrangements made with Australia, Brunei, France, India, Indonesia, South Africa, Taiwan, Thailand, and the USA.

    In the question asked by 'As romanista2001' - "why we spend so much ?" - he is referring to the Defense Budget.

    Was it about supporting the "arms industry" owned by the Singapore Government ?

     

    One must remember the history of Singapore - how independence was forced on us in the midst of the turbulent politics that led to Singapore being thrown out of a rushed merger to be part of Malaysia.

    Prior to Singapore being forced out of Malaysia - there was a plan to send two battalions of Royal Malay Regiment to force a coup de'tat that would have locked up the entire Singapore State Government, put Singapore under the direct control of the Malaysian Federal Government.

    It was then UK PM Harold Wilson who prevented this from happening.

    This led to a frantic introduction of legislations to implement National Service based on the same lines as Israel, as she was the only country that responded to our call for help when others have declined.

    The amount spent on Defense has been gradually increased as the SAF mature in the step-by-step development - as seen in the purchase of second hand AMX-13 tanks that were first introduced, as well as the second hand Hawker Hunters fighter jets, the SkyHawks; then there were the old County Class LSTs, while new patrol boats were built locally.

    The size of the Defense Budget today is to ensure the SAF is kept on a sharp edge with all the modern equipment and technologies purchased, and also to ensure that the fighting capabilities are integrated to ensure the SAF is confident of the 'force multiplier effects' when different components are put together for any missions.

    The fact that the SAF need to train overseas is due to the limited land size that we inherited and live in.

    The bigger terrain made available overseas will allow the SAF to test the basic skills of individual soldiers training in unfamiliar ground, and which also allow larger SAF formations to familiarize and get experience in strategic manoevres - as well as to test new equipment, technologies and systems that are introduced to the make the SAF a sharp fighting force.

    Only countries without the technological knowhow or have a careless approach to maintaining effective systems  see their weapons deteriorate quickly and have to replace them in 20 years or in many cases much sooner.     Buying spare parts is key to maintaining an effective system - any system, not only weapons systems. Again one does not need to look far to see that shiny new equipment without spares or maintenance quickly become scrap metal.
