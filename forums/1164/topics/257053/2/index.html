    As for the deformation of a bullet hitting a target (or body), I suppose a projectile might just avoid deformation provided that most of the energy has been dissipated, but there is just enough for it to embed itself in the target. So, I suppose a possible scenario which may apply is if the target is hit just outside of the effective range of the bullet, where the projectile is almost out of steam, and doesn't have the speed/energy to smash itself into the target. Having said that, I leave you to figure out the the odds of finding someone who has gotten a non-deformed projectile dug out of them.

    More or less, within 100 meters for your average 5.56mm ball round, the energy of the round exceeds the mechnical strength of the bullet within human flesh, hence it will fragment violently and cause a far nastier wound then it's caliber would suggest. This effect drops out with range and eventually the round behaves like your classic bullet.

    The AK 7.62mm will not fragment or deform in flesh unless it hits something solid like bone, after penetrating to a sufficent depth it will start to yaw coming to rest eventually base first, and it is during the yawing where it causing a much larger wound track.

    BTW, the NATO 7.62mm will behave like the 5.56mm in fragmenting if you drive it fast enough using non-standard powder loads. You certainly don't want to be hit by that!
