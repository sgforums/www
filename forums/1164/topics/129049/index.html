    From Straits Times

    China, Indonesia forging a strategic partnership

    JAKARTA - CHINA and Indonesia yesterday signed agreements to establish a 'strategic partnership' that represents an extension of Beijing's influence in the region.

    Under the cooperation deal, Chinese President Hu Jintao promised billions of dollars in aid, loans and private investment. Jakarta was also hoping that it would lead to the supply of military equipment from China, after being subject to a US arms embargo for more than a decade.

    The far-reaching 'strategic alliance declaration' focuses on cooperation in areas such as trade, defence and technology.

    Mr Hu signed the deal with President Susilo Bambang Yudhoyono one day after attending the Asia-Africa summit in Indonesia over the weekend.

    At a joint press briefing after the signing ceremony, Mr Hu said: 'This demonstrates not only that Sino-Indonesian relations have entered a new development stage but will also promote the development of Sino-Asean relations.

    'We want stronger ties with Indonesia - and to make serious efforts to create a new international order in politics and economy that is just and rational, and to safeguard peace and world progress.'

    Calling the agreement 'a new historic milestone', Dr Yudhoyono said: 'Today, the People's Republic of China and Indonesia have moved forward.'

    Dr Yudhoyono said the two countries had agreed to step up bilateral trade from the current US$14 billion (S$23 billion) to US$20 billion.

    Officials said last week that China would also provide more than US$10 billion in private-sector investment to help revive Indonesia's economy.

    Apart from trade and investment, Defence Minister Juwono Sudarsono said Jakarta was seeking possible cooperation in arms and military supplies.

    'The Ministry of Industry and the Ministry of Science and Technology will work with me to decide on strategic industries in China, incorporating the army, navy and air force, specifically in light arms,' he told The Straits Times.

    'We think that conventional weapons cooperation is best established through countries like China because they have the capability, technology, and the specification that we need - but most of all the pricing should be right,' he said.

    The two countries would work out the arrangement over the next three months, he added.

    International relations analyst Bantarto Bandoro saw Beijing's pledges of aid and investment as part of a drive to usurp Washington as the dominant political and economic force in South-east Asia.

    'China wants to be a major political power in politics and economy, and Indonesia is seen as one of the key players in the region,' he said.

    He believes that improved ties might not have been possible under previous Indonesian leaders.

    'Mr Yudhoyono is a moderate leader who understands the opportunities for Indonesia in being cosier with China,' he said.

    The two countries restored diplomatic relations only in 1990 after severing them in 1967 following an attempted coup by the Indonesian Communist Party.

    Other components of yesterday's agreement are:

    # A memorandum of understanding on infrastructure and natural resources;

    # China's pledge of a further US$300 million worth of low-interest loans for infrastructure development and reconstruction efforts in Indonesia's disaster zones, on top of the US$400 million it already committed;

    # China's pledge of 30 million yuan (S$6 million) in financial and technical assistance, and US$1.5 million in cash and US$500,000 in goods for earthquake-stricken Nias;

    # A deal on technical cooperation between Indonesia's Meteorological and Geophysical Body and China's Earthquake Administration in the fields of earthquakes and tsunamis, and

    # A maritime pact that includes joint efforts to combat smuggling and piracy, as well as arms supplies.

    The strategic partnership with Indonesia follows China's move to resolve outstanding disputes with India last month.

    Analysts have said Beijing is vying for a slice of US trade and influence in the Asia-Pacific region.
