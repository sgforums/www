    Malaysia, Brunei fail to end offshore oil row

    22 Aug 2003 11:26

    KUALA LUMPUR, Aug 22 (Reuters) - Talks between Brunei and Malaysia failed on Friday to settle rival territorial claims on oil-rich waters off Borneo island, with Kuala Lumpur saying its proposal made in May was its best offer.

    Malaysian Foreign Minister Syed Hamid Albar said after a meeting between Brunei's Sultan Hassanal Bolkiah and Malaysian Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad that it was now up to Brunei to consider the proposal.

    Exploration activities in the disputed area have been halted after the Malaysian navy earlier this year chased off a drilling team belonging to a consortium led by France's Total .

    "The prime minister has made it very very clear that we have submitted our proposal and that proposal is the maximum we can live with," Syed Hamid told reporters.

    "It's up to Brunei to look at what we have said, so we will wait for their response," he said. "At this meeting, they did not make any final decision that will be lasting and durable."

    The stakes in the dispute shot up last year after Murphy Oil made a sizeable discovery in a Malaysian block near the disputed area, and put preliminary estimates on the Kikeh field's recoverable reserves at 400 million to 700 million barrels.

    Syed Hamid declined to say whether the halt on exploration activities in the area would continue.

    Malaysia has proposed a production-sharing agreement, according to a source in Brunei with knowledge of the sultanate's policymaking.

    But Brunei's U.K.-based advisers have warned such a pact may mean conceding Malaysia has territorial rights, the source said.

    Brunei, a tiny nation of 330,000 people surrounded on all sides by Malaysia, lacks the muscle to back up its claim, however, and may lose more by digging in against its neighbour.

    Syed Hamid said there were areas both sides could agree on.

    "I think we have moved nearer to a final solution," he said, adding it was possible to resolve the issue before Mahathir steps down in October. Brunei's exclusive economic zone (EEZ) stretches 200 km (124 miles) offshore and the sultanate awarded exploration blocks.

    But Malaysia bases its overlapping claim on possession of some tiny islands and reefs in the South China Sea, and argues that Brunei only has jurisdiction over its continental shelf, in water up to 200 m (656 ft) deep. About three-quarters of the EEZ is in deeper water

    Singapore expresses regret at KL's move over reclamation works in Tuas and Tekong

    By Asha Popatlal

    Singapore has expressed regret at Malaysia's move to halt the Republic's reclamation works in Tuas and Tekong.

    On Friday, Kuala Lumpur applied to the International Tribunal on the Law of the Sea to get Singapore to stop the reclamation works.

    Singapore's Foreign Minister Professor S Jayakumar told reporters on Saturday at the opening of the APEC Secretariat that Singapore had hoped to settle the matter amicably through talks.

    Malaysia first objected to the reclamation works being carried out - within Singapore's waters - in Tekong and Tuas in January 2002.

    Kuala Lumpur claimed that the works are causing damage to the marine environment and prejudice the rights of Malaysia.

    Singapore repeatedly asked for technical reports to support these claims.

    But it was more than a year later on 4th July 2003 that Malaysia produced reports to support these claims.

    It gave Singapore two weeks to respond - or else it would refer the matter to the International Tribunal for the Law of the Sea - to get Singapore to stop reclamation works.

    Singapore responded on 17 July with relevant reports and suggested the two parties meet.

    Both sides met on 13 and 14 August for talks which a Singapore spokesperson said was a good start.

    Singapore proposed a second round of talks but before this could happen, Malaysia brought the International Tribunal into the picture.

    Professor Jayakumar said: "As recently as late August, further documents and reports were exchanged by both sides. In other words, both sides were in the process of talks and discussion. The talks had just begun and both sides were exchanging and examining each other's documents. In the midst of all this, they have suddenly decided to go to the International Tribunal on the Law of the Sea to apply for provisional measures."

    Malaysia has made it a pre-condition that all work stop before there are any further talks.

    The next step would be for Singapore to submit its case to the Tribunal in Hamburg.

    The Foreign Minister says Singapore's Ambassador At Large - Professor Tommy Koh - will lead the team.
