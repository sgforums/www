    With regards to your first point regarding the the source of the "culture of insecurity," one could also argue that Singapore was initially an enthusiastic member of the Federation, in which Singapore's leaders proclaimed at the time that the separation of both states would be detrimental to either side. The main cause of spearation lies not in the structure of the federation, but also, the policies that determined where Singapore would stand in the federation. As such, the latter would stand to lose out in a Malaysian-dominated federation, since the practice of aboriginal affirmative action policies was in direct conflict with the practice of meritocracy.

    We may also go further and argue that the identity created is a product of the underlying circumstances of how Singapore was evicted from the federation and the conditions that faced the city-state thereafter in its formative years. That is to say, the water securitization issue came about because one party employed it as a political bargaining tool, to which the other party has to respond. In this regard, the socially-constructed identity between SG-Malaysia comes about as a product of realpolitik issuses that has shaped it.

    Singapore's response to its removal from the Federation is only rational, given its circumstances and conditions that prevailed at the time. The alternative scenario to the city state's "competitiveness and exceptionalism" in its foreign policy would be to merely remain a separate state in name, while having its foreign policy dictated by its larger neighbours.

    Secondly, the reduction of Singapore's dependency on Malaysian water may on the one hand reduce its sense of vulnerability, but the country's geographical disdvantage remains. Therefore, the desecuritization of the water issue does not necessarily reduce its vulnerability in other areas. It merely lowers the odds that SG faces.

    While it is unlikely that relations between both states would deteriorate to the extent that an interstate war would take place, the existence of mutual deterrence has helped maintain rationality in its bilateral relations.

    It is promising however, given the recent changes in state leaders in both states, and the new commitment to improving bilateral relations.

    ***The case of comparing Israel and Egypt may not be accurate however. The Camp David Accords came about after the latter state finally acknowledge that the state of Israel was here to stay. In other words, it was after having been defeated by Israel in two wars that Egypt was willing to recognize Israel as a state (classic case of "if you can't beat 'em, join 'em"?). Again, what would have been the alternative scenario, had the Egyptians (and Syrians) won? The accords thus, was a product of a win-lose situation at that time.
