    RMAF Needs Four Aircraft To Bolster Air Defence

    KUALA LUMPUR, June 1 (Bernama) -- The Royal Malaysian Air Force (RMAF) needs four aircraft to bolster its radar defence system, its chief Gen Datuk Seri Abdullah Ahmad said Sunday.

    "RMAF will propose to the government the type of aircrafts suitable for Malaysia," he said at a media conference after attending a parade held in conjunction with RMAF's 45th anniversary celebration at the Sungai Besi TUDM Base here.

    The aircraft would help Malaysia modernise its Air Force, monitor its airspace via the radar system and keep abreast with other developed countries. Among the aircraft considered is the E-2 Hawkeye.

    There is no allocation in the Eighth Malaysia Plan to procure the aircraft but RMAF will attempt to purchase them, he said.

    Abdullah said 45 years ago, RMAF had only 12 personnel and one aircraft, but now it owned 200 aircraft with 15,000 personnel.

    Electronic warfare had developed rapidly and airborne early warning and command aircraft (AWAC) are crucial for the RMAF to monitor the airspace effectively, he said.

    The parade included the Air Force College Colours, and the 3rd and 9th squadron fly by involving 22 fighter jets - comprising F/A 18D, Hawk, and the MiG 29N - as well as the CN 235, C130 transport planes and Nuri helicopters. -- BERNAMA

    RMAF eyeing Airborne Warning and Control System aircraft

    Leslie Andres

    KUALA LUMPUR, June 1: The Royal Malaysian Air Force hopes to buy four Â“Airborne Warning and
    Control SystemÂ” (AWACS) aircraft costing more than RM1 bilion each by 2005.

    RMAF chief Jen Datuk Seri Abdullah Ahmad said today that four was the minimum required to ensure adequate defence surveillance.

    He did not rule out the possibility of an early purchase if a deal could be struck involving long-term payments or barter trade.

    The AWACS, also known as Airborne Early Warning and Control System (AEW&C), can track multiple air and maritime contacts over a vast expanse and is considered a crucial element in air and maritime defence.

    The aircraft allows the armed forces to identify and handle possible threats from a distance.

    It is one of several components of electronic warfare (EW), considered to be one of the most important schools in military operations today.

    "Air superiority is important and with this system the network will be complete.

    "An assessment team is looking at all possible aircraft and systems (for the AWACS). We will see which is the most appropriate for us," Abdullah said.

    He was attending the RMAF's 45th Air Force Day at the Kuala Lumpur air base in Sungai Besi.

    Three types of aircraft are being considered as front runners to supply the RMAF with AWACS capabilities.

    The first is the Grumman E-2C Hawkeye, currently the favourite of the United States' navy.

    A turbo-propeller aircraft, the Hawkeye normally carries a crew of five.

    The second is the Boeing 737 AEW&C is equipped with a Multirole Electronically Scanned Array radar. The 737 AEW&C requires a crew of between eight and 12.

    The third is the Brazilian-made Embraer EMB-145, which is a variant of its ERJ-145 regional jet.

    The EMB-145 is fitted with a Swedish-made Erieye radar. The military variant was developed for Brazil's Sistema de Vigilancia de Amazonia (surveillance system, Amazon region) programme, made up of both airborne and ground-sensor systems designed to monitor activity throughout the Amazon basin.

    At today's celebrations, Abdullah took the salute from a parade comprising 11 officers and 202 other ranks led by Lt Kol Mohd Salleh Osman. He also witnessed a fly-past by 25 aircraft comprising the F/A18D Hornet and MiG-29N fighter aircraft, Hawk advanced jet trainers, Pilatus PC-7 trainer aircraft, C-130 Hercules and CN-235 transport aircraft and Nuri and Allouette helicopters.

    Abdullah also presented certificates to six air force personnel for outstanding achievements.

    Editorial: An aerial view of national defence

    Editorial

    June 3: IN modern warfare, firepower alone would make less of a bang without the equally high-powered means to deliver it. And so it is with air-power without Airborne Warning and Control System (Awacs) aircraft.

    Defence planners insist on it for maximum effectiveness in deployment and logistics.

    But they cost a bomb. The four Awacs aircraft requested by the Royal Malaysian Air Force will cost a total of RM4 billion Â— an amount which has to be weighed against other competing priorities and the country's exposure to external dangers. From the point of view of defence, this may well be money well spent if we are to protect our skies and seas from unfriendly intrusions, including that of piracy. As the air force is buying more advanced aircraft, an Awacs capability will be a necessary enhancement.

    However, Defence Minister Datuk Seri Najib Tun Razak said in response to the RMAF proposal yesterday that, "There is certainly a need for it in the future but not at the moment. The purchase of such sophisticated aircraft will incur huge expenses as well as the need for proper planning. Therefore it cannot be done hastily. Besides, the Government is not in a position to do so financially now." In these circumstances, land-based radar is the only option even though its range is limited by the earth's curvature. There is still life and sufficient security without Awacs, although Singapore, a tiny and therefore insecure island amid much larger neighbours, has the advanced aircraft.

    It is an irony that the best way to maintain peace is to have a credible military force that will deter foreign aggression. In these days of high-tech warfare, a defence force needs to be armed with the latest weapons, because technology does give the military a vital edge in an armed conflict. The reality is that courage and numbers alone are not enough in this era of electronic warfare.

    Although Awacs is a necessary part of this process, at this particular moment it does not rate the highest priority.
