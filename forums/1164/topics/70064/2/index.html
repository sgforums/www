    Sardaukar, you can express your willingness to join an elite unit during evaluations. This does not guarantee that you'll get in, but it improves your chances greatly. There are still psychological, physical and character evaluations that you need to pass, along with manpower requirements. However, if you MUST be in an elite unit, you should simply sign on as a regular in the unit you want. If you pass selection you will automatically be there.

    Before you do that, however, you need to ask yourself several things, as others in this thread have said. Life in these units is TOUGH. Tough in the Army means a whole different thing from tough in civilian life. It is not glamorous once you are on the inside. It is not at all like what you see on TV or the movies. Make sure you know what you are getting yourself into before jumping headfirst into it. Do a bit of NS first, then decide.

    Mindset wise, you need to ask yourself WHY you want to join these units. If you need to join to validate yourself, to give yourself something to be proud of, you are already in trouble. Nothing can make you proud of yourself. You seem so boyishly enthusiastic that I am a bit worried. The best soldiers are usually the reluctant ones. I will leave it up to actual Guards and Commandos (like Gedanken) to tell you what constitutes a good mindset for someone in their units.

    Erm, OK.

    I'm going to ascribe this to a matter of expression, but this talk about shooting people in the head and throwing TSR into the air really does worry me a bit - it's all very trigger-happy. The last thing I'd want to do is go on a mission with someone like that - either he'll get me killed or we'll have a repeat of My Lai.

    At the end of the day, it's a job. Certainly it's an honourable job, and certainly it needs to be done right, but you will not be encouraged to take too high a level of view, because in the big picture, you are simply the instrument of government policy.

    All you really need to do is focus on doing the job right. Regimentation was certainly not a major part of the Commando mentality when I was there, and we that probably made a major contribution to our reputation as being poorly disciplined. We took breaks whenever we had a chance, but when it came time to switch on, we switched on all the way.

    Our second OC went on to become Force commander for SOF, and his understanding with us was that he would treat us as professionals as long as we made sure we got the job done, and he turned our company into a highly efficient machine - we turned out the best platoon of what was the best combat unit for that year. Some time after he returned to SOF, we got a company 2i/c who converted from Armour, and man, was he ever a boy scout with regimentation. It turned out to be a disaster - he brought along his bullsh*t-baffles-brains approach, and of course the boys didn't appreciate it - the company's efficiency went all the way downhill because we had too much bullsh*t to take care of. Hell, even the CO didn't like the guy. Eventually, though, he learned to shut up and stop with the jingoistic chickensh*t nonsense, and the company ran fine after that.

    There was another guy in my platoon, an old schoolmate of mine, who had a similar mentality as well, and he was so caught up in it that nobody could work with him. He made it through to get the beret, but when we went operational, he was relegated to rear-echelon tasks.

    The worrisome thing, Sardaukar, is that what you've written sounds a lot like these two guys. Remember, it's all about the job, being professional about it and making sure that when the time comes, you can do it right. Leave the rest of it at the door.

    Ego? Of course there's ego involved - I doubt that the SAF ever had a man who put on a red beret and was completely unaffected by it.
