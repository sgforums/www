    JDW 2 July 2003

    Singapore, Thailand prepare to receive air-toair missiles

    Singapore and Tailand are expected within a few months to take in-country delivery of Raytheon AIM-120C Advanced Medium Ranage Air to Air Missles (AMRAAMs), purchased by some time ago but stored in Guam under a US restriction tied to the sale.

    Singapore bought 100 AMRAAMs under a package deal worth $85 million, which also included two captive air-training missiles and 60 LAU-129A/A missile lauchers. Thailand's acquisition involved an initial eight AMRAAMs under a package worth $6.1 million.

    Concerned over introducing beyond-visual-range (BVR) missiles to the region, Washington insisted that the weapons remain in storage on US teritory until required.

    Singapore and Thailand accepted the condition; however, Malaysia, which has been offered the same deal, refused. South Korea and Taiwan also bought the AMRAAM; the former sale involved 147 missiles and the latter 200.

    The US appears to have eased its restriction on BVR missile deliveries as the technology become more widespread- including via Russia sales to China and Vietnam, along with unsuccessful efforts by Myanmar to btain missiles from Ukraine.

    Also, Taiwan was already operating the MBDA MICA meduim range AAM, obtained from France, and th indegenously produced Tien Chien II (Sky Sword II) meduim range AAM.

    At the same time, Raytheon lobbied strongly to sell and deliver the AMRAAM system more widely in the Asia-Pacific region.

    The breakthrough with Singapore and Thailand occurred with an agreement, quietlyconcluded in May, to realease the missiles. Deliveries will take place once some technical issues have been resolved, including transport arrangement s and devlopment of suitable local storage facilities.

    According to one source, the AMRAAMs should be in Singapor eand Thailand around September and October.
