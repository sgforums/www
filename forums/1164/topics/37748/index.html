    Bush Considers Dropping U.N. Resolution
    1 hour, 45 minutes ago

    By RON FOURNIER, AP White House Correspondent

    WASHINGTON - Forced into a diplomatic retreat, U.S. officials said Thursday that President Bush (news - web sites) may delay a vote on his troubled U.N. resolution or even drop it Â— and fight Iraq (news - web sites) without the international body's backing. France dismissed a compromise plan as an "automatic recourse to war."

    Amid a swirl of recrimination and 11th-hour posturing, the White House began planning for a possible overseas meeting this weekend between President Bush and his two staunchest allies on Iraq, British Prime Mininster Tony Blair (news - web sites) and Spanish Prime Minister Jose Maria Aznar.

    Senior U.S. officials said the meeting, tentatively planned for a neutral nation overseas, would allow the leaders to review final diplomatic and military strategies. The officials, who spoke on condition of anonymity, said all three leaders and the host nation had not signed off on the summit Thursday night, and there would be no final word on the prospects for a meeting before Friday.

    News of the meeting first surfaced Thursday morning, but officials said planning had stopped only to confirm hours later that talks had begun again. Early in the day, U.N. Secretary-General Kofi Annan (news - web sites) raised the possibility of a global summit "to get us out of this crisis."

    The government of Iraqi President Saddam Hussein (news - web sites) exulted in the diplomatic tumult over a U.S.-British backed resolution that would demand that Iraq disarm by Monday. The allies "have lost the round before it starts while we, along with well-intentioned powers in the world, have won it," the popular daily Babil, owned by Iraqi President Saddam Hussein's son, Odai, said in a front-page editorial.

    Bush spent a fourth day on the telephone, consulting leaders of Britain, Bulgaria, South Korea (news - web sites), Poland, El Salvador (news - web sites) and Norway.

    The U.S. diplomatic drive was centered on Chile and Mexico, both members of the U.N. Security Council, a senior administration official said. Their support would ensure the United States of the minimum nine votes need for adoption of the resolution.

    But France's threat to veto is taken seriously, and the administration may decide not to give France the chance by withdrawing the resolution, the official said on condition of anonymity. Bush was ready to drop the resolution, several aides said, if British Prime Minister Tony Blair didn't want it put to a vote.

    The president has pushed for a U.N. vote thus far out of respect for Blair, whose support of Bush has drawn severe criticism in Britain.

    Trouble loomed at every diplomatic turn.

    Irish Prime Minister Bertie Ahern, visiting Bush at the White House, said, "If there is not a resolution, Ireland cannot engage in support of military action, because we work under the U.N. resolution."

    Bush sent a letter to incoming Turkish Premier Recep Tayyip Erdogan and Vice President Dick Cheney (news - web sites) called the leader in hopes of securing permission to invade Iraq through Turkey. Hours later, Navy ships armed with Tomahawk missiles were told to move out of the Mediterranean into the Red Sea, a move that indicates weakening U.S. confidence that Turkey will grant overflight rights for U.S. planes and missiles.

    In Baghdad, the Iraqi government rejected a British compromise plan that would list six disarmament requirements Baghdad would have to meet or else face "serious consequences." Bush had signaled he would be willing to push back the March 17 deadline seven or 10 days if the gesture would help Blair.

    Russia said it would consider the plan. China said it doubted the plan could lead to consensus.

    The French dismissed the effort outright, sparking a trans-Atlantic shouting match.

    "We cannot accept the British proposals insofar as they are part of a logic of war, a logic of automatic recourse to war," said French Foreign Minister Dominique de Villepin.

    White House press secretary Ari Fleischer (news - web sites) said of France: "They rejected it before Iraq rejected it. If that isn't an unreasonable veto, what is?"

    Bush, meanwhile, backpedaled on his pledge to have a U.N. vote by Friday. Fleischer told reporters a tally could slip beyond the weekend.

    Several top administration officials said a growing number of advisers believe the resolution is doomed and they want the president to cut his losses and withdraw it. Others still hold out hope for the measure.

    The officials, all of whom spoke on condition of anonymity, agreed that a key is whether Blair wants Bush to give diplomacy more time.

    Bush and his advisers debated Thursday whether to press forward with the vote or withdraw the measure and pivot quickly to war footing. Bush has long planned to address the nation shortly after the U.N. debate is resolved and give Saddam a final ultimatum, perhaps including a deadline, for war.

    "We are still talking to members of the council to see what is possible," Secretary of State Colin Powell (news - web sites) said. "The options remain, go for a vote and see what members say or not go for a vote."

    That's a change of policy since last week, when Bush said he wanted U.N. members to "show their cards" even if that meant the measure failed.

    The U.S. ambassador to the United Nations (news - web sites), John Negroponte, didn't call for a vote Friday and diplomats doubted one would be called for Saturday.

    In London, Iain Duncan Smith, leader of the opposition Conservative Party, emerged from a meeting with Blair to say the prime minister believed war was more likely because "the French have become completely intransigent."

    Powell, testifying on Capitol Hill, said the "day of reckoning is fast approaching" for Iraq.

    US deploys B-2 bombers nearer to Iraq, orders missile-firing warships to Red Sea

    The US has deployed its B-2 stealth bombers to bases nearer to Iraq and plans to move as many as 15 cruise missile-firing warships to the Red Sea, signs that key weapons are taking up striking positions.

    A spokeswoman at the B-2s home at an air force base in Missouri confirmed that the bombers left overnight for an undisclosed forward base.

    She didn't say how many had been deployed, but defence officials had said orders had called for the deployment of four of the aircraft.

    And according to a defence official, the Pentagon late Thursday approved an order to send 10 to 15 warships from the Mediterranean to join more than 60 other US ships arrayed against Iraq.

    The official said the cruisers and destroyers, many carrying more than 100 long-range Tomahawk cruise missiles, would soon move through the Suez Canal into the Red Sea.

    Both the B-2 bombers and sea-launched Tomahawk cruise missiles are typically used to open an air assault on heavily defended enemy targets.

    The US Air Force has built special climate-controlled shelters for the B-2 stealth bombers at the British Indian Ocean island of Diego Garcia and Fairford Royal Air Force base in England.

    The bombers have flown combat missions in Kosovo and Afghanistan, but always flying non-stop from their base in Missouri.

    Positioning them closer to Iraq will allow for increased sorties and more intensive bombing campaign in the opening days of a war.

    The bombers are able to carry up to 16 satellite-guided 900kg bombs or eight 2.2-tonne bunker buster bombs.

    It is the only US warplane equipped to deliver the GBU-37 "bunker buster," which has a hardened nose and can bore through six to nine metres of rock or reinforced concrete before detonating.

    The B-2's mission is to penetrate heavily defended territory and "kick down the door" on the opening night of conflict.

    The main targets would be leadership bunkers, command centres, radars, airfields, air defences.

    Besides the bombers and warships, there are now well over 250,000 US troops deployed for a possible Iraq war, including 140,000 in Kuwait, where US and British troops have been massing on Iraq's southern border for an invasion, US defence officials said.

    The United States has between 600 and 700 combat aircraft deployed and five aircraft carrier battle groups divided between the Gulf and the eastern Mediterranean.

    A sixth aircraft carrier battle group led by the USS Nimitz was on its way to the region from the US West Coast.

    As advertised by military analysts and defence officials, US war plans call for massive air strikes and near simultaneous ground assaults from all directions to quickly topple Iraqi President Saddam Hussein.

    Security Council stalemate over Iraq shows no signs of abating

    The stalemate at the UN Security Council over Iraq shows no sign as abating, with members preparing to meet once again to find a breakthrough.

    Six "swinging" nations - Angola, Cameroon, Chile, Guinea, Mexico and Pakistan - are now working on new proposals to bridge the gap between the anti- and pro-war camps

    French President Jacques Chirac told British Prime Minister Tony Blair on Friday that Paris was ready to seek compromise with London over disarming Iraq but would not accept any ultimatum leading to war.

    Mr Chirac, who on Monday pledged to veto any war-authorising resolution, also wants weapons inspectors to come back to report to the Security Council before it decides anything.

    Agreeing, German Chancellor Gerhard Schroeder feels there can be a peaceful solution to the Iraq crisis, and that weapons inspections should go on.

    Mr Schroeder also believes that nearly everyone else on the Council - and not just Germany, France, Russia and China - are against war.

    With little support for military action, the US has dropped its demands for a United Nations vote by Friday.

    But it says it will keep trying until Monday, March 17 -- the deadline in a draft resolution for Iraq to disarm.

    US President George Bush meanwhile will travel to the Azores Islands in the mid-Atlantic Ocean for a hastily-arranged summit with Mr Blair and Spanish Prime Minister Jose Maria Aznar.

    Expected to top the agenda will be strategies for salvaging the trio's troubled war resolution.

    "In an effort to pursue every last bit of diplomacy the president will depart Sunday morning for the Azores to meet Prime Minister Blair and with Prime Minister Aznar to discuss prospects for resolving the situation peacefully with diplomacy in final pursuit of a United Nations resolution," said White House spokesman Ari Fleischer.

    Mr Bush has warned before that the US is prepared to bypass UN approval altogether, and go at it alone.

    Talks on a British proposal to avert war have also proved inconclusive.

    Iraq, France and Russia have rejected the plan, saying it does not prevent military action.

    And in more signs of Iraqi cooperation, Baghdad is set to hand over documents to weapons inspectors to prove that it has destroyed its stocks of VX nerve agents.
