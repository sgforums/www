    Agreed. I realised that our current fighter force has been reduced by the retirement of older types and new ones not being bought in sufficient numbers. Of course, the expected argument is that " An advanced warplane can do the job of 2 or more older ones " I beg to differ. This argument is true ASSUMING that your potential adversaries remain at a particular technological level and do not purchase any new equipment at all! In fact, this was highlighted by the Royal Australian Air Force, which stated that while the F-35 was an advanced 5th Generation aircraft, the projected buy of 60 warplanes would barely be sufficient to maintain the edge given by the current combined fleet of (100+) F-111s and F/A-18s.

    For instance 20 - 40 F-15Ts to replace 68 A-4/TA-4SUs appear completely reasonable at first. Not so when we take into account the rising numbers of Sukhoi Su-27s and its advanced variants entering service in the region. Add to the fact that it is extremly difficult to have a 100% flyable fleet due to equipment failure or maintanance, a sufficient fleet size must thus be maintained.

    After adding all warplanes locally based, overseas detachments and even those on order, I estimate the RSAF frontline fighter fleet to be over 130 warplanes. (TA-SUs and S211s not included as these are considered secondary combat assets) However, if the F-5s are retired before any replacement aircraft enter service, the RSAF fighter fleet may drop below 100 aircraft, a situation we have never been in since the late 70s.

    Of course, these are my personal views. For all we know, the RSAF may have enough aircraft in storage to nearly double our strength in times of emergency.
