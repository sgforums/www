    Super Skyhawks fly high for one last time

    If you spotted a Republic of Singapore Air Force (RSAF) A4-SU Super Skyhawk flying overhead on 31 Mar morning, you had actually witnessed a part of history.

    After 31 years of operations, the RSAF withdrew its fleet of Super Skyhawks from operational service on 31 Mar.

    The RSAF's 142 Squadron has been flying these fighter planes since 1974, and was the first RSAF squadron to be equipped with the upgraded A4-SU Super Skyhawks.

    To mark this significant milestone, a special flypast was conducted over Tengah Air Base. Twelve A4-SU Super Skyhawks took to the skies for one final flight over Singapore.

    Flying with the pilots were some of the top brass of the Singapore Armed Forces (SAF), including Chief of Defence Force, Lieutenant General (LG) Ng Yat Chung, and Chief of Air Force, Major-General (MG) Lim Kim Choon.

    Speaking at a simple ceremony after the flight, MG Lim called the fleet of Super Skyhawks "a faithful friend", one that had accomplished many missions and achieved much throughout its nearly 300,000 hours of operational flying.

    The A4-SUs' achievements included flying directly from Singapore to the Philippines, incorporating the RSAF's first air-to-air refuelling mission in 1986, as well as the excellent aerobatic display of the 'red and white' Super Skyhawks flown by the RSAF Black Knights during Asian Aerospace 1990.

    Thumbs up for a good flight! Chief of Defence Force, LG Ng Yat Chung (right) and MAJ Theng Fai Yew, take to the skies in a Super Skyhawk for one final time.

    Commanding Officer of 142 Squadron, Lieutenant-Colonel (LTC) Tay Boon Chong, who flew with MG Lim, was also full of praise for the aircraft he has been flying since 1992.

    He likened the planes to a Singapore "icon" for their many appearances at National Day Parades through the years.

    "I think they have a place in the hearts of Singaporeans and I am sure they have inspired many to join the RSAF to become fighter pilots," said LTC Tay.

    In recent months, the Super Skyhawks had continued to perform exceptionally, participating in various exercises and even coming out tops in a competition among the six RSAF fighter squadrons in February.

    Said LTC Tay: "I am glad we are ending on a high note." - CyberPioneer

    Off topic.....

    The combat performance of A-4s is something I've been following closely, both from the armchair ploughing through books and walking the flightline at various place and talking to people.

    The Argentine air force and navy Skyhawks were operating almost at the limit of their combat radius.

    It would have been a different story if:
    1) They recognised their fuzing problems early. Many bombs were dropped too low. Several bombs that hit their targets were not armed and failed to explode on impact, example the one that hit the Type 21 frigate HMS Antelope. The UXB exploded when it was being disarmed. Those that exploded did so with devastating effect, example: the sinking of Type 42 destroyer HMS Coventry.

    2) The A-4s carried more bombs per sortie. Because of the extreme range, many A-4s flew with only one 1,000Ib bomb on the centreline pylon.

    3) On a related point, if the A-4s had enough fuel to make more than one pass in San Carlos water, i.e. bomb alley, for strafing runs.

    4) The A-4s had better close range AAMs for self defence. It's no contest between early model AIM-Bs versus all-aspect AIM-9Ls on the "black death" Sea Harriers.

    5) There was more better coordination between Argentina's air and naval forces.

    6) A-4s worked with Airborne Early Warning support.

    Again off topic, I'm not sure the F-16 can best replace the Scooter in the CAS role... but that is my personal opinion.

    This in fact only affected the Type 21 frigates [Antelope and Ardent] that were lost, as these had aluminium used in their superstructures - this class was originally a private sector venture, and the aluminium useage was shown as an example of weight-saving on an 'economy' hull design.
    The Type 21's were then commisioned for the Royal Navy as a stop-gap measure while the Type 42 and Type 22's were beng developed and the Leanders [Type 12] and Type 82's replaced and cancelled respectively.

    The rough seas of the South Atlantic had initially caused enough damage to the structural integrity of the ships, and cracks were observed on the hull, and although alot was made of the aluminium content of these ships at the time, not one subsequent report pointed to this as a major contributing factor to the ships losses.
    Aluminium useage in the construction of any RN ships was ceased post-war anyway, with only the Type 21's ever having significant amounts of aluminium used in them.
