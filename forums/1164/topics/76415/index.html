    We tend to forget or don't even know what these weapons do to someone up close. Many veteran pilots have acknowledged that they try not to consider the man in the plane they shot down and tend to think of the planes themselves as targets; doing that can impair their trained instincts as moral judgements come into focus. I suspect many infantry riflemen have the same view prefering to think only of the "us vs them " until shown the results up close and personal (unless of course the combatants already view the individual enemy soldier as less than dogs).

    We do need these constant reminder that war is a terrible thing, just or unjust (and even that is philosophically/morally debatable, what makes a just war?). We can only hope that our leaders have the balls NOT to resort to war and try to end disputes by other possible means.

    Just one dead body can get pretty traumatising (ask any local police officer who had to pull out a dead person from a noose hanging from the ceiling, or those coast guard guys who have pulled out bloated bodies from the sea), multiply that by a few tens or hundreds and psychological numbness sets in.
