    Alright, it's true that Haig was easily one of the worst commanders-in-chief during World War I, sending thousands of his countrymen to death at battles of the Somme and Ypres.

    But Petain, prior to his disgrace as a Nazi collaborator during WWII, was actually one of the most outstanding generals of WWI. He led the defence and succeeded in holding Verdun against the German onslaught in one of the fiercest battles of the war, and he revived French morale and fighting spirit after the mutiny of 1917 by showing genuine concern for the welfare of his soldiers. His subsequent collaboration with the Nazis had more to do with his personality rather than any actual belief or endorsement of Nazi ideology. Petain was a staunch patriot and possessed a somewhat melancholic character, and his military skills were geared more towards conservation and defence rather than attack and conquest. When the Nazis broke the power of the French army with Operation Sichelschnitt, Petain believed that the only way for the French Republic to continue in existence was through collaboration with Hitler, and hence he agreed to become the head of the Vichy. This I believed stemmed more from his patriotism than from any belief in Nazism, and so I think we should not see Petain in such a bad light as some others have made him out to be.

    If you are interested, you can try reading Corelli Barnett's The Swordbearers, which has a good deal of material on Petain during WWI.
