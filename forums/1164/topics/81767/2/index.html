Micro-ly, the US Navy was already tangling with German U-boats in the months prior to Pearl Harbour.

    Concurrently, the Neutrality Act was already been repealed bit by bit, the US miltary was starting to mobilise its troops (the entire pre-war active army was smaller than MacArthur's Phillipines command, if I'm correct), & FDR actually sanctioned the nuclear-war progam a day before Pearl Harbour.

    Also, the cracking of the Japanese cipher is a true event; the USA knew beforehand that the militarists had won the day & that they've given up on diplomacy.     (a) what if Japan didn't attack Pearl Harbour, as the topic title indicated?     I found a good book - Dan Van de Vat's "The Pacific Campaign: the US-Japanese Naval War 1941-45" - from my personal library that summarized well what went down.

    The Japs' Southern Strategy was operationally devised from their late '41 "Outline for the Execution of the Empire's National Policy http://www.ibiblio.org/pha/monos/150/150app02.html - which recommended 3 offensive stages:

    (a) invading into, &/or securing aerial superiority, in SE Asia (southern Thailand, Malaya, Philippines, Borneo, Dutch East Indies);

    (b) consolidating & strengthening the perimeter of its newly-acquired colonies (i.e. the various Pacific islands);

    (c) sit tight within its fortified sphere, ward off counterattacks & sap the US will to fight, while exploiting natural resources.

    Militarily, Pearl Harbour - "PH" - had to be included in Stage A since, as with the other great naval base (Singapore) they hosted the maritime bulwark that could've stymied any invasion maneuvers. The difference is Singapore had to be occupied, while Pearl Harbour was only to be neutralized so that Japan could occupy - with reference to American interests - Guam & Wake Island according to Stage B.

    In this light, I'm very doubtful about "what if the Japanese didn't attack PH". They had to deter the US Navy enough in their own Pacific "backyard", whether or not the Americans are going to proactively aid Britain in the Far East.

    But if they really hadn't, or if the forewarned Americans had beaten them off, then IMHO Wake Island would've been the Midway of a parallel universe, whether FDR declared war or not.

    The reality was that Wake managed to held out for around 2 weeks (until withdrawal orders was given), unlike Guam which fell very quickly. The Phillipines was losing - Bataan, Corregidor, etc. - but MacArthur won't formally leave for 4-5 months. So perhaps the US Navy would've either used its new staging area to traverse & beef up its strength there or - if aggressive enough (topically unlikely) - invade Jap-occupied Formosa & threathen their main islands, with or without Chiang Kai-Shek's help.

    Too speculative a scenario. There's just too many underlying variables - some diverging - that could've subtlely influence the Pacific-theater developments in one way or another. Like, what if certain naval vessels hadn't (or had) been sunk, or the Zero fighter losing (or just depleting) its aura of superiority, or the relative impact on American morale (with all pre-attack preparations in place, thanks to their codebreakers).

    At the very least, I'll say that Japan would've reclaimed the psychological edge of homeland "immunity" which the Doolittle Raid had earlier debunked. Midway was the direct consequence of this daring incident, & not so much being Hawaii's "backyard" as the latter similarly is to continental USA. IMHO Japan's original defensive perimeter wasn't supposed to extend thus far eastwards either.

    The preceding Battle of Coral Sea was variously interpreted in its results - it's fairest to poclaim a statemate, with a tactical win for Japan but a strategic victory for America - but if they weren't stopped at Midway (let alone lost the battle), I think the Japs would've mightily strengthened its geopolitical status to eventually sue for "detente" as originally planned.

    My bet is it's also likely, as far as the US-Japan naval confrontation goes, the battle arena would've continued north of Australia in the South Pacific. There's no internal consensus that Japan wanna invade the ANZAC countries, who remained that staunchly pro-Allied & who took in the withdrawing British that didn't aim for India. Japan did desire to colonize all of the Pacific islands nearby - Guadacanal, Papua, New Guinea (Port Moseley), New Caledonia, etc. - as part of its defensive perimeter. Coral Sea was fought partly with these territories as prizes &, later, the US Marines' costly "island-hopping" attempts to retake them also showed just how palpably sound the Japs' "fortified boundary" blueprint was.

    Without a Midway defeat, IMHO Japan would've constructed even more airfields in these territories in order to entrench their air-cover advantage. Whether they would've become springboards or staging posts for the ultimate assault on Australia is open to debate, but they would've certainly rendered it more difficult for the US Marines to re-secure than in actuality once the Americans were on the offensive.
