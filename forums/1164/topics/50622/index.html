    agree wholeheartedly with a), b) c) and e). However Poland didn't really test the mettle of the Blitzkreig theory to the full. The Polish were too weak and ill-prepared for the job. I would say that the pivotal moment for Blitzkreig was the invasion of France and the Low Countries in 1940, where the Germans took on the French and BEF who knew they were coming (albeit using inferior tactics).

    Another pivotal point was the bitter struggle for the SW Pacific, including the battles for Guadacanal, the Solomons, PNG etc. Many historians regard Midway as the turning point of the Pacific War, but the fact was that Midway balanced the war, but the SW Pacific battles was the one which turned the tide in that it wore the Japanese war machine down and allowed American industrial supremacy to come through.

    Laser51088, Market-Garden was a grand f***-up of historic proportions. It basically showed

    1) why you shouldn't underestimate the enemy
    2) Get intel, AND believe it
    3) Never advance on a narrow front
    4) Ignoring (3) invariably makes your flanks and logistical tail hard to defend
    5) No plan survives first contact with the enemy

    Have you read A Bridge too Far by Cornelius Ryan and Citizen Soldiers by Stephen Ambrose? Both are must reads if you want info on Market Garden, although the first is more detailed since it was focused only on that op, although Citizen Soldiers covered the Western Allies advance up NW Europe so had less focus on Market-Garden, it has access to newer info (since it was about 20 years later than Ryan's work).
