    Vexing Issue of Workshare Resurfaces Over Eurofighter
    Aviation Week & Space Technology
    10/20/2003, page 55

    Douglas Barrie
    London

    Eurofighter Typhoon partners haggle over workshare issues

    Percentage Game

    The highly charged issue of work-share is reemerging as Eurofighter partner nations play brinksmanship as they struggle to conclude tricky Tranche 2 negotiations.

    Britain, Germany, Italy and Spain are locked in contract negotiations for the second production run of the Typhoon aircraft. Along with industrial issues, the four partner-nations are trying to thrash out the technical baseline for the Tranche 2 standard which will have a significantly improved air-to-surface capability.

    The most recent target date for signing off on Tranche 2 is by year-end but some industry executives involved in the Typhoon are concerned that, given the difficulty of the discussions, this may not be met.

    Failure to conclude a production contract by year-end courts the emergence of a "production gap" between Tranche 1 and 2, warned one executive. Stop-start production would raise costs on the program, placing further strain on funding.

    The suspicion that Britain faces the near inevitability of having to cut Typhoon numbers is fueling the debate on workshare among the partners. At present the U.K. has 37%, Germany 30%, Italy 19% and Spain 14%, predicated on purchases of: 232, 180, 121 and 87 aircraft, respectively. Germany was forced to relinquish work on the program when it reduced its aircraft numbers in the mid-1990s. Agreement on the present workshare arrangements was reached in 1996.

    The U.K. is taking 55 Typhoons from Tranche 1, with 89 to be procured as part of Tranche 2. At present, the Defense Ministry will also contract for 88 aircraft as part of Tranche 3. However, there is a substantial doubt as to what extent, and even whether, any of this batch will ever be achieved.

    Britain also had an option for a further 65 aircraft, but this already appears to have been quietly discarded.

    With a Defense White Paper to be published around year-end, the British Defense Ministry has been reviewing program commitments alongside a study conducted on future combat aircraft number requirements.

    Industry officials close to the program have told Aviation Week & Space Technology that both Germany and Italy are pushing to see workshare on the program reconsidered in light of concern over a drop in the number of aircraft Britain purchases.

    While publicly the British government and defense officials adhere to the 232 aircraft, senior officials have privately admitted this figure is almost certainly untenable. With considerable pressure on medium- to long-term defense expenditure, there is an expectation the U.K. will have to reduce Typhoon numbers.

    Against this backdrop, the other partners, and Germany in particular, are looking to address workshare.

    Were Britain, however, to unilaterally reduce the number of Typhoon combat aircraft it intended to purchase, it would then be liable to compensate the other partner nations for any increase in unit cost. The penalty-clause payments would effectively negate expenditure savings in terms of initial procurement costs.

    Industry executives and defense ministry sources have previously suggested U.K. strategy was to try to conclude present Tranche 2 discussions before broaching the thorny issue of the number of aircraft for a third production run.

    BAE Systems is the U.K. industry lead and one of the three companies involved in Eurofighter GmbH.; the others are EADS and Finmeccanica subsidiary Alenia Aeronautica. BAE declined to discuss the issue of workshare within the context of ongoing Tranche 2 negotiations.

    Any discussion on workshare distribution is compounded by the systems' area being allocated a large part of the development work. Some program insiders admit that systems-related work already has a bias toward BAE.

    From an industrial position, BAE will be loath to relinquish work, while EADS will push hard to garner additional responsibilities for its German plants.

    In parallel, the partner nations are also trying to pin down the exact configuration of Tranche 2 aircraft, and the provision of potential upgrades avenues for further enhancements.

    As of late last week, all Typhoons had been temporarily grounded following a problem with the braking system on a German aircraft. This is being attributed to a single-component, rather than a system, failure.

    Singapore assured of French support in warplane deal
    Article dated 3rd November 2003

    Singapore gets French government support in joint on-board radar development and greater access to training areas in France if Rafale is chosen over two other aircraft...

    Should Singapore go with the French aviation giant, Dassault Aviation, in opting for Rafale as the warplane to replace the SAF's aging A-4 Super Skyhawks, greater collaboration is expected in the pipeline. For one, a joint development of the Rafale's on-board radar - the advanced RBE-2 radar, is expected. This was reinforced by Marc Perrin de Brichambaut, the French Defence Ministry strategic affairs director, "It's true that if you get involved in the Rafale, the development of the new radar is to be done jointly."

    Another plus factor for choosing the multi-role French fighter craft would grant "air space scarce" Singapore greater access to air force training areas in France.

    Currently, MINDEF has short listed 3 fighter crafts from 3 respective contenders - F15T Strike Eagle from Boeing; Rafale from Dassault Aviation and Euro Typhoon from a consortium of European aircraft companies, in supplying sophisticated warplanes for the Republic's Air Force. If MINDEF does go with the Rafale deal, this will supplement its previous order of 6 stealth frigates from France's Direction des Constructions Navales International (DCN). The 110m-long frigates are being built both in France and in Singapore. They are expected to be commissioned here in Singapore in 2005.

    The stealth warship is built with maximum security and firepower under its hood. It will be armed with guns, anti-aircraft and anti-ship missiles, making it self-contained. To add to its already "invincible" status, each frigate will have a naval helicopter on board for search and destroy missions of enemy submarines. According to Mr de Brichambaut, the frigates are equipped with the best equipment, "...you (Singapore) have tended to choose the best equipment on these frigates. Most of that equipment has not been integrated before on a project of this scale."

    A spin-off from this defence arrangement is France benefiting from Singapore geo-political knowledge of the region. Singapore's awareness of regional developments in a broader context will as a consequence aid France it its counter-terrorism planning. - Patrick, G.
