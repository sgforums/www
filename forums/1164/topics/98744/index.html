    I thumbed through my library shelves last night ....

    IMO One quite-detailed recent account of the Mutiny can be found in Ban Kah Choon's "Absent History" from '01. He expounded that its biggest legacy was actually the founding of the Special Branch (forerunner of the ISD), cuz the British woke up to the fact that S'pore was no longer a prosperous colony insulated from worldly developments. Even as instant retribution was severe - the riot ringleaders were publicly executed - more colonial attention would be paid to internal security of British possessions & interests.

    The actual jolt was caused, foremost, by troops dissatisfid with cantonment duties & indifferent leadership. But there's a larger dimension, too, lurking in the background:

    (a) politics: WWI had broken out half a year earlier, & Turkey sided with the Central Powers. Together with Germany, they attempted to subvert against the British Empire. Some of the mutineers would attempt to free German POWs at Tanglin Barracks.

    (b) religion: Turkey issued a jihad, plus Malayan & Indian-Army Muslims didn't like Britain warring against it anyway. The humiliating Dardanelles/Gallipoli campaigns would occur later in the same year (1915).

    (c) nationalism: Indians were inspired by the anti-imperialist successes of the Bolsheviks, Japanese & Chinese.

    (d) ideology: the Ghadr militants advocated a violent overthrow of the colonial warlords.

    Ban's account actually downplayed the role of the constabulary actions. S'pore was still quite ulu then, with the mutineers numbering some 300 vs. 600 defending troops. The eventual casualty rate was put at around 40, & most disturbances occurred on the 1st day before fizzling out came the new dawn (due to no real objectives). The whole thing was relatively isolated as well: the German POWs & local defence forces refused to join them, & one group of riots who ventured towards town was easily stopped. Then it was a straightforward matter of rounding them up for trial.
