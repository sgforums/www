    Ship collision trial: Prosecution grills expert
    By Ben Nadarajan

    THE prosecution in the trial of two navy officers charged with negligently causing a collision on Jan 3 last year reminded a defence witness yesterday that his duty was to the court, not to his 'paymasters'.

    Attacking Captain John Third's conclusion that it was the actions of the container vessel ANL Indonesia which caused the collision with the navy ship RSS Courageous, the prosecutors tried to undermine the master mariner's credibility.

    Deputy Public Prosector Hamidul Haq first said that Capt Third had not sailed for more than 20 years and that he had never been in command of a ship like the ANL Indonesia. But Capt Third pointed out that he had been investigating ship collisions for more than 20 years and that he was very familiar with how container ships operated.

    DPP Hamidul suggested Capt Third's testimony was biased: 'You have offered your services as an expert in court for a fee to any client that wishes to engage your services and to present a case for them.'

    Capt Third, a partner of marine surveyor Brookes Bell Jarrett Kirman Marine Consultants in London, retorted: 'No, if that was the way I conduct my business, I probably wouldn't have a business.'

    When the DPP pursued this line of questioning and again said Capt Third was testifying as 'part of a business exercise', the witness said: 'Yes, it is an essential component of living that one has to earn money.'

    DPP Hamidul then said it was very strange that in his expert reports, Capt Third had not found any fault with the navy vessel, but for a 'one-liner' saying that the Courageous had breached the rules by going against the flow of traffic.

    He asked: 'So you are saying the Courageous did everything by the book and did not break any rule and norms of good seamanship?'

    Capt Third said his role was to determine the cause of the collision and, in his view, the two navy officers had not caused it. 'I've never really regarded what they did not do, but I have been looking and focusing my attention on what they did do.'

    The cross-examination continues today.
