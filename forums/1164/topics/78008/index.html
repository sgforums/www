    Israel has more strategic depth than Singapore for it is a bigger country, protected from Syria by mountains i.e. the Golan Heights and Egypt by the Negev desert. Population centers are relatively protected in the heart of Israel. But I agree with your point. Israel's acquisition of nuclear capability despite its conventional superiority demonstrates the importance of nuclear deterrence. Still, however much we try to portary Singapore as the Israel of Southeast Asia, this analogy is not very helpful. Israel and her Arab neighbours as well as India and Pakistan fought 3 all-out wars since their independence. In every one of those wars, the very existence of Israel and Pakistan was at stake. This explains why they wanted to acquire nuclear weapons. But how many wars have Singapore fought with our neigbours? ( Confrontation is not a proper war for it involves only the limited use of force) Israelis dun go shopping in southern Lebanon but we do in JB. Singapore and Malaysia are each other's main trading partners (if not first then second I believe). Furthermore, we are not only integrated economically but also politically and socially. Destroying our neighbours with a nuclear strike is tantamount to destroying ourselves. You want a military capability capable of hurting but not destroying your potential regional adversaries.

    A nuclear balance of power, any balance of power for that matter, is never stable. The US and the Soviets almost started WWIII over the Cuban Missile Crisis. Indian and Pakistan narrowly averted a nuclear exchange over Kargil. Mutually Assured Destruction is just that, MAD. Furthemore, note that MAD doctrine is not very effective in the case of close regional neighbours. The proximity between these countries is such that any missile will only take seconds to arrive, as compared to the several minutes the US and USSR had. Since the margin for error is very low, missile units in both countries will want to fire first for they do not want to be annihilated in a first strike. Any misunderstanding will be catastrophic. The temptation for a first strike can be reduced by a second strike capability. But I do not see Singapore acquring SSBNs in the future. No country will sell and to make one will be crazy. The other option is to keep F-16s armed with nuclear gravity bombs in the air at all times (Think Clancy's Sum of All Fears). But then, there is a reason why the US developed the B-2 bomber. Strong air defences can readily take bombers out of the sky, especially since high-value targets e.g. KL will be well-protected. At the end of the day, acquiring a nuclear deterrent does not mean conflict will magically disappear. Miscalculations and misunderstandings can still prompt nuclear states towards a nuclear exchange.
