    From ChannelNewsAsia

    Malaysia to pay for Polish tanks with cash, goods

    Malaysia said it would pay Poland for the supply of 48 attack tanks worth 1.4 billion ringgit (US$368 million) through cash and barter trade.

    Malaysia's Defence Minister Najib Tun Razak said the PT-91-M tanks would arrive in Malaysia in batches over a one-year period from February 2006.

    He said the deal included supplementary equipment such as recovery vehicles, training for some 150 military personnel in Poland and technology transfer.

    Mr Najib said the main battle tanks would improve the firepower of the armed forces.

    "Tanks give a shock treatment. They are important components in ground offensives. They give us the firepower and capability which we do not have in our inventory," he said.

    Earlier, Malaysian defence ministry officials signed an agreement with Poland's FTE Bumar Ltd for the supply of the tanks.

    Malaysian Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad first announced the decision to buy Polish tanks during a visit to Warsaw in March, 2002.

    Visiting Polish Defence Minister Jerzy Szmajdzinski said the sale of the tanks would boost the countries' economic and defence ties.

    Poland-Malaysia to sign contract for 48 tanks

    WARSAW (AFP) Apr 09, 2003

    Poland and Malaysia are set on Friday to sign a contract for Poland to supply 48 tanks to Malaysia under a contract totalling 400 million dollars (372 million euros), the Polish defence ministry said on Wednesday.

    "It involves 48 PT-91-M tanks with supplementary equipment and services, which will be provided by Poland up to 2006," defence ministry spokesman Eugeniusz Mleczak told AFP.

    The contract will be signed on Poland's behalf in Kuala Lumpur by Roman Baczynski, the head of Bumar, the company which makes the tanks, and in the presence of Poland's Defence Minister Jerzy Szmajdzinski who heads to Malaysia on Thursday.

    The contract will be Poland's biggest weapons deal since 1981 and Malaysia's first purchase of assault tanks, Mleczak said.

    "Malaysia wants to use it to built its first armoured division," Mleczak said.

    Malaysian Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad first announced the decision to buy Polish tanks during a visit to Warsaw in March, 2002, but no details were given of the number involved.

    At the time he suggested that Malaysia pay part of the cost with commodities.

    Last year Poland also sold India an arms package, including tank systems and technical assistance vehicles, worth 147 million dollars and Mleczak said that Poland is also working on another "big" arms sales contract with India.

    Poland's economy ministry says Poland could export between 1.5 billion and two billion dollars' worth of arms over the next two or three years.

    Contracts With Malaysia and India

    By L.Â¯.
    20 February 2003

    Polish companies stand the chance of earning more than $600 million by delivering military equipment to the Far East.

    On his Far Eastern visit last week, Prime Minister Leszek Miller encouraged industrial cooperation between Poland and Singapore and India.

    Tanks for Malaysia

    In a $300-million contract, Poland will sell Malaysia 48 cutting-edge PT 91 M Twardy (Hard) tanks and technical support vehicles. The final agreements are expected in March, Miller said Feb. 16.
    The Malaysian contract, if finalized, will be of great importance for Poland's tank manufacturers. The negotiations lasted many months. An important part of them applied to compensation that the Malaysians demand for the tanks and accompanying equipment they are ready to buy.

    As part of the offset program, the Malaysians would want some of the tank and component production to be moved to Malaysia. The settlement for the arms deliveries would be made in cash and barter trade. As payment, Poland would accept palm oil and textiles that could be re-exported, among other products. In the future, the end assembly of the tanks could take place in Malaysia. Poland would also want to sell the Malaysians ammunition production technology.

    Miller said that if the program succeeded, there was a chance to expand the Malaysian order to include further batches of equipment worth $200 million.

    The Hard tank offered to Malaysia will be fitted with air-conditioning, a powerful 1,000-hp engine from PZL Wola and fire targeting systems from France's SAGEM company.
