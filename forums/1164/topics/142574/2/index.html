    Hmmm - let's see. The CEP of the American Peacemaker is 100 metres, while the CEP for the Chinese Ju Lang 2 and Dong Feng 31 missiles is anything up to 300 metres. As a rough measure, you would need 10 Chinese warheads to have the same effect as one American warhead.

    Bottom line, losing all cities east of Xian is an estimate so optimistic as to border on manic. Even if the Chinese get the first shot off, a nuclear shootout would mean that all Chinese cities, not just east but west, north and south of Xian are going to be turned to ashtrays. Mind you, that's without factoring the US Navy's SLBM capability into the equation.

    As for the issue of shooting down a reentry vehicle, it's a matter of speed. An MIRV travels in excess of 24,000 km/h. The PAC-3 is designed to intercept tactical ballistic missiles travelling at about a quarter of that speed.

    That said, the critical factor here is that the missile travels towrads its target instead of chasing after it, so the speed of the PAC-3 is not a deal-breaker here. More importantly, it's the speed at which the trajectory of the missile and target, and the precise point of the PAC-3 warhead's detonation, can be calculated .

    The trouble they had with the original Patriot's 24-bit guidance system was that it was about 0.34 seconds slow in making its decisions. However, it is essentially a software problem. It's only a matter of time before they get their algorithms correct and improve the software so that it can intercept an MIRV - with a range of 100 km, the PAC-3 has more than enough range to catch a MIRV reentering the atmosphere.
