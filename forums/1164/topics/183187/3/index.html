    Balasore (Orissa): Akash, the indigenously developed surface-to-air missile, was test fired from the Integrated Test Range at Chandipur-On-Sea, about 14 km from here today, DRDO sources said.

    The sophisticated multi-target missile was test fired from a mobile launcher at about 12.30 pm.

    Earlier, Lakshya, the Pilotless Target Aircraft, was test flown from the ITR at 12.10 pm and Akash was fired at an object attached to the PTA as its target.

    The missile has a range of 27 km with an effective ceiling of 15 km, the sources said. It uses an Integral Ramjet Rocket Propulsion System and has a low reaction time.

    The sleek missile, with a 700 kg launch weight, could carry a warhead of 60 kg.

    Akash operates in conjunction with Rajendra Surveillance and Engagement Radar being developed by Electronic Research and Development Establishment. The radar is capable of tracking 64 targets and guide upto 12 missiles simultaneously.

    The missile, part of India's Guided Missile Development Programme, is being developed by the DRDO and had undergone a number of trials.

    The current thrust of the test was on its guidance system, the sources said adding that the development was also aimed at increasing its speed, maximum altitude and extend the range upto 60 km.

    Indian SAM decision may influence other procurements

    David C Isby

    India's long-standing competition to select a new low-altitude system to replace the Soviet-designed S-125 (SA-3 'Goa') in the reorganised Western Air Command Area is considered one of the most significant in Asia, writes David C Isby. The value of the contract for 12 truck-mobile batteries is reported in the Indian press as INR1,500 crore (USD340 million).

    While a number of other systems have been considered, the Israeli-designed Rafael-IAI Spyder MR system (using Python 5 and Derby air-to-air missiles) and the MBDA MICA vertical-launch system (VLS) are reported to have been short-listed since 2005. Both competitors have existing strong co-operative ties with Indian missile programmes. The Israelis are working with India on improved versions of the Barak naval SAM while MBDA has built a close relationship with India's Bharat Systems on a number of programmes.

    The winner is expected to be announced in May or June, and the successful bidder will have a potential advantage in the upcoming Singapore competition to identify a replacement for both its IHAWK and Rapier Blindfire within the next few years. Singapore is also likely to be looking for a replacement for its RBS-70 surface-to-air missiles (SAMs).

    While a formal procurement process has not yet started, Singapore is already being strongly marketed with a range of international competitors offering both single-system and integrated solutions. In addition to the competitors for the Indian procurement, a land-based version of the Eurosam ASTER SAMs already ordered for the Singapore Navy is believed to be under consideration. Singapore is also believed to have been offered new short-range missile systems (most notably from Russia) and a number of overhaul and upgrade packages for the RBS-70.

    Malaysia and Pakistan also have long-standing requirements for medium SAMs that are likely to be filled in the near future. While both countries are being targeted by many producers, current thinking is that Malaysia is likely to select a Russian-designed system and Pakistan a Chinese-designed one. Other major land-based SAM programmes in Asia include the Republic of Korea's indigenous development of the M-SAM (Medium SAM), which is intended to replace the IHAWK, and procurement of the SAM-X (intended to replace the Nike-Hercules), and Japan's procurement of the PAC-3.
