    The injuries were first seen in July, when Israel launched operations in Gaza following the capture of an Israeli soldier by Palestinian militants.

    Doctors said that, unlike traditional combat injuries, there was no large shrapnel found in the bodies and there appeared to be powder on damaged internal organs.

    "Bodies arrived severely fragmented, melted and disfigured," said Mr Jumaa Saqa'a, a doctor at the Shifa hospital, in Gaza City. "We found internal burning of organs, while externally there were minute pieces of shrapnel. When we opened many of the injured people we found dusting on their internal organs."

    It is unclear whether the injuries come from a new weapon. The Israeli military declined to detail the weapons in its arsenal but denied reports that the injuries came from a Dense Inert Metal Explosive (Dime), an experimental weapon.

    "Due to operational reasons, the IDF (Israeli Defense Forces) cannot specify the types and use of weapons in its possession," the military said.

    In the United States, Dime is still in the early stages of development. It contains fine tungsten particles and causes a very powerful blast in a radius that is smaller than other explosives.

    According to Israeli newspaper Haaretz, Dime is highly carcinogenic and is harmful to the environment.

    Dr Saqa'a said the small pieces of shrapnel found in patients' bodies did not show up in X-rays and most injuries were around the abdomen.

    Doctors also found that patients who were stabilised after one or two days suddenly died. "The patient dies without any apparent scientific cause," Dr Saqa'a said.

    Photographs of some of the dead from Shifa hospital showed bodies that had been melted and blackened beyond recognition. In several cases doctors amputated badly burnt limbs.

    The health ministry in Gaza reported that these injuries came from an "unprecedented type of projectile". It called for an investigation into the cause of the wounds.

    "We have never seen this before," said Mr Khalid Radi, a health ministry spokesman.

    An Italian laboratory that analysed tissue samples from Gaza patients reportedly said its results were compatible with the hypothesis that a Dime weapon was involved.

    The International Committee of the Red Cross, which monitors weapons used in conflicts, said it was collecting information on the case.
