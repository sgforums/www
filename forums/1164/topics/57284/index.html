    There were quite a number of things that went wrong with the Collin class subs. Most of the problems can be trace back to 2 major attributes, Australian lack of submarine building and combat system integration skills and Swedish lack of building ocean going sub skills.

    The Australian company did relatively well in such circumstances not without massive help from the US (GE Boats) though.

    The Collin class design were based on a scaled up A-17 design with greater integrated combat systems. Problems were identified in the area of:
    1. the cavitation noise from the sub hull (design problem), which resulted in US modifying the sail with an added curve to smooth out water flow, etc.

    2. Propeller generated cavitation noise. Redesign of the propellers with US help again

    3. Integrated combat systems design locally with Swedes help were not performed as well. After the purchase of Kockum by HDW, the German wanted to rectified that but the Aussie govt had already engaged US Navy for help resulting in Raytheon getting involved in a redevelopment of the combat system

    4. Periscope problem. Resolved quickly.

    5. Disael engine problem. Resolved.

    6. Burst pipes which flood 1 compartment. Problem traced to welding quality done in Sweden.

    etc.

    I won't say, therefore, Kockum sucks. On the contrary, they are good, but they still lack significant skills in developing subs for different environment from the Baltics. The German with that U209 and the French with the A209 Agosta seem to fair better.

    Torpedoes is another form of artillery shell or missile but fired from below the sea using the submarine as a platform (instead of an artillery gun, tank, multi-rocket launch system, or from an airborne platform).

    An extract from the followings website will give information on the current design of torpedoes:

    Torpedoes
    The torpedo has been the staple weapon of submarines since WW I but it is now a much more sophisticated and reliable device than back then. No longer a 'point and shoot' weapon the modern torpedo is almost always wire guided with its own sonar built in for the terminal homing run.

    Propulsion is either via the traditional propeller or the more advanced pump jet, with power supplied either by large batteries or the more powerful (but not very safe to be near if it leaks) OTTO hydrogen peroxide fuel, giving a maximum speed of 50 - 65 knots (92 - 120 km/h) and a range of 10 - 15 nm (18 - 27 km) at high speeds or 20 - 30 nm (36 - 55 km) at low speeds.

    The 'heavyweight' torpedoes employed by submarines are about 6 - 7 m long and usually have a 250 - 350 kg warhead designed to explode on impact (best against submarine targets) or upwards from underneath the target (to 'break the back' of a surface vessel).
