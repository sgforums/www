    The RAVs look very useful for taking the point and entering buildings before the infantry, but I would expect heavy losses. Even a 40mm M203 grenade or hand grenade would result in a certain mission kill at least (if not a hard kill), not to mention automatic fire or LAWs. The Spartan could maybe mount some armor, but due to its larger profile I would also not put its battlefield lifetime at anything more than a few minutes in an urban scenario. It will certainly not survive a LAW, and grenades would probably mission kill it too. Still better than losing men definitely, but not cost efficient.

    The problem with unmanned platforms is how easily mission killed they are. Things come loose or jam in any weapon system, particularly in combat. Unlike a manned platform where the crew can perform recovery drills, an unmanned platform cannot recover from faults unless that capability is built into it, which comes at a cost. For example, if an MG on a RAV jams, it has to drive back to base for someone to clear it, unless it has a jam clearing machine, which means more weight and something else to break. It is definitely not just cock the weapon and go. If a weapon impact breaks the optics, disables communications or destroys a servo, the whole system is rendered useless unless it has spares, which means more weight and cost again. If you think that machines are reliable, you need to spend more time working with them.

    That is why as far as unmanned platforms for land combat are concerned, I prefer simple, cheap and numerous devices until the technology matures. My thinking is that since machines are unreliable, a large number of redundant systems should be employed, which means they need to be simple and cheap. Micro UAVs to perform recce. Stationary gun turrets to perform sentry duty or spring ambushes (think of the movie Aliens). Smart land mines. Small remote sensors you can drop on the floor or stick to a wall. That sort of thing.
