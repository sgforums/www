    Hi,

    The Ultimax's 100-rd drum magazine can be hand- loaded without any acessories in the field.

    Back in 1994-1993, when my NSF platoon conducted day and night gunnery live-firings, each soldier in the platoon and more fired 90-rds each with the drum mag on the SAW. There were 3 SAW at the firing points. We did not oil the drum mag. The only field care we performed was to oil the barrel and the chamber after each firing details. Sustained bursts were fired too, though avoided since the rounds drifted away from the targets. We loaded 90 rds instead of 100rds to avoid mag malfunction due to no-spring tension.

    I guess it is for practical reasons that S'pore infantry and other foot-sloggers are not equipped with the 100rd drums. I was/am transported on armour vehicle. I have a mechanized mule to lug the cumbersome fully- loaded 4x100rd drum carrier around. Fully loaded, the 4x100rd drum carrier is HEAVY. Look at the GIs and Australian Diggers in the Vietnam War. The big buggers were carrying minimum essential combat load to function effectively in the harsh weather and difficult terrain conditions. Fast forward to the Presence, the lesson is still applicable. Keep the individual soldiers' skeleton battle order light so that we can be effective in Asia environment.

    I recalled a study done by SPC, Soldier Performance Center, in the 90's. For an average soldier to remain combat effective in combat tempo, his individual combat load should be limited to 15% of his body weight. When we conduct route marches in the past, we went to great length to distribute our platoon stores and combat load among the soldiers. Now, with more electronic gadgets, more batteries and maintenance kits, we have to introduce the Load-Bearing Vest, bigger full-pack to lug the load!!! It is not gung-hu at all. We had to wear armour vest now since we are weight-down and lumbering. Singapre's foot-sloggers' days just got tougher.

    Hi,

    Can't imagine an average SAF SAW gunner going on assault with 4xdrum mag and 9xbox mag. As it is clearing SOC will be a nightmare During my NSF days, the 4 drums were carried in a sling-style carrier. Imagine a Milo-tin with one sling except that the tin open sideway with 4 divided- space.

    SAF fighting doctrine is blitz-oriented. That mentality permeates down all the way to the infantry section tactics. Compare our 7-men infantry section to the traditional 9>men fighting section, our fire footprint is heavier but narrow. The limitation in frontage has to be mitigated by aggressive maneouver by each member of the section.

    When we assault, the self-contained SAW gunners leap & bound forward as well as part of the assault element. They fix bayonent as well. Such aggressive tactics serve to carry the enemy ahead of us as we close in. No quarters are given nor asked when we have to fight with lesser strength. Your unit's aggressive posturing & attitude will intimidate the enemy and exponentially heighten your unit's confidence and integrity. Such aggressive tactics were ably demonstrated by WWI German storm-troopers and WWII SSs and more recently, in Falkland conflict by the British paras. It is brutally effective when you have the determination and brawn to pull it off. At such close range, when the fire-fight is vicious and you can see the white of your enemy's eye, box mag is faster to switch; remedying stoppages is less heart-stopping. (GPMG gunners are equipped with small capacity assault mag for such situation).

    GPMG, HMG and small-calibre cannon fitted with tripod, pintles and etc should be dedicated to the stand-off sustained firing role. Their barrels are constructed for such express purpose. I can't imagine a section carrying half-dozen of spare SAW barrel for close-in fighting. I would rather carry more M203 / hand grenades and box mags for sharing with my riflemen and SAW gunners.

    Of course, having said all of the above, I love the drum mag They are reliable and simple to use. In fixed positions, when we are emplaced, the drum mag is a real confidence boaster against infantry assault waves.

    Hi,

    Take a closer look at the twin-drum mag for this modified version. When loaded on the weapon, it will give the SAW a fatter side profile compared to the present 100rd single-drum mag. It utilized a tall M16-style box mag lip to feed rounds from both drums into the weapon. The twin-drums distribute the ammo load evenly on both sides of the weapon. The 100rd single drum mag will protrude and occupy more space below the weapon. In ergonomic sense, both drum designs are unsuitable for close-in fighting. Otherwise, let's equip all our riflemen with drum mag for their SAR21 Let find out more about the mag design specification and field trial observation before we conclude on the reliability of both design.

    Based on this modification, I assume the Americans are uneasy with the current well-less mag feed construction. Since the side-mounted 2-pin mag catch release button has been retained on this modified version, I assume the present 2-pin mag catch has been retained.

    Actually, I see merits on the well-less design. Allow easier access to clean the firing chamber and clear any stoppages such as double feeding, ruptured cartridges and etc. My platoon never encounter the mag-drop-by-accident scenario. Anyone care to share their personal experience?
