    Originally posted by fudgester:
    I doubt it. You'd need lots and lots of armour to defend against a Harpoon or a Gabriel, and that would weigh the ship down. And even then, that's no guarantee that the missile won't cause damage.
    .
    When the US re-activated their 4 mothballed battleships in the 1980's, armour against modern missiles (such as Exocet, with up to 160kg warhead), proponents claimed, was not an issue for battleships. That's because the battleships, with at least 16 inch (nearly 45 cm) of hardened steel armour is almost impervious to modern, relatively lighweight missiles (unless they hit at the right place).
    Remember the biggest battleships, Yamato and Mushashi, had to be sunk by over twenty 500 pound bombs + torpedo hits. Of course credit goes to the crew for excellent use of waterproof compartments to keep these warships afloat so long. For a bigger missile like the Brahmos, I think you'll still have to have multiple hits to cause significant damage.
    Although these BB have been retired, certain advantages they offer have been missed by the US Navy and Marine Corp - including their 16 inch guns for shore bombardment (range >20 miles) as a prelude to amphibious landing. True, the WWII era 16 inch guns are less accurate than missiles and these ships require a crew of nearly 2000. But shells are cheaper to deliver than using aircraft (not to mention the loss of aircraft if shot down, political cost of a pilot captured as POW etc). Besides, many (including those on the receiving end) say the psychological impact of having a 16 inch shell (2700 pound shell, enough to clear a helicopter landing zone in the Vietnamese jungle) fired at you is nearly as bad as enduring a B-52 bombing raid. Anyway, the Littoral combat ship (LCS) is supposed to address some of these loss in capabilities (shore bombardment using two 155mm howitzers) plus its own stealth features, ability to operate in shallow waters, small crew (under 100), multi-mission capability etc.
    I doubt if the BB will ever be revived, but sort of re-incarnated in another smaller, more survivable, more versatile form.
