    I think the original post is a bit inaccurate. The actual word used in the papers was comatose. To substitute the word comatose for coma is misleading as most people without first aid training don't really get the difference between the two.

    Under the Glasgow Coma Scale which is used by medical professionals to assess a patient's level of consciousness, a casualty can be given a total of 15 points (GCS 15) to a minimum of 3 points (GCS 3).

    A GCS 15 rating means that the casualty is alert and able to respond both verbally and with movement (of muscles, limbs etc...).

    A GCS 3 means that the casualty is totally unresponsive. Usually, GCS 3 will mean the casualty is near death.

    Now, under medical classification, it only requires you to have a GCS 7 to be considered comatose. At GCS 7, a casualty quite literally has his eyes closed, can still make some sounds and can react to pain. Someone who's 'comatose' could have simply fainted from anxiety or stress and needn't be hosiptalised.

    Thus, it might be understandable why the Air Force people didn't immediately want to medevac when they heard 'comatose'.

    Still, I do find it strange why the MO just gave a one word ( "comatose" ) reply when asked about his patient's status, why didn't he give something more detailed like "GCS 5, Drowning"?

    P.S. I'm only a registered First Aider not a licensed Medic, hopefully my post can be understood clearly. Perhaps a Combat Medic can comment on what I've posted?

    P.S.S. Info on Patient Assessment in the SAF can be found on this document on the School of Military Medicine website: http://www.mindef.gov.sg/joint/smm/downloads/healthfacts/PAMsingle.pdf (Non-Restricted)
