    ASSOCIATED PRESS

    SINGAPORE, June 20 Â— Two Singapore officers were charged Friday with causing a collision between a navy ship and a Dutch cargo ship that killed four sailors, a defense ministry spokesman said.

    Lt. Ng Keng Yong, 27, and Lt. Chua Chue Teng, 23, were released on $5,870 bail, the defense ministry spokesman said. The official, who declined to be identified, said he could not say what the exact charges against the men were. The two are scheduled to appear in a court July 8.

    Ng was watch officer when the RSS Courageous, a 500-ton submarine hunter, collided with the ANL Indonesia off Malaysia's southern coast on Jan. 1, killing four servicewomen. Eight other officers were injured.

    Former Chief of Navy, Rear Adm. Lui Tuck Yew, called it the worst naval accident in Singapore's history.

    In a report in April, Singapore's Maritime and Ports Authority accused Ng of ''errors of judgment,'' including failing to tell Chua, who was under his command, how to steer the navy vessel out of the cargo ship's path.

    The SAF operates on the basis of responsibility. Everything is someone's responsibility. If it screws up, there is automatically someone to blame. I am not in the Navy, but I am quite sure it applies there too.

    As I understand it, when the ship is underway, the ship's navigation is the responsibility of the Officer of the Watch. If the ship's navigation is bad, it is that person's fault, regardless of how it happened. In this case, the Officer of the Watch was overseeing a trainee, who was the one that actually issued the incorrect orders. Nevertheless, it is not the trainee's fault, but the OOW's (sp?) since it is his responsibility and he must do everything in his power to ENSURE (favorite SAF term) that it is done correctly. He did not.

    Now, the Officer of the Watch was surely trained by senior officers, received his orders from senior officers, etc. His decisions were in turn carried out by junior officers and affected by their performance. So everyone on the ship "caused" the incident. Is it everybody's fault now, since everybody contributed to it? No. It is the Officer of the Watch's responsibility. There is no "victim culture" in the SAF. You cannot stand up and claim you screwed up because this person did that and that person did this. Your responsibility, your failure to ensure, your backside gets torn.

    The trainee is only responsible for her training. At the worst, she can be found to have been insufficiently diligent in her midshipman studies and failed out of the course. She did not work hard enough to learn the right way to navigate. Her lack of diligence is her fault and she should be appropriately punished for that. But the loss of the ship is not her fault, and the punishment should fall on her overseer.

    Of course, this is all unfortunately only theory. Things do not always follow the official model. We will see in this case. They should be doing things by the book since the case is so high-profile, but I would not be surprised if they were skipping some pages for one reason or another.
