    Also to add, the FM-2 / F4F "Wildcat" has plenty of armor due to its extremely fat body. This allows US pilots to adope an aerial combat manuever (ACM) called the "Teche weave[/color]". When a "Zero" saddeled on one of the "Wildcat's" tail, his wingman will then be able to get into a advantage attack position when they cross each other in the weave.

    Those above points are how naval planes defend themselves... either using speed, armour or wingman tactics...

    More Singapore aviation history trivia. When the Japanese surrendered in 1945, the Allies found a large stock of Ohkas in Singapore. They were apparently stored here and planned for use when the Allies invaded Malaya and Singapore (plans were afoot but the surrender meant it was never carried out). Which was also the reason why Japanese presence here was strong.

    In addition, the British also found numerous Japanese aircraft here, in various condition (some wrecked some airworthy). The Allied Technical and Intelligence Unit - South East Asia (ATAIU-SEA) took over several of these aircraft (some A6M Zeros, a couple of J2M Raidens 'Jacks', a A6M2-N Rufe floatplane, a G4M Betty bomber and a Ki-46 Dinah recon) and put them through technical evaluations in Tebrau, just across the Johor Straits from Singapore.

    Several pictures exist of these aircraft being flown with British markings and "ATAIU-SEA" painted on them!
