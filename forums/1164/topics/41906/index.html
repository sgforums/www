    RSAF as a whole does not have many weaknesses other than those already discussed above. RSAF is very integrated, from air-defense components to the fighter jets. The multi-tiers engagement model works very well in a very tight airspace like Singapore. Essentially RSAF can quickly achieve air-superiority within the 200km radius around Singapore.

    If we are to compare RSAF with other air forces in the world, I would rate RSAF very highly. But having said that, RSAF is very much oriented to defensive doctrine and it is starting to build its offensive capabilities (especially long range... I shouldn't elaborate more here). RSAF also has a major gap which it is quickly filling up very quickly in the area of BVR engagements. In the past, RSAF can only conduct WVR operations or training as Sidewinders were the only missiles they have. Now, they have AIM-7 Sparrow, AIM-9, AIm-120, Python, etc, the variety and capability in BVR engagements have significantly improved. Of course I would like to see the introduction of latest BVR missiles such as Mica or ramjet powered AMRAAMs in the near future.

    My RSAF friend also told me how foreign air force pilots were pleasantly surprised at how RSAF performed during war games like the Red Flag. The pilots were quick to react to tactical changes well. Some of them even demonstrated capability to "think outside the box", employing tactics that were not normally "taught" or used (again, not going into any details here... going to be too long-winded). That shows the quality of our individual pilots.

    RSAF lacks the experience in conducting large packet strikes unlike the USAF or even RAF. RSAF are usually tailored more to smaller packets. However, this is slowly overcome with the pilots joining various big exercises with foreign power in war games. The Viper training in US is providing RSAF significant capabilities improvement. The bi-annual exericses like Cope Thunder and Pitch Black are providing RSAF invaluable experiences.

    Yes, RSAF pilots lack real operational experiences, but having joint exericses with many countries whose pilots having gone through real operations, even wars, helps. RSAF are getting real -life operation experience through these exercises. You don't get any more real than these exercises. The only difference is that in exercises like these, the pilots do not have to face real incoming SAMs or missiles.

    FInally, with topics like this, it is difficult to discuss because there is no proper benchmark in which the discussion is made. What is weakness? How do we definte weaknesses? Are we talking about general organisation wise? tactics? ORBAT? etc???? Are we comparing RSAF with region air forces or other foreign power? We can say RSAF cannot project force outside xxx KM, send an offensive force to, say South America, does that mean it is a weakness? No of course, because it is not even Singapore sphere of influence or RSAF operational theatre. Thus, when we do not have a benchmark, how do we spot what are weaknesses?

    RSAF is constantly acquiring new capabilities. During the learning process, do we can the lack of these new capabilities weaknesses????

    Lastly, how do you discuss about weaknesses without revealling too much info...
