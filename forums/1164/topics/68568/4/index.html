    The screw-up that nearly took a life.

    It was night when we arrived at the Kranji Ammo Base slipway, and after launching the amphibious ferry equipment, we rested for the night, in preparation for next morning Armoured Brigade - River Crossing Exercise.

    Dawn came slowly, and it was low tide that got half the platoon to the innocuously 'dry out' swampy ground, and they trudged in firm mud trying to catch the scampering crabs for lunch later.

    Soon, an effeminated Lance-Corporal got his ankles stuck in the mud, and the more he struggled the deeper he went - and soon he was slowly sinking and the mud level reached just below his kneecaps.

    The other platoon mates placed one Assault Boat on each side of the stucked soldier, attempting to pull him out of the mud, and in the process nearly tore his arms out instead.

    Everyone panicked, and tried to carry him out of the swampy mud by the armpit, but the suction from the thick mud was too much.

    With each failed attempt to lift the stucked soldier, he went deeper into the mud, until it reached his waist.

    Things got desperate as the tide slowly began to come in according to tidal forcast, and the muddy surface began to have the seawater mingling at the base of the Assault Boat.

    The wrist watch alarm buzzed, and I was shocked at the situation that began with an innocent hunt for crabs; and it was less then 45 minutes for us to start engines and get into position for the Start of the Exercise.

    The tide was coming in slowly but surely, and the soldier was already sinking into the mud swamp, with only his chest above his belt line showing.

    After assessing the situation, the only way to save this soldier was to overcome the suction at the source - from the feet and below.

    Shouting for hard cartons - from previous night's nite snack packaging - I got the boxes torn open, spread the open carton out, and placing it like a mat next to the trapped soldier, so as to support and spread my 70kg body weight, as I lay flat on my chest on the very soft, and sea water logged swampy mud.

    Taking the standard SAF Jack-knife, in an attempt to cut the boot lace and give the trapped soldier a chance to free himself from the boots stucked in the mud.

    The older contract service Technical Sergeants from Logistics were all skeptical of my plan, and gave loud discouraging remarks, even though their ideas to help save this soldier had failed in their last half hour of effort.

    Shouting for everyone to be silent, and for the Comms NCO to send the code to the Exercise Commander for 'Exercise Stop' due to Life Threatening Situation, and proceeded with the rescue plan.

    Spreading the surface mud apart, my face was flat and placed deeper onto the muddy depression, to reach further down into the mud, as the soldier was slipping down with the mud reaching his chest and nearing his collar bones.

    My entire long arms reached deep into the mud, up to my shoulders, as I lay flat on my chest.

    With both hands I blindly felt for the boot laces, and tried to cut it; but end up cutting my other hand, my thumb, and my fingers.

    I gave up the attempt to cut the boot lace, and decided on an alternative.

    This time to break the suction - by creating an 'air pocket' BELOW the boots of the trapped soldier; and using my bare hands to lift his boots from the underside, as he put pressure and stepped on my hands, and with me lifting him up from below his feet.

    Slowly but surely, it worked.

    After 20 minutes he was clear out of the mud.

    The effeminated Soldier was exhausted after the long struggle, and the exercise proceeded with barely 10 minutes to reach the 'Green Start Line'.

    The exercise proceeded with the commander in a "new camo" uniform that was quite different from everyone.

    For those who may face similar Life Threatening situation, whether you are in a Leadership role or not, this piece is a real life experience for the record.

    We will need to be CONSTANTLY ALERT to the SITUATION around us.

    We must be QUICK, LOGICAL and URGENT in our ASSESSMENT as our Eyes absorb the situation.

    We must listen to all the views and good counsel - but the FINAL DECISION must be made based on our own Leadership Assessment.

    When a DECISION is made, make all effort to accomplish it, and IF OBSTRUCTION is encountered, slight variation must be made quickly so as to reach the Final Goal.
