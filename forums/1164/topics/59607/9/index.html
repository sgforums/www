    I've been lurking around this thread for awhile reading the responses and all...seems to me that some of us don't know why there's a misconception regarding CST and the applicability of it.

    The big reason why its conducted for specialized troops is because of the nature of their profession. Snipers are always in for a hard time when they are captured...history has proven that the enemy isn't very kind to snipers. You might have an auto-injector, but at that moment you might hesitate...no offence espanol_man...I was also from 7 SIB myself...

    So goes the same for BRC-types and cdos. Why? They are regarded as the enemy that is small in concentration, but cause the most havoc. As a result, it requires that these types of soldiers need some form of realistic training to give them a taste of what they might experience 10-fold in a real situation. I'm not saying that it should breach training safety parameters. I went through CST myself...the treatment given to Infantry, Armour, and Guards is actually quite different. I believed for reasons of historical rivalry, Gdsmen actually get it a little worse than others...lol....those who have gone through CST might remember Staff Bala...

    I made it through the course...hated being tied up, blindfolded and subjected to water torture, crawling in mud, being shoved around, staying dirty for days on end. But I came out of it realizing my endurance limits better, and knowing what to expect if my team was ever compromised. It made my team work harder during training exercises to prevent such an eventuality.

    The CST was only one part of it. I even suggested back then that us specialized types should even go through a kind of 'hell week' and even the Airborne course. I'm not gung-ho or anything, but my logic is that since we are already trained to such an extent, we might as well go all the way...

    Maybe the problem is that we don't properly screen the types of soldiers that are going into these vocations. I suggested in the other thread that maybe it might be better that they have a system of voluntary service...and the applicant should have an outstanding fitness, marksmanship (esp for snipers), high motivation, no psychological problems, no major health problems if any...the reward? a better vocation pay. I know it sounds highly theoretical, but I believe it might work better, irregardless of education level determining the years of service; much like the cdos...all serve for 2 1/2 yrs...

    CST training has to be kept realistic. BUT it has to remain within certain safety parameters. The balance between realism and safety however, has to be redetermined from time to time.

    No, in principle, the course is not impractical - the current practice, however, is something I strongly disagree with.

    laser51088 almost got it correct, except for one thing. I am not assuming that anyone has gone to the extreme. The fact that we have a dead trainee shows that things have already gone to the extreme - there is no assumption here. Anybody defending the instructor's perspective is by extension defending taking things to that extreme.

    It seems that those who take a different view to Nathan or myself seem to be getting at the principle of "whatever does not kill you will make you stronger". Well and good, except that it operates upon the assumption that it does not kill you. In this case, the assumption has obviously been violated.

    For those who argue for realism in training, the question is, what is the point at which increasing realism produces diminishing returns?

    For argument's sake, let us go down the alternative line of argument. In the event of a real war, the enemy will try drowning POWs to get information out of them, so we train our troops to withstand that. Well and good. But wait, they might also try driving hot skewers into people to get information. Fine - we'll turn our trainees into kebabs. Hmm - they might also try skinning prisoners like the Lebanese do. Okay, let's string the boys up and peel them like oranges.

    See where this is going?

    Well and good that we prepare our troops for the realities of the situation. At the end of the day, the objective is to try to ensure that they don't sing like canaries when they are captured. Within limits, there is a point to CST training inasmuch as we have to get the troops prepared for the event that an enemy will not be civilised and respect the Geneva Convention. There is no utility in taking it to the point where one trainee is drowned and five others are hospitalised. That is just a waste of manpower, and it makes 2Sgt Hu's death pointless.

    One point I take particular exception to is 35MM Gunner's question, "did it occur 2 u tat they (instructors) r actually prolonging their lives (trainees)????? ". I would have thought that the answer is fairly obvious - 2Sgt Hu was KILLED in the training. One would have to be blind, deaf or particularly stupid to not see that this is a case of SHORTENING lives, not prolonging them.
