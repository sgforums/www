    ATM's Modernisation Not Linked To Flashpoint Theory

    KUALA LUMPUR, Sept 15 (Bernama) -- The modernisation being undertaken by the Malaysian Armed Forces (ATM) is not in preparation for a possible outbreak of armed conflict in Southeast Asia, a region military analysts describe as a "time bomb".

    The water supply and Pulau Batu Puteh issues between Malaysia and Singapore, the Jemaah Islamiah militant group, overlapping claims on Spratlys, the Philippines' claim on Sabah and political instability in Indonesia are considered potential flashpoints leading to war and intervention by foreign powers.

    In the mid-90s, predictions of regional turmoil almost came true when two Vietnamese navy vessels sank after being shot by Chinese warships as a result of territorial dispute over the Spratlys, which are also claimed by Malaysia.

    ATM's modernisation in the early 90s, which saw it acquiring the R-77 Beyond Visual Range (BVR) guided missile from Russia for its MiG-29N/UB, had led defence analysts to regard Malaysia as starting an arms race with Singapore and Thailand.

    None of the other countries in the region had the BVR system, which allows an MiG-29 to fire at enemy aircraft from more than 60km by merely picking them up on the radar screen using a location detection device.

    The United States Congress had initially refused to approve the sale of the American BVR system to friendly countries in the region but finally allowed the sale of the AIM-120 (a type of BVR) for use by the F-16 fighter jets of Singapore and Thailand.

    Malaysian Armed Forces Chief Jen Tan Sri Mohd Zahidi Zainuddin dismissed the notion that tension was running high in the region and that Malaysia was involved in an arms race.

    Five years ago, the situation was truly tense in the Spratly islands and many parties had predicted that the area would be a flashpoint of armed conflicts, he said.

    "However, it did not happen and in fact the issue has abated, because we have military diplomacy preventing such a situation from happening," he said in a recent interview with journalists in conjunction with the 70th anniversary of ATM.

    Furthermore, political and military leaders in the region practised "bilateral cooperation" to minimise such risks, he said.

    He said ATM's modernisation was to attain Minimum Deterrent Capability (MDC) to defend the country from outside attacks because the ATM had just switched from counter-insurgency warfare to modern warfare.

    The purchase of the Astros II multiple rocket launcher, Scorpene submarine and Sukhoi 30MKM aircraft was not meant to create threats to any country because Malaysia's defence budget was still low compared to those of neighbouring countries, he said.

    "We need at least RM24 billion to meet our defence needs but the government approves only about RM7 billion a year, and this is far from achieving our MDC aspiration," he said.

    ATM's present assets may be seen as modern and possibly stronger than those of their regional counterparts but Zahidi admitted that Malaysia still lagged behind in terms of electronic warfare because it lacked an Airborne Early Warning System.

    He said ATM only wanted a fair fight if it was attacked by enemies.

    He took pride in the fact that ATM had quickly transformed itself from an outfit primed for guerilla warfare to a conventional force which had successfully served under the United Nations banner.

    ATM now has a strength of 118,000 personnel and this is small compared to Thailand (225,000 personnel), Indonesia (343,000) and Singapore (70,000 regulars and 250,000 reservists).

    "Our current strength of 118,000 might be expanded according to our needs. The government previously wanted to reduce the number of personnel so that modernisation of assets can be implemented," he said.

    The conclusion is, after going through two crises -- the emergency and confrontation -- ATM is undertaking modernisation in line with the country's development and this, according to Zahidi, has nothing to do with the concept of "bargaining power".

    The ATM has never used its strength as a power projector to resolve issues with neighbouring countries, and instead it views diplomacy as a key weapon for maintaining the country's peace.

    -- BERNAMA
