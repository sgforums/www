    kenhor,

    To begin with, we have about 15000 regulars and about 40000 full-time NS men. 15000 regulars is also about 0.5% of our population (actually a bit less). The number of regulars is always limited by demand, rather than military expenditure. There just aren't that many people who want to be soldiers all their lives.

    I understand your assertion that it might be better for the Total Defence of Singapore if military expenditure is cut back. It is true that the economic, psychological and social benefits of releasing some of the SAF's expenditure into the economy might outweigh the miliitary cost of doing so. However, in this case, I think you are mistaken.

    By law, military spending in Singapore is capped at 5% of GDP. Even if we were to completely shut down the SAF, it would not make that much of a difference. It would only allow everyone to earn about 5% more in the long run (yes, I know that it is more complicated than that, but that's the approximate value). The loss in productivity from having our men undergo 2.5 years of full-time NS and 13 years of NSman training also needs to be taken into account, but it is only a small part of the total productivity of a male citizen over the span of of his lifetime. Again, only at most a 5% increase in economic output if NS were done away with.

    You seem to have the impression that the SAF consumes vast resources. I do not think you realize how large the Singaporean economy is and what a shoestring budget the SAF runs on. The reason why the SAF is so much stronger than her counterparts in the region is because she is also very cost effective. Having numerous draftees that you pay almost nothing to and being very cautious with military procurement and development leads to this.

    What does this mean? Even drastic cutbacks in SAF spending would make very little direct difference to the economy. Instead, the presence of the SAF gives us many tangible and intangible benefits that you seem to not appreciate. It is a unifying force socially, it inspires investor confidence and it is a source of national pride. It safeguards our interests in diplomacy and trade. It is also psychologically very important for Singapore to have a strong military, for unique cultural reasons. It is my belief that the money spent on the SAF could not be spent better in any other way.

    Also, consider that military spending only bears fruit in the future. If a war breaks out 5 years from now, we cannot start spending money immediately and expect it to make a difference. Just because there is not a threat now, does not mean that there will not be one decades from now. It would be extremely shortsighted to cut the SAF's funding. The 3:1 ratio of attackers to defenders that you cite only applies in conventional situations, and the defence of Singapore is not a conventional situation due to our small size. If we do not strike out, the ratio will be the reverse, if only due to starvation and dehydration.

    In order to strike out to at least secure our food and water supplies, we need a certain minimum level of military strength. To defend against an opponent who insists on using standoff tactics (which is the correct approach for an entrenched, concentrated enemy like a new Fortress Singapore), we need even more military strength. To not merely defend ourselves, but have the possibility of victory, we need yet more strength. And that is the whole purpose of the SAF, to be strong enough that the enemy is faced with the possibility of defeat in attacking Singapore, thus preventing a war from starting in the first place. It's called deterrence, and you need to be strong enough for it to work. The strength of the SAF preserves peace. And peace is a lot cheaper and better for the economy than war.

The first is that all the money pouring into India, China and the rest of the developing world finally raises their standard of living to the point where it no longer threatens us. This will only delay the coming storm. Protectionism is like patching a dam with superglue. The third is if China or India "get stupid" and have a revolution, go to war or something that gets the investors out of their countries. However, I don't see that happening for a while.
