    A key factor in what Nathan and ST have been talking about is situational awareness. SA covers the tracking of all objects within a three-dimensional space, not only of the position of the object but of its nature (e.g. friend or foe). The USS Vincennes incident is a classic example of SA failure, where all the correct data was collected, but was interpreted using the wrong framework - an Airbus on the radar was therefore misidentified as an enemy aircraft.

    A few ways of testing SA have been designed, such as WOMBAT - however, these tests still require a lot of development and their progress follows research that defines measurable elements of SA.

    If you want to look into SA more, I'd recommend Mica Endsley's work - her writing is very clear and makes for a good introduction. I have some copies of her articles, but unfortunately I've left them in Melbourne.

    On the other hand, there are other factors such as attentional narrowing. As stress levels increase, the individual has a tendency to focus upon some of the salient (obvious) features of the situation, ignoring some of the less obvious but nonetheless important it of information.     Another aspect that can be influenced by training is group SA Â– the SA shared by teams of people, such as flight crews. In such cases, everybody must have the same understanding of the situation so that they do not end up working at odds with each other in a tight situation.
