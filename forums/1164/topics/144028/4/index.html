    Pirates May Have Also Attacked U.N. Ship
    MIAMI Â— The crew of a luxury cruise ship used a sonic weapon that blasts earsplitting noise in a directed beam while being attacked by a gang of pirates off Africa this weekend, the cruise line said Monday.

    The Seabourn Spirit had a Long Range Acoustic Device, or LRAD, installed as a part of its defense systems, said Bruce Good, a spokesman for Miami-based Seabourn Cruise Line. The Spirit was about 100 miles off Somalia when pirates fired rocket-propelled grenades and machine guns as they tried to get onboard.

    The subsidiary of Carnival Corp. was investigating whether the weapon was successful in warding off the pirates, he said. The ship's captain also changed its course, shifted into high speed and headed out into the open sea to elude the pirates, who were in two small boats, he said. He had no further details.

    Device maker American Technology Corp. said earsplitting "bangs" were directed by trained security personnel toward the pirates. That, combined with ship maneuvers, caused the attackers to leave the area, the company said.

    The LRAD is a so-called "non-lethal weapon" developed for the U.S. military after the 2000 attack on the USS Cole off Yemen as a way to keep operators of small boats from approaching U.S. warships.

    The military version is a 45-pound, dish-shaped device that can direct a high-pitched, piercing tone with a tight beam. Neither the LRAD's operators or others in the immediate area are affected.

    American Technology, based in San Diego, compares its shrill tone to that of smoke detectors, only much louder. It can be as loud as about 150 decibels, while smoke alarms are about 80 to 90 decibels.

    The devices have been deployed on commercial and naval vessels worldwide since summer 2003, the company said.

Approximately 250 B757-200 aircraft is expected to be converted in the next 20 years, while more than 900 Boeing 757-200s have been delivered to 52 airlines since the aircraft first flew in 1982.

    The 'lab on a chip' is still being perfected, but results of initial trials

    The device uses special test solutions to assess samples of suspected
    biohazards, which are placed on a glass slide.

    The slide, about the size of a postage stamp, is inserted into a handheld
    reader and scanned electronically to help the researcher find out what the
    sample is.
    It can detect the presence of biohazards that SAF personnel could encounter
    in training or combat situations, including the mosquito-borne dengue virus, or
    the soil disease melioidosis, which is present in soil bacteria and can cause
    potentially lethal blood infections.
    Last year, 98 people caught melioidosis and 60 people have been infected so far this year, according to the Health Ministry. Dr Yap, 41, is head of the

    Research Institute, a facility that comes under DSO National Laboratories,
    Singapore's biggest defence research and development lab.
    Tests on the device are continuing in the lab, located next to the National
    University Hospital, to broaden the computer database to include information on as many types of viruses as possible.
    'We can detect what we know, but how do you detect what you don't know, like new viruses or bacteria,' said Dr Yap. 'We don't have samples of everything from everywhere.'
    At yesterday's award ceremony, Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean also gave out four team awards to projects advancing the performance of air defence radars and technology that helps SAF commanders lead and control their units during operations.

    S'pore duo claim their liquid ceramic coating can make them lighter and
    stronger

    STimes 02.11.2005

    TWO Singaporeans have come up with a liquid ceramic coating that can strengthen
    bullet-proof vests against gunfire and bomb fragments.
    The off-white secret concoction brewed in a Jalan Kembangan shophouse is the
    brainchild of car salesman-turned-entrepreneur Norman Lim, 40, and his
    childhood friend and business partner, Mr Richard Lim, 40, a former computer
    software salesman.
    In two years, using $100,000 of their own money, the duo - who have between
    them a theology diploma and a master's in information technology - may well
    have come up with a formula that beats research at top defence laboratories in
    the United States.
    The coating invented by the Lims strengthens materials like kevlar - widely
    used in bullet-proof vests.
    Their idea is similar to a two decade-old effort pursued by the US
    military's Army Research Laboratory.
    But the end product using the Singapore duo's formula weighs only a third of
    the armour protection from the US lab - an important edge as body armour can
    add about 8kg to a soldier's load.
    Their tests have shown that a vest made up of 20 layers of paper-thin kevlar
    stops a 9mm bullet in its ninth layer.
    When treated with the liquid ceramic and resin mixture, the bullet is
    stopped at the fourth layer.
    In a thicker form, the coating they developed can also be made into a
    kevlar-like material.
    A third product they have come up with is a special resin and fibre coating
    that can be brushed onto any surface, making a regular kitchen tile, which
    shatters when dropped, bullet-proof.
    After successful overseas trials, the duo have decided to move their
    research next month to California, where they plan to sell their know-how to
    firms that make body armour or bullet- and blast-resistant protection kits for
    vehicles.
    Mr Christopher Foss, editor of the authoritative Jane's Armour & Artillery
    yearbook, said that if their invention delivers on its promise, the market
    potential is huge, going by the US military experience in Afghanistan and Iraq.
    The shortcomings of existing body and vehicle armour are being exploited by
    insurgents there using improvised explosive devices.
    The Lims' next project will be to see if lightweight armour can protect
    vehicles against rocket-propelled grenades - another weapon of choice for
    insurgents in many conflicts.
    Mr Norman Lim's interest in composite materials and resins started in his
    teens when he made dioramas, or scaled-down landscapes, for his plastic models
    of tanks and warplanes.
    He took a slight detour in his mid-20s, when he studied theology in
    Oklahoma. After completing his diploma, he helped in churches and orphanages in
    India but soon returned to Singapore.
    'We can't function without money. We have to make some money too,' he said.
    He returned to work on composite materials, but graduated from model
    vehicles to the real thing, making car body kits for rally cars and racing
    bikes.
    'I have a friend who used to work for Lotus, the race car company, and he
    taught me a lot about composite technology,' he added.
    By working on hi-fi speakers using kevlar, he also began to appreciate the
    qualities of this tough yet supple material.
    He then decided to combine his childhood interest in military equipment and
    his knowledge of composite materials.
    In November last year, he gave up his $10,000-a-month job and roped in his
    old neighbour Richard, whom he has known since they were five.
    Did he ever imagine he would end up in the defence industry when he was
    studying theology in Oklahoma in 1991?
    It's not all that different, he said. 'My work is all about saving lives.'

    High explosive squash head (HESH) was also used by other military forces, particularly those which had purchased the early post-war British 105mm Centurion tank, such as Sweden, India and Israel; in the US it was known as HEP, for "High Explosive, Plastic".

    HESH rounds contain a warhead filled with plastic explosive and a delayed fuse. On impact the plastic explosive in the shell spreads out to form a disk on the surface of the armour. The fuse then detonates the explosive, creating a shock wave which travels through the armour, causing flakes of metal to spall off the armour's inside surface.
