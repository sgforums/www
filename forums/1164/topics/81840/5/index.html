    LazerLordz, I can see your point, but I propose that there are a few things to consider here.

    I don't think think Lion02 is hiding behind the culture. His post was in response to Soldier I's point, which in fact implies that the Commandos are being judged in comparison to other units' cultures.

    The unit culture does not justify the incident that led to Sgt Hu's death. However, to suggest that the Commando culture is one of violence is inaccurate, and serves to highlight Soldier I's point that the rest of the SAF fails to understand the Commando culture.

    The question I would raise is this: do Commandos choose to live in a vacuum? Let me raise just one example through which I can answer this question.

    Of all the units in the SAF, we are the ones who spend the most time performing small unit operations. We are the four-man teams stuck 150 km into enemy territory, with little, if any, chance of receiving backup or replacements if things turn bad.

    This one fact, amongst others, creates a culture that is unlike what you would find in other units. Because of the small team sizes, every man is trained to take over command if the section leaders are either killed or incapacitated - that's why the lowest rank in the unit is corporal.

    Because of the diminished distinction between leaders and men, you will find that the unit culture involves a much lower level of attention to hierarchy than that of other units. From my experience working with other units, this has been interpreted as poor discipline, and we have received a lot of undeserved criticism from other units' soldiers because of that.

    As corny as it sounds, we Commandos are a very misunderstood lot. From my experience with soldiers from other outfits that specialise in small-team operations, this seems to be a phenomenon that occurs in other countries' armed forces as well - the "snake eater" syndrome. Because we operate so differently from other units, we get discriminated against.

    Therefore, it's not us who choose to live in a vacuum - it's other units that choose to stick us there.

    Picture this - u go thru months of sometimes mentally-exhausting (tortuous would be more agreeable to afew) of training, never knowing if you'll eventually make it through - and after u finally turn ops, you've lost count of how many times you were pushed to the limits and beyond (by choice or otherwise), you reach a new level of confidence in survivality. Not arrogance nor garang-ness, but the ability to look at a situation and deal with it under otherwise seemingly unsurmountable odds. It's about the "thinking" and mentality enhancing survivability rather than physical ability, though sometimes hardened by trainers in a somewhat negative fashion. It's the first 2 phases of NS in Cdo training that makes a person believe whatever he is going through now, it will not be worse what it felt in that first 6 months, but it's also that time that entrain the person the will of survivality and the ability to perform under difficult circumstances.

    That's just the personal development part. Through it all, you'll realise you're not the only one being brought upteen times almost to your knees (and giving up is not an option - only an extension to whatever you and others are going through). Do it alone and your mates suffer with you, do it well together and everyone lives to see another day. And when the crunch comes during a mission, everyone believes in and depends on each teammate. Everyone is equal in a team, but we think as a whole, not as individuals.

    As Ged said of operating behind enemy lines, we don't think of being killed 150km into unfriendly territories because that's where we're trained to operate, but we certainly think about how best to enhance our survivality and carry out our job the best we can in that kind of situation, without compromising others knowingly of course. Garang-ness will only lead to the demise of the whole operation. However, survivality does not encompass treasonable acts if captured by enemies, though sometimes it might mean extreme (or "by left" ) measures under extreme situations.

    It's hard to explain, and the culture might be similar in small-unit teams in other units. Its part of what i believe in, my mentality, though i know i didn't put it well enough to be comprehendable or fully summarise my thoughts. Perhaps the only way to understand cdo culture is to go thru it in the atmosphere and environment of the Hendon grounds itself, and realising your spot in the scheme of things that is the formation's mentality and structure, almost a family to some, and a source of pride to many.

    What's said in this post is entirely my own thoughts, and probably different from what others think. And is in no way related to the title of this topic . Apologies.

    Lion02, Ged, psykror...I guess I know what you guys are talking about when you discuss Cdo culture. As much as my formation (Guards) and yours are rivals, I've never personally seen it that way. Like you guys, we operate in small teams of 5 behind enemy lines, although we don't engage them unless absolutely necessary. But it doesn't change the fact that we still had to be independent and survive by our wits and sometimes, luck. As most of you guys that have gone through ATECs know, there's always a huge bounty (extra off-days) for capturing scout teams...well, the opposite applies to us if we get caught...lotsa extras, and we sometimes have to buy beers for the rest of the teams (luckily its SAF beer, if not surely broke in no time).

    As a result, we were cross-trained heavily as well, and there was a distinctive lack of hierarchy in the field...soldiers from line companies always thought of us as show-offs, and believe me, we displayed the same, if not higher, level of disdain for them and their officers.

    I guess the 'elite' within any formation will always have a different culture from the rest. Not because we choose to be different, but rather, the nature of our training, our size make-up and our vocation made us so. If so, then I dare say that we have earned the right to be different....and I dare say our espirit-de-corp is stronger...

    The death of a fellow Guardsman hurts. Since we all had to go through CST as well, I've always assumed (and had been told by people beforehand) that the TSR book doesn't apply during CST. And I hate to say this, but that is the nature of the training. Nevertheless, it should have been within TSR limits, if there were ever any. But the death happened, and playing the hypocritical blame game isn't going to change things.

    I wish the Cdos well and have never thought that it was ever the arrogance if you may, of the formation that resulted in Sgt Hu's death. We were all proud of our status as being 'special' and arrogance came as part of the package. Afterall, we were soldiers once....and young...
