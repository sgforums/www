    Nov 17, 2005
    SAF battlefield sleuths - hush hush no more

    IF YOU attended this year's National Day Parade, or one of its rehearsals, chances are you were caught on film by intelligence gatherers from a new army unit.

    Patrolling the crowds dressed in civilian clothing, more than 100 soldiers from the 1st Military Intelligence Battalion (1 MI Battalion) worked with the police and security agencies to secure the area around the Padang, using remote-controlled cameras.

    The sensors they carried allowed them to send images to their headquarters instantly.

    For the first time yesterday, this once hush-hush unit, formed on Oct 11 last year, showed off its suite of battlefield sensors to the media.

    The 1 MI Battalion is the only unit whose sole job is information-gathering. Lieutenant-Colonel Arthur Kuan, commanding officer of the battalion, said the unit was formed to support the intelligence requirements of the Singapore Armed Forces (SAF) during peacetime.

    Its motto - First In - underlines its responsibility to be at the front line, even before a front line exists, to provide intelligence for the SAF. 'Until this battalion was formed, we didn't have the people to plan for and provide this capability,' Lt-Col Kuan said.

    His unit works with the police to safeguard events that could be targeted by terrorists. The unit, based at Pasir Laba Camp in Jurong, also helps test sensors and concepts for gathering intelligence. And though it is an army unit, it operates sensors both on the ground and in the air.

    The 4.5kg Sky Lark miniature unmanned aerial vehicle is one type of drone used by the unit. Operated by two soldiers, it has a camera for aerial surveillance up to 8km away, day or night. And its single propeller is powered by batteries, making it impossible to hear once it is in the air.

    used by the unit. Operated by two soldiers, it has a camera for aerial
    surveillance up to 8km away, day or night. And its single propeller is powered
    by batteries, making it impossible to hear once it is in the air.
    The unit also has unmanned ground sensors, developed by DSO National
    Laboratories, Singapore's largest defence research lab. These consist of a
    slender camera-equipped spike, which is driven into the ground. Ground
    vibrations or noise activates the camera and the sensor beams the image to the operator, who can be hidden up to 2km away.

    Lt-Col Kuan said the battalion will spearhead army intelligence's
    transformation into a more sophisticated, third-generation force.

    He said: 'The current reality is we still have soldiers out there like
    scouts equipped with binoculars, exposing themselves to danger just to report information on the enemy back to headquarters. We're going to use a lot of unmanned technology and imaging technology to pass more information, more accurately, quicker and faster back to headquarters for them to act on.
    'At the same time we also increase the survivability of our scouts and recce
    troopers because they can do all these things from a safe position ... instead
    of exposing themselves to danger to report on the enemy.'
    The battalion has more than 100 personnel, of whom more than 70 per cent are full-time national servicemen. One of the NSFs, Third Sergeant Cheng Wei Han, 19, a recce (or reconnaissance) specialist, said: 'We're at the vanguard of testing new information-gathering technology in the SAF. We experiment with
    whatever new sensors or so-called toys that the defence scientists and
    engineers develop to give them feedback so they can improve them.' - DAVID
