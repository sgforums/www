    Three years ago, the U.S. Navy/ Boeing/Raytheon APG-79 Active Electronically Scanned Array team began developing an advanced radar that would allow the F/A-18E/F to counter the evolving threat beyond 2020. Led by three program managers, Capt. (select) Dave Dunaway, Don Thole of Boeing and Tom Kennedy of Raytheon, the team has met every milestone since contract award in 2000, including 100% on-time delivery.

    The APG-79 is the first completely new airborne fire control radar built by Raytheon in more than 25 years. It has an advanced active array, the next-generation receiver exciter and a brand-new fiber channel-based processor.

    Top Pentagon officials say Active Electronically Scanned Array (AESA) radars eventually will be at the heart of the U.S.' network-centric defenses. These multi-element systems will contribute to many advanced capabilities. For example, AESA will allow stealth aircraft to use radar while still avoiding detection. In various forms, they eventually will be carried by the F/A-22 Raptor, F-35 Joint Strike Fighter, Unmanned Combat Air Vehicle (UCAV), B-2 and the Air Force's new E-10 multi-sensor command and control aircraft.

    THE APG-79 PROMISES to be more lethal, survivable and reliable than any airborne fire control radar today. The Navy anticipates never having to open the nose of an aircraft to repair the radar. Its officials say they are putting a 12,000-hr. radar in a 6,000-hr. aircraft.

    The APG-79 also provides "limitless choices" operationally, according to Dunaway, making the F/A-18E/F Super Hornet a far more capable precision-strike aircraft than its predecessors. The AESA radar makes the Super Hornet, now on operational duty in the Middle East, "more survivable, supportable and affordable," explained Thole.

    During a Nov. 20 exercise, the team demonstrated the first fully integrated engineering manufacturing development model, three months ahead of schedule. The team completed Operational Test and Evaluation Phase IIA earlier this year and is on track for first flight test by June. The "leap-ahead" technology incorporated in the APG-79 is the basis for other major programs including MP-RTIP; the new, long-range ground surveillance radar for the E-10; and next-generation radars for the F-15 Eagle, B-2 and UCAV.

    Dunaway is a U.S. Naval Academy graduate who became an F/A-18 pilot with VFA-151 and later a test pilot and test director. He received an M.S. in aerospace engineering from the U.S. Naval Postgraduate School. Dunaway is currently the Radar IPT lead and program manager for AESA.

    Thole has worked at Boeing on F-4, F-15 and F/A-18 programs since the mid-1980s. Under his leadership, a Boeing/Raytheon team fielded USAF's F-15C AESA. Building on the F-15C radar upgrade experience, Thole is heading up the APG-79 development effort for Boeing.

    Kennedy earned his B.S.E.E. at Rutgers University, M.S.E.E. at the Air Force Institute of Technology and Ph.D. at UCLA. Prior to joining Hughes Aircraft Co., he was a USAF captain. Kennedy is the Raytheon program manager for APG-79.

    These program leaders are supported by hundreds of professionals who have made the APG-79 a success.
