    Hmm - interesting.

    Without meaning to sound too fuzzy, the answer really does depend on what one defines as war, from which perspective the point is taken, and whether we are talking about actual war or the threat of war.

    Let's look at the first point: what is defined as war? Do tanks have to go rolling across front lines (the traditional definition of war), or could it be a case of terrorists letting off bombs once every 18 months (geurilla warfare), or one country strangling the sea routes to another (economic warfare)?

    In the first case, the point doesn't stand - history has shown that in traditional military terms, the aggressor rarely wins in the long run.

    In the second case, we have the instances of Israel and Vietnam as evidence. These nation states exist owing to the actions of the Stern Gang and the Viet Cong respectively.

    I raised economic warfare more as a mitigating factor than as a method. Does one country need to engage in war with another in order to ensure its economic survival? This of course leads to the second point - what was the objective of starting the war? Was it to set an imbalanced situation right?

    The third point is the definition of war. MAD and Singapore's defensive stance are examples of using the threat of war to maintain peace. The first Gulf War and the two World Wars, on the other hand, are examples of using actual warfare to return to a peaceful situation. In the former case, Sun Tzu's "the best war fought is one that is won without a single shot being fired" applies, while the old saying "we don't start fights, we just finish them" applied in the latter case.
