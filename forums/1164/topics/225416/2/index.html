    You need a 20' barrel to get the most magic out of the 5.56. Using that barrel and your typical ball ammo, the 5.56 is actually more lethal then the AK 7.62 bullet within 100 meters due to its fragmentation effects. Start messing around with the length and you'll see issues like not being able to stop your targets start to appear.

In theory, you can carry more 5.56 ammo for 7.62 and in the short ranges typical of FIBUA your weapon is actually more effective at killing the enemy.     The Belgians, who make some of the finest military small arms of this past 100 years, feel that a 17.6-inch barrel for their 5.56mm FNC is more than enough.

    Interestingly, the list showed that amongst CONVENTIONAL layout 5.56mm weapons, the M-16 and Galil stand out as the rare few having the full length barrels of 20 to 21 inches. Most people settle for around 18-inches.

    Even SG made 2 AR with 18-inch barrels before coming up with the bullpup SAR-21.

    Also interesting to note that a shorter barrel does not necessarily mean a lighter weapon. The SAR-80 and SR-88 with 18-inch barrels are both HEAVIER than the M-16 with a 20-inch barrel.

    The Russians had been, and continue to be, very satisfied with 16.3-inch barrels and feel that accurate aimed shots out to 200m more than sufficient.

    In fact, I get the feeling that they feel that accurate aimed shots are in practice not the most important thing. They also continue to use the open v-notch rear sight which is much better at quick acquisition of targets (but less precise aiming) than our M16 ring-type rear sight.

    On the other hand, China abandoned the AK-47 in favour of their own Type-81 (also in 7.62 x 39mm) with a slightly longer barrel cos it is apparently much more accurate. They continue to seek accuracy and longer barrels with the new Type 95 in bullpup with a full length 20.5 inch barrel.

    Just when everyone is adopting bullpups with full length barrels, the Israelis did the complete opposite by adopting a bullpup that has an 18-inch barrel as standard, shorter than either their current M-16 or Galil.

    It is quite wrong to assume that the Russians (or Soviets in the old days) decided upon the AK based on having an expendable population.

    First of all, the Russians are technically proficient enough to build an accurate rifle if they feel that's what they need. But the AK tapered cartridge and the rifle itself is designed with loose tolerance for reliability over accuracy.

    Secondly, their experience in WW2 with generous issue of SMG "burp guns" showed that high volume of automatic fire actually worked as well as - if not better than - aimed single shots.

    And besides, a Russian infantry platoon has several sharpshooters armed with scoped Dragunov sniper rifles to deal with the accuracy and longer range issues.

    Also, they have many SAWs in the platoon in the form of RPK which has a longer barrel and more accuracy if fired single shot. So long range engagement for a Russian rifle platoon is no problemo.

    And once the infantry get to close quarters urban or jungle, an AK firing bursts is a lot more devastating than those coming from M16-armed infantry.

    Originally posted by moca:
    And once the infantry get to close quarters urban or jungle, an AK firing bursts is a lot more devastating than those coming from M16-armed infantry.
    Erm, an close in burst from an M16 using normal ball ammo is far more lethal then AK burst. The tendency for such 5.56 to fragment at high velocities after entering the body causes larger wounds then the size and velocity of the round would account for. The AK 7.62 on the other hand, only starts to cause significant injury when it starts to yaw and tumble after having penetrated sufficent tissue... otherwise the AK round produces the typical "ice pick" effect.

    Hence at FIBUA distances a 5.56 round is actually more dangerous. Unless of course for some reason you are using the current standard issue Yank greentip ammo which removes this fragmentation effect and rendering the round very much less lethal.

    Above 100 meters however, the AK round is more dangerous... but this is offset by it being less precise. The real gem of the AK is in its reliability, given an IA in close quarters is death... something that is more likely with an M16.
