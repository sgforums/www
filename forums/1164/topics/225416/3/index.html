    Aye no argument there, and as you have seen I've already noted the effects and implications of knockdown power.

    It has been noted however that knockdown power is not a reliable means of stopping people, as by and by it relies a lot on the mental condition of the person. Some people have fallen or fainted when they have taken a slight graze from a bullet or were completely missed altogether.

    On the other hand there have been known cases where a sufficently motivated/enraged person could easily shrug off even after taking several mortal hits that would have brought down anybody. There was this case where a guy took a shotgun slug to the chest, leaving a gaping hole but yet was able to chase his shooters and empty a magazine at them before running out of gas.

In many cases in Iraq many of the Yanks who were wounded by AK 7.62mm still managed to carry on with purposeful actions and kill their enemy. Had the same area been hit by a fragmenting 5.56mm they would almost certainly been out of action.

    In the end the most reliable method of stopping someone is still taking a chunk out of them. A person stunned or wounded by an AK round may drop to the ground but still be a threat moments later when they recover enough to get by to the fight. Being shot in the same area with fragmenting 5.56mm provides a higher probability of putting that person out of the fight for good. So in the end it's still a mixed bag of the pros and cons of various rounds.

    You can drive NATO 7.62mm with sufficent modifications to get the same fragmenting effect that is native to 5.56mm (but this is not possible with the AK round), but of course in many cases it turned out that it was decided that it was far cheaper and more useful to carry more of a smaller caliber that gives just about the same effect.

    Having said that, from my personal military experience and training I have not come across a situtation that really struck me as a requirement to switch immediately from semi to auto. Often it's either a case of going in already on semi or auto, or taking a breather during a situtation to switch.

    And more so it's not as if switching to auto takes that much more time (just take your non master hand off to the rear as if exceuting a mag change and tap the fire selector) it does not even take a second and certainly well within the time frames that would be considered useful for combat. You can do it certainly well within the time frame it takes to change a mag or take cover.

    The same argument could be made for the notion that conventional layouts change mags marginally faster then bullpups. But going by that logic it could be argued that a belt-fed system is the best as it requires the least mag changes of all.

    At the end of the day I'm not arguing that the SAR-21 couldn't be improved by giving it an M-16 style fire selector. I am just pointing out that it is not as big an issue as people might put it out to be. If I had my way the SAR-21 would be able to take all kinds of mags (not just its own), have an M-16 style fire selector, tritum tipped backup sights, IR LAD, a ease spring lever and all that other stuff that I could think off. But at the end of the day all weapons are designed with many considerations in mind.

    There is certianly nothing in the design of the SAR that prevents it from being given an M-16 style selector. As I have speculated some posts before, it is likely that ST designed the SAR with SAF doctrine of mostly semi operations in mind, and decided that adding the extra linkages for the fire selector to be in the master-hand postion (given this is a bullpup) was edged out for making a more robust, and simpler system.

    My take on this? It is an acceptable compromise. Your weapon is pretty much screwed if the fire selector is broken. In a bullpup this would be a system consisting of linkage rods and plates more complex then your conventional design. Not to mention this is a feature that at best has dubious need to be used that quickly and the current feature in the rifle still does its function at an acceptable speed.

    I would have an issue if the safety was in the fire selector position, but this is not the case.
