    " The clean Typhoon, with 50% internal gas and 6-8 AAMs is firmly in the class of the F100-PW-229 powered F-15F, on dry thrust, and about 15% behind the F-15F on reheat. Where the Typhoon falls behind the F-15F is when its operating radius is stretched and additional external gas is being carried. If we take a Typhoon with 3 x 1000L external tanks, and an F-15F with 2 x 600 USG external tanks, we have configurations which deliver very similar endurance and operating radius for a point intercept. In the latter situation, approaching the target, the Typhoon is around 12% behind the F-15F in critical reheated thrust/weight ratio. If we compare a Typhoon with CFTs, 3 x 1000L external tanks against an F-15F with only CFTs, we get a shortfall of about 20% in thrust/weight ratio in addition to the drag penalty of the external tanks. These are very approximate estimates, not accounting for combat gas, but even doing a very accurate simulation would yield the inevitable conclusion - an F/A-18 sized fighter, no matter how agile when clean, cannot compete in thrust/weight ratio with an F-15 sized fighter at extended operating radii. "

    Britain survived the battle of britain for a number of reasons.

    One, they operated over friendly territory, and had then, sophisticated radar systems that permitted them to intercept the Germans. Their pilots that were shot down could be picked up, patched up, and sent back to their squadrons. Luftwaffe pilots were either captured or worst, froze to death in the Channel.

    Second. Like you said, the Germans had a tactical air force, not a Strategic one. Luftwaffe escorts had limited fuel that they could only engage or fend off the Brits for short period of time before returning back to base. German bombers were also less effective in the role they were supposed to play.

    Third, a less known detail. The luftwaffe expended much of their veteran flight instructors over the Netherlands in transport planes. Many of them were shot down, and valuable flight instruction experience lost.

    Britain was rather useless in contributing to the Strategic bombing campaign over Nazi Germany simply cause their fighters couldn't escort the bombers all the way. It was until the Mustang showed up, where the Americans were able to have their bombers escorted all the way to the target area and back.

    Personally, I wouldn't give the Typhoon's agility too much credit in modern air warfare. Most people who follow up in this, or even if you ask Air Force officers who are senior enough will tell you that no one wants to press for a merge. Especially if you are numerically inferior. Hence, it is unrealistic to discount BVR engagements.

    Secondly, high off-boresight weapons such as the Sidewinder X, R-73 makes agility less of any issue. As usual, superior turn rate is preferred over the ability to sustain a high-aoa-energy bleeding turn. If they don't kill in that turn, they are in trouble. Even if you get shot by one of these missiles, that little agility edge isn't going to save you.

    As for whether the RAF planners are myopic, I wouldn't say that. But I'd say that they are politically committed typhoon already, and cannot back out. Besides, its not a totally lousy plane or anything. It serves its purpose as a dual role fighter, and has exceptional handling. No reason to call the Brits myopic.

    Neither is there any reason to deem the F-15SG to be incapable of handling flanker threats. You can't kill what you can't lock. Its not stealth, but it has the next best thing, powerful jammers.
