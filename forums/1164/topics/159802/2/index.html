    The Pegasus is the world's first heli-portable 155mm howitzer with a self-propelled capability

    Ammunition Loading System (ALS)
    Powered by the APU, the ALS automatically loads ammunition to reduce crew fatigue. This allows the gun crew to operate the Pegasus for a longer period while maintaining a burst rate of 3 rounds in 24 seconds.

    Ease of Deployment
    As a complex system that needs to be readily deployed for mobility, firing and heli-lifting, the Pegasus is easy to configure without any lifting support in the field. Through a simple seesaw action that shifts the gun's centre of gravity to suit the different missions, the Pegasus can be rapidly engaged for deployment in less than 2.5 minutes with a detachment of 8 men.

    Lightweight Materials
    The Pegasus employs lightweight materials like titanium and high alloy aluminium that provides the strength and stability required to withstand the recoil force of a 155mm system.

    Innovative Recoil Management
    The recoil of the Pegasus is a third lower than conventional 155mm howitzers. This is achieved through innovative recoil management design.

    Straits Times

    Singapore gun a world first

    David Boey , Defence Correspondent

    Lightweight howitzer can be flown or driven into battle

    SINGAPORE defence engineers have scored a world first with a lightweight 155mm howitzer which can be flown into battle by helicopter and driven to where it's needed using an engine mounted on the gun.

    The Pegasus signals a new, more effective means of supporting the SAF heli-borne infantry, as the big gun can follow these troops into combat to blast enemy positions up to 30km away, said the Singapore Army's Chief of Artillery, Colonel Lim Chin.

    The gun has been been in development since 1994 by the Defence Science and Technology Agency (DSTA), the national authority on weapons procurements, by Singapore Technologies Kinetics, a weapons maker, and the SAF.

    'We started a worldwide search for a heli-portable 155mm gun in 1994 and explored collaborating with a few partners,' said Mr Kenneth Quek, the DSTA project manager who helped design Pegasus. 'But nothing in existence or under development met our requirements.'

    He said the joint project team finalised the gun's design last year, after extensive trials during which about 4,000 rounds were fired.

    Many types of guns can fire shells 155mm in diameter. But fewer than a dozen are light enough to be transported by heavy-lifting helicopters, such as the CH-47 Chinook.

    Pegasus beats them all as far as mobility is concerned because its gunners can drive the weapon to where it's needed after it is dropped by helicopter, rather than wait for a truck to be flown in to tow the gun around.

    Pegasus has the same maximum range as the FH-88 heavy artillery gun, but at 5.4 tons, it is about 60 per cent lighter than the Singapore-made FH-88s.

    Pegasus has almost twice the range of the older French-made LG1 105mm light gun which it will replace.

    The new gun was unveiled yesterday.

    The introduction of the Pegasus means all Singapore Artillery guns will use the same 155mm ammunition, making it easier to resupply artillery battalions during combat.

    Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean unveiled the weapon by spray-painting the number-plate '5801' on the first Pegasus.

    Mr Teo said: 'This weapon... can bring heavy firepower into the field and support the troops more quickly.

    'It's got its own power unit, which gives it self-mobility in the field - something that other lightweight howitzers of this class do not have.'

    Mr Christopher Foss, editor of the authoritative Jane's Armour & Artillery yearbook, believes a weapon like Pegasus will interest other armies, especially those with rapid deployment forces.

    The first Pegasus guns have been added to the arsenal of the 23rd Battalion Singapore Artillery.

    Full-time national service gunners in the battalion are being trained to operate the weapon. They are due to conduct their first live-firing using Pegasus during Exercise Thunder Warrior, to be conducted in New Zealand in January.
