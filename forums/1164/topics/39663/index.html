    Tokyo ready to examine offensive military system

    Japan's hawkish defence chief has said the government was ready to examine whether Tokyo should possess an offensive military capability to counter future missile attacks.

    Asked if the government planned to consider the introduction of an offensive military capability, Shigeru Ishiba told the parliamentary defence committee: "It is worth considering it."

    "It is necessary to examine (the issue) from various points of view. If we stop considering it, we will be unable to take responsibility for the peace and independence of our country."

    The remarks are likely to spark further consternation and anger in neighbouring Asian countries such as China and North Korea, which continue to demand Japan apologizes for its military aggression before and during World War II.

    Japan has one of the most powerful military forces in the region, but its troops have no guided missile systems capable of attacking another country as the post-war Constitution denies Japan's "right of belligerency of the state."

    During the committee session, Ishiba also repeated his support for the introduction of the US-made Patriot anti-missile system.

    "It is in line with an exclusively defensive posture and the only means we are able to consider right now," Ishiba said, referring to the possibility of introducing a missile defence system.

    "There is no reason to deny it," he said. "We have to consider its cost performance and understanding of neighbouring countries, but it is desirable to solve these problems and realise it."

    Discussions are under way between Japan and the United States over their bilateral missile defence initiative amid concerns about a potential missile attack from North Korea.

    Japan is stepping up its vigilance concerning North Korea following news reports that Pyongyang may test fire a ballistic missile in the near future. North Korea test fired short-range anti-ship missiles on February 24 and March 10.

    In February Ishiba suggested in an interview with a foreign news agency that Japan would launch a military strike if it had firm evidence Pyongyang was ready to launch ballistic missiles against it.

    The previous month, in an interview with The Times of London, Ishiba said the adequacy of Japan's purely defensive capability should be a matter of debate.

    9.29AM GMT, 28 Mar 2003
    North Korea's Foreign Ministry has said Japan's launch of a spy satellite was a "hostile act" and warned of a new arms race in northeast Asia.

    "The satellite launch deprived Japan of any justification and qualification to talk about the DPRK'S (North Korea's) satellite launch," said a foreign ministry statement.

    "Japan will be held wholly responsible for sparking a new arms race in northeast Asia," it added.

    A rocket carrying Japan's first two spy satellites was launched last week in an intelligence-gathering effort that some feared could prompt neighbouring North Korea to test-fire a ballistic missile.

    The satellite deployment, which will give Japan its first independent chance to scrutinise North Korea from space, was planned after Pyongyang's 1998 firing of a Taepodong ballistic missile, which passed over Japan and landed in the Pacific Ocean.

    In another statement, North Korea said that the United States was getting ready to attack it next after the war in Iraq.

    "It is becoming certain that, in case the US imperialists' invasion of Iraq is 'successful', they will wage a new war of aggression on the Korean peninsula at the same time or move the stage of 'anti-terrorist war' to the Korean peninsula in high spirits to invade the DPRK after occupying Iraq," North Korea's ruling party newspaper said.

    In a spate of commentaries that attacked the United States, the Rodong Sinmun daily took aim at the annual US-South Korean war games, which wind up on April 2.

    "We cannot but take a serious view of the large-scale frantic test nuclear war, a preliminary war against the DPRK staged by the US on the sidelines of the Iraqi war," the daily said.

    President George W. Bush has said Iraq, Iran and North Korea are part of the same "axis of evil".

    The Rodong Sinmun said after the first two acts of US military strategy under the Bush government, in Afghanistan and Iraq, "there is no doubt that they will open its third act on the Korean peninsula".

    "But they should clearly understand it. The DPRK is not Afghanistan nor Iraq," the daily said.
