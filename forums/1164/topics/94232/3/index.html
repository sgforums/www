    Whoever control Midway will have a control over a wide expense of the Pacific if they have sufficient naval and air power to police it.
    Sorry, wrong example. Midway was part of the Americans' Pacific atroll/island interests that ended up as a vital wartime operational base for them, but they didn't stake (or garrisoned) it that significantly prior.

    From the Imperial Japanese standpoint, the biggest Allied naval-base threat to their territorial ambitions was two-fold: Pearl Harbour (USA) & Singapore (GB). Hence their quick strategic strikes in late-'41/early-'42. Midway became important cuz it happened to be geographucally the next further American Far West staging post to stem the Japanese tide.

    Sidetracking: One of the most enlightening discussions I've attended, one that happened to involve JC students, occcurred at the Fall of Singapore Anniversary conference a couple of years back. It was based on a side comment from one of the speakers, a NUS lecturer, who briefly highlighted on a great "what if" missed opportunity. The premise was this: the post WWI peace-treaties accentuated the mistrust between the Western colonial powers & militarist Japan, & the British adopted the "Singapore Strategy" - i.e. building of Sembawang Naval Base - to facilitate the deployment of a naval taskforce to out East.

    The missed opportunity was what if British & Holland had put aside their colonial rivalry & enter into a marriage of military convenience against a common enemy (Japan)? What if, instead of Singapore, a concentrated naval base had been cooperatively built in, say, Bintan? Perhaps subsequent regional geopolitical developments would've created a different set of national soverignities, but Bintan could've still stood out ... as the South-east Asian version of Pearl Harbour before the Americans fortified their Pearl Harbour!

    The JC students also informally took up that point further - I was more of a silent listener - & suggested that if ASEAN ever unites enough into a Confederation (CONFSEAN, anyone?) or single Republic (Republic of South-east Asia?), that would be the kind of naval strategy the new entity would adopt, which in turn would make Australia a "strategic competitor" - to use George W. Bush's once-description of China.

    Fascinating scenario, but IMO more plausible than the idea of regional armed forces buying aircraft carriers when most of them are still oriented towards counterinsurgency (rather than for conventional warfare).
