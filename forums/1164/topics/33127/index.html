    ST has developed a 35mm anti-missile gun which can fire 35mm rounds filled with hundreds if not thousands of sub-ammunition bomblets.
    A radar will first detect any hostile missile. The computer will predict its flight path. The gun will fire the 35mm rounds into the missile's flight path. These rounds will release these bomblets into the predicted flight path of the missile. So when the missile hit the bomblets, it will be rendered useless as the bomblets damaged the missile's seeker, fins etc. This sysetm was exhibited in the 2000 and 2002 Asian Aerospace along with a AIM-4 Falcon missile damaged by the weapon system. ST may have copied this idea and weapon system from Bofors which had also developed such a gun.

    here's an recent article on the Terrex:

    ST Kinetics' new 8x8 Terrex AV81 has been designed to its requirements by Timoney Technology of County Meath, Ireland. In addition to designing its prototype, Timoney was also responsible for the welding of its hull, assembling it and carrying out preliminary automotive trials prior to its unveiling at the DSEi exhibition in London in September 2001 (see IDR 11/2001, p16).

    The design and construction of the prototype were accomplished in a remarkably short period of 18 months. This stands in striking contrast to the protracted gestation of some other contemporary 8x8 armored vehicles, and can be attributed in part to the experience Timoney acquired in the design of similar armored vehicles and to the use of Timoney's previously developed axle and suspension modules.

    Timoney's experience goes back to the mid-1970s when it built a small batch of 4x4 armored carriers for the Irish Army. The report on this by IDR (see IDR 4/1975, pp578-580) attracted the attention of the Belgian authorities and 146 Timoney 4x4 armored carriers were subsequently built in Belgium under license as the BDX. By 1990, Timoney had designed and built a heavier 6x6 armored carrier, which led to similar CM 31 prototypes for the Republic of China (Taiwan) as well as designing other vehicles and the chassis of the P.23 8x8 Crash Fire Rescue vehicle, 250 of which were built for the US Air Force (see IDR 8/1990, pp883-885). CM 31 is now being developed in Taiwan into the 8x8 CM 32, and its design foreshadowed in some respects that of Terrex AV81.

    All the vehicles designed by Timoney have incorporated its integrated axle-cum-suspension modules, which have been refined progressively over a period of more than 20 years. As a result they are available, fully developed, for Terrex AV81, providing it with the advantages of a proven independent suspension and axle design. Its effectiveness is attested by its adoption also by the manufacturers of other vehicles, who wanted to benefit from the advantages of independent suspension instead of following the common practice of using rigid-beam axle truck suspensions but did not want to invest time and money in the development of their own systems. Examples of this include Volvo military trucks and the 4x4 M1117 Armored Security Vehicle produced by Textron Marine and Land Systems for the US Military Police. Another vehicle currently using Timoney axle-suspension modules is the Bushmaster Infantry Mobility Vehicle designed by Timoney and built by ADI for the Australian Army.

    The independent suspension, which forms part of the modules, is of the double wishbone type with a single coil spring and a concentric telescopic damper acting on the lower wishbone. In addition to the suspension elements each module includes a differential unit, universally jointed half-shafts and hub-mounted disc brakes actuated by an anti-lock braking system. The wheels which go with this are fitted with large diameter 395/85 R20 tires connected to a central tire-inflation system which allows them to be deflated on the move to reduce the ground pressure for the crossing of soft ground.

    Four modules support the hull, which is welded from high-hardness steel plates thick enough to provide in its basic form all-round protection against 7.62mm x 51-ball ammunition. However, higher levels of protection up to 14.5mm armor-piercing bullets are to be provided by add-on armor. As ST Kinetics' Bionix has already been fitted with the MEXAS ceramic tile armor produced in Germany by IBD, this is also likely to be fitted to Terrex AV81.

    In keeping with the importance currently attached to protection against mines, considerable attention has been paid to it in the design of the hull. One of the results of this is that the hull has a double floor, with its outer layer being V-shaped to help deflect mine blast, and there is a blast-deflector plate under the drive line. In designing the hull against mines, Timoney benefited from its experience in Australia with the Bushmaster, which successfully competed with a vehicle from South Africa, the country that has led in the design of mine-resistant vehicles. In consequence Terrex AV81 is claimed to be able to withstand the explosion of a 10-12kg mine anywhere underneath it.

    The hull has an internal volume of no less than 12.2m3, which is more than sufficient to meet the contemporary demands for capacity. This enables it, among other capabilities, to accommodate up to 12 persons in addition to a crew of two when Terrex AV81 is used as a personnel carrier. In spite of this, the overall dimensions of Terrex AV81 are somewhat smaller than those of other 8x8 armored vehicles currently being developed, the length being 6.8m, width 2.7m and height to the top of the turret roof 2.1m. One of the benefits of its relatively compact dimensions and an unladen weight of only 13,000kg is that Terrex AV81 is transportable in C-130 aircraft.

    An interesting feature of the hull design is that its roof is removable to ease modifications required by different weapon installations or by demands for even more space - in particular headroom - for command-post and ambulance versions. The ability to remove the roof does not affect the integrity of the hull's monocoque structure, and represents a more effective approach to the problem of adapting a vehicle to different roles than the replacement of most of its hull by a separate, exchangeable 'mission module'. The latter has been adopted for some vehicles currently being developed, but is less efficient structurally and makes them heavier as well as more complicated and more costly to build.

    As in other 8x8 armored vehicles, the front part of the hull houses the engine compartment. Alongside it is the driver's station, which is to be fitted with a novel type of cupola that is currently the subject of a patent application. The cupola is rotatable and has a 'bulletproof' windscreen on one side of it and a face of armor surmounted by periscopes on the other. The driver does not therefore have to drive 'head out' when he wants a better view of the road - exposing himself in the process to the weather and minor threats - but can look through the windscreen and stay under cover at all times, rotating the cupola 180Âº according to the conditions in which his vehicle operates.

    As the rotatable driver's cupola was not installed on the prototype, the latter was fitted instead with the same arrangement of three fixed periscopes as the Bionix.

    The engine which powers Terrex AV81 is a Caterpillar C 9 6-cylinder turbocharged diesel, developing 420hp (313kW) at 2,200rpm and coupled to an Allison HD 4560P automatic transmission with six forward and one reverse gear. The drive from the transmission is through a single-speed drop box and a single-speed transfer case at the second axle unit and then to the front axle rearwards to the third and fourth axles, all eight wheels being permanently driven. The whole drive train as well as the engine is designed to give the vehicle a maximum road speed of 120km/h.

    When lightly laden the vehicle has sufficient buoyancy to float without aids, but these are being developed to enable it to operate in water even when its gross weight is at its maximum of 24,000kg. In water it can propel itself by means of two water-jet propulsion units made by Ultra Dynamics. These are hydraulically driven and one is mounted on either side of the rear of the hull.

    At DSEi, Terrex AV81 was exhibited with an ST Kinetics 40mm automatic grenade launcher and a 7.62mm machine gun, both mounted externally and remotely operated from within the hull. Possible alternative weapon installations include a 25mm automatic cannon in an ST Kinetic two-person turret, which is already used on the Bionix, and low recoil-force 105mm tank gun.

    The prototype has now been shipped to Singapore where decisions are to be taken about the second prototype.
