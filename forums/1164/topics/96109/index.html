    Seriously speaking, other than telling us the time of exposure of snap targets, there is no special requirement on the time taken to engage a target and fire off.

    A hasty shot is never a good shot. Let's say that you are going to engage a moving target. What I do is to suck it in, watch the target move, plant my crosshairs ahead of the target, then constrict my air passage, slowly leaking out my breathe, and when the target reaches a known distance from my sights, do a quick snatch, all the while maintaining the "leak" even after the shot has been fired. That's due to a need to follow through. You can't do that with an M-16 though, the trigger pressure is too high, you risk missing from a snatch, but you can do that with an SSG due to its very light trigger weight and supported prone position.

    Special care need to be taken for trigger modification also. For stationary targets, smooth trigger action is suitable. For snap targets, consider a double pressure.

    Originally posted by beavan:
    erm do you think that the SAF way of hunting a sniper is abit funny?when a sniper shoots at you,do you guys have the time to count the time from when the bullet hits to the time when u hear the bang?
    Yes. It's called the "crack and thump". The human sensory memory mechanism consists of this component called echoic memory(sonic). Echoic memory retains information for 7-8 seconds before it is being forgotten, compared to the human iconic memory (sight and vision) which lasts for only half to 1 sec. Unless actively excercised and recalled the moment it becomes registered, it will be virtually permanently lost within 10 sec. The echoic memory store retains whatever your brain registers while your central processing is busy with other stuff ie. running for cover, and is recalled and processed after that. Yes, you can count the seconds. But usually that is the business of the sniper, not normal infantry. Counter sniping uses this to find the other sniper.
