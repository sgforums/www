    Originally posted by Theatre Of Dreams:
    heez what is extractor btw? and backpressure refers to the reverse flow of exhaust gases?
    Put simply, extractors are pipes leading directly out of the exhaust ports which allow exhaust gases to flow more freely out. As the exhaust gases pass through pipes, they tend to compress at certain points, causing high-pressure spots that restrict the gas flow. Extractors are shaped, or tuned, in such a way that the high-pressure spots do not occur.

    Backpressure refers to the pressure created by residual exhaust in the combustion chambers (which are never entirely expelled in the exhaust stroke). Engines are designed with backpressure in mind, so that the compression is optimum when the residual exhaust gases and the fuel-air mixure (taken in during the following intake stroke) are compressed.

    If you add extractors to the car, there is less residual gas left in the chamber than was accounted for in the engine's original design. In turn, the pressure of in the chamber at the top of the compression stroke is lower than it would be in the original specifications, resulting in less pressure when the fuel-air misture is ignited. As a result, the engine loses low-end torque.

    If you add extractors to the car, you need to be careful to ensure that the intake is also modified such that the pressure at the top of the compression stroke is the same as specified in the original design (i.e. it makes up for the loss of backpressure). That way, you don't lose low-end torque, and because the exhaust gases flow out more freely, you get better power at high revs too, ecause the exhaust stroke saps less power.
