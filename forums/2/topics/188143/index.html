    a hybrid car runs on more electric motors which are more efficient than petrol engines. these motors often either use hydrogen fuel cells, solar cells(in the early development of the whole hybrid car concept), a dyno that turns the motion of the wheel into stored electrical energy, or simply a battery that u charge up from a static charging station. They work in tandem or independent of the petrol engines at times, hence reducing FC as the petrol engine is worked less.
  • HENG@
    29 Apr 06, 15:15
    i neglected to mention that depending on the design scope of the vehicle, hybrids can be very powerful as electric motors have very high torque from very tiny motors. that torque is also instantly available from the word go. How much torque is unleashed is dependent on how much u depress the accelerator, like a dimmer switch for your light). if right from the start u step pedal to the metal, then almost full torque's what u get, unlike a petrol car where torque is very dependent of the engine's current rpms and that band where torque is high can be fairly limited. There is virtually no lag because an electric motor does not need to hit high rpms to deliver high torque figures, and can deliever that full or almost full torque from as low as 500+ rpms all the way and still manage to rev to 12,000 rpms.

    all of this can be clearly seen as evidenced by the Lexus RX400H

    it has a 3.3l V6 petrol engine and 2 electric motors.

    the V6 engine manages 288Nm of torque, while the front motor generates 333Nm of torque and the rear motor generates 130Nm of torque. Together the electric motors generate a monsterous 463Nm of torque. to put that into perspective, the 7th generation of Evolutions 'only' did 393Nm with a turbocharged petrol engine.

    the very heavy RX400H, with a total curbweight of over 2500kg(2.5 tons) still manages to hit 0-100km/h in a respectable 7.6 seconds.
