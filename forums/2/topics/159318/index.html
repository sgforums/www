    The fact is that (and this shows my disappointment in the system) the car market here has become a big casino.....where you don't know your buying price till you actually buy it.....and you don't know your selling price till you actually sell it. In this nationwide casino, the house wins every round

    Generally though.....cars here depreciate most in the first few years of their legal lifespan. They depreciate least in the last 2-3 years of the same 10-year lifespan.

    There are many good catches now in the used car market (especially those which are 1-2 years old), mainly because the first owner has taken the bulk of the depreciation. Then again, buying used always carries some risk. Plus you may need to factor in the cost of psychiatric treatments after dealing with some of the used car dealers (not all, there are some honest and reliable ones as well).

    Sorry to show you a deadend, but the best advice I can give is to come back, then consider again using the data available at that time....

    Hi again!

    Buying from the owner doesn't mean you'll get a better price/deal...unless, of course, you personally know and trust the owner. Owners will also check for what their cars are selling for in the market before deciding on a price. There is also the trouble of all the admin work (which is actually not that tough). Owners always sell "as is". Car dealers sometimes sell "with limited warranty". If you've noticed, not many owners sell directly anyway.

    Whether the used car dealers will allow for 3rd party inspection varies from case to case, depending on the character of the salespeople and condition of the car, no doubt.

    If they do allow 3rd party inspections, they would probably ask for a pre-agreement that if everything is OK, you have to buy the car. They basically check for emissions, illegal modifications, any signs of previous accidents, etc.

    If I'm not mistaken, AA inspections are almost S$200 and takes a few hours (maybe worth it). However, there are some crucial parts of cars which cannot be inspected to great detail (eg. gearbox and engine block).

    Take heed of my previous post where I mentioned to read the fine print carefully. There was a reported case where the buyer has been told to give a monetary deposit in order to bring the car out for inspection. Documents were signed, deposit was given, car was brought for inspection. After the inspection, he decided not to buy the car, but the used car dealer refused to return his deposit.

    Upon careful inspection of the signed documents, the buyer discovered that he did agree to that (he didn't read the fine print). Buyer lost the case.

    One very important thing you have to remember is that in the Singapore car industry, whether its brand new or used, whether is a private company or government......once you've paid any deposit, you probably won't get your money back in cash anymore. This is true even if the deal is called off, not by any fault of yours!

    I remember that there was once when I was looking at used cars. I wanted to test drive it, but the sales guy told me to agree on a price first which I considered fair value....and if the test drive was fine, I will agree to buy it. My reply was direct - that I will not value a car which I've not test-driven, that a test-drive is no guarantee that I will buy. But if I didn't test-drive it, I know for sure I will never buy it. I was sincere about it and not just looking for a joyride - and I understood some of the used-car salesman's difficulties too, but I simply will not submit to high-pressured sales tactics (I'm in sales myself, by the way). Fortunately, it was a cordial (superficially) discussion.

    To protect myself, I further insisted that all conditions should be written down on the invoice/receipt - in-house warranty, max. duration to completion of handing/taking over, etc etc.

    In the end, I test-drove the vehicle, liked it and bought it. Happy ending.
