    Budget surprise

    Chana's first effort – the Benni is cheap but far from nasty
     
    Justin Lee
    news@newstoday.com.sg
     
    Over 1.3 billion people, or nearly a fifth of the world's population, live in China. The country is also known as the bicycle kingdom of the world, with each household owning, on average, 1.43 bicycles.
    .
    Two-wheeled pedal power is, however, on the decline as the country's newly minted middle class turn to car ownership.
    .
    The booming demand for cars has led to the rise of many state- and privately-owned automakers. Most are contract assemblers for established European and Asian brands but a few have branched out and started producing cars with their own brand names.
    .
    Chongqing-based Changan Automobiles is one of them. The Benni compact hatchback is Changan's subsidiary Chana Automobile's first model to be sold in Singapore.
    .
    First impressions of the car are good. Large projector headlamps flank a simple grille at the front, while the rear has contemporary vertical tail lamps.
    .
    Overall, the Benni's design is neat and inoffensive. Buyers will be pleased that the car doesn't look glaringly cheap in any way.
    .
    A modern 1.3-litre 16-valve engine producing 84bhp and 110Nm of torque sits under the short bonnet. It's a willing performer that responds readily to throttle inputs, giving the car a lively, nimble feel.
    .
    However, the lack of sound insulation means the engine makes itself heard constantly. The never-ending wave of mechanical noise, even under mild acceleration, makes for a rather tiring driving experience.
    .
    The standard five-speed manual gearbox has a pleasantly short throw but the lever has a sticky action, especially when shifting from third to second.
    .
    Plus points are the positive clutch action and firm brakes, which help give the Benni the reassuring feel of a properly engineered product.
    .
    The car acquits itself quite well on the road and, despite its tall stance, doesn't feel too top-heavy around corners. There's a bit of body roll and the steering is vague but this isn't going to bother drivers at this end of the price spectrum.
    .
    For a small Chinese car, the interior is surprisingly solid. No unsightly gaps or rough-edged trim pieces here. A factory- fitted stereo sits in the neatly designed centre console and the main instruments are easy to read.
    .
    There is still room for improvement, though — some of the switches look flimsy and the plastic panel covering the seat belt came loose on the test car. The air- conditioning takes a long time to cool the cabin down on a hot day, too.
    .
    This aside, spending time in the Benni is not laborious. Both front and rear passengers enjoy plenty of head- and leg-room, while the large windows give a good view out. The rectangular boot is a good size.
    .
    A driver's airbag, front power windows, 14-inch alloy wheels and a rear spoiler come as standard. Not too bad for a car that costs just $38,999 with COE. An automatic version with a higher level of equipment will be launched later this year.
    .
    This car is a positive indication that, after a not-too-auspicious start, China's automobile industry is now moving in the right direction.
    .
    The Benni is an honest-to-goodness compact that meets most people's basic transport needs and does so with a modicum of style.
    .
    Chana Benni 1.3
    .
    Engine 1,301cc, 16V in-line 4
    .
    Max power 84bhp at 6,000rpm
    .
    Max torque 110Nm at 4,400rpm
    .
    Gearbox 5-speed manual
    .
    Top speed 145kmh
    .
    0-100kmh 13.0 seconds
