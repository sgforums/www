    Too small to be global players

    BEIJING — Despite their global ambitions, Chinese car makers are still not big enough to take over their troubled foreign rivals, analysts and company officials said.
    .
    Reports have swirled for weeks on persistent rumours that Chinese car makers are eyeing the chance to buy Sweden’s Volvo from United States giant Ford Motor or to take either Swedish unit Saab or American all-terrain vehicle-maker Hummer off the hands of General Motors.
    .
    The companies at the centre of the rumours, such as Geely, have repeatedly denied the reports while at the same time fanning the rumours by saying they are open to foreignacquisitions.
    .
    Such acquisitions are a route through which Geely could access capital, new markets and international partnerships, according to the car company’s website. Analysts, however, said they do not expect to see any startling Chinese auto acquisitions in the near future.
    .
    “Compared with the more than 40 billion yuan ($8 billion) needed to acquire Volvo, Geely’s market value is only about 3 billion yuan,” business strategy consultancy Roland Berger said in a research note, listing the Chinese car makers that would be most likely to bid.
    .
    “The total assets of Chery Automobile are about 30 billion yuan, with capital of 3 to 4 billion yuan and the acquisition requires about 40 billion yuan.”
    .
    Another analyst with Global Insight concurs. “I do not see any kind of possibility that a local company would acquire Volvo or Hummer. It is not realistic financially or even in terms of management capability,” said its Beijing-based analyst John Zeng.
    .
    “For a number of Chinese companies, their sole experience with foreigners is their joint ventures in China. They have never operated overseas.”
    .
    One exception is SAIC’s acquisition in 2004 of South Korean car maker Ssangyong — which now faces bankruptcy.
    .
    Analysts such as China Automotive Industry Consulting and Development’s Jia Xinguang said Chinese companies lack the necessary management experience.
    .
    But size is the main obstacle.
    .
    “Any overseas acquisition needs National Development and Reform Commission (NDRC) approval and at this time the NDRC has concerns about Chinese companies’ capacity to run such acquisitions,” said Mr John Shen, an analyst at Roland Berger.
    .
    Last week, Beijing urged China’s crowded domestic auto sector to consolidate.
    .
    But the international opportunities remain attractive as a means to acquire the technology that the Chinese car makers need. “They do not have strong brand technology and are still relying heavily on foreign partners,” said Global Insight’s Zeng.
    .
    Mr Shen said that it would be more realistic for Chinese car makers to buy some core assets that they need, rather than whole companies. That could make more sense at a time when several Chinese companies want to establish their own brands.
    .
    It is a path Geely seems to have taken with its acquisition on Friday of Australian auto parts manufacturer Drivetran Systems. Geely said the deal would improve its capability to develop and produce gearboxes, or transmission — a key technology that Chinese car makers must learn to master to be globally competitive. AFP
