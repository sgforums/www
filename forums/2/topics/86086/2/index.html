    Originally posted by rumie:
    correct me if i'm wrong. here's an example:

    suppose a car needs $120,000.

    and a person pays a downpayment of $20,000. which means the person still needs to pay $100,000.

    this person borrows a loan from the bank. the longer the repayment length (usually it's 5, 7, 10years), the higher the interest.

    for 7years loan @ 2.6% per annum, this interest rate is calculated for the $100,000 with the following (agar-agar) formula:
    Total amount to pay over 7 years
    = $100,000 x (2.6 x 7) + $100,000
    = $118200

    The monthly repayment will be
    = $118,200/(7x12)
    = $1407

    Of course, it'll be slightly lesser than this but it's an alright approximation.

    Now, suppose for the same car of $120,000 this person takes a 0-downpayment (if he's lucky in able to do that).

    Then for the same 7years loan @ 2.6% per annum,
    Total amount to pay over 7 years
    = $120,000 x (2.6 x 7) + $120,000
    = $141,840

    The monthly repayment will be
    = $141,840/(7x12)
    = $1688

    That's a difference of about $250/mth. SOunds little, yes. But it WILL matter a lot when you start worrying about the monthly petrol, road tax and insurance...

    So as you can see, the more the downpayment initially, the lesser you'll have to pay per month.

    So it'll be better if you take on a 10yrs loan right? since the repayment period will be extended to 3 more years...

    Of course, the best to spend per month on a car is zero, i.e., don't get a car.

    However, if you die die must buy a car, you could:

    1) Pay as much downpayment as possible. D/p of 25-30% should ensure you don't have nightmare every night throughtout the tenure of your loan.

    2) Opt out of the 10-year loan. Opting for 10-year loan is like digging your grave with a changkul.

    Those opting for 10-year loan normally pay very little d/p. This is akin to digging the grave with an excavator. You had better hope you don't lose your job or suffer any financial crisis during the tenure of the loan. This is because you are in a negative-equity situation. When things get bad, you have to sell your car - and you have to top-up a neat sum in order to sell it. Your car value will be lower than the outstanding loan amount!

    Paying installments is only one of the difficulties of owning a car. You have to think abt the cost of parking, fuel, servicing (which is rather expensive if you opt to go to the agents), and the occasional "greeting cards" from the authorities.
