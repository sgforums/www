    AFAIK, the engine oil stops circulating once you stop running the engine, and it will slowly collect in the pan.

    When you first start yr car, it takes a while for the engine oil to recirculate throughout the engine to provide the lubrication. Getting the engine to warm up a little bit is good practice. It ensures that the engine parts are sufficiently lubricated before you start engaging it hard, and ensures you won't suffer premature wear and tear.

    Idling too long is also not a good idea. It is something to do with your spark plugs.

    A good guide is to wait for the engine rpms to drop to abt 1000rpm then just drive slowly and gently first thing.

    Hi speedy,

    Warming up of the engine would mean warming up of the engine oil. I agree with dh on that point.

    There is an optimum working temperature for the oil and this would definitely be part of the engine warming factor.

    With the optimum working temperature, the engine oil will serve to lubricate your engine parts more efficiently rather than from the cold start stage.

    There may be other areas that need to be warm up. But with technology nowadays, engine are built with very good tolerances, perhaps the wearable parts , like seals and bearings, would not be damaged, if the tolerances are within controlled, when running the engine without proper warming up.

    You may not notice the effects immediately, but the stresses built over prolong exertion of poor lubricated engine parts can lead to premature failure of the wearable parts and even to the engine itself.

    Warming up may not be limited to the engine exclusively, but being just a car enthusiast, my knowledge is not quite as far reach as an automotive builder.
