    think there's a better chance of engine wear for autos leh bro. the point to consider is that the autos work on the principal of a torque convertor using viscous coupling to generate a turning effect. this means that the auto tranny is constantly "slipping" at a optimal level such that the engine doesn;t stall and the rpms remain reasonably low.

    the downside of doing this as compared to a manual tranny is that the engine is always under a certain constant load whilst in the "d" position as it works against the torque convertor to maintain this condition. for a manual, the engine doesn't experience any load coz either the clutch is depressed or the tranny is in neutral (otherwise the car will stall wat) over prolonged periods, this would mean that a engine with manual tranny willl be subjected to less overall stress factors as compared with an auto equipped engine.

    hehe, missed out on this one.

    there's a few schools of tot for engine braking. some say that too much will stress your engine, others swear by it although there's no doubt that the majority of these are from the racing fraternity. the truth is, the engine doesn't go through any load under engine braking. loads only occur under the combustion cycle, when the engine is burning gas and pumping out power to move a car against it's weight. however, this is not the case when the car is slowing down under engine brakes. the only wear the engine will experience is the rubbing of the piston rings against the cylinder walls, which is perfectly acceptable if u ask me.

    this i feel, does not really apply to auto cars as i understand, the engine is constantly putting in fuel even as u lift off the accelerator (ie no engine brake effect). notwithstanding the auto owner deliberately dropping to a really low gear.(which most auto gearboxes i know are incapable of) whether or not this causes more strain/wear on the engine has yet to be known (by me at least )
