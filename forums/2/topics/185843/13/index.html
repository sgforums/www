    I attended the Stamford talk last Sunday and must say that it was petty well organised. But I think Stamford was too ambitious and tried to introduce too many products such that it finished at past nine instead of the scheduled 6.30pm. But I felt that Stamford went to a lot of trouble for us. Their staff were still cleaning up at 9.25pm when I left. Their enthusiasm for their job and/or their company is really eye-opening. I would mention it to Mr Wee Kok Wah or Dawn if I see them

    As usual the food was good, thanks to the organisers and/or Stamford.
    Since I was talking too much I missed Faded Mango's cheesecake! I am still kicking myself after all these days. Well at least I am not suffering from withdrawal symptoms.

    The talk started with the Stamford representative telling us about alloy wheels.
    I am sure all of you know much about alloy wheels. The only thing I will add here is that alloy rims are stronger than the standard steel rims. In the case of an accident or when your car hits a bump or obstruction on the road, at speed, you will be infinitely thankful if the tyre does not burst due to a cracked rim. As you know, a cracked rim or a burst tyre at speed will cause your car to lose control, or worse, tip over. More so for the March I think, unfortunately. The advantages, a slightly faster acceleration, perhaps, are inconsequential. However, since they typically cost only a fraction (less that 1 %) of the cost of the car, they only have to save your skin (and a_s) once and they would have paid for themselves many times over. This is something like health insurance, if we have to make a claim once, it will pay for itself many times over. Of course many of us consider the cosmetics more important and I don't disagree. Alloy rims definitely look sportier, better and cooler than steel rims. (I am not advertising for Stamford).

    Next there was Broquet. For the uninformed and for those who missed the dinner last Sunday, Broquet is a metallic additive that is inserted into your fuel tank OR connected between your fuel lines OR both (for the kiasus). It is supposed to catalyse the fuel and change the molecular structure such that they carry a higher octane rating and become more easily combustible. Net result? Better combustion in the piston chambers, leading to less carbon deposits in the chambers, more energy released, better efficiency in power creation, better fuel economy, lower fuel costs, more power. As less carbon build-up is experienced over time, the engine stays more efficient over time also, and the whole process is repeated, better fuel economy, more power, better betters etc., etc., lah, lah...

    The product life is for 400,000km and so the agent says you can disconnect it when you change your car. 400,000km approximates 20 driving years for us here in Singapore.

    Is this true? I only know of a friend who has used it and he claims it works very well. Unfortunately I also asked a Nissan engineer and he claims it does not. So who is right? I hope many other Broquet users here give their FRANK opinion about their experiences with the product. It will benefit not only us but also the general motoring public
