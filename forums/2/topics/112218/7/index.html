    Okay, how can I put this succinctly?

    Neither the M5's nor the F50's engine can be considered an F1 engine by any stretch of the imagination. Both are well over 3 litres, and both rev at about half the speed of an F1 engine.

    That's where the similarity between the two engines ends. The F50's engine used an actual F1 engine as a base, after which modifications were made - by that measure, it is an F1 engine modified for road use.

    On the other hand, the M5's engine has never at any point been an F1 engine. It may borrow technology from the F1 program, but it is not a road-going F1 engine.

    Hopefully that clears things up.

    In that case, I think we're ready to go back to the original point, where you said that BMW's F1 participation is something you would take into consideration when buying a car.

    We've established that unlike extreme examples such as the F50 and the OX99, the relationship between F1 constructors' road and race cars is tenuous at best. As such, how much can you really count on the constructors' F1 performance as an indicator of their road cars' quality?

    I assert that F1 participation is not a very good indicator at all. The road cars do not share the chassis, suspension or engine designs of the race cars, and as such are so different that any inference is dubious.

    What we're left with, then, is the engine management system and transmission. Sure, the E60's EMS is a you-beaut unit, running three 32-bit processors and making a couple of million calculations per second, but that ultimately affects smoothness, not power. Plenty of people have taken a big-block engine and cranked out lots of horsepower - a Chevy V8 can be made to pump out 500 bhp quite easily, so the E60's power is more a function of its size and rev speed, not the fact that it's got individual throttles for each cylinder. As for semiautomatic transmissions, they're becoming pretty common these days, with even Alfa producing shifters mounted on the steering wheels. To your average road driver, what difference does 50 miliseconds between gears make?

    The bottom line, therefore, is that when you're behind the wheel, the manufacturer's participation isn't going to make much of a difference.
