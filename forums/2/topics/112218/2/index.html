Audi drivers were getting pretty annoyed with how easily the rims were getting dented by potholes, so they started switching over to aftermarket rims. I see Toyota's problem being similar to Volvo's - they both need to get away from their "safe but dowdy choice" image.

    As for F1, Merc has been in the wilderness since beryllium was banned, and overall the team strategy leaves much to be desired. Changing 96% of the parts on a championship-winning car was nothing short of stupid, and 18/19's nose was a mistake that they took two years to correct. Ferrari's current dominance is owed to the fact that while Ferrari are building logical developments on their already successful cars, the other constructors are trying new approaches and dumping them before they've had a chance to bear fruit. As always, the development priorities for the engine builders is reliability, power and weight in that order. Both Toyota and Honda have spent the last few years working on reliability and power, and now it's time to get the weight of the engines down. Renault, in the meantime, should have stuck with their wide-V design instead of following the crowd with a 90-degree design - despite having less power, the wide design allowed for better weight distribution, and was actually starting to come good when Renault dumped it. Big mistake.

I'd say that the guys to look out for at the moment are Raikonnen (even though he had a crummy year last year), Alonso, Webber and Button.
