    I observed that there was a pattern between checksum letters, through observing SMRT buses on the run.

    An example: TIB 425 R.

    Next letters:

    TIB 426 M
    TIB 427 K
    TIB 428 H
    TIB 429 E
    TIB 430 A

    Now, take a look at the letters in an upward direction, starting from R.

    A
    B

    M
    P
    R

    Notice that they are in alternatives with the exception of "C". This is because at a start of a new tens (in this case, 430.), the next alternative letter is skipped and replaced by the alternative to the next alternative.

    Next, let me give you another example.

    TIB 589 B

    Here's a pretty reasonable rationale I found.

    TIB 500 G

    TIB 800 R

    TIB 700 X

    Going forward again...

    TIB 801 M

    TIB 900 K

    What do you notice?

    The checksum letter of any second number of a hundred always appears as the first checksum letter of any consecutive hundred. This applies to ALL registration plates.

    Also, any checksum letter with the numbers "XX89" always appears as the first hundred of "X(X+1)00".

    Now, you would ask... why? I don't really know, except that it makes it easy to track when you've got a easy-to-understand pattern on hand.

    TIB 1000 B?

    It corresponds to TIB 600 B.

    TIB 1100 X corresponds to TIB 700 X
    TIB 1200 R corresponds to TIB 800 R
    TIB 1300 K corresponds to TIB 900 K

    Will 1400 be B again? That's for you to find out.

    In summary,

    Let the checksum letter of any number be "N".

    XX00 N = XX[00 + 18] N = XX(18 + 17) N = XX(18 + 17 + 17) N = XX[18 + 17 + 17 + (18 + 17)] N.

    XX02 N = XX(2 + 87) N = X(X+1)00 N.

    X6XX N = (X+1)0XX N but not equals to (X+1)(0+4)XX N.

    I'll give you now the pattern of checksum letters.
