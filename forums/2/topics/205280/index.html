    Originally posted by TwinTurbo_Supra:
    If hard tops are better, why do Ferrari and Lamborghini put soft tops on their convertible models?

    and hard tops have a higher center of gravity as compared to soft tops.
    this is a no brainer question.

    compare for the same car, the difference in performance figures between the hardtop and softtop.

    u go check it up. 360 and 360 spyder, DB9 coupe and convertible etc.

    Chopping a top off means a loss of chassis rigidity. Chassis rigidity refers to how much the chassis flexes. You can have the best suspension system in the world, but if your car chassis flexes like a slinky u might as well be driving swiss cheese down the alps.

    That rigidity can be regained with thicker welds and reinforcement at other points in the chassis, but that means an increase in weight, easily up to 200+kg more over the coupe model. Add the need for a mechanism to raise the soft top and the weight it adds, and it becomes clear why soft tops GENERALLY fare slightly worse than their equivalent model coupes.

    Additionally, having a soft top means top speed is restricted as the existance of a seam increases air friction, which becomes more notable at higher speeds. Then the added question of having to make a soft top mechanism which can withstand the lift at extremely high speeds, u have car manufacturers choosing to limit the car to a slightly lower top speed anyway, just so the soft top doesn't tear away while blasting along the autobahn.

    soft tops have less complicated mechanisms, hence easier to manufacture, easier to maintain and less likely to malfunction.

    but for soft tops, there have been cases where vandals have used lighted cigarettes to burn a hole in it.     windnoise in a soft top is louder, and because a soft top creates more friction against the airflow, AND is more likely to get ripped off by wind lift, its top speed should be affected more, disregarding the differences in power and weight.
