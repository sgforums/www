    ehh.. SOHC and DOHC has got nothing to do wif compression ratio leh..

    DOHC and SOHC basically refers to single or double OVERHEAD cam shafts. In the past, cam shafts were not located "OVERHEAD" (as in over the cylinders).

    In a single cam shaft config, cam lobs for intake and exhaust valves are all located on the same single shaft. DOHC has their intake and exhaust cam lobs located on different shafts (which is why DOUBLE cam shafts, one intake the other exhaust).

    Some V engines has Quad shafts.. this is effectively 2 sets of DOHC each mounted on different engine heads.

    And which is better? I suppose the more the better lah.. but I have this feeling DOHC is more for "marketing" purpose, coz in a normal everyday car (not referring to high performance cars), I see no reason why we would need DOHC.

    In fact, modern day manufacturing process can be so advance you can even have several different sets of cam lobs on a single shaft (such as Honda SOHC v-tech engines).

    As siaokao mentioned, the OHC design is pretty much standard practice these days, as opposed to the older OHV (overhead valve) engines in which the camshaft drives a set of pushrods which in turn operate the valves.

    The advantage of the OHC over the OHV is that, because of the longer train of components driving the valves, flexion of the metal causes OHV engines to have less precise operation of the valves at higher revs.

    As an extension of that, I would expect that the DOHC may have a slight advantage over the SOHC because with each camshaft being simpler, it may twist less when the torsion increases at high revs. Perhaps Heng could confirm if my suspicions are correct.

    As for compression, the only inherently weaker design was the L-head, which was completely phased out in the 1950's by the OHV design.

    i think the primary advantage of DOHC over SOHC is "tuneability".

    with 2 cams, u can select diff profiles, advance, retard, alter phasing with respect to each cam to achieve optimal performance over a wider rpm range. with technologies such as variable timing/ lift, the potential for tuning such parameters become virtually infinite.

    something mechanically impossible with a single cam set up.
