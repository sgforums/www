    As for other factors, I would nail it on the gear ratios, and in-gear accel, low speed road handling,long dist crusing ability, drivability in urban areas, emission standards, and finally, being truthful to the heritage and character of Aston Martin cars.     Stats for Aston cars:

    Vanquish S(V12)

    power: 520bhp
    torque: 576Nm
    0-60: 4.6s
    weight: 1875kg
    bhp/ton: 277bhp/ton
    torque/ton: 307Nm/ton
    top speed: 200mph/320kmh

    DB9(V12)

    power: 450bhp
    torque: 569Nm
    0-60: 4.7s
    weight: 1800kg
    bhp/ton: 250bhp/ton
    torque/ton: 316Nm/ton
    top speed: 186mph/297.6kmh

    Vantage(V

    power: 380bhp
    torque: 409Nm
    0-60: 4.8s
    weight: 1570kg
    bhp/ton: 242bhp/ton
    torque/ton: 260Nm/ton
    top speed: 175mph/280kmh

    If u compare the cars down the range, u should also see why the stats are thus placed. The vanquish, altho being the flagship car, was introduced much earlier to the DB9 and the Vantage. One of the most impt criteria would be that lesser cars in the company's range should not outperform its flagship car. And altho that may be the case, I would say that is not the primary criteria. A low revving V12 generating per 500bhp and handfuls of torque both fits the image of an Aston, and is pretty impressive. V12s in cars like Lamborghini and Ferrari are very highly strung equipment, and have totally different charactristics. Whats e point of an Aston ending up behaving like a Ferrari? I want my Astons to be Astons, and Ferraris to be Ferraris please. If everybody ended up producing cars that behaved the same way engine-wise, whats the point of having different brands?

    Anyways I'm going to include a Mitsubishi EVO 9's stats for comparism as well:

    EVO 9 FQ-300

    power: 305bhp
    torque: 402Nm
    0-60: 4.5s
    weight: 1400kg
    bhp/ton: 218bhp/ton
    torque/ton: 287Nm/ton
    top speed: 157mph/251kmh

    looking by the stats, the EVO might beat the astons for 0-60, but past 60mph/100kmh, the Aston would leave it for dead. The issue for a standing start is always the weight and traction. An EVO is lightweight and uses 4WD to overcome the issue of traction. Lambos are heavy but use 4WD to overcome the traction issue as well. Ferraris are lightweight and hence do not suffer as much frome a standing start, although its certainly not the most luxurious interiors u could find. Astons, being heavy(on luxuries) and RWD only, would have to use traction control to find traction. As any racing driver worth their salt would tell u, TC saps time. Afterall, the purpose of TC is to cut power to any wheel it detects to be losing traction. When yr car's power is not being laid down to both its driven wheels, 0-60 time must surely suffer some. Once traction is not a problem, it would easily leave most lesser cars for dead.

    And finally, can an EVO move off from a standstill in 4th gear and hit 216km/h all in one gear? I know an Aston can. That tells me a lot about where their design criterias for that engine and drivetrain lies.

    wheelspins means ANY time the wheel loses traction, not just when the car is standing still. As long as the wheels are spinning at a rate higher than that which the ground is moving past, it is wheelspin.

    The technique for getting the best start from a standstill, is to get a microsecond of wheelspin, to overcome the car's inertia(inertia will be higher the heavier a car is, and the Aston is a damn heavy car). This requires a delicate touch on the throttle. Once inertia has been overcome, and traction found, u can just bury the pedal to the metal.

    As for traction control, like i said earlier, while that keeps wheelspin in check, the reality is that relying on it, wastes time, if u're trying to get the fastest times. The reality of the Aston's "unimpressive" (in your opinion) 0-60 stats is due to its weight. Have an Aston and an EVO at 50km/h and both accelerate hard in the right gear, and there will be no doubt who the winner is.
