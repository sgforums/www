    actually, it is generally more advisable to get a set of springs that matches the damping rate of the shock absorbers to prolong the lifespan of the whole suspension setup. having lowering and harder springs may give you the initially feel of better cornering, but it shortens the lifespan of your shocks drastically.

    imagine the stock shocks were meant for x mm of travel and you change to a springs which compresses the shocks to x - y mm. the additional stress will cause the shocks to fail/ leak prematurely and cause you even more money later on to replace it.

    a new set of suspension is a long term investment. choose it wisely and dun compromise on it.

    bunny, if ur dad finds the "springs" soft, the prob may not lie in the springs. in any suspension setup, the shock absorbers should be the ones doing the primary job of damping. the springs are there to "cushion" the oscillations the shocks go through (if any). if the car is "bottoming out" too much, it could be the shocks are not stiff enough to stop the car from doing so.

    changing to stiffer springs may alleviate this prob, but ultimately, u'll still want a stiffer pair of shocks to prevent the weight of the vehicle to rest entirely on your springs as this will cause your ride to become bumpy. remember, spring bounces, shocks do not.
