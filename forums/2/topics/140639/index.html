  • Gedanken
    6 Jul 05, 17:38
    Backpressure is the pressure from the gases that are left in the combustion chamber at the end of the exhaust stroke. Many engines are designed to take advantage of this backpressure, so that the residual gas and the gas taken in during the intake stroke contribute to the pressure in the chamber at the point of ignition.

    Extractors help exhaust gases flow out of the combustion chamber more freely, so that less gas remains in the chamber at the end of the exhaust stroke. In turn, there is less gas (and less pressure) at the point of ignition, which results in less force pushing the piston down.

    Extractors give you an advantage at higher revs, where it allows the exhaust gases to be pushed out faster by a faster-travelling piston. However, because of the lower compression at ignition, you'll experience less power per stroke, and without the advantage provided at higher revs, you'll find that the engine will be more sluggish at lower revs.

    Basically, you're left with only two corrective options at that point: either go back to the old exhaust, or modify your intake so that more gas (in the form of fuel-air mixture) is taken in with every stroke, thereby increasing the compression at the point of ignition.

    I haven't had any experience doing this with a fuel-injected engine, but with a carburetted engine, modifying the intake usually means getting a bigger carburettor. You can turbocharge or supercharge the engine, but that becomes a question of whether it's worth the cost and trouble.
