    6 Oct 06, 02:02
    Was involved in this incident a few days back. Was a little fed up with myself for being a little too guillbile. Thought I share my experience with people so that they have a clearer picture of what is actually going on in companies like this.

    I was called up by some fella asking me whether I'm interested in a job and all. Me, having nothing better to do, decided to meet up with the contact at Clarke Quay MRT the next day. I met up with him the next day at about 3 in the afternoon, where I was directed to this building somewhere nearby the station. He brings me to the highest floor, where he briefly introduces me to the company, the acheivements of his boss (there was a television show where he appeared on, talking about how he got back on his feet after repeated failures), and the products they sell.

    Ok. After the sight-seeing, he brings me a few floors down to this "activity room", where he brings me around to see further acheivements made by the company and staff (showing me stuff like photocopied pay cheques in 4-digit sums), and introduces me to some of the product distributors. In the next couple of hours, I'm given a briefing on how this supposed SLM (Single Level Marketing) "business concept" works and another on the kind of products they're selling. Up to this point, everything looked professional. The me then was compelled to join, as the profits they claimed were indeed rather attractive. It sure looked like a lucrative industry for people to make money, AND with relative ease, I might add.

    Went for a short break in the form of a late lunch at the nearby coffeeshop in between the briefings, accompanied by one of the staff, where I was told and shown (by the posh cars of the senior staff parked nearby) of more achievements made by the people in the line. I also supposedly managed to get my "doubts" clarified by him. The little chat that was held helped to "let my own guard down".. And having been won over by the warm hospitality of the people in the office, I decided to stay on and find out more.

    .. And that was what things started getting awry for me.

    Back later in the office, one of the SDMs persuaded me (now that I think of it, it's more of PSYCHO, not persuade) to get one of the products, some bed that makes use of magnetic therapy to improve blood circulation. Thinking back, he was rather smart in his choice of words and systematic approach to get me hooked on the deal. First he emphasizes on the aspect of fillal piety, using my grandmother as the reason to get the product. Then he focuses on the differences in profit a 1st-Level distributor gets in comparsion with a 2nd-Level one. I was idiotic enough to be completely convinced by the offer, and it wasn't long enough before he brought out an order form for me to sign. A few seconds later, I freaked out at the amount I saw - $1650. It was probably peanuts to him, but it definitely wasn't to an 18 year old.

    I tried to shove the deal aside, but was once again reassured by him, saying that he will pay the remaining of the cash first (I agreed to pay an initial deposit of $200). A few minutes later, I find myself being accompanied by a female staff who led me to draw money at one of the nearby ATMs. But, thankfully, as my pay for my previous job didn't go through yet, I was left with insufficient money to pay the deposit. Thus, I was told instead to pay a sum of $70 which was apparently for the distributor rights to DSAS. I blindly complied with the request.

    A little later, back in the office yet again, I find myself being shown around to the people all around, congratulating me on becoming a member of their "big family". I was introduced especially to many of the SDMs, those people who have made it, who gave me advice on "putting in the effort in the job to make it far" and "not telling others about my decision". After a brief chat with the guy who "closed" the deal with me, I was told to come back the next day at 1230 for a "group meeting" where I would be informed of what to do. I still remember him flipping through a magazine showing a series of posh cars before he left in a great hurry.

    "If he can do it, why not I?" I thought to myself.

    On the trip back though, I freaked out once again. The thought of starting a job which requred yourself having to break even quite a substantial amount was startling to me. I thought with much gloom about the long hours I had to endure, about the time I would have to sacrifice with my friends in order to clear the financial deficit I got myself in.

    Back home, having my suspicions getting the better of me, I decided to look online for any sources of information that could let me know better about the job and all. After a few moments of searching, I landed myself in one of the threads in Sgforums, and to my horror, I saw posts about all sort of negative things about the job itself. Exaggerated as many of the posts seemed to me, it still made sense and logic in return to the advice I was offered earlier. It was only until then that I realized I had been well and truly conned.

    I went to the DSAS site, hoping to understand the regulations. To my relief, I found a line which stated that there was a 7-day cooling off period where customers could cancel their orders from their sellers should there be no monetary or product transcation taking place, which had to be clearly printed in font 12 text on the product invoice. Well, at least it wasn't that bad now, knowing that I didn't have to pay for something that could actually be gotten at a much cheaper rate (as what I had discovered in the forums).

    So the next day comes. After some serious consideration, I decided to forgo the offer, thinking that I wouldn't have the time to commit myself to the job. I msg-ed the SDM thanking him for his time and effort, cancelling my order and requesting my $70 back. I was then later told to come down where he could talk with me over my decision, to which I gave him a meeting time and place. Strangely enough, he hasn't replied since then.

    I do not know whether he is busy or whatsoever, but from the way it seems, it does appear evident that it will be a challenge just to get my $70 back.

    While I admit I voluntarily agreed to all the things, there is definitely something with their business ethnics that I find very questionable. WHY THE BIG HURRY FOR PAYMENT? Hey, I just signed for something, and the next thing I know, I'm told to go down to get the money. I do not understand in any way why they are rushing customers to give them the cash.. Or more specifically in my case, why am I being rushed to supposedly "invest" in a company product where I've not even given time to consider whether I should go ahead with the job in the first place?

    In a way, the case is reflective of their sincerity - It gives the impression that they just want to close the deal quick, get the cash quick, then shake the customer off once he foots the entire payment and gets his product. I feel fortunate that my lack of funds saved me from making a potentially wrong choice.

    Another thing is that while I admit that there looked to be people who can make it big in this line, how many actually don't? Sure, it looks impressive with all the figures the SDMs are boosting to get each month, but how many actually are fortunate enough to get to this stage with all the effort they put in? In my opinion, many of the promises that the people claim are not quite what they are. I understand that the coporate world is a complex place - But using such a method to give youngsters a wrong mentality towards money-making as an easy thing to do.. I think that's rather underhand.

    I am not sore regarding the possible loss of $70.. I just feel the need to highlight that it takes more than just effort to climb the ladder. If you can go against your conscience and say that money is more important than anything else, then I'm afraid I can't say anything further.

    People interested in the line, please do seriously consider before you decide on anything.
