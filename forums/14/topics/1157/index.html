    I know this is often debated: whether it's useful, brings any new meaning to life, that kinda thing. I have always thought it was useful, i.e: it gave the schools and the teachers more inspiration to do their jobs better and it gave the student body some form of identity and pride.

    Yet, there was always the problem of getting overly-devoted with this ranking system, where the institution forgot about educating the students in the way appropriate, but rather by making them adhere to certain conditions which would make the school look better. Perhaps this is not often seen in secondary schools, but in junior colleges, it's prevalent.

    Most colleges make their students drop one subject that they are weak in not only to help them concentrate on the other more promising subjects, but to make their ranking higher. Colleges are ranked on 3 A Level subjects although most students begin with 4. I have heard stories from my friends in schools where the principal calls them in and asks them to drop the subject right after the first common test, where the student is just realising the difficulty level of the subject. Some schools never let the student decide on what they want, they encourage dropping so strongly that some students feel silly to hold on.

    Dropping subjects may seem like a good idea, I mean, who doesn't want more free periods, right? But the fact that the subject combinations was one that the student chose and probably can excel in if enough effort was put in is often ignored. By encouraging 3 A Levels and making it a norm of some kind, isn't the institution limiting the breadth of what the person knows and studies? Isn't the institution teaching students giving up is okay? Perseverance isn't realistic if you know you're gonna fail all the time?

    And why am I launching into this? Because my teacher came in today and made it clear to us that it was the students who are in the particular stream that I am are pulling down the percentile of students getting As. That it doesn't look nice (well at least, that's what it sounded like to me) and that if you are consistently getting Ds and Es, you should drop it now.

    Maybe I'm just in a bad mood and somehow disgusted with the way we are manipulated, made to believe what they want us to, to 'beautify' the school's overall results. In fact, I believe that schools cannot be solely judged on the ranking, but rather, the ranking of value-added. Some schools have always been somewhere at the top, simply because they receive the top students. That doesn't say that a school is an outstanding institution. In fact, it becomes a "constructed reality" where the best school remains the best only because the best can get in and that remains as the perception. Not that the teachers were fantastic or that the institution cultivated minds really well. It's all constructed reality.

    This is just a bit of cut-and-paste from a related piece of ranting I did a while back.

    Streaming is based on standardized testing, and standardized testing is an unreliable way of determining an individual's future learning potential. They measure what a person learns, and not how a person learns. Except for the most basic cases, the latter is always immeasureably more valuable than the former. One can only teach students to recognize their own abilities, not attempt to measure them externally.

    Because of the predorminance of streaming in Singaporean education, standardized testing is overemphasized. It no longer serves as only an evaluation tool, but instead becomes the centre of the act of teaching. The popular use of the "Ten-Year-Series" as a textbook testifies to this.

    Streaming also has the tendency to suppress the individual nature of education. It detaches the student from his or her own education by placing little control of his future in his own hands. One may argue that a student can choose to work hard or not, and that that is a choice in itself. The truth is that when streaming comes into the picture, things are made one-dimensional, and the different streams are ranked against each other and pegged to value judgements which become impossible to shake. Once a person, in spirit, starts learning for others and not for himself, he becomes incapable of self-evaluation, of self-criticism, and of independent thought. Creativity goes too.

    Lastly, the social environment created by streaming is unrealistic, as abilities in personal competition are emphasized over the ability to work as a team.

    It's also elitist, and students are encouraged to bond with others of the same 'intellect', instead of learning to accept society as a whole and learning to work with a range of people. The kind of Egalitarianism required for a Civil Society are thus destroyed.
    Groups with a common educational interest, formed during the course of official education, are also broken up during continuous remixing. The substantiated sense of belonging one can have to a group, by virtue of a common base of internally-defined logic, is destroyed here.
