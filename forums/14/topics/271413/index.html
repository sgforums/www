    GNC has a programme in which it encourages its customers to recommend its brand to their friends/relatives, in which if they do so, they will get bonus points in which they can redeem additional products.

    This is also an example of earning from participating in such a scheme.

    The primary aim of an MLM company is supposed to distribute its products and services, but due to human greed, many play up the promise of high income that is connected to the company's compensation plans to levels that may not be considered very ethical.

    To most laymen, a definition of an investment is to input a sum of money or capital into something in order to gain profitable returns, as interest, income, or appreciation in value.

    But what about sums of money used to purchase products and services (of value, not worthless), or when an income is generated only when work and effort is being applied? Would it still be called an investment? If a person spends a sum of money to open a business, develops it into a profitable SME and that sum of money is considered an investment, then yes, MLMs are also an investment of sorts.

    However the current definition of investment in SG is to place capital somewhere without purchasing retail products and services, and to expect returns for that capital alone.

    The line is thin but defined.

    By the way, the company has only 1 standardised way of disseminating its plans and operations. Anyone who deviates from the standard information risk charges of misrepresentation from the company and may face appropriate action. If fellow affliates choose to continue promoting information not approved by the company, they risk not only themselves but also the company's reputation.
