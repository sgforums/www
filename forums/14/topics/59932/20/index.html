    The New Paper - 06 Mar 2004

    THE OFF-BATAM CLUB THAT NEVER GOT BUILT

    First money, now people gone... like magic

    By Arul John

    HE eager civil servant thought it was a good deal.
    For around $3,800, Mr Edmund Chua, 28, could join a country club near Singapore with its own marina.

    As he dreamt of owning his own yacht someday, he thought the proposed PT Magic Kingdom Island Resort Paradise (PTP) would be perfect for him and his homemaker wife.

    But his dream club on an island near Batam never materialised.

    The developers, PT Magic Kingdom (PTM), dropped the project, he learnt last year.

    By then he had already paid more than $3,000 in instalments.

    He has little hope of getting his money back as the firm that promoted PTP is nearly bankrupt.

    HE DIDN'T TELL HIS WIFE

    Mr Chua's wife of three years does not know of his predicament and he is the sole breadwinner earning $3,000 a month.

    He did not tell her about the club, hoping to surprise her by taking her there one day.

    He learnt about the club in October 2001 at a roadshow in Suntec City. The club was to have its own golf course, shopping mall, sea-sports club and jackpot rooms.

    Besides the $3,800 membership fee, he only had to pay a monthly subscription of $15.

    In November 2001, he signed up as a member, paid $400 up front and had the balance deducted in monthly instalments of $170 payable to PTP through his credit card. Every month, he received receipts from NOP Investments (NOPI), the club's marketing agents, for the deductions.

    But last June, he checked his credit card statement and found no deductions made since April 2003.

    He called NOPI and was told the project had stopped development since May 2003. He was then given an appointment to meet one of NOPI's managers, Mr Bob Lim.

    He said: 'In June or July last year, I and about four other members met Mr Lim and his secretary, Ms Jenny Chan, at an office opposite Lau Pa Sat. He said the PTP project failed because of the poor world financial situation since Sep 11, 2001, the Bali bombings on Oct 12, 2002 and the Sars outbreak last year.

    'When another member asked why the members were not informed of these problems earlier, Mr Lim said NOPI did not do so for fear of a panic and because they wanted to wait for the audit results before making an official announcement.'

    When Mr Chua, who paid $3,120 for the club membership, called Mr Lim last November, he was told the audit was still on.

    NO MORE WORD FROM NOPI

    Mr Chua said he had called Mr Lim and Ms Chan at least 10 more times but could never get through.In late January, he went to the Consumers Association of Singapore (Case) for help to recover his money, but was told his chances were slim as NOPI had filed for insolvency.

    He was advised to make a claim through the Small Claims Tribunal (SCT), but has not yet done so because of his work schedule.

    He said one of the four other members went to see the police but was told it was a civil matter and referred to Case instead.

    Construction firm director Don Ang, in his 40s, would only say he had paid 'not much' for the club membership so far. A friend is trying to help him get his money back.

    Mr Chua still dreams of a place to berth his dream yacht and practise his golf swing.

    'But this time, I will make sure the club is up and running before I buy a membership.'

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------
    CLUB WOES

    About 1,000 Singaporeans paid about $3,800 each in 2001 to join the club, according to
    The Straits Times. The club was supposed to be built on Rempang, an Indonesian island linked to Batam by a bridge, and start operations last July.

    But NOP Investments (NOPI), the marketing agents appointed by the Indonesian developers, called off the project last May.

    A Case spokesman said seven investors had complained to them so far. He added it would be hard for them to get back their money since NOPI had filed for insolvency.

    When contacted by The New Paper, Mr Bob Lim of NOPI declined to comment.
