    It sounds like your dilemma is based on 2 factors: Happiness and Money. The combination of which results in the following 4 situations:

    1. Happy doing what you do, and making good money from it.
    2. Happy doing what you do, but not making good money.
    3. Unhappy doing what you do, but making good money from it.
    4. Unhappy doing what you do, and not making good money from it.

    Clearly (4) would be the least desirable situation and (1) would be the most desirable situation. You sound resigned to the fact that you are only faced with (2) and (3).

    What you have to realize is that regardless of your choice of disciplines, these 4 situations always exist. Granted, the probability distribution of these 4 are not equal for all disciplines which is why people concede that it is easier to succeed in certain disciplines than others.

    You should also realize despite the higher probability of success in some careers more than others, the probability of failure still exist. Which is why you should never back the guy at a casino who tells you "If you did Accountancy/Law/etc you're sure to make lots of money and live happily ever after!" or "If you did Liberal Arts/etc, you're sure to be poor and unhappy!"

    The only sure things in life are Death and Taxes. Anything else is subject to the laws of probability. What you can do is manipulate the odds through choices, effort and luck. Work smart in addition to working hard!

    I do suggest that whatever you decide to do, make sure that you always have options. Even at this point, it's hard for anyone to know for certain what they'll be doing a year from now, much less 5 or 10 years from now.

    Liberal Arts in itself is not a career. It is a discipline that prepares you for a variety of career paths. It is not inferior to any other discipline. Neither is Accountancy/Law/etc superior to any other. The right combination of all disciplines results in the synergy that is success. Similarly, Singapore was not built on the backs of accountants, lawyers, doctors or engineers alone.

    Decide what it is you can see yourself doing 10 years from now, then figure out what you need to do from now right up to that point to be able to get there successfully.

    Life is not a routine; it is an experience. To take a quote from an outstanding movie from Peter Weir (Dead Poet's Society) - "Seize the day. Make your lives extraordinary."

    Be the best you can be in whatever you do, benefit from the wisdom of those who have gone before you... And as Mark Twain so aptly put it, "Above all else, to thy own self be true."

    Good luck... you have your whole life ahead of you!
