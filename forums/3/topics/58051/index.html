    Semi Final Fixtures
    -------------------

    Dec 6th 94: Singapore v Selangor
    Pahang v Kedah

    Dec 10th 94: Selangor v Singapore
    Kedah v Pahang

    Singapore beat Pahang 4-0 in the Final on 17th Dec 94 at the Shah Alam
    Stadium in front of a full house of 81000 fans. The score was 1-0 at half
    time. Abbas Saad, Singapore's Australian import player, scored a hat
    trick, while Singapore's own soccer legend Fandi Ahmad netted the fourth.

    It was a rather one-sided match, the Pahang side was depleted with the
    absence of sweeper Alan Davidson, midfielders Dollah Salleh and Zulhamizan
    Zakaria were out due to injury and suspension respectively. Pahang's
    young players were overawed by the occasion. So was their custodian, who
    is Malaysia's 1st choice goalie. His performance was below par. I guess
    it's because that night was the first time the team played in front of
    such a huge crowd, more than two-thirds of whom were Singapore fans.

    The fans themselves were sporting...most of the time. Except for the
    times when the ref's decision was against Singapore, every such decision
    would be greeted with a vulgar 5-letter Malay word. The air horn would
    hoot 9 times and the fans would shout the word in a disgusting fashion.
    They had no respect for the FAM President, the Sultan of Pahang and the
    Crown Prince of Pahang. Also present were Malaysian ministers. I've been
    to other matches involving other teams. I have to admit, that, while the
    Singapore fans are the most disciplined in the region, they are also the
    most vulgar.

    Where's my jersey?

    By Ryan Lim

    The S-League, Singapore's very own domestic league has been around since 1996. It has also become a good ground for Jan Poulsen to look for potential talent for the National Team.

    A lot have been done in the past six years to put Singapore into the path of reaching the World Cup in 2010. There are now full-time players, 12 professional S-League clubs run by full-time staff and fan clubs and clubhouses.

    Kudos to the FAS (Football Association of Singapore) for promoting the local football scene and letting the good old memories of the Malaysia Cup become but a part of history.

    However, there is still one very important 'element' that is missing to make sure the game gets the full support of Singaporeans and Singaporeans at heart. The Lions jersey is one integral part of football but is yet overlooked by the FAS.

    Every local football fan who turned out at the National stadium used to don the Lions jersey during the 1994 season when the Lions were crowned Malaysia Cup Champions beating Pahang 4-0 at the Shah Alam Stadium.

    Sad to say, it is missing now and many people don't even know where to get their hands on one.

    A month ago, a German friend of mine came over to Singapore for a holiday. I took him to the National Stadium to watch Team Singapore play Uruguay in a friendly match on the 20th of May. He noticed a 'sea of reds' in the half-filled stands and thought that those fans were 'die-hard' Singapore fans donning the proud jersey of the home team.

    On closer look, the 'sea of reds' was actually the jerseys of English Premier League teams of Arsenal, Manchester United and Liverpool! It was a rare sight to see someone wearing the Singapore National team jersey except for one fan who was wearing the 1994 Malaysia Cup winning team jersey with the number "17" on his back.

    I was dumbfounded for an answer when my pal asked me why Singaporeans preferred wearing the jerseys of English clubs rather than their local teams . Two days before he return to Germany, I told him that I would get him a Lions jersey as a parting gift.

    The next day, I went all over the island and could not find a single shop that sold the Lions jersey. I began to panic and luckily, I managed to finally find one shop that sold the Lions jersey.

    When I entered that particular shop, it was crowded with football fans buying jerseys to support their teams during the World Cup. The shop owner greeted me with a smile and said "Italy? Argentina? England? I also have Korean jerseys!" When I asked for the Singapore National Team jersey, he stared at me in disbelief. The customers in the shop start to giggle and pass some annoying remarks. I felt so humiliated but waited for the shop owner to show me the jersey (of my choice).

    The Lions jersey wasn't even on the rack, it was in his storeroom. When he returned, the shop owner said with a hint of sarcasm, "Here you go, only $40. The cheapest jersey around, Thank you for being the first customer to buy it!". Needless to say, the others customers were amused by my patriotism. By the way, I also did not notice any Geylang United, Jurong or Balestier jerseys around the shop.

    My German pal accepted the jersey with a lot of appreciation especially after a torrid time looking for it. But he rather curious about the brand the jersey carried, and asked me whether the logo 'TIGER' was a local sports brand. I was rather taken aback by his question remained speechless for a while. After a long explanation he understood the whole theory behind the "Tiger Issue".

    A lot have been done to promote local football. The FAS is bringing in teams like Liverpool, Manchester United and now, Uruguay.

    What that is truly lacking is the 'sea of reds' that has suddenly vanished since the "Malaysia Cup days".

    To those who own the Singapore team jersey, I'm proud of you. I also would like to thank the guy who was wearing the 1994 jersey with 'Fandi' and that number 17 on his back during the Uruguay match. Hopefully, the 'sea of red' will return to the National Stadium and most importantly, the jerseys are made more easily available. Lastly, I would like to thank 'Tiger Beer' for being the sponsor of the national team.

    By the way, I did tell my German pal that you are a beer company and not a sports brand.
