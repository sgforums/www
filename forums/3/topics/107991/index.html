    Hi!

    I've never won any sprinting contests and could never run long distances for nuts. So here are my amatuerish comments :

    Long distance running is aerobic (cardio) and good for overall endurance (for heart and lungs). It is the most effective way I know to lose weight. Look at it this way....I've seen fat aerobics instructors, bodybuilders, sportspeople, etc etc....but I've never seen a fat long-distance runner!

    I feel that sprinting is more anaerobic than aerobic. It involves sudden hard muscle usage. Doesn't burn much fats. To put it really really simply, sprinting stamina is different from long-distance running stamina.

    To tone up muscles (eg. legs), there are many things you can do. I seriously don't think long distance running and casual sprinting can build muscles at all (unless you are a professional sprinter who sprints many times everyday). Some weights (with someone guiding you) can help tone up leg muscles (or any other muscles) too. It should be very light weights with high reps. Professionals always recommend working out a few ways at the same time (running, walking, weights, etc.).....instead of relying on just one way.

    To improve sprinting (or any other sport), many things we can done. With modern sports science, we now can use weights, machines and other stuff to build up/condition the necessary muscles. But it is VERY important to realise that we cannot ignore the actual exercise itself - thus, the only true way to improve in sprinting is to sprint more!

    After all, sports is not only about muscles...its about heart, lungs and MIND. No doubt your heart and lungs need to move fast enough (without being to stressed) to support your sprinting. But your mind must not be too nervous during the event, like its the first time seeing the track, feeling anxiety from the crowd's eyes/attention, etc. So simulating the real thing helps - practice sprinting on the track itself, if possible, when there are people around, looking at you. I feel that its useful to get used to the feeling.

    I guess this is what people mean when they say that confidence is half the battle won. Even if your body is physicall prepared....but you are not mentally ready to face the crowd/track....its no use. Under pressure and anxiety, your performance will be different from your quiet, solo practice sessions.

    Hope I wasn't talking rubbish all the way.....

    Er.....I think we have to remember that sprinting also involves muscles of the whole body (eg. arm and shoulder muscles for propelling the legs, tummy muscles need to be tensed, etc.).

    Its very different from a slow, long run (which works mainly the legs, lungs and heart)...and the rest of the body is much more relaxed than during a sprint. For this reason, I feel that long distance running may have its limitations in improving sprinting abilities.

    I suppose that any resistance to movement will create further strength. So perhaps the purpose of tying weights around the waists was to "weigh down" the person to train the legs to move something heavier.

    Since running up a slope is also adding resistance by "weighing one down" (instead of using added weights around the waist, the runner is now using gravity). So I guess it does help in sprinting abilities.

    Actually, I would rather recommend running upslope than tying weights around the waist (because if you have weights around your waist and fall/twist your body wrongly....you may get injuries like slipped discs, etc. Also, that rope around your waist would probably cause some rope burns, or at the least, scrape/tear some shirts).

    Hey, of course there is an upper limit! Or else with sufficient training, we'd all be able to run 2.4 km in one second, which we know is impossible! The limitations are natural - we are two legged creatures, our muscles can only contract so much/fast, etc. It also depends on body condition....a body which is conditioned to be fast can move faster. And there are some people who are just born with fast muscles/body types too.

    another e.g. is distance - is there an upper limit to how far a person can run, such that beyond that no matter how hard he trains, he wont be able to run any further perhaps without injuring himself, etc?
    or does this limit not exist at all, and its all a matter of training?

    Um....you haven't been watching too much Forest Gump, have you?

    Again, of course there is a limit. To put it simply....when we move our bodies, we burn the energies stored inside us. When this spare energy completely runs out, we die (like a car which has no more fuel). At the same time, we also lose fluids, etc through sweat. We need to replenish all these losses in energy and fluids, etc.

    Don't forget that you also need to eat, drink, sleep, go to the toilet, socialise, love, etc. These are all necessary in life! So even if your body can run forever, your mind will be so unhappy it will boycott and stop your body.

    how should one deal with general knee pains and discomforts from frequent running? strangely, i do not usually experience pains in the knee while running, but only during normal walking and swimming. can such discomfort be safely ignored?

    Good grief, NO!!! Knee pains (or funny "lose" feelings) are a sign that there is something very wrong and if you have them, please stop till the pain goes completely away....then rest a while more and GRADUALLY start up again. May take days or weeks, depending. Meanwhile, you can swim (no stress on knees), as an alternative exercise. You may want to know that its often not possible to fully repair a damaged knee, so you'd want to take care of it. Some problems will show up only in your middle-to-old age. If you're young, you may thing that its far away...but I promise you, the time WILL come. And you will regret.

    Knee pains can be caused by many things - from simple to complicated. Eg. bad shoes, running on overly hard surfaces, foot pronation (how your foot aligns with the horizontal), or just simply overdoing it, etc. If necessary, you may want to consult an expert/doctor.

    For more information on pronation, please see here http://www.steenwyk.com/pronsup.htm and here http://www.drfoot.co.uk/pronation.htm
