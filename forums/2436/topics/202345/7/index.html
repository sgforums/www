    In Rabbinical Literature:

    The name of Ishmael is an allusion to God's promise to hear the complaints of Israel whenever it suffered at the hands of Ishmael (Gen. R. xlv. 11). Abraham endeavored to bring up Ishmael in righteousness; to train him in the laws of hospitality Abraham gave him the calf to prepare (Gen. R. xlviii. 14; comp. Gen. xviii. 7). But according to divine prediction Ishmael remained a savage. The ambiguous expression in Gen. xxi. 9 (see Hagar) is interpreted by some rabbis as meaning that Ishmael had been idolatrous; by others, that he had turned his bow against Isaac. According to the interpretation of Simeon b. Yoḥai, Ishmael mocked those who maintained that Isaac would be Abraham's chief heir, and said that as he (Ishmael) was the first-born son he would receive two-thirds of the inheritance (Tosef., Sotah, v. 12, vi. 6; Pirḳe R. El. xxx.; Gen. R. liii. 15). Upon seeing the danger to Isaac, Sarah, who had till then been attached to Ishmael (Josephus, "Ant." i. 12, Â§ 3), insisted that Abraham cast out Ishmael. Abraham was obliged to put him on Hagar's shoulders, because he fell sick under the spell of the evil eye cast upon him by Sarah (Gen. R. liii. 17).

    Ishmael, left under a shrub by his despairing mother, prayed to God to take his soul and not permit him to suffer the torments of a slow death (comp. Targ. pseudo-Jonathan to Gen. xxi. 15). God then commanded the angel to show Hagar the well which was created on Friday in the week of Creation, in the twilight (comp. Ab. v. 6), and which afterward accompanied the Israelites in the wilderness (Pirḳe R. El. xxx.). But this was protested against by the angels, who said: "Why should Ishmael have water, since his descendants will destroy the Israelites by thirst?" (comp. Yer. Ta'an. iv. 8; Lam. R. ii. 2). God replied: "But now he is innocent, and I judge him according to what he is now" (Pirḳe R. El. l.c.; Gen. R. l.c.; et al.). Ishmael married a Moabitess named 'Adishah or 'Aishah (variants "'Ashiyah" and "'Aifah," Arabic names; Targ. pseudo-Jonathan to Gen. xxi. 21; Pirḳe R. El. l.c.); or, according to "Sefer ha-Yashar" (Wayera), an Egyptian named Meribah or Merisah. He had four sons and one daughter. Ishmael meanwhile grew so skilful in archery that he became the master of all the bowmen (Targ. pseudo-Jonathan to Gen. xxi. 20; Gen.R. liii. 20). Afterward Abraham went to see Ishmael, and, according to his promise to Sarah, stopped at his son's tent without alighting from his camel. Ishmael was not within; his wife refused Abraham food, and beat her children and cursed her husband within Abraham's hearing. Abraham thereupon asked her to tell Ishmael when he returned that an old man had asked that he change the peg of the tent. Ishmael understood that it was his father, took the hint, and drove away his wife. He then married another woman, named Fatimah (Pekimah; Targ. pseudo-Jonathan l.c.), who, when three years later Abraham came again to see his son, received him kindly; therefore Abraham asked her to tell Ishmael that the peg was good.

    Ishmael then went to Canaan and settled with his father (Pirḳe R. El. l.c.; "Sefer ha-Yashar," l.c.). This statement agrees with that of Baba Batra (16a)Â—that Ishmael became a penitent during the lifetime of Abraham. He who sees Ishmael in a dream will have his prayer answered by God (Ber. 56a).

    Scholarship: The Significance of the small silent aleph
    Clifford Denton

    (This article was written in April 1991 and resulted in the launch of the Journal Tishrei. The message is still relevant today.)

    One of the pleasures I have each week is to turn to the last but one page of the Jerusalem Post International Edition. Just to have this weekly communication from Jerusalem seems hard to believe. Is this the same Jerusalem that I read about in the Bible?

    That is not a straightforward question to answer of course, but for one, like me, who has had his eyes opened wide to the significance of the modern day Jerusalem, there is a very special time of reflection each week, as I can hold this newspaper in one hand and the Bible in the other.

    However, it is on that last but one page that I wish to focus. To turn to the last but one page of most of our newspapers would be quite common for a lot of people. There is a little light relief in reading the sport's page before turning to the international news towards the front of the paper. In the Jerusalem Post International Edition it is not so. The attraction there is a half page called 'Shabbat Shalom'. This is where a meditative message is given for the Jewish Shabbat by one of the Rabbis. It is usually in the form of a message from the Torah. I have often been given some new insight on a passage of scripture which is as much a part of our Bible as it is the Torah. There is sometimes a little Jewishness to filter out, or a wider perspective to take on a passage, when it is related to the New Testament as well as the Old Testament. However, one has to confess that the Rabbis have something to teach us.

    On 16th of March this year Rabbi Riskin, Dean of the Ohr Torah institutions and chief Rabbi of Efrat set me thinking again about the need for Christian scholarship in our generation through his analysis of one word in the first chapter of Leviticus.

    The Rabbi quoted from the Israeli poet Nahman Bialik, who said that studying a Bible translation is like kissing a woman through a veil. He was saying that even the 'best' of translations, including the King James' version, is bound to miss the full impact of some of the words whose meaning is only conveyed by the original Hebrew.

    Scolarship part2

    In the first chapter of Leviticus, an English translation records an incident as follows: 'And the Lord called unto Moses and spoke to him out of the tabernacle of the congregation, saying .... '. The Rabbi then goes on to say that a child reading this from the Hebrew will have greater understanding than 'even a learned but Hebrewless cardinal'. The reason is as follows.

    The Hebrew word vayikra has the basic translation 'and the Lord called'. As the Rabbi states, 'the last letter of the Hebrew word vayikra, the silent aleph, is enjoined by the scribes to be written in an uncharacteristically tiny script, our tradition to this day.'

    The Rabbi goes on to say, 'No matter how exquisite King James' English may be, a little aleph cannot be translated; it must be experienced. Indeed, any letter written smaller than others takes on significance, but when it is an aleph, the first letter of the Hebrew alphabet, its smallness looms even larger.'
