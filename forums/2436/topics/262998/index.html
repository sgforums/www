    The Power of Love
    By Rabbi Shlomo Jarcaig

    The Shabbos (Sabbath) between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur is called Shabbos Shuva (return). This Shabbos offers us a unique opportunity to focus on teshuva (repentance) in a way we cannot during the rest of the week. What are the mechanics of teshuva?

    Our Sages teach us that when one does teshuva out of fear of G-d, then the sins he transgressed intentionally are viewed as if they were committed accidentally, but if one repents from a love of G-d, then his previous sins become transformed to merits for him. How does this work? One of the greatest manifestations of G-d's love for His children is His desire to accept our repentance and overlook our wrongdoing. But why does He view them in a positive light afterwards and why is this perspective unique to when the teshuva was done out of ahava (love)?

    Michtav Me'Eliyahu (collected writings and discourses of Rabbi Eliyahu Dessler (1891-1954) of London and B'nai Brak, one of the outstanding personalities and thinkers of the Mussar movement) explains that one of the main components of teshuva is remorse. When a person genuinely regrets his past deeds and repents out of fear, he acquires a new perspective of life: he stops to think about the greatness and awesomeness of G-d and the impropriety of violating His will. As this perspective develops and his outlook changes, he will now view his past transgression as accidental. He simply did not grasp the ramifications of his actions; had he, he would have never done them.

    But teshuva out of love is different. True love is about giving. By definition, we cannot give anything to G-d, but the closest thing we can do is appreciate and express our gratitude for all He does for us. When someone develops his appreciation for the vastness of the array of G-d's constant giving - from the dozen involuntary breaths he takes every minute of every hour of every day to the breathtaking sunrise he witnessed this morning - he fortifies his love relationship with his Creator. Thus, when he contemplates his past misdeeds and realizes that, despite them, G-d continues His abundant benevolence, his gratitude is multiplied. The repentance borne of this sentiment is much more profound and builds an even greater bond. The development of this greater love is a result of the misdeed, so his repentance actually turned those misdeeds into something positive in generating a closer relationship with G-d.

    Shabbos is designed specifically to allow us time each week to appreciate G-d and what He does for us. There is a specific mitzvah (commandment) to eat tasty foods that we do not have throughout the rest of the week to allow us to focus on His magnificent benevolence. Capitalizing on these opportunities to develop our consciousness of G-d's love for us will facilitate our return to Him out of love. Thus, Shabbos Shuva offers a unique prospect for an elevated sense of gratitude to turn our misdeeds into merits.

    This is a special Shabbat day... it is also the day of Yom Kippur...

    The Most Holy Day

    Yom Kippur, or The Day of Atonement has been known through the centuries as the holiest day on the Hebrew calendar. It is a day scripture tells us to have a complete day of rest and also a day to deny ourselves. What is the meaning of this special day?

    In Leviticus, we read of a special sacrifice Aaron was to offer on this day. It was an offering of atonement for all of Israel, corporate and individual. As we understand from the book of Hebrews though, the blood of bulls and goats could not take away sin. This offering above all other offerings was looking forward to the ultimate sacrifice of Yeshua, our Messiah. Today we look back to His redemptive work in our lives, but is this all we are to do on this day, look back and remember?

    Yom Kippur is a day each of us should approach with great reverence. It is a day to not only consider and appreciate our redemption in Yeshua. It is a day to examine our lives and our relations with others.

    First we are to examine our own lives. Have we allowed the unclean to slip back into our lives or our homes? Have we in the past year taken the word of HaShem seriously? Have we lived by conviction, or by convenience? Have we, by our disobedience, trampled the shed blood of Messiah under foot? On this day, we should ask HaShem to examine our lives, our attitudes toward Him and to reveal the sin which we have neglected or attempted to hide from view.

    We then examine our relationships on this earth. Yeshua said if we have wronged our fellow man, we are to leave our gift at the altar and go make it right before continuing to present ourselves to Him. This tells us that HaShem is just as concerned about our horizontal relationships with others as He is about our vertical relationship with Him.

    In churches and congregations today there are many people who may have things right with their God, but are walking in division, strife and hurt with their fellow man. There are marriages and relationships with parents, spouses, family and friends which need desperately to be healed. Yom Kippur should be a day of reaching out in repentance to each other as well as to our God.

    It is taught that Yom Kippur is also a day which will have a final fulfillment just prior to the Millennial Kingdom. It is the day in which all people will stand before HaShem to give an account for their lives. In that day, there will be no excuses and nothing hidden from view. The light of His presence will reveal all.

    Today, we have the option to accept His sacrifice for our lives, turn from our sin and receive forgiveness by the blood of The Lamb. We can be restored to our relationship with our Creator as well as seek restoration with those around us. We can also choose to reject Him, continue in our sin and stand on the final Yom Kippur with our sin in hand to be judged and rightfully sentenced to an eternity without Him. The choice is ours.

    Let us approach this most holy day with reverence, if not with fear and trembling. It is a day to deny ourselves the pleasures of life and ask that our lives would be opened before Him for examination. It is a day to go through the closets of our lives, both spiritually and naturally, asking for a detailed cleaning to occur.

    I challenge you today to take your scripture, a notepad and paper, and spend some time alone on Yom Kippur. Depending on your method of counting the days it will either be this Shabbat or it will be the first day of the week. Take these items, find a quiet place, tell your family you do not want to be disturbed for any reason and spend time with HaShem. Ask Him to reveal your life as He sees it. Write down what He reveals and then act on the revelation. We may find that in the midst of the time there will be phone calls to make or people to see as He works on not only our vertical relationship, but also on our horizontal relationships.

    Remember, the Feast of Tabernacles is coming soon. The joy we experience during that Feast may directly tie to our reverence and seriousness on Yom Kippur.

    Shabbat Shalom,
