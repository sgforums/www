    New performance standards set for trains

    Commuters can now expect to wait no more than 2 to 3 minutes for trains during peak hours under new performance standards set by the Land Transport Authority.

    LTA has included a new indicator called train headway, or interval between trains, as part of revisions to the operating performance standards or OPS.

    With the revision, operators will have to ensure that the intervals between trains during lunchtime as well as the morning and evening peaks, do not exceed the new OPS.

    Transport Minister Raymond Lim shared these details after visiting the Boon Lay Extension MRT line.

    "The OPS standard is to give us a benchmark so that across all services they are not assessed across the benchmark so that everybody know this is it you can't go below that particular minimum that you have set."

    Another revision to the standards - the tightening of the train passenger load from 1700 passengers per train down to 1600 passengers.

    This indicator sets the maximum number of passengers each train can carry.

    CEO of SMRT Saw Phaik Hwa says says they have currently met both standards in their operations.

    "The OPS as stated is what we are doing today and we already provide this level of service. As you know during peak we are already doing the fastest possible in headway wise, so now we have already done the best and this is very much within the headway wise."

    The revised standards will take effect today. --938Live

    Well... the refurbished C151 trains are not that bad - it's the same layout as the C751B trains, and they removed two seats per row mainly to accomodate wider seats and more standing space around the doors - the latter being a place where many people like to stand around, so having more space there could at least mean that people who like standing beside the doors have less of a chance to block the doorways.

    Though, that said, having more space around the doors could also unintentionally encourage people to crowd around the area more, a problem the new layout on the refurbished C151 trains is trying to solve; this could be rather counter-intuitive. In that case, 8 or 9 wider seats per row and absolutely no standing room around the door could have helped encourage passengers to move inward. Another possibly could be to split up the row of seats into two, with space in between deeper into the interior of the train car - that may also help encourage people to move in.

    That said, at least it's 7 seats per row right now - the C651 trains that participated in the experimental layout program (the blue cars) were slightly even worse - 6 seats per row.
