    The 2004 Grand Prix of Bahrain marked the first ever round of the FIA Formula One World Championship to be held in the Middle East and the official culmination of a multi-million dollar project started back in September 2002 when the Kingdom of Bahrain signed a long-term deal to host the event.

    Located at Sakhir, 30 km south-west of the island's capital, Manama, the Herman Tilke designed circuit contains no less than five track layouts within one complex. Construction began in November 2002 and in the months prior to its March 2004 completion, work was going on around the clock. Over 12,000 tonnes of stone were used in the build, a third of it Welsh granite, chosen for the track surface due to its excellent adhesive qualities.

    The 5.411 km Grand Prix circuit was designed with the spectator in mind, with 50,000 grandstand seats, all providing excellent views. Those spectators (a total of 100,000 over a race weekend) get to see the cars heading into the external desert area, before coming back into the oasis-styled infield. Up to 500 journalists can also witness the action from the venue's purpose-built media centre.

    The track offers the driver a unique experience in the way the circuit's width varies at the end of the different straights. This allows for diverse racing lines, and the 12-corner design provides at least three genuine overtaking opportunities.

    * The first Bahrain Grand Prix in 2004 was Formula 1Â’s first visit to the Middle East. Since then Middle Eastern firms' involvement in F1 has grown substantially.

    * The Bahrain International Circuit in Sakhir was designed by F1's circuit designer Hermann Tilke, with the Government of the Kingdom of Bahrain underwriting the cost of the development which cost around Â£83 million to build.

    * The state-of-the-art facility was built on empty desert land (formerly the site of a camel farm) in the space of 16 months.

    * At a peak, more than 3,000 workers were involved in the construction of the circuit, with a total of 8,265,000 man hours worked.

    * The 3.366-mile circuit places huge braking demands on the cars due to the proliferation of fast straights connecting tight hairpins. Even so, the average speed is some 135mph.

    * Sakhir features 15 corners, nine right and six left, with four straights and race distance comprises 57 laps.

    * The kingdom of Bahrain is an archipelago of 33 low-lying islands located in the Arabian Gulf off the eastern shore of Saudi Arabia. The land area is just 440 square miles.

    * The largest of these islands is home to the capital city, Manama, and most of the countryÂ’s 650,000 population.

    * Bahrain was a British protectorate until 1968, when the late king oversaw a smooth transition to independence. It is one of the most liberal and democratic of the Gulf states.

    * For most of its history Bahrain has been a sea-faring country but in the 1930s it first discovered the potential of what has become its most valuable resource: oil.

    * Tourism is a fast-growing industry Â– the country welcomes more than three million visitors per year.

    * It is also the financial hub of the Middle East and boasts the regionÂ’s largest number of banks and financial institutions.

    * Islam is practiced by 85 percent of the population. However, freedom of religion is granted to other faiths including Hinduism, Judaism, Buddhism and Christianity.

    * Falconry and horse racing are BahrainÂ’s most popular sporting pastimes.

    * Michael Schumacher completed a hat-trick at the circuit's first grand prix in 2004, taking pole, race victory and fastest lap. Rubens Barrichello secured a 1-2 for Ferrari, with Jenson Button taking the final podium place.

    * Fernando Alonso swept to pole and victory for Renault in 2005, although the standout performance of the day came from McLaren test driver Pedro de la Rosa who set the fastest lap.     * Bahrain 2005 was also the place where one of the sport's most astonishing sequences came to an end. While chasing Alonso for the lead on lap on lap 14, Michael Schumacher's brand new Ferrari F2005 broke down with a hydraulics failure. It was the first time since Germany 2001 - some 59 races before - that the German had retired with a mechanical failure.

    * Last year Bahrain became the first country other than Australia for 11 years to host F1's season-opening race.

The German took pole ahead of new team-mate Felipe Massa, but Alonso jumped the German at the final round of pit stops to take an important victory.

    * Kimi Raikkonen has finished on the bottom step of the podium in each of the past two years.

    HISTORIC PODIUM FOR HAMILTON

    Lewis Hamilton ensured his place in Formula One history after claiming his third successive podium finish in the Bahrain Grand Prix on Sunday - then warned his rivals that McLaren are going from strength to strength.

    The young Briton writes a new chapter with every race this season and his second-place finish on the Sakhir circuit means he becomes the first driver to climb the podium in his first three races.

    It was also perhaps the closest he has come yet to a maiden victory but in the end Hamilton ran out of laps as Felipe Massa defeated the 22-year-old by 2.3 seconds.

    The Brazilian bounced back in style from his disappointment in last week's Malaysia Grand Prix where he let slip his place at the front of the grid to finish fifth.

    Ferrari team-mate Kimi Raikkonen finished third, BMW Sauber's Nick Heidfeld claimed fourth while two-time world champion Fernando Alonso took fifth place.

    It means Hamilton is now joint top of the drivers' championship, alongside Alonso and Raikkonen.

    "It is fantastic to be joint points with Fernando and Kimi," he said. "I think the team have done a fantastic job.

    "We had very good pace this weekend, possibly the pace to be able to be on pole but Felipe did a fantastic job in qualifying.

    "In the race, usually the Ferraris pull away but we generally had similar pace if not quicker.

    "We have made fantastic steps forward and I am really happy for the team and I know this next race coming up is going to be really, really good.

    "We are going to be working extremely hard to make sure we make some more steps forward so we can go to Barcelona even quicker."

    Hamilton enjoyed yet another race in which he made few mistakes, although he did struggle to keep the pace on Massa after making his first pit stop.

    A third stint in the pit lane rectified that as Hamilton changed to hard tyres and started to make up ground on pole.

    "I didn't think I made many mistakes that cost me that much," he continued. " I struggled a bit on the new tyres, the balance was not very good and I locked up a couple of times but that was it. We got back onto the hard tyre and we were able to keep up the pace.

    "It was definitely good for experience not having to defend my position and to have the pace to put pressure on Felipe.

    "It is great we had pace, especially in the first bit where we were able to keep with him and I think for sure if we were in front we would have pulled away."

    Massa, meanwhile, insisted it is still too early to say who is the number one driver for Ferrari with both he and Raikkonen having now scored successes this season.

    He said: "Every result is important for me, Kimi and for the team and so I think there will not be any decision until the championship is almost done.

    "The team has to work very hard to put both cars in front of McLaren which is quite difficult and our target is the championship.

    "We need to keep pushing very hard as a team, trying to improve everything to get even better."

    Hamilton was the only Briton to finish the race - the three other representatives retired.

    Jenson Button suffered disappointment in his Honda once again when his race came to an end only minutes after climbing into his car when Toro Rosso driver Scott Speed collided with the 27-year-old.

    David Coulthard was once again unable to complete a race but it was extremely disappointing for the Red Bull driver as he had climbed from 21st on grid up to seventh before flames under the car brought an end to his race midway through.

    Super Aguri's Anthony Davidson also retired late on with smoke pouring from the back of his car.
