    Here's what Steve Matchett has had to say in 2004's "The Chariot Makers" about communications between drivers and engineers. Just in case you don't know about Matchett, he's the technical editor of F1 Racing magazine and a former Benetton mechanic.

    How often have you seen the driver lift his hand out of the cockpit and perform a little mime while talking to his engineer over the radio? It happens all the time, they all do it. The driver, gloved hand held parallel to the ground, uses it to indicate how the car is reacting over the bumps, dips and corners of the circuit; stabbing his fingers forward to indicate the front of the car, swinging his wrist side to side to explain how the rear of the car is working.

    These actions are always accompanied by a stampede of vocal explanations too, thoughts stream from the driver like water bursting from a dam, his brain still computating quick enough to handle decision-making where information is arriving at speeds in excess of a hundred metres per second. The more urgent his hand signals, the wider the driver's eyes, the worse the car is handling. You don't need to hear the conversation, his body language speaks volumes.

    While the driver is in full flow, gesticulating and downloading his impressions of the car, his patient, long-suffering engineer will be watching, listening, nodding, scribbling notes, translating jabbed fingers and wide eyes into a potential set-up changes to camber, ride-height, suspension and front wing. But not only that, the engineer will also be working to keep his driver's confidence on a high; keep him motivated, keep things moving forward. Lose the driver's confidence in the car (its predictable handling and its ability to only improve) and the whole house of cards collapses.
    This passage was written two years ago, not twenty. What does it say about the communications between the driver and the engineer?

    1) The driver has to describe how the car is handling at various parts of the track - as Matchett describes, "the bumps, dips and corners of the circuit".
    2) The engineer has to listen to what the driver says about how th car is handling.
    3) The engineer also has to keep the driver motivated and confident.

    So how's any of the above changed with technology? If a car's understeering at a corner, the driver would have said it twenty years ago as he would have today, and the engineer would have taken notes back then as he does today.

    Now Matchett talks about "camber, ride-height, suspension and front wing", all of which have been around for a lot longer than twenty years, but I won't go into that - the key issue here is that his job is to translate the driver's feedback into changes in the car's setup.

    Sure, these days the mechanic may have to turn the screw up 180 degrees instead of 360 degrees to get the same increase in downforce, but between the driver and the engineer, what has changed? The driver says the car's understeering, the engineer works out that the front wing anlge needs to be increased, and the mechanics receive instructions to make the adjustment before sending the driver out again.

    So what happens when a driver and engineer start working together? If the car's understeering and the driver says so, why should the engineer understand that feedback any differently? Does any new technology change that?

Driver aids don't perform any fundamentally different function from suspension or aerodynamics - they all work to keep the car stuck to the ground as firmly as possible. Race strategy still comes down to deciding when to come in and how hard to push at any given point of the race - pre-1994 it was a case of working out how much fuel was left in the car and how the weight affected tyre wear. Post 1994 it was a case of how much fuel was left in the car and when to come in for a top-up. Either way, the drivers and the engineers would to some extent have a say on when to come in, and the driver could receive the instruction to come in on a given lap and choose to either comply or stay out if he saw some advantage to that. Regardless of the rules, that hasn't changed.
