    In 988 the figure of Jesus on a crucifix mysteriously began to weep in the abbey of Pucelles at Orleans, France. No one was surprised when soon afterwards a catastrophic fire devastated the city. The weeping crucifix had been seen as a forewarning of disaster.

    Weeping and bleeding images are among the most frequently reported miracles. A recent case occurred in Siracusa, Sicily, where a small image of the Virgin Mary had been given to two newlyweds in the spring of 1953. On 29 August, while pregnant and very ill, the wife was praying before the statue when she saw tears trickling down its face. The woman made a miraculous recovery. The weeping lasted four days, and a delegation of police, doctors and priests witnessed the miracle. It was recognised by the Church in December, and today a special chapel houses Our Lady of the Tears.

    Such phenomena are by no means confined to traditionally Catholic countries. At Akita, Japan, a statue of the Virgin began to bleed in June 1973. It also perspired, giving off a scent of lilies and roses and later was seen weeping. This happened 101 times, until it stopped in 1981. Four years later an image was seen to weep at Naju, Korea. The owners of both statues were sick women, and the phenomena were deemed expressions of sympathy by the Virgin Mary.

    Some people are known to have faked such miracles. In 1967 London faith healer Alfred Bolton admitted his bleeding crucifix contained strategically placed pieces of raw meat. In 1986 US physicist Shawn Carlson, intrigued by reports that a weeping statue had drawn over 300,000 visitors to a Chicago church, set out to create a fake. At a 1987 meeting of the American Committee for the Scientific Study of Religion, he caused a sensation with a Mona Lisa that wept tears. The technique was simple, he said, and the ingredients had been readily available for centuries, but would not elaborate.

    In some cases believers may be genuinely deceived by natural processes, such as the effect of condensation. Yet every year produces a fresh crop of miraculous statues to challenge skeptics and inspire believers.
