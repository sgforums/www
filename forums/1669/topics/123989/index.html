It may be that your partner actually wanted to change and likes the new them, in which case you'll likely be resented for trying to get them to "go back". However, if you really believe your guy or girl to have changed for the worse (i.e., drug or alcohol addiction, self-destructive behavior, etc.) then you'll at least want to give it a shot.

    To start, make sure you don't sound like you're motivated by anger or that you seem threatened in any way. Tell them that you've noticed what's been going on and you're simply concerned for their well being (this works especially well if there's been a weight-gain, or if they seem to have let themselves go in some way).     Also, if you can, try and get to the "root cause" of the change. If your ladylove has suddenly ballooned, it's not likely that it's just because she really likes pie. More likely there are emotional or psychological reasons for her overeating, and working on those is really the only way to get her to go back to her slender self for good.

    Okay, so maybe you've already pulled out all the stops and you can see there's just no going back. They are who they are now, and you need to either love 'em or leave 'em. If you're going to love them, it will take some doingÂ—on both your parts.

    3. Hightail it out the door and leave
    Though it may sound cold, there are some times when you just gotta look out for No. 1. And if you're not happy with your partner, you know they won't change, and there's just no bridging the divide, it's probably time to walk away. In other words, you gotta know when to hold 'em, and know when to fold 'em. Just make sure that before you decide to fold 'em, you did all you could to salvage the relationship.     As your relationship becomes more serious your family and friends may become more vocal in their stance on you and your partner. Here's how to recognize the signs and keep your family and friendships strong for years to come:

    1. Understand People's Fear of Change
    As you devote increasingly more time to your partner, family and friends will likely notice a decrease in the time you spend with them. Your family and friends may even go so far as to say they prefer the old you, the one who prioritized them over a boyfriend or girlfriend.

    Start by understanding that these feelings from your family and friends are normal and come with the territory of adjusting to a new, important person in your life. Make sure you spend quality, one-on-one time with your closest friends and family explaining that you still value their relationship, you just need to nurture your partner to give the relationship a chance to grow and flourish. Help them understand there is no competition for love and you have enough to offer everyone important to you.

    2. Don't Be an Open Book
    When you hit a bumpy patch in your relationship, it's easy to turn to friends & family and complain about your partner. This offers the short term gain of venting about your problems. Unfortunately, the long term impact is the creation of a negative impression of your partner among friends and family.

    If you think you and your partner have a real chance at long-term happiness, beware the negative picture you may be painting by only sharing the downside of your boyfriend or girlfriend.

    3. Think in Terms of a New Family
    Your parents may want to hold on to the past when you were their child and you, your siblings, and them made up a happy family. It can be hard for parents to understand that your relationship represents a chance for you to start a new family. Plus, this family is supposed to come first in your life.

    Take the time to thank your parents for all that they did to raise a smart, well-adjusted adult. Help them understand that starting a new family is the natural progression in life. Also, tell them that the beauty of starting a new family is the chance to produce grandchildren and grow the family.

    4. Change Your View of Friendship
    As you get older, the type and number of friends you need changes with your circumstances. In college you may have thrived with several acquaintances. As a young adult, you may have preferred a group of single friends to check out the bar scene. Now that you're settling down, your single friends may miss the old, party-oriented version of you.
    Start to realize that friends serve different purposes at different stages in your life. Now may be the time to seek out new friends in serious relationships who better understand the pressures and priorities of romance. Or if you want to keep your single friends in your life, take the time to discuss how you can remain close even if your friend wants to hit the party scene while you now prefer renting movies and snuggling.

    5. Play the Part
    Even if you're not fully comfortable with the transformation from a bachelor into a relationship-first person, act the part now. Change your actions first and you'll find the attitude and beliefs about yourself catch-up soon enough.

    Your family and friends may struggle with this new you, but if you stay consistent as the new you, sooner or later they'll come around.
