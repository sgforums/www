    ok now regarding foot drills in NCDCC...

    there has been alot of hoo-haa on who is right and who is wrong.... so i'm here to clear everything up....

    which standard of foot drills do we follow?
    we are NCDCC (part of Home Team Cadet Corp HQ "HTCC HQ" as well as MOE)
    therefore we do not come strictly under Singapore Civil Defence Force, we just learn their Civil Defence skills and knowledge....

    Singapore Civil Defence Force drills come mainly in form of following the army styled foot drill manual...
    However, being under the NCDCC, we do not follow the army terms of foot drills... because we are under the HTCC HQ, we follow what has been thought from HTCC HQ, therefore we strictly come under the Singapore Police Force (SPF) standards of foot drills...

    clarification on check-in-bang
    the term check-in-bang is not for people with short legs, and also NCDCC are not the first to use it as Police trainees in Home Team Academy and back at Old Police Academy have been using this standard for a very long time....

    the difference between [check-1-bang] & [check-in-bang]
    check-1-bang... --> at the point of "1", you take one more step with you're left foot forward, overshooting the position of the right foot, before coming to a compete halt...

    check-in-bang... --> at the point of "in", you actually "slide" your left foot so that it coincides its position together with the right foot before coming to a complete halt...

    so why do we use check-in-bang?
    the reason is clear... people marching have to maintain their "one arm and 3 inches" spacing from the person infront... by using "check-in-bang" we actually get to visualise that every one would reach the spot of halting before they actually commence on the bang.

    if you use "check-1-bang", you can never tell where is the spot that they will eventually halt on BECAUSE everybody doesn't have a constant spacing once you "bang"... literally put it this way... soem people might take a bigger step at "1", some people might take a shorter step... therefore there is inconsistency, yes you may halt together, but the dressing of the squad at halt position would be TOTALLY off...

    so yeah... hope this can clarify on the drills... anything can message me and i'll try to answer as much as i can...

    4.4 - Presidential Lance Guard Pennon

    On National Day Parades, one can see the Presidential Lance Guards who will form up upon the President's arrival and departure. They carry lances with a pennon attached beneath the spearhead. Lances today function as mainly ceremonial items.

    Shown here is the pennon of the Lance Guards from the Armour formation, as used at National Day Parades. It is triangular, embroidered and fringed. The pennon features the insignia of the unit/formation supplying the Lance Guards which has traditionally been from the Armour formation.

    The lances will be kept upright at all times, until the President arrives or when he is leaving at which points, the lances will be held at horizontal position until the President takes his place.

    BTW, I've come across this intresting info:

    Four-part commands

    The typical parade commands are spoken extremely clearly and emphatically, and consist of four major parts.

    1. Identifier, or who is to follow the command. This is typically (number) Section, (number) Platoon, (letter) Company, or (name) Regiment, although the prefixes are often dropped when there is no ambiguity (Section, Platoon, Company (Coy,) or Regiment.) Parade can only be given by the parade commander, and always refers to the entire parade, regardless of size.
    2. Precautionary, or what is to be done in an adstract sense: Move to the Advance, Move to the Retire, etc. (This is not always used in American usage)
    3. Cautionary, or the first part of what is to be done. In American usage, this is called the "preparatory command."
    4. Executive, or execution, a single syllable on which the soldiers actually move. This is as true in the United States armed forces as in other services; however, the accent of the commander often leads this to be heard as simply huh on commands such as "Present Arms" and "Order Arms." There is always a significant pause between the Cautionary and the Executive. In American usage, the executive command is always given a greater emphasis than the preparatory command.

    i.e.

    * 4 Platoon (Identifier,) Moving to the Right in File (Precautionary,) Right (Cautionary,) Turn (Executive.)
    * B Coy (Identifier,) Advance (Precautionary,) Left (Cautionary,) Turn (Executive.)
    * (U.S. usage) Battalion (Identifier), Right (Cautionary), FACE.
    * Brigade (Identifier).

    Often there is no chance of ambiguity, and much of the command can be unspoken. In such cases there must always be the Cautionary and the Executive.

    * Present Arms
