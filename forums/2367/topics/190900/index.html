    The big lifts that drive full body gains load the body's entire musculature and load it proportionately. The mindset that each and every muscle needs to be isolated and trained specifically is incorrect and considering that isolation exercises are comparatively very ineffective at adding muscle makes it very inefficient unless you identify a specific need (basically, don't shotgun blast exercises - focus on core lifts and increasing capacity). Put 100lbs on your dead and squat. 50lbs on your bench and barbell row. Your traps, shoulders, and calves will reflect the hard work as will your whole body. How many guys over at elitefitness' board used the 5x5 and were amazed that their rear delts started growing and biceps grew all from core exercises and rows. No direct biceps or delt work. Hmmm.

No fat or muscle gain = not enough calories to support any growth. Eat big, lift big and muscle you will have. The body does a fairly good job of staying in proportion so worry about it 20lbs of muscle down the road when you see a problem (you likely won't).

    Another interesting comment

    Everyone wants big arms, few people have them. The best way for the Hardgainer to focus on their arms is to forget about them. What's that? Simple, we've already established that over training is the reason for lack of progress, and for best gains the minimum amount of training that can be done and still cover all body-parts will result in the fastest gains.

    So how to best accomplish the task? Use compound movements that hit many muscle groups at once. I assure you once you can do chins with your palms facing you with body-weight + 35-60 pounds strapped to your waist for 6-8 reps you will have biceps about as big as their going to get.

    Once you can dip body-weight + 75-100 pounds for 6-8 reps or do close grip bench presses with 100% of body-weight for 6-8 strict, you will indeed have big triceps. This is the way for a hardgainer to get big arms. The 12 sets for biceps 12 for triceps routines are the fast-track to failure for all but the more advanced or gifted athletes.
