    Lymphoma is a relatively uncommon type of cancer and until recently we know so little of them that even the names of lymphoma were in dispute. The two main groups are Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin lymphoma. Among non-Hodgkin lymphoma, they can be subdivided into B-cell, T-cell, NK/T cells or histiocytic subgroups. There are many different disease entities in each subgroup.

    Your question is about the cause of lymphoma. For Hodgkin lymphoma, many are due to a virus known as Epstein-Barr virus (EBV). You said you are interested in B-cell Non-Hodgkin lymphoma. The problem is that there are many different disease entities under this heading. The cause is known for some of them and unknown for many of them. To discuss further, we need to know what type of B-cell Non-Hodgkin lymphoma you are interested in. For example. one type known as extranodal marginal zone lymphoma of MALT (mucosa associated lymphoid tissue) type is common in the stomach and is usually due to a bacteria known as Helicobacter pylori. Another type which is very aggressive, known as Burkitt lymphoma, is due to the EBV virus, and is endemic in parts of Africa but we do see it in our population as well. However, the cause for many other subtypes is still unknown although we have some idea of the mechanism and the genetic changes leading to the cancer.

    Originally posted by rainee:
    How does one get those virus? I thought HIV is for AIDS one?
    Yes, HIV can give rise to lymphoma as well. What actually happens is that because of the immune deficiency caused by HIV, another virus, EBV becomes active and causes a number of lymphoma types.

    The EBV virus is common all over the world and gives rise to a mild flu-like infection in most people known as infectious mononucleosis. It is transmitted by close contact and respiratory droplets...we used to call it the kissing disease as it tends to come up each year when we have a new batch of undergrads...so rainee and emperor beware most people recover with no further problems but in a small proportion of people it causes nasopharyngeal carcinoma (nose cancer) and nasal NK/T cell lymphoma ( type of Non-Hodgkin T cell lymphoma) later in life. This virus also has a receptor for B-lymphocyte, so in immune-deficient patients like those with AIDS, it can re-activate and cause a B-cell lymphoma or Hodgkin lymphoma.

    HTLV-1 as mentioned by renorenal is not seen in Singapore. It is endemic in Japan and the Caribbean. It causes a rare type of lymphoma known as Adult T-cell leukaemia lymphoma (ATLL).

    Of the aggressive forms of Non-Hodgkin B cell lymphomas, the most aggressive is Burkitt lymphoma, which is caused by the EBV virus. This disease has to be treated very quickly with strong chemotherapy but a cure is possible, although not everyone can withstand the strong drugs that have to be used. Various forms of large B-cell lymphoma are also rather aggressive.

    With aggressive lymphomas, the treatment may be as likely to kill the patient as the cancer so the doctor has to consider the health and youth of the patient, the aggressiveness of the cancer and the patient's own desire to decide on the treatment. It is therefore important to type or classify the lymphoma accurately, so that the oncologists can predict its prognosis and use the most appropriate therapy.

    A little lump in the throat was likely an enlarged lymph node. Bruises all over the body can be due to low platelet count, which may occur because the cancer has replaced the bone marrow. Swelling of the face and breathing problems when lying down may occur with a large mediastinal tumour such as primary mediastinal large B-cell lymphoma more common in females and located in the chest cavity but to know the type, you need to look at the histology. We can keep on speculating but there are quite a number of possibilities so you will have to ask the doctor as to what is the histological diagnosis.

    Many people do not realize that with cancers it is not the GP, surgeon or oncologist you see who makes the diagnosis. It is made by a pathologist, who works behind the scene to provide consultation to various medical doctors. Lymphoma diagnosis is more difficult than other cancers, requiring advanced techniques using immunohistochemistry and increasingly, techniques in molecular genetics. For example, in this case, one must confirm or exclude Burkitt lymphoma, a very aggressive tumour, by its immunophenotype and whether there is a translocation of a gene, known as c-myc.

    Originally posted by rainee:
    How does one get those virus? I thought HIV is for AIDS one?
    HIV is transmitted through shared needles, pregnancy, sex, blood transfusion, hemodialysis and needle prick injuries from the affected.

    HIV leads to immunosuppression as it affects the T4 cells causing it to decline over time rapidly. The body tries to regenerate and compete with the loss initially, but over time, the body is itself exhausted over such potential. When this happens, AIDS steps into the picture and takes control of the body leading to poor immune system and development of opportunistic infections such as tuberculosis (which is common amongst the prisoners, crowded conditions and poor hygiene). Partnership between AIDS and TB has created a strain of MultiDrug Resistant TB (MDRTB) which is resistant to its common baseline of drugs.

    Aside from TB, they also partner up with loads of complications such as skin diseases (fungal infection, which do not normally affect healthy individuals), succumbing to prolonged cold and fever which never seems to get well, unhealing wounds and more...

    Because T4 cells are the fighting cells (first line) in the body against most cellular infections (B cells are humoral, most of them forms antibodies to attack against foreign agents and are considered second line) such as all the bacteriums, its destruction or depletion or dysfunction will lead to an increased risk of infections...
