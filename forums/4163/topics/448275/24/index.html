    Why are broinchrist and alize debating about the objectivity of morality? Is alize an atheist?

    In my experience with atheists, I find that they have morality standards contrary to common impression - they care a lot about what is right or wrong and will argue for them. Christians fall into 2 groups - the bochup one (who avoids all religious/moral debate) and the other group are ones who will scour scriptures to find answers.

    Also, in my experience and observations, atheists know the bible fairly ok. They aren't ignorant of what the bible writes, although I have to say their interpretation and reading of the scripture is a little off at times. They tend not to separate the OT with the NT and lump everything together - hence a jealous God is the only God they see most of the time, due to OT stories.

    Morality IS important. I guess the question of whether they are objective depends very much on whether you hold a modernist or a post-modernist worldview (i.e. something that is absolute, like "truth" vs. post-modern thought, which is "perception is reality")

    It's one of those discussions where the debate will always ends up, "how do you know if Tim, whom I am looking at now isn't really a purple triangle in reality?" It's the whole, "how do you know the earth is truly spherical?" debate.

    This also ties in with how and why different churches run things differently. A megachurch tends to be post-modernistic ("perception is reality" camp), even though they hold God to be an absolute. Hence, there is a lot of emphasis on making "things look good" because they believe what people see is the truth. One pastor always said, "'Man looks at the appearance, God looks at the heart.' Why not take advantage of it?" My counter argument is: why do you use the scripture against God's intention? Those words are meant to teach us NOT to look at outward appearances rather than exploit man's weakness.

    I think it's an endless cause to argue intellectually about whether morality "simply is" or are subjective to man. There are arguments in either camps. In the post-modern view, there are political science writings such as "The social contract" (by Rosseau), where what society deem as moral or immoral are a result of an unspoken "contract" where an individual gives up some of his rights in exchange for a society ruled by laws (the legal system), so that randomness and arbitrary actions are reigned in. It is really hard for an individual to predict what will happen if anything and everything is allowed - someone is allowed to rob you or murder you without consequences. Hence he gives up some of his "freedom" to allow a state sanctioned legal system in which the rules are applied for everyone. Religious or not.

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Social_Contract

    It's a non-religious explanation of why there are laws that applies to everyone regardless of his faith (or lack of one, as in the case of an atheist). For me personally, my personal conviction remains a Christian one, where God is the final moral standard and that no mortal human can meet His perfection, except Him who came in the flesh.
