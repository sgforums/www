    Police are investigating a Facebook post by a full-time national serviceman (NSF) that criticises Islam.

    This is the second such incident this week after an ex-Young PAP (YP) member was discovered to have posted an offensive photo on the same social networking site against Islam.

    A police spokesperson confirmed that investigations into the matter are going on after a letter of complaint was sent to The Straits Times Forum Page, Defence Minister Ng Eng Hen, Senior Parliamentary Secretary for Defence Mohamad Maliki Osman, former PAP MP Maidin Packer and sociopolitical blog The Online Citizen (TOC), reported The Sunday Times (ST).
     
    The NSF who posted the picture of text has been identified as Christian Eliab Ratnam. His post included inflammatory statements about Islam, stating, among other things, that Islam is not a religion but "an authoritarian, political doctrine which imposes itself by force", wrote the same paper.
     
    Ratnam's Facebook account has since been shut down.
     
    In a post on Friday on TOC, Ratnam apologised for the post, saying that he was not affiliated to any group or organisation, and added that he did not want to "cause disharmony and unrest in Singapore".
     
    ST also discovered that Ratnam is apparently a pro-Israel and Jewish supporter.
     
    Not only did his Facebook page contain the image of Israel's flag and the symbol for the Israel Defence Forces, he is also a member of the Facebook group for Israelis and Jews living in Singapore.
     
    Ratnam also made a post on a Facebook group called "anti-feminist empire", where he directed readers to the group called "Submissive and Obedient Wives (For G-d and Husband)", which was started by his wife.
     
    It calls for wives to be submissive and obedient to their husbands and quotes from the Bible and Torah.

    MHA investigating offensive postings on religion on Internet

    SINGAPORE: The Ministry of Home Affairs (MHA) said police are investigating several complaints of offensive postings on religion on the Internet.

    Their statement comes after a third posting this week came to light of a racist comment that was reposted with a religiously insensitive picture on a blogger's Facebook page.

    MHA said the public should refrain from adding comments that may further inflame the situation.

    It added that the right to free speech does not extend to making remarks that incite racial and religious friction and conflict.

    MHA said the authorities take a very serious view of all instances of racial and religious incitement.

    Any person who re-posts or contributes offensive comments to the postings that are the subject of on-going investigations, and is also found to have committed an offence, will be dealt with in accordance with the law.
