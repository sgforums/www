    SINGAPORE: A man who cheated OCBC bank by forging 176 invoices, which led to S$2.6 million being credited to his company's account, had his prison sentence halved after a High Court judge allowed his appeal on Wednesday. Tan Thiam Wee was the director of software company Idealsoft, which had a factoring agreement with OCBC. This meant that, if it had given a customer an invoice that had not been paid, the company could ask OCBC to advance 85 per cent of the value. As his company was facing cash-flow problems in late 2007, Tan created false invoices and submitted them to OCBC to maintain the company's working capital. He also created false invoices and purchase orders to support those documents. Between September 2007 and October 2008, Tan forged 176 invoices, of which 160 were accepted, and S$2.6 million was credited to his company's account. The offences came to light when seven police reports were lodged against Idealsoft by its customers after they received letters of demand from OCBC for repayment. Tan was sentenced to five years in jail by a district judge last December after pleading guilty to 12 charges of cheating. Another 164 charges were taken into consideration. In appealing against the sentence, his lawyers, Senior Counsel Philip Jeyaretnam and Mr Derek Kang, argued that this was "not a conventional cheating case" and that the "underlying objective" was to ease Idealsoft's cash flow difficulties. Deputy Public Prosecutors David Chew and Serene Chew, however, pointed out that Tan "had the audacity" to include legal institutions such as the Singapore Academy of Law and the Law Society of Singapore in the fictitious invoices. Justice Lee Seiu Kin said "the striking feature" in this case was Tan's intention. "He had carried out the fraud in the hope that it would tide him over a tight cash flow situation. His motivation was to stave off what he thought was temporary insolvency so that his company could survive and his employees could remain in their jobs." While this did not relieve him of his culpability, Justice Lee said the situation was "vastly different" from one where the perpetrator commits an offence for direct financial gain or to repay gambling debts. The bank's loss totalled S$634,075 as some money was recovered from Tan's customers and the company's current account. Justice Lee allowed Tan's appeal, which meant he would now be jailed for two and a half years. - TODAY

Chief Justice Chan Sek Keong increased the jail term to six months on Thursday, as he agreed with the prosecution that the sentence "was too lenient". On Sept 21, 2010, Amir collected 32 polymer bags from Clementi Police Division Headquarters, including one which contained cash. When he returned to his base, he brought all the found property except one containing the cash, which he hid in the van. Allowing the appeal, CJ Chan agreed Amir "took advantage of the situation". Despite Amir's plea for leniency, he noted the accused had shown "no remorse".
