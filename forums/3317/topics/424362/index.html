  • QX179R

    MALAYS' declining share of the resident population could, in future, affect their ability to bridge the gap between them and the other communities, leaders of the Association of Muslim Professionals (AMP) said at a forum on Saturday.

    Several panellists, however, felt the picture was not all bleak, and pointed to growing social mobility in the community, especially over the past decade.

    Consultant demographer G. Shantakumar said: 'It makes more sense to compare where you were on Saturday against where you will be tomorrow, and not so much against other groups.'

    The retired associate professor at the National University of Singapore (NUS) foresees Malay fertility declining a little further, but on the plus side, the community now has a larger proportion of youth who can lift its economic stature.

    Associate Professor Tan Ern Ser, an NUS sociologist, noted that a recent survey by the Institute of Policy Studies shows that Malays were least likely to feel politically alienated.

    Various indicators suggest considerable social mobility. The community made significant improvements in income, education and in the size of their homes.

    -- ST

    Malays get 'A' for efforts to integrate

    SIX MPs and two ministers on Monday gave upbeat views on the state of racial cohesion in Singapore and, in particular, the Malay-Muslim community's efforts to integrate.

    Speaking during the parliamentary debate on the budget of the Ministry of Community Development, Youth and Sports, Mr Seah Kian Peng (Marine Parade GRC) said he shared Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong's view that the Malay-Muslim community has made great efforts to integrate with others to join the mainstream.

    PM Lee spoke in the wake of views expressed by Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew in a new book, that Malay-Muslims here did not integrate, causing some unease.

    Said Mr Seah: 'My Malay friends eat with me, shake hands with me and we speak in the same mix of Malay, Hokkien and Singlish that we do with all races.

    'We have had four decades of national service, economic ups and downs, our children have been schooled in the same language and, by and large, they share the same world view.'

    He noted that Malay grassroots groups in his GRC had non-Malay members, and two key grassroots leaders in his Braddell Heights ward are Malays even though Malays form less than 6 per cent of residents there.

    -- ST
