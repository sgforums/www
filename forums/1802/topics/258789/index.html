    REPORTS on recent discussions of the Land Transport Authority (LTA) with the public on a review of land transport did not bring out any plausible solutions likely to be acceptable to the LTA or transport operators.
    Bold steps are needed.

    As a senior citizen who travels on a concession pass, I find our public transport system good, comparable to the First World.

    The only thing I wish to see removed is restricted hours so the elderly do not have to watch the clock and remember the concession window.

    I am sure more bus operators competing for the same customers will further improve the service level.

    Taxi service is part and parcel of public transport in any city. It is not a private chauffeured service but rather a better version of the minibuses in Hong Kong.

    Conveniences carry a premium, and the efficient management of the taxi fleet in the land transport system makes the difference.

    To solve the perennial problem of taxi shortage requires political will from the LTA to try new approaches with boldness.

    Fair benefits for cabbies, operators and commuters should be in the equation. Perhaps the following drastic changes will ease the problems:

    Abolish all surcharges and make phone booking a means to direct cabs where they are needed most. This will eliminate negative incentives to cabbies and increase passenger-carrying capacity of every cab on the road.

    Increasing surcharges will only exacerbate the problem. The recent increases did not improve the situation. The crux is imbalance of supply and demand due to unavailability of drivers and not cabs.

    By all means increase the flag-down rate and charge more for extra distance travelled.

    Don't blame lack of cab drivers for inability to cope during peak hours. Allow foreign drivers to fill the gap. If Singaporeans don't want the job, let foreign workers keep the cabs moving and be productive. Don't let lack of cab drivers hold us hostage.

    Let cabbies earn more with higher productivity and hard work. Waiting for phone bookings for extra money is counter-productive and nurtures a bad work attitude.

    If cabbies can earn decent fares without coerced incentives, why should they wait for phone bookings and resort to 'hiding'? Instead, they will be hunting passengers so they can earn more income.

    Paul Chan Poh Hoi

    (Straits Times 4 June 2007)
