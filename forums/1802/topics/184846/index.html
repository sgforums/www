    This article :-

    Taxis can learn from Nanjing

    I Visited Nanjing last month for the first time. We spent four days there and stayed in a four-star hotel. We visited recommended places of interest, and no one would want to miss the Memorial Hall to the victims in the Nanking massacre.

    Nanjing has a population of about five million. As senior citizens, we avoided public transport like underground trains or buses. Our only means of transport was by taxi.

    I was surprised that this Third World city has a First World taxi service. We could get a taxi even at peak rush hour less than 10 minutes from our hotel. It was easier to get one on the road than in Singapore. There is no on-call sign and there is no need to book taxis. Moving around is easy.

    There are about 12,000 taxis in the city area. The flag-down charge is 2.40 yuan (50 Singapore cents) and 2 yuan per kilometre thereafter. The cabs are not brand new but most are comfortable and clean enough for a Third World country.

    What impressed me most was the level of service requirements and the details on the print-out receipt. The information allows easy tracking of abuse. A closer look showed the following items:

    City authority stamp

    Name of taxi company

    Company telephone number

    Code number of tax invoice

    Tax invoice number

    Taxpayer's ID number

    Taxi plate number

    Vehicle registration number

    Date - year, month and day

    Board time - exact hour and minute

    Disembark time - exact hour and minute

    Flag-down charge - 2.40 yuan

    Distance travelled - down to 0.1km

    Grand total

    When we travelled to the outskirts, the driver asked if we wanted to go by highway (and pay toll) or congested city road. He did not ask us which way or how to go. He just gave us a choice - longer travel time or shorter time at a slightly higher price.

    I asked some drivers why their company does not provide phone booking service. They said, with so many taxis around the city chasing customers, why waste money on a service that is not required?

    Cabbies pay 150 yuan for day shift and 80 yuan for night shift. Some told us that working an honest 12-hour shift gave them fairly good returns.

    I wonder why we need to call cabs and pay $3.50 when there are more than 40,000 taxis in our small city of four million. Can Singapore take a leaf from Nanjing and do away with all the frills so commuters, taxi drivers and taxi operators all benefit?

    Paul Chan Poh Hoi
