    Confusing taxi prices

     
    Published on Nov 03, 2013

    Commuters are so baffled by the profusion of flag-down fares, metered fares, surcharges and phone booking charges that many can't keep up with the changes. One might ask if the public would prefer a freewheeling, lightly regulated taxi system with a variety of services and fares, or a planned, orderly and competitive model instead. But consumers are not wedded to a system and would be happy with anything that ensures affordable fares, safe rides, a reasonable quality of service, and sufficient taxis especially during busy periods, rain or shine.

    Taxi fares were deregulated in 1998. The Government liberalised the sector in 2002, which saw several new players entering the fray. There are now some 30 types of cabs here, across seven different brands. There is no question that taxi operations are profitable; for example, taxis form the single biggest income generator for ComfortDelGro Corporation. So, are taxi drivers working as hard as they do in, say, Hong Kong and providing value for money? Hong Kong's 18,000 cabs cater to 1.1 million trips per day. Here, the 28,000 cabs do under 1 million trips per day. Fares in the two countries are comparable.

    Some argue that taxis companies and fares should be regulated like for buses and trains. But would this represent a backward step? In the debate over overcrowded buses and trains, calls for greater state intervention have gone as far as nationalisation of the entire systems. It's clear that private enterprise is a better bet to ensure higher efficiency and consistent standards, providing it operates within an acceptable regulatory framework. But when public discontent rises, there will be pressure for more regulation. So, if taxi operators wish to retain flexibility, it is in their interest to review the current confusing state of play. They should seek more consumer feedback, put forth practical suggestions and work with the authorities and consumer groups to iron out the kinks in the system, so no one need feel being taken for a ride.

    Still an offence if driver moves off before parking ticket is issued

    Director, Media Relations, Land Transport Authority

    We refer to Mr Bala S Rajaratnam’s letter, “Parking tickets should be issued with evidence” (Oct 23, online). Based on our records, he was booked for illegal parking along Burnfoot Terrace, where a yellow zigzag line was drawn.

    It is an offence to park or wait along a single yellow zigzag line, except for picking up or alighting passengers, irrespective of whether the vehicle’s hazard lights are switched on, if there is someone in the vehicle or if the engine is running.

    There can be circumstances where a Notice of Parking Offence (NOPO) is not issued, for example, if the motorist drives off while the officer is issuing the NOPO, or if the driver refuses to accept the NOPO.

    In such instances, the enforcement officer may note the details of the offence and lodge a summon report. Thereafter, an official Notice of Traffic Offence will be served to the registered owner of the vehicle. We thank Mr Rajaratnam for the opportunity to clarify.

    -- TODAY

    Taxi issues not so easily resolved

     
     

    SOME of Mr Toh Cheng Seong's suggestions ("How taxis can be viable alternative to private cars"; Forum Online, last Saturday) may not work in Singapore.

     

    Exempting taxis from the certificate of entitlement (COE) and Electronic Road Pricing (ERP) systems defeats the Government's objective of managing road usage.

     

    Although taxis are considered public transport, each carries only up to four passengers, while a bus carries about 40.

    Passing the COE and ERP cost savings to taxi commuters may lead people to shun other forms of public transport in favour of cabs.

     

    And if the timing for changing shifts is adjusted, it would affect how taxi hirers and their relief drivers split rental costs. Also, there may be limited take-up for taxi pooling as some people may not like sharing a cab with strangers.

     

    Paying fixed salaries to cabbies might decrease productivity. After all, why should a cabby work harder if he is a salaried worker? Even with the current rental system, the Land Transport Authority had to set service standards requiring 70 per cent of each operator's fleet to clock at least 250km a day because some cabbies refuse to drive for long hours.

     

     

    Francis Cheng

    THE Government will relook the taxi fare structure to make it simpler and easier to compare across cab operators, said Senior Minister of State for Transport Josephine Teo.

    Admitting that today's taxi fare structure is "complex and confusing for commuters", Mrs Teo said on Tuesday that the Land Transport Authority will work with the Public Transport Council and cab companies to look into retooling the fare structure.

    This will take into account "the impact on taxi drivers, ultimately to have a taxi fare structure that best serves commuters' interests", Mrs Teo told Parliament.

    Responding to Mr Liang Eng Hwa (Holland- Bukit Timah GRC), Mrs Teo said taxi fares were deregulated in September 1998 to allow cab operators to set their own fares to be "more responsive to market conditions".

    On whether fare structures now ensure enough taxis ply on the roads, Mrs Teo said the different surcharges have been imposed to better match taxi supply with demand by giving incentives to cabbies to serve locations and time periods where demand is high.

    Taxi companies are required to publicise fare revisions at least one week in advance in the media and on their websites.
