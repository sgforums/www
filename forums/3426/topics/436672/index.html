    Singapore
    Published August 23, 2011
    PRESIDENTIAL ELECTION 2011
    Good to raise taxi drivers' age limit to 75: Tony Tan

     

    By LEE U-WEN

     

    THE age limit for taxi drivers should be raised from the current 73 years to 75 years, said presidential candidate Tony Tan, as this would be in line with the government's ongoing push for older Singaporeans to remain working as long as possible.

     
    Dr Tony Tan: Notes that there is no such age limit for taxi drivers in major cities such as HK or Tokyo

    This was one of several issues that the National Taxi Association (NTA) - which represents some 12,000 drivers - raised during a closed-door dialogue with the former deputy prime minister yesterday.

    Dr Tan, 71, said this was a good suggestion and he would raise this with the government if he was eventually elected as Singapore's next president after Polling Day this Saturday.

    'Provided the taxi driver passes a medical examination and is fit, there is no reason why he cannot continue to drive a taxi,' he told reporters gathered outside the dialogue venue at the NTUC Building in Shenton Way.

    He noted how there was no such age limit for taxi drivers in major cities such as Hong Kong or Tokyo. Singapore, too, does not impose an age cap for anyone wishing to own a drivers' licence provided he or she has passed the necessary medical tests, said Dr Tan.

    Overall, he said that the hour-long meeting with some 25 executive committee members of the NTA allowed him the opportunity to 'understand the concerns and problems' faced by the taxi driver community.

    The NTA was formed last year and includes six taxi operator associations affiliated to the National Trades Union Congress. These are Comfort, SMRT Taxis, CityCab, TransCab, Premier Taxi and SmartCab.

    Among the other issues discussed, Dr Tan said that taxi drivers talked about their healthcare coverage given that they were self-employed. The drivers also shared how they put in long hours on a daily basis in a 'strenuous job' that did not have a favourable image by others.

    There were fewer public events for the four presidential hopefuls yesterday - the sixth day of the nine-day campaign - as they spent most of the afternoon recording their joint television forum, which will be broadcast at 8 pm tonight.

    Dr Tony Tan managed to visit the Redhill food centre to catch the early dinner crowd there before having another closed-door dialogue, this time with the Tamils Representative Council.

    Former NTUC Income chief Tan Kin Lian, meanwhile, was out and about just after sunrise as he mingled with Singaporeans at Clementi Mall. His programme also took him to Little India where he visited a temple, followed by a dinner with the Singapore Foochow Association.

    The 63-year-old did not think that being endorsed by the unions was necessarily an advantage as he believed that those in the unions would 'make their own decisions' about who to vote for.

    'I am not upset that I have not been endorsed by unions. I prefer to be endorsed by the people,' he said.

    As for former senior civil servant Tan Jee Say, his busy schedule included an evening stop at Tampines Central as he looked to garner more support ahead of his election rally that will be held tonight at the Toa Payoh Stadium.

    Former Ayer Rajah member of parliament Tan Cheng Bock, meanwhile, was busy shaking hands and taking photographs with people outside Kovan MRT and the nearby Hougang Mall.

    The 71-year-old is the only candidate yet to announce whether or not he will hold an election rally. Time is fast running out as Thursday is the last day to conduct any rally. Friday is a cooling-off day with all election campaigning prohibited.
