    Book looks at world of Tokyo taxis

    ARTS & CULTURE FEB. 12, 2012 - 05:18AM JST ( 1 )

    TOKYO —

    “Tokyo Taxi” is a unique collection of taxi signs from one of the world’s most exciting cities, by photographer Alexander James.

    Tokyo at night is a thrilling place. Neon lights up the buildings, while the pavements and bars are packed with bustling crowds. And, as in many other cities around the world, the streets are full of taxis. What makes Tokyo exceptional, however, are the signs on top of its taxi cabs. Hundreds of taxi firms operate in the city, and each has its own distinctive illuminated sign. Stars, globes, lanterns, cherry blossoms and even frogs and waving cats all feature in an array of vibrant colors.

    James set himself the task of capturing the most eye-catching of these signs – no mean feat, because some of the firms have as few as four cars on the road.

    In spite of having to work long, unsociable hours, the drivers take great pride in offering the highest standards of service and in keeping their cabs spotlessly clean, allowing the bright lights to reflect off the gleaming surfaces. Sharing some of the anecdotes he has heard from taxi drivers during his late-night travels, James portrays a city that literally dazzles after dark.

    The Japanese regulators have awarded Jetstar an operator's  certificate Source: AFP

    JETSTAR Japan remains ahead of schedule after the Japanese regulator yesterday issued the crucial air operators certificate needed for it to start flying.

    The approval from the Ministry of Land, Infrastructure and Transport ticks Jetstar's policies and procedures in areas such as cabin services, ground operations, engineering and customer service.

    This means the airline is on track for its accelerated launch date of July 3 and allows it to start selling fares. It will conduct proving flights between now and the entry into commercial service.

    "This approval means we're on track to start flying five months ahead of schedule, making it easier for people in Tokyo, Okinawa, Osaka, Sapporo and Fukuoka to connect," Jetstar Japan chief executive Miyuki Suzuki said.

    The Japanese joint venture is targeting $1 billion in revenue within two years and has 24 aircraft committed to the venture, with $150 million in start-up capital. Jetstar has been serving Japan as an international carrier for more than five years and the new start-up, initially set up with Japan Airlines and Mitsubishi, has attracted considerable interest.

    It recently added Japanese giant Century Tokyo Leasing as a cornerstone shareholder after more than 100 contenders expressed interest in a stake.

    It also announced two weeks ago that it was starting up a joint venture in Hong Kong with partner China Eastern. The Hong Kong venture has generated discussion about whether it can get the necessary approvals, although both partners say they are confident they can.

    Jetstar chief executive Bruce Buchanan yesterday pointed to the Japanese approval as evidence of the group's experience in working through regulatory issues.

    "As a group of airlines operating in 16 countries, Jetstar has the experience to work closely with regulators and meet operational requirements in various jurisdictions," Mr Buchanan said.
