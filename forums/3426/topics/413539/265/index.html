    The Ground Zero of the Atomic Bomb Explosionï¼ˆFeaturedï¼‰

    A park to mark the site of the atomic bomb explosion

    At 11:02 a.m. on August 9, 1945, an atomic bomb exploded 500 meters above this spot. The monolith of black stone shows the hypocenter. The fierce blast wind, heat rays reaching several thousand degrees, and deadly radiation generated by the explosion reduced the city center to ruins. About one-third of Nagasaki City was destroyed and 150,000 people killed or injured, and it was said at the time that this area would be devoid of vegetation for 75 years. Now, the hypocenter remains as an international peace park and a symbol of the aspiration for world harmony.

    Glover Garden

    A beautiful garden that looks down on the streets of the city of Nagasaki

    The former Glover residence, which was designated as a national property of cultural importance, as well as other famous Westerner’s residences such as the Ringer and Alt residences that were formerly dotted about the city, have been relocated into the garden. Since Nagasaki is a city of many hills, moving sidewalks and escalators are provided throughout the whole garden, as well as attractive wall fountains and cobbled walking trails. Over a million people from home and abroad visit this exotic sight seeing spot every year. The view from the Glover residence is extraordinary. The garden is open at nighttime from the middle of July to the beginning of October. The illuminated Western-style buildings make for a splendid atmosphere, totally different from the daytime.

    Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum

    This museum and archive is a plea against nuclear weapons from Nagasaki

    The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum has on display the trajectory of the atomic bomb from its time of release, including the disastrous scenes that followed, the reconstruction of Nagasaki from the atomic bombing till now, the history of nuclear weapons, and the desire for peace with no nuclear weapons in an easy-to-follow format. The Peace Study Room is where atomic bomb victims tell their story, and a documentary on the atomic bombing is shown in the video room. The Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum brings to people’s attention the threat of nuclear weapons, inhumanity, and the tragedy of war.

    Nagasaki Museum of History and Culture

    Learn about the history of exchange between Nagasaki and the rest of the world

    The Nagasaki Museum of History and Culture, functions as a museum along with reconstructing the Nagasaki Magistrate’s Tateyama Office, which existed at the site. The Nagasaki Museum of History and Culture is positioned as the center of studies for Nagasaki with an exhibition theme of “The History of Overseas Exchanges of Contemporary Nagasaki,” and has narrowed its focus on Nagasaki, which was the sole location where overseas exchanges took place during the period of Sakoku, Japan’s period of isolation. The museum has on display roughly 48,000 artifacts including nationally important cultural properties and works of art with a “hands-on” style. The “Nanbanjin Raicho no Zu” (17th Century), a folding screen depicting western ships entering Nagasaki harbour and the manners of westerns, and the “Toranzu,” a painting by Keiga Kawahara, the exclusive painter of Phillipp Franz von Siebold, the resident physician of Dejima, are both nationally recognized important works of art. The Nagasaki Museum of History and Culture also has important historical documents telling the story of the history of overseas exchanges distinctive to Nagasaki.

    Peace Park and Peace Statue

    A park created as a symbol of the wish for world peace

    In the north side of the park and located on a slightly-elevated hill is a 9.7 m peace statue. The message from a girl wondering around asking for water is engraved on the Nagasaki Peace Fountain. Peace monuments donated from around the world have been erected here, and this park has become a symbolic area of Nagasaki where people come to wish for lasting world. Held in front of the Peace Statue is Our Peace Prayer Ceremony, an annual vigil. Near the Peace Park is the Nagasaki Atomic Bomb Museum, the Urakami Cathedral and the Nyokodo, built by Dr. Takashi Nagai, a victim of the atomic bombing who died in 1951 due to leukemia.

    Memorial to the Martyrdom of the 26 Saints of Japan

    Here visitors can learn the history of modern Christianity in Nagasaki

    In 1962, in commemoration of the centennial of the canonization of the 26 Martyrs of Japan that occurred on Nishizaka hill, the Memorial Relief of the Martyrdom of the 26 Saints of Japan was constructed concurrently with the adjoining Saint Filippo Nishizaka Church. The Memorial Hall that stands quietly behind the life-sized bronze statues of the 26 saints is divided into two themes: things handed down from Christian times and things created today in order to clarify history. It introduces the history of Christianity from the time of Saint Frances Xavier’s visit until the Meiji period (the end of the 19th century). Exhibitions include a letter by the priest Nakaura Julien from the Tensho youth facility, letters addressed to Portugal’s King John III from Saint Francis Xavier, the record of the battle of Shimabara, the Maria Kannon sculpture, and a bronze Pieta from the 16th century, as well as frescoes and Japanese paintings.

    Nagasaki Shinchi Chinatown

    A small Chinatown district that’s very popular with sightseers

    The Chinese residents who live here now forming the district of Chinese eateries and shops selling souvenirs from China began when the first immigrants moved there some 60 years ago. Vermillion Chinese gates stand at the entrances and exits in the cardinal directions, each of the gates depicting animals from Chinese legends. The things to eat here are the chanpon and sara-udon noodles and cubed pork buns. There are other shops selling Chinese confections and shops that sell foods and other goods imported from China in this historical “Little Chinatown” that is still lively today.

    Dejima

    A recreation of the man-made island used for trading when Japan was closed off from the rest of the world

    During the period known as Sakoku, when Japan was intentionally isolated from the world for some 200 years (1639 – 1854), this man-made island was the sole piece of land in Japan where trade with the rest of the world was officially permitted. Because of this, Dejima possesses an unique atmosphere where Japanese and Western cultures fused together. In the 19th century, Dejima had 49 structures including houses, cooking areas, warehouses and guard posts. Out of these 49 structures, 25 have been reconstructed. Presently, five structures have been newly built, including the Captain’s Room, and join the 15 other structures open to the public.

    Specialty Products

     
    Shippoku Ryouri (Shippoku Dish)   Chanpon & Sara-udon (Noodle Dishes)

    The influx of foreign culture in Nagasaki in the past has resulted in the development of specialty products. Many foreign foods, whose recipes were originally brought to Nagasaki, have been redeveloped by Japanese who made them their own."Shippoku Ryouri” or Shippoku Dish, which is a fine blend of Japanese, Western and Chinese cooking, epitomizes dishes made in Nagasaki. 
    "Castella" and "Cas-doce" are confectionery originally developed in Nagasaki."Chanpon and Sara-udon" which are noodle-type dishes rearranged from original Chinese foods are still popular even among tourists from China.
    Facing the beautiful sea and mountainous areas, Nagasaki Prefecture is also rich in natural resources. Using these resources, we produce: processed fish products such as "Kamaboko” (steamed fish paste), processed agricultural products such as "Soumen (Japanese fine noodle)" which has a large market share of the Japanese market, and art works such as "Coral Ornament" and "Pearls".

    Other important products include traditional art works such as "Vidro (glass art)", "Hasamiyaki (Hasami Ceramic Ware)" and "Mikawachiyaki (Mikawachi Ceramic Ware)". The Japanese government has designated both two wares as Japanese traditional art works. All the art works with their fine designs and qualities have been developed by skilled artisans.

Fukusaya has been baking divinely fluffy and moist Castella since 1624, and their website quite clearly explains how the cakes are baked. As impressive as the taste, on the other hand, is the packaging. Let’s go layer by layer…

    Fukusaya Castella

    First, a yellow wrapping paper that depicts the founding date of Fukusaya, the shop’s various locations, and its signature bat-shaped logo. In Chinese (which the Japanese also use in the form of Kanji), “bat (è� )” has the same pronunciation as “happiness/luck (ç¦�)” , which is why Fukusaya chose the bat as its store logo.

    Outer packaging

    Inside the yellow wrapping paper is a thin but sturdy yellow paper box, again with the bat logo.

     2nd layer

    Opening the paper box reveals a sealed washi paper-textured spun polyester sleeve with more bat logos.

    2nd layer & 3rd layer

    3rd layer

    Opening the spun polyester wrap reveals a white waxed paper sleeve.

    4th layer

    A sweet fragrance fills the air as the wax paper sleeve is lifted, and voila! a beautiful cut of Castella is finally revealed.

    A seal, and instructions on how to cut the cake

    There are light indentations on the waxed paper sleeve, which are used as a cutting guide for portioning individual servings. This is just a guide, of course, and I happily ignored it as I sliced myself a huge cut.

    The cake is covered by a release-paper

    Finally, the cake itself

    Fukusaya Castella is moist and fluffy, but more resilient than just a piece of fluffy cloud. It’s completely oil-free and perfectly sweet. With a cup of tea, this would make my perfect afternoon :)

    Fluffy, moist, simple but divine

    If you visit Japan, bring back a box or two for your own enjoyment or as gifts for family and friends. Check out their website for additional information on where the cakes can be purchased and the exact price for different cuts.
