    Kagoshima Kurobuta Shabushabu

    Cuisine, kagoshimacuisine, Year round Activities

     

    Kagoshima food is also known as “Satsuma ryori”, the district of Kagoshima used to be known as Satsuma.  Kagoshima cuisine is quite distinct from the rest of Kyushu because of its location at the South-most part of the prefecture.  Also because the Satsuma region controlled Okinawa for a period, the food here has also taken influences from Okinawa food tastes and styles.

    Kagoshima “Kurobuta” (black pig) pork is famous throughout Japan.  The Berkshire black pig breed (which was imported from England) stands out from other pig breeds for its juiciness, flavor, and tenderness, it is pink-hued and heavily marbled.  Which makes it wonderfully suited to the Japanese style of meat cooking “shabushabu”.  In “Shabushabu” the customers are brought a pot of boiling water/stock and a plate of thinly cut raw meat.  The guests then use “hashi” chopsticks to swish the meat through the boiling stock, and eat it straight away.  It is from this swishing through the boiling water that it gets the name “shabushabu”.

    There are many well known shabu shabu restaurants available in Kagoshima.

     

    Price Expect to pay anywhere from 2000 yen upwards.

    YAKUSHIMA, DAY 1 AND 2: FLYING FISH AND YAKUSUGI LAND

    June 14, 2013 · by Rachel · in Food, Sightseeing, Yakushima. ·

    Yakushima is an island south of Kyushu that is part of the Kagoshima Prefecture; however, since it’s a separate island, I won’t put it under the “Kagoshima” category. The forests in Yakushima inspired the forest in Hayao Miyazaki’s film Princess Mononoke. I have mixed feelings about Yakushima. The places we visited in Yakushima are probably the most amazing and beautiful that I’ve ever seen, but staying in Yakushima itself was slightly annoying. It rains all the time, the buses run once per hour, and did I mention that it rains all the time? Supposedly, Yakushima rains around 360 days per year. That is ridiculous. On the day we arrived, it rained beforehand so the ground was wet, but we got lucky on the second day.

     

    Not much really happened on the day we arrived. It was definitely annoying because the port was around 20 minutes away from the minshuku–a Japanese-style bed and breakfast–we were staying at. There were three girls in a tiny room which was pretty inconvenient, but we survived, so it’s okay. The most interesting part of our first day in Yakushima was dinner. Yakushima specializes in flying fish (é£›é­š; tobiuo). Because this is a fish that I have never tried before, I decided to be adventurous and order it–not really, though, since most of my other classmates ordered it too.

    Head on!

    Weirdly enough, I think this might be the first thing I’ve eaten in Japan with bones that I had to actively pick out while eating. I think I mentioned in one of my posts about goals that I’m not really comfortable eating things with bones because I feel like it requires too much effort for something that’s supposed to be enjoyable (eating). That being said, I can’t say I was surprised when I got a plate with a completely fried flying fish. It sure looked delicious, but also…really bony.

    Look at those fins!

    This was incredibly hard to eat, but it was also pretty yummy.

    On Day 2, we went to Yakusugi Land (ãƒ¤ã‚¯ã‚¹ã‚®ãƒ©ãƒ³ãƒ‰), a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Yakusugi refers to cedar trees in Yakushima that are over 1000 years old. I don’t know much about forests, but I would liken it to the sequoias in California; I guess, in that case, Yakusugi Land would be Japan’s Sequoia National Park. I’m not really sure if Sequoia National Park requires as much hiking as Yakusugi Land, though.

    These obviously aren’t cedar trees, but I like this photo.

    There are a few hiking paths through Yakusugi Land: 30, 50, 80, and 150 minutes. We chose to do the 80 minute path first. These paths don’t take as long as they claim, but the hike is still strenuous. At least I was able to complete my yearly work out.

    Did you know that moss only grows on the north side of trees?

    Yakusugi Land is very pristine and well-preserved; however, at times, I felt like it wastoo well-preserved. The hiking paths were very unstable: a lot of parts were really slippery and uneven, and there were cliffs without any sort of fencing to prevent people from falling off. By the end of the hike, I was just glad that I made it back alive.

    I think I liked taking pictures of rivers because I was thirsty.

    The scenery and the environment were so worth the fear, backache (I brought a really uncomfortable backpack), Jell-o legs, and general exhaustion, though.

    For a place that is known for cedar trees, I sure took a lot of river pictures.

Some of mine were: why aren’t I one of those people who work out everyday?; why did I think that studying abroad in Japan and getting exhausted like this was a good idea?; and why didn’t I just stay behind and pretend to be sick?

After lunch, we still had around three hours before the next bus because the buses don’t run very often. Our professor decided that we should go on the 150 minute hiking trail. Luckily, one of our classmates stayed behind so I could leave my backpack at the visitor center for her to watch.

    We speculated that the 150 minute trail wasn’t necessarily longer than the 80 minute trail, but it was definitely more physically taxing. The trail is much more dangerous and the incline is steeper. For that reason, I didn’t take a lot of photos the second time around.

    The last picture I took of Yakusugi Land.

    I know that I’ve spent most of this post complaining about how hiking is so exhausting, but it was still worth it. I don’t think I’ll go to Yakusugi Land again, but I’m really glad that I did. I think Yakusugi Land is a place that you have to see for yourself.

      Why may Shizuoka people be justified in assuming they eat some of the best in Japan?

      Sushi & Sashimi: Eat Local Fish, Seafood, Meat and Vegetables!

      Saurel pike/Aji from Suruga Bay, Shizuoka Prefecture

      Very few people will disagree with the notion that Japan is the ideal place to discover and savour sushi and sashimi in the whole world. Nevertheless, there are a few rules of the thumb to respect even in this gastronomic paradise.
      The overriding rule is that you should try and eat only local fish or seafood.
      Tsukiji might be considered a sushi paradise by Tokyoites, but the cheap prices enjoyed by tourists cannot conceal the reality: the fish and seafood are “imported” from all over Japan and beyond!
      More than often, Edomae (Tokyo) sushi is nothing but a clever way to “dress up” ingredients to lure officionados (and customers) into believing they are eating top quality sushi (with the consequent prices).
      Now, if you have the chance and time to explore Japan beyond Tokyo, you will discover an unfathomable treasure trove of gastronomic pleasure and knowledge!
      After all, this country is a vast archipelago stretched across greatly different seas and climates, making for a diversity difficult to equal.
      So, even if you cannot possibly explore all the shores of this nation, make a point to learn about the food available wherever you choose to stay.
      The same goes for residents, not only for their own sake, but for that of their visitors and friends!

      Sushi set with fish all caught in Suruga Bay, Shizuoka Prefecture

      You also ought to do some homework. Sushi chefs worthy of their salt will be only too happy to answer questions to genuinely interested customers and come up with revelations of their own.
      As an example to illustrate the need for some basic knowledge, in Hokkaido “oyakodon” is not cooked chicken and omelette on a bowl of rice, but raw salmon and its roe spread on top the same bowl of rice!
      Likewise, the same fish will more than often be sold under a myriad of names.
      Many morsels will not be found anywhere else as “sakura ebi/cherry shrimps” and fresh”shirasu/sardine whiting” in Shizuoka Prefecture.
      Sashimi in most cases has to be perfectly fresh as typified by “kubiore saba” in Yakushima Island where fishermen break the neck (“kubiore”) of mackerels (“saba”) to preserve their quality upon catching. The same fish will be served within a few hours, or less, on the local tables.
      On the other hand, tuna sashimi is best consumed after ripening for a few days in a refrigerator.
      In Hokkaido, large shrimps, especially “botan ebi” will be served only raw, whereas “kuruma ebi” will be first boiled in other regions.

      If you ask for “tataki”, make sure it means the whole fish, especially “aji/mackerel pike” that will be served finely cut as tartare atop the dressed fish.

      Flying Fish/Tobiuo sashimi from Yakushima Island

      On the other hand, sushi follows different rules.
      Fish and seafood placed on “donburi” (bowl) are usually of the freshly brought variety but fish served as nigiri is prepared in a different way.
      The greatest sushi (and this cannot be done in Tsukiji!) are made with fish which has been gutted and cleaned live within seconds, then dressed into strips/fillets left to mature in a refrigerator on clean cloth/kitchen paper. This can be done only with fish caught locally!
      The same obviously goes with shellfish and other marine ceatures: One cannot sample better “uni/sea urchin” away from Hokkaido or sakura ebi from Shizuoka.

      Vegan and vegetarians, upon finding a restaurant willing to satisfy their priorities should also ask for food grown locally, a search easier than one might think at first as there are many non-meat eaters in this mainly Budhist country.
      The same vegetables will make for the perfect combination when associated with local fish!

        Shiratani Unsuikyo  # 3   of 4 most visited
        sights in Yakushima

        The Shiratani Unsuikyo Ravine is a lush green nature park containing a number of the island's ancient cedars such as the Nidaiosugi, Kugurisugi and Yayoisugi, the park's most accessible yakusugi which can be reached in a 15 minute walk. Shiratani Unsuikyo is one of the most popular destinations of Yakushima as it is a great place to see the forests without requiring too strenuous hiking.

        The park consists of a number of hiking circuits running along a ravine with English signs throughout. The recommended circuits vary in length from one to five hours long, but you are free to wander around and choose your own route among the paths. The trails vary in difficulty from a historic footpath built in the Edo Period to more developed ones paved in stone and wood.

         

          The inspiration for Mononoke Hime.

        One of the main attractions of Shiratani Unsuikyo is a part of the forest that served as the inspiration for the Stugio Ghibli animated film Mononoke Hime (Princess Mononoke). Oga Kazuo, the lead artist for the film, spent lots of time here working on sketches for the movie's forests. The mountains of the film were further inspired by Akita Prefecture'sShirakami Sanchi Mountains.

        The small grove is at the far end of the park on the trail to Tsuji Toge Pass. If you continue on this trail it will lead you to the Jomonsugi by way of meeting up with the Anbo Trail about halfway to the entrance of the Okabu Trail. The round trip to the Jomonsugi takes approximately twelve hours and is more difficult than the Arakawa Dam Trailhead route.
