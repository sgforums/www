    Fellow cabbies,

    Among various jobs, taxi driving is a rather hazardous job. More so if we push ourselves to earn more. I have always told myself that I must be prepared if I meet my fate on the road (choi choi touch wood). As careful as I try to be, one never knows the future because other drivers can make mistakes.

    1. Have u ever wondered what happens if you are a single bachelor TD with no close relatives?

    If your body is not claimed within 24 hrs, it will be used by surgeons to practise on.

    http://statutes.agc.gov.sg

    MEDICAL (THERAPY, EDUCATION AND RESEARCH) ACT
    (CHAPTER 175)

     

    2. If you are a non-muslim, did you know your organs will be forcibly removed and transplanted into a suitable recipient? 

    HUMAN ORGAN TRANSPLANT ACT
    (CHAPTER 131A)

     

    Now I have nothing against helping other people who are dying, but hey taking my organs out even if relatives object can be very traumatic. So I have chosen to opt out here.

    http://www.moh.gov.sg/mohcorp/legislations.aspx?id=1672

    That way my family is free to either donate or to "sell" my body parts.

    The most common type of organ transplant is the kidney. HOTA only covers kidney, liver, heart and corneas. So as far as organ donor priviledge goes, it mostly applies to kidneys. 


    If one is poor, one is unlikely to be able to afford the waiting time for say a kidney. For many poor people, early death may be a more attractive option than a prolonged life that results in even more financial difficulties.

    If one is rich, buying a kidney poses little problems.

    The highest chance of finding a good organ donor-recipient match is from one's siblings. For such cases, dont even need to queue up if the sibling or relatives are willing to help.

    Waiting list for a kidney is in term of years. This organ donor priviledge act benefits only a small minority of people. Have you seen the anguish on the relatives faces when their loved ones die, and the surgeon shoos them away and remove a kidney from their deceased loved one?

    Really all these laws are only concerned with helping out the medical community. The surgeons need corpses to practise on, the organ transplant team needs ready access to fresh organs to ply their trade. The plastic surgeons need bodies to perfect their art. But the medical community is not willing to pay for dead bodies. A fresh corpse is worth a lot of money. Why else is the SGH mortuary surrounded by high fences with many red notice boards saying "protected place". The mortuary is actually the most secure place in the whole hospital. Why? Because dead bodies are more valuable than living diseased bodies lying in the wards.
