    By special request, here's my observations:

    a) This is not road rage. In fact there's no specific law as road rage, it's a word coined by the press just like burglary.

    The law as far as road rage  (and all assault cases) cases usually would be:

    a) VCH Sec 323 Cap 224

    b) VCGH Sec 325 Cap 224 or

    c) VCH by Dangeorus means or with dangerous weapons ( I forget the exact section)

    b) and c) are seizable offences - means the police can arrest without warrant, and these kind of cases almost certainly will be pursued and the offender charged in court.

    For a) VCH - this is not seizable and the victim will have to seek medical treatment and then lodge a complaint at the Crime Registry at the Sub Courts. The Magistrate then may direct the police to investigate this case. Or if the case has some public implications, the police themselves may decide to investigate the case. Either way the decision to prosecute the offender in these cases, lies with the DPP, not the police. If the DPP declines to pursue, then the victim may take a civil suit against the offender.

    I shall not go into details as to what these may be, as there are a lot of factors that go into this.

    Once the cases goes to court, it lies entirely with the judge to impose sentence if he finds the offender guilty.

    For a) VCH - can range from a fine for non serious injury or a short jail term, alternatively this case can be compounded (settled out of court) if the offender pays the victim who agrees. The court must also agree to this, or the court can deny this and instead call the offender to stand trial.

    b) and c) - longer jail term and almost certainly caning, for VCGH and most VCH by dangerous waepons. Sentences can range from weeks to months even years depending on injuries.

    Serious injury here  means broken bones, fractures, loss of use of limbs, permanent disfiguration of face - a swollen nose or eye that heals is not serious injury under the law. The offender will be charged under Sec 323 and likely be jailed but no caning.

    The Courts have ruled that cases of assault resulting from incidents on the road (hence the term road rage) will almost certainly result in imprisonment unless there are compelling mitigating circumstances. Furthermore Courts also have taken a very dim view of assaults of public service drivers (inc TDs). Jail is also the norm for these cases. Length of jail term depends entirely on the charge and degree of injury. So that's why you see some cases, sentences appear lighter then others.

    For Kenluc's case, this is not a case of assault, and hence the advice given by the policeman was correct. This was a dispute on the road, albeit one which traffic offences may have been committed. The police can only advice to lodge a report for civil action for damages (to the vehicle) and traffic offences for the others (also subjective here, because person reporting is not a police officer, TP will call both up and see what transpires. if both give conflicting views without independent evidence, then most probably just a stern warning).

    As serious as it may look to you, under the Law that's what it is, definitely not road rage. If he hit you, then it may become road rage. But also as is aid, it depends which category the assault comes under. You may have to first lodge a report at the Sub Courts. Only if the offender is found guilty of a charge of assault resulting from an incident on the road, is that 'road rage' which would result in higher penalties.
