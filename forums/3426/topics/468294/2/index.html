    Improve taxi availability, service levels

     
    Published on Apr 15, 2013

    THERE are two areas in which visitors to Singapore commonly give negative feedback on.

    First, it is hard to get a taxi, especially during peak hours. When waiting in long queues for taxis, tourists would assume taxi drivers here are doing a roaring trade. However, locals often hear taxi drivers lamenting that it is a challenging business.

    Comparatively, in Hong Kong and Taipei, taxis are easily available, regardless of the time or location.

    Second, the level of service here is sorely lacking. When I was at Muji Bugis recently and made an inquiry, I received only a curt reply from a staff member. In fact, she seemed more interested in chatting with her colleagues than serving a customer.

    A friend related a similar experience years ago at a high-end boutique in Orchard Road. She had to demand to see the manager before the store associate finally served her.

    This compares unfavourably with the experience in Hong Kong and Taipei, where customers are provided with friendly greetings, offers of help and efficient service, even if they are buying a HK$100 (S$16) T-shirt or a NT$35 (S$1.45) papaya milk drink.

    No city is perfect, but Singapore can learn from cities overseas and improve.

    Patrick Ngiam Kee Soon

    Set up centralised call centre for taxis

     
    Published on Apr 22, 2013

    IT APPEARS that multiple bookings of taxis, as highlighted by Mr Teo Kueh Liang ("Taxi passengers, stop making multiple bookings"; April 13), is a common and widespread problem faced by many cab drivers.

    One way to solve this problem may be for taxi operators to merge their respective booking centres to form a centralised one.

    The market leader could run this centralised booking centre jointly with the other operators, and its operating costs could be apportioned based on their fleet sizes.

    The benefits of a centralised booking system are as follows:

    • Multiple bookings can be easily detected and eliminated.
    • Operators will enjoy economy of scale by not duplicating their booking systems. This would lower such related costs accordingly.
    • Passengers will need to remember and call only a single number to make a booking.

    The success rate of allocating a taxi to a booking call would be increased when all the taxis are within the same pool.Moreover, as some operators do not have a dedicated 24-hour hotline for lodging lost-and-found reports or feedback, the centralised booking centre, with its increased manpower and perhaps an upgraded IT system, could perform the role concurrently.

    This is particularly important as a timely lost report would greatly enhance the chances of a successful find.

    More significantly, many passengers, especially tourists, may not know the operator of the taxi or the licence plate number of the taxi in which the loss had occurred.

    A centralised booking centre would not only solve the problem of multiple bookings faced by cab drivers but would benefit the taxi operators and passengers too.

    Ng Chee Kheon

    Make taxi booking systems more efficient

     
    Published on Apr 22, 2013

    AFTER reading about the hardships faced by taxi drivers due to double bookings and irresponsible behaviour by some passengers ("Taxi passengers, stop making multiple bookings" by Mr Teo Kueh Liang; April 13), I would like to offer a passenger's view.

    Earlier this month, I tried to book a taxi using the ComfortDelGro app on Android.

    I received a message that the server was busy so I tried to book one via SMS.

    I was informed that I had an existing taxi booking. My wife received a similar response when she sent an SMS booking even though she had not tried to book a taxi before this. No taxi arrived to pick us up that day.

    Last month, I was at Labrador Park with my friend and I used the app to book two taxis. The first booking went smoothly and my friend boarded the first taxi when it arrived.

    I waited so long for the second booking that I decided to board a taxi which had stopped to drop off a passenger.

    The confirmation for my second taxi booking came as I was boarding. The estimated waiting time was seven to nine minutes.

    I promptly cancelled the booking but later received a call from the driver of the booked taxi. It turned out that he had not been notified of the cancellation.

    Then, last Sunday night, I was in Little India and was using the ComfortDelGro app without success to book a taxi.

    Eventually, we managed to flag down a cab. Five minutes later, I received an automated call from the booking centre about a waiting taxi. The app did not show any booking and I cancelled the taxi by SMS.

    As these incidents show, sometimes, it is not the passenger's fault. Passengers who do multiple bookings may do so as they have had the experience where none of their bookings went through.

    Taxi companies should boost their booking systems and procedures so as not to unnecessarily inconvenience passengers and drivers.

    Kunwar Bir Singh
