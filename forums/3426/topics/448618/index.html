    SINGAPORE: The Land Transport Authority is imposing tighter licence requirements on operators to improve taxi services.

    In particular, there will be new measures to ensure call booking services are more reliable and efficient.

    Some commuters have complained that drivers use the "on-call" sign to refuse flag-down passengers, especially just before surcharges kick in.

    To minimise abuse, within two years, the sign will be centrally-controlled, and switched on only when the driver accepts a call booking.

    Measures have also been put in to improve booking reliability.

    From October, operators have to meet higher standards, in terms of the answer rate for call bookings, the percentage of calls successfully matched with taxis, and the time taken for the cab to arrive.

    And soon, all cabs will have to be equipped to handle call bookings, where previously this wasn't mandatory under current operator licence requirements.

    And to ensure operators have the critical mass to provide adequate service, the Land Transport Authority has doubled the minimum fleet size, from 400 to 800.

    Out of the seven existing operators, Prime and Smart cabs are the smallest, with 700 cabs each. They have to meet the minimum when they renew their operator's licence.

    For Prime, that's 2017. But for Smart cabs, it's next year. Smart said ramping up the fleet size is not its main worry. Its concern is that fluctuating car premiums will make it harder to set monthly rents that are market-competitive.

    Its managing Director, Johnny Harjanto said: "Suppose a car costs me S$40,000 and COE costs S$60,000 now. Based on the total of S$100,000, I set a monthly rent of say, S$100. What if a few months down the road, COEs drop and my competitor buys the car cheaper? They can set a lower rent, but when that happens what can I do?"

    Smaller operators tend to perform badly in quality reviews.

    For instance, in the December Quality of Service review published by LTA, Smart failed in three out of five service categories, including being unable to cater to 70 per cent of call bookings. It also failed in both safety requirements. Prime cabs also fared badly in terms of meeting call bookings.

    Observers said they could consider integrating some of their operations.

    "Not necessarily in terms of real business management, but to form a common call centre so they can share their taxi fleet between the different companies. Also from the passengers' point of view, they are given another, more practical choice," said Associate Professor Lee Der Horng, from the Department of Civil Engineering at NUS.

    Authorities are also studying how to utilise taxis better.

    In the last five years, the number of taxis grew by 20 per cent, but total distance travelled remained the same.

    Transport Minister Lui Tuck Yew, said: "I have asked LTA to study the feasibility of regulating taxi companies to have a certain minimum availability for their fleets, particularly during peak hours when availability is crucial."

    LTA is also studying how to increase the number of dual-shift cabs on the roads.

    The study is expected to be completed later this year.

    - CNA/cc
