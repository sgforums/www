    Surge in numbers seeking to drive taxis

    By Christopher Tan Senior Correspondent

    MORE people are applying for a taxi vocational licence, drawn by the work flexibility, improved earnings and even access to a car.

    Between 2002 and last year, the number of applicants averaged 7,880 a year. But in the last three years, it had jumped by 16 per cent to 9,105, according to the Land Transport Authority (LTA).

    Applicants go for 21 three-hour training sessions and must pass four theory tests, as well as one practical, before they get a licence. Each year, the passing rate is between 42 per cent and 63 per cent, the LTA said.

    Close to 96,000 people now hold the licence, versus fewer than 90,000 before 2007. Only about half are active cabbies.

    "More people have come to realise the value of a taxi licence," said a spokesman for the Singapore Taxi Academy which trains close to half of all the applicants. Transport firms ComfortDelGro and SMRT train the rest.

    "There is also an increasing sense of job insecurity," added the spokesman. Banks have, for example, shed staff because of global economic uncertainties.

    The profile of applicants now includes more women and new citizens. Only Singapore citizens are allowed to drive taxis.

    The women like the job flexibility, the spokesman said. "They can fit in their household chores and fetch the kids any time."

    Mr Tony Heng, SMRT's director of taxis and transport solutions, said applicants are getting younger. "This could be due to more people viewing driving taxis as a viable career choice."

    According to an LTA poll, the biggest group of two-shift cabbies saw a 17.1 per cent rise in their gross earnings to $363 a day from 2010 to September last year.

    Single-shift drivers saw an 8.3 per cent increase to $235. After deducting taxi rent and fuel, that translated to about $4,250 and $3,650 a month respectively.

    For applicant Chua Wen An, the main draws are independence and flexibility. The 33-year-old, who has a hotel management diploma and works in a hotel, said: "I'd rather be self-employed."

    He added that having a car to run errands is also a plus.

    Another applicant, Mr Paul Lim, 42, a rice importer, said: "Who knows, 10 years down the road, when I think I want to spend more time with my family, I can drive a cab."

    Mr Ricky Lau, 44, chief operating officer of a Thai-based rubber company, said he will be completing his contract soon.

    He added that he could drive a cab while he looks for a new job.

    Getting that licence, however, is not that easy.

    Dispatch rider Yasin Salim, 43, has been trying for it since last October, resitting the tests more than seven times.

    "The English part is very difficult. I understand only simple English," he said.

    The taxi academy spokesman said most applicants secure the licence after sitting the tests three or four times.

    christan@sph.com.sg
