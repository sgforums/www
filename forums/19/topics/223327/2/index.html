    The oyster here refers to the Oyster case, a name that Rolex has used since 1926. It refers to the design of the watch's case, whereby the caseback and crown are both screwed in, creating a hermetically sealed case within which the movement is housed.

    The design of the case has evolved over that time, with the crowns now being Twinlock (2 sealing gaskets) ot Triplock (3 gaskets), some models having crown guards protecting the crown and coming in various sizes from 29 to 40mm diameter. Despite all the developments, the hermetically-sealed case is still denoted by the Oyster name.

    Uhh, beg to differ.

    Rolex's professional (sports) line holds the highest resale value, and more often than not it's the stainless steel models that draw in the highest prices owing to providence.

    For example, you're likely to get a 18K Datejust at a markedly lower price than that of a 5512 Submariner (which is made of stainless steel) of the same age. Also, if you're buying brand new, you're likely to pay the same price for the stainless steel Daytona as you would for a Rolesor (18K/SS mix) Daytona.

    No, it doesn't make any sense, but what you do have to keep in mind is that Rolex's reputation is largely based on its stainless steel models. My Sea Dweller, which is all stainless steel, was purchased at S$6650 in 2004, but if I wanted to offload it now, I could get at least $8600 for it in less than a week.

The 80% rule applies to many products, but you'll find that Rolex sports watches are often sold at a price higher than that of the original purchasing price.

    Of course stainless steel has little resale value in and of itself, but let's keep it in context here. As I said, as far as Rolexes go, there's little, if any, issue about the watches being made of stainless steel because the market's there for it.

    As for the brand name issue, people buy them for various reasons. For myself, I like their after-sales service. When I send my watch in for a service, it not only gets its movement cleaned, oiled and regulated, but Rolex goes over every single part and restores it to brand-new condition - it's as good as getting a brand-new watch all over again. On top of that, Rolex's 31XX series of movements are regarded as being the most rugged automatic movements around, although something like the IWC Ingeneuir is a lot more over-engineered. A number of fellow Rolex owners I know have taken up watch making courses to further appreciate the history of the developments and the work that goes into the design of their watches. To write it all off as a brand-name issue would be an overgeneralisation.
