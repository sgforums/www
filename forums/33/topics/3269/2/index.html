    I had only started following this thread, so kindly pardon my age-old comments.

    Anyway, talking about equal chances to JC and Poly students. After students have got their O's and are making decisions to move on, the obvious choices for most will be either poly or JC. JC is undeniably more difficult to get in as compared to poly, hence give the JC students some credit for being able to get a place in JC. As for poly, I know of some students whose grades can allow them admission to JC, but they opt for poly instead, as they actually want to get into the working society asap.

    At this point, perhaps you can think about the JC students being disadvantaged. If you look at the job ads, job qualifications equate an 'A' cert and an 'O' cert, which essentially mean that a JC student, upon graduation, is still looked upon as having the same qualifications as he had 2 years ago when he graduated from secondary school. You may even consider going into JC as a risk, coz uni would seem as "the only route" after grad. If they can't get into uni, there is not many other places they can go to! As for poly grads, at least they can choose to work after grad, except for the few who will pursue further studies in the uni, local or overseas.

    Like mentioned by someone in this thread, the syllabus planned for both groups are different to cater to the needs anyway. Students enter JC - with the main aim of making into uni - are taught the fundamentals which will be discussed more in-depth when they go into uni, whereas poly students will be taught skills development which will prepare them in the working society. So most students should actually be pretty sure about their future routes by the time they get their 'O' results -- JC -> Uni -> work ; Poly -> work.

    Also, let me assure you that poly students ARE ABLE to get into local universities. I did have a few uni classmates who were poly students. As for those which were said not being able to get into uni despite fantastic results, perhaps they were vying for vacancies in too-popular faculties, which of course would post more difficulties of entering uni. Anyway, these difficulties are not unique to poly students, they were also shared by the JC students as well.

    By the way, I had also seen comments in this forum about local unis not being as good as those overseas. Not that I disagree about this, but why in the first place the laments about "how it difficult it is to get into local unis"?

    This topic is kinda old but let me say a few words to those who are willing to listen.

    Yes, indeed the Singapore government is biased, but why do you think that JC students with Cs and Bs are able to enter into NUS? The truth is, not many of them will be able to enter into "Good" courses. What do I mean by "good" courses? Its those popular ones like Engineering, SoC, etc.

    When I entered into NUS 2 years ago, the cut-off grade for NUS Engineering was AAB, can you believe it? It means that students who score BBB or BBC are not able to get into NUS Engineering! Besides that, the entire cohort has almost 40-50% students who scored either AAA or AAAA (+ S-papers ) in the A levels.

    Let me ask you back the question. Who then, should be allowed to enter into the course? Those with AAAA or those with a COM from poly?

    Besides, when I entered into year2, Electrical Engineering took in some numbers of Poly grads. Those poly grads who are supposedly to be the creme de la creme of the polys. But sadly, when I looked at the way those poly grads who topped their cohort, the truth does not reflect well on them.

    Let me raise a real-life comparison. A JC student who scores only, say AAB (the minimum grade allowed) and a poly grad who scores ADs and is the top 1% of his cohort. They are both in the second-year. When you do a comparison between their level of understanding, and their ability to apply concepts, the answer is really obvious as who comes out as the winner.

    I must agree that poly grads are good at hands-on stuffs. When it comes down to more theoretical concepts, they are not able to grasp it. I am generalizing.

    Also, the writer of this thread says that the government is restricting the number of people allowed to pursue university education. But lets put it this way, university education is not for everyone. The annual fee is $5650 (subsidised). Without subsidy, the annual fee comes up to $20000. That is to say that, the government is subsiding a student up to $80K per student. If the government allows any Tom, Dick and Harry to enter into the university, it is simply wasting the taxpayer's money.

    Look we are talking about input and output. The government inputs $60K and then gets a grad without substantial standard, is it worth it? The question here is really whether it is worth it to push everyone through university education.

    In the case of the foreign student, I do not agree with the writer's views. Foreign students are very very very intelligent. They have exceptional IQ and the ability to grasp concepts. I know tonnes of them. They are hands-down winner when it comes to understanding. Therefore, it can be understood why the government is willing to sponsor them.

    We're talking about Gaussian distribution here (Normal Distribution). Among their cohort, perhaps the poly grads seemed to be able to fare better. But once they are pit against the creme de la creme of the nation (+foreigners), they are at the bottom end of the ladder. Need I say more? It does not matter if you are the best of the worst. What matters most is being the best of the best.

    It is a competitive society. Life sucks. C'est La Vie. Get used to it.
