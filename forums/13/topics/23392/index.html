    hahaha.. like henry said, stability in the corner has got to do with a lot of other factors... assuming you're using a properly designed and constructed, and ASSEMBLED single-sided swing arm, no difference from the dual-armed types... perhaps i should have added the icon to my previous posts... just to name a few bike specific factors for stability in corners: fork rake angle, tire profile/surface/temp/pressure, preload/compression/rebound, frame rigidity, damper or no damper, lean angle, rider's actions as a whole... the one or two side swing arm doesn't play a part here lar... i got a friend who can bua ka tar on the right corner, but just not left... really it's up to the rider...

    as for single sided swing-arms allowing for better shocks to be used.. i mean.. er... huh? how?? anyway, a suzuki TL uses its custom-made rotary rear shock on its DUAL-sided swing-arm...

    anyway, single sided swing-arms help in terms of weight (usually lightweight/hi-strength alloys) and ease of maintenance like changing of tires, adjustments etc... the most important thing is that it is properly assembled back whenever the wheel comes off. mechanics who know what they're doing use torque wrenches for securing the wheel. a torque wrench allows you to set the torque on the nut when tightening. torque settings are important because the nut can't be too loose or tight for obvious reasons esp when the wheel is mounted on one side only... hence my emphasis on the proper use of tools.

    ah.. single or not single still a bike... i love my dual swing-arm planet...
