    Same traffic rules for all

    I APPLAUD the Traffic Police's new campaign to educate young motorcyclists on road safety. It is high time that appropriate attention be given to this issue.

    In particular, I am very concerned that motorcyclists tend to forget that they are always at the losing end when they are involved in accidents. Yet, some seem oblivious to such dangers with their reckless and thoughtless actions, such as squeezing between vehicles and making illegal turns.

    However, I am just as baffled by the fact that motorcyclists are not required to observe the same traffic regulations as drivers, even though it is proven that motorcycles are more vulnerable to accidents.

    For instance, they can squeeze anywhere they want even if they are in a single lane, or overtake from any side, in any direction, whichever allows them to move on faster.

    But when they are hit by a car in the course of such manoeuvres, they are injured and the driver gets penalised.

    Surely, this does not make sense. To promote road safety for all vehicles, should not all vehicles, big or small, be subject to the same traffic rules and regulations?

    Only by imposing such regulations on all can road users better predict the actions of others and hence minimise the chances of accidents, especially those involving motorcyclists.

    Education through campaigns may raise awareness on road safety, but it is only by imposing and enforcing strict traffic regulations can we can hope to raise the standards of road safety.

    Errant drivers pose danger to m-cyclists

    AS A driver and motorcyclist for the past 10 years, I feel sad when I hear of young people losing their lives on Singapore roads.

    From the article, 'They're young, they've wheels and they're dead before 30' (ST, March 25), motorcycle fatalities as a percentage of total fatalities remain high at approximately 45 per cent.

    Speeding, failure to keep proper control of the motorcycle and not keeping a proper lookout for other road users have been cited as the causes of these unfortunate fatalities.

    However, I feel that under certain circumstances, not only should we look at these deaths from a riding perspective, but also look into the skills and attitude of other road users.

    While 'P' platers, with their lack of road experience, generally pose a greater risk to themselves on the road, we must not overlook the fact that some drivers risk the lives of motorcyclists by driving without consideration or due care.

    I have often seen drivers switching lanes or making turns without signalling or without checking their blind spots, almost hitting the vehicles at their sides. Drivers who tailgate slower vehicles are also a common sight.

    In the event that these errant drivers hit another vehicle, both parties usually pull over to the side of the road to sort things out. If the same thing happened to a motorcyclist, blood is often spilt on the road.

    I believe that putting Class 2B trainees through additional courses and tests is not a cure-all.

    The Traffic Police should also look into the need to raise awareness among drivers with regards to proper road etiquette in relation to other road users, be it other drivers, motorcyclists or pedestrians.

    In the end, no matter how many defensive riding courses a motorcyclist takes, all it takes is for a drunk or distracted driver to slam into a motorcyclist from behind, and the result would be fatal.
