    1. You wont really know if its worth buying, because you have no experience with bike engines. And at the bike shop, they have minimum petrol in the bike which you most like going to hear it a while instead of hearing its problem on the longer running. You can only maybe hope that the following is in good order before you buy.

    a) engine sounds smooth, with no pop sounds or smoke from the exhaust.
    b) chains looks tight instead of slacking
    c) tire threads are there or that the shop will change it for you.
    d) handlebar is straight when you sit on it and stand it upright straight and its looks straight and the wheels are all aligned straight. get a friend to look at it for you.

    2) deal is not that easily done, you usually have to put in a deposit with the seller or the shop and provide them your nric and licence for them to do the transfer at the LTA. If its a private seller, you need to double check at the LTA that the bike does not have outstanding installments from any bike shop. If it does, there is no way you can do a transfer yourself.

    3) And no. You need to get a insurance first before you can do a bike transfer.
    So if you are not doing it with the shop, you need to get the log card or letter of that bike, bring to a insurance agency and get that insurance yourself. No. there is no need for anyone above 21 to sign for you. I am not sure on this point because I never went thru the under 21 process. Being old has its privileges I guess.

    You will need, log card of the bike, your licence, your NRIC & preferably the licence of a sub rider, preferably a family member or a really close friend that can help you out in times of trouble. ANd of course, your cash.

    4) Yes, insurance can be done on the spot, if you are doing it yourself. Then you go to LTA and do the transfer of the ownership with the insurance, inspection cert (if any); the log card or letter; the owners NRIC and yours. Then you can ride the bike home after you have paid the seller.

    SOme private sellers might prefer to be on the spot to sign for the transfer instead of trusting you.

    Important note:

    a) the bike is cleared of installments or the bike shop control or LTA will not allow transfer.
    b) When was the last inspection? Need new inspection?
    c) Road tax due?
    d) Insurance ready
    e) Transfer fees?
    f) Petrol. Make sure you top up the petrol once you take over the bike. Ride a short distance if possible, to the petrol station first.

    viciouskitty74 has done a great job on this writeup! It'll really help those who are getting their bikes for the first time and I'm sure it's a rather exciting moment for them. There are some points, however, I would like to correct.

    [quote]Originally posted by viciouskitty74:
    [b]
    a) engine sounds smooth, with no pop sounds or smoke from the exhaust.

    smoke from the exhaust is actually common for 2-strokes bike. So, if anyone is getting 2-strokes bike, you don't have to worry about the smoke actually.

    3) And no. You need to get a insurance first before you can do a bike transfer.
    So if you are not doing it with the shop, you need to get the log card or letter of that bike, bring to a insurance agency and get that insurance yourself. No. there is no need for anyone above 21 to sign for you. I am not sure on this point because I never went thru the under 21 process. Being old has its privileges I guess.

    You will need, log card of the bike, your licence, your NRIC & preferably the licence of a sub rider, preferably a family member or a really close friend that can help you out in times of trouble. ANd of course, your cash.

    There is also no need for the log card at all now. I recently had a transfer of bike and I didn't even see the log card. After doing the transfer for the bike to be under your name, LTA will issue you a letter stating you're now the owner of the bike as well as other information regarding the bike such as the number of transfers.

    For the insurance part, you'll just have to call NTUC and tell them what's the number plate of the bike you're buying the insurance (that is if you're buying the insurance from NTUC)

    After that, just head down to any of their branch to pay the premium and you'll get the policy.
