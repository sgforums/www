    Opposition also eyes Holland-Bukit Timah, signalling plans to contest 5 GRCs so far

    By Zakir Hussain

    http://www.straitstimes.com/News/Home/Story/STIStory_594599.html

    Tanjong Pagar GRC could see its first electoral contest since it was formed in 1991 at the coming polls, if the Reform Party (RP) delivers on its plans.

    Party chief Kenneth Jeyaretnam has declared the party's intention to contest the six-member group representation constituency, if it has enough candidates.

    The People's Action Party (PAP) team representing Tanjong Pagar GRC is led by Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew and includes Acting Minister for Information, Communications and the Arts Lui Tuck Yew. Since 1991, Tanjong Pagar GRC MPs have all been returned unopposed.

    The number of GRCs that the RP plans to contest has grown to five, with the latest additions being Tanjong Pagar GRC and Holland-Bukit Timah GRC. Mr Jeyaretnam, 51, mentioned those two GRCs for the first time in an interview with Malay daily Berita Harian, which was published yesterday.

    Earlier, the RP said it would contest Hong Kah and West Coast GRCs, and the Chua Chu Kang single-member constituency (SMC), on its own. It would also run in the Bishan-Toa Payoh GRC in a tie-up with Potong Pasir MP Chiam See Tong's Singapore People's Party.

    Contacted by The Sunday Times, Mr Jeyaretnam said: 'One of our objectives is to see that every seat in Parliament is contested.'

    Yesterday, two-term Tanjong Pagar GRC MP Indranee Rajah welcomed the possibility of an electoral fight. 'Tanjong Pagar MPs are ready for a contest,' she said, adding that opposition members have been seen visiting parts of the sprawling GRC.

    It covers a large area in central Singapore and spans Balestier Road in the east to MacRitchie Reservoir in the north and Commonwealth in the west.

    The RP picked its five GRCs because they had not been contested in recent elections. The son of the late opposition leader J.B. Jeyaretnam added that West Coast and Tanjong Pagar GRCs also have historical connections with his father.

    'My father contested Telok Blangah, which is now in West Coast GRC, and was elected MP twice in Anson, which now comes under Tanjong Pagar GRC.' Mr J. B. Jeyaretnam contested Telok Blangah in the 1979 by-election and the 1980 general election, losing both times.

    He became Singapore's first opposition MP in 15 years when he won Anson in the 1981 by-election. Then the Workers' Party chief, he defended the seat in 1984. But he had to vacate it in 1986 after being fined $5,000 for making a false declaration on his party accounts. He set up the Reform Party in 2008 but died later that year.

    The opposition parties are now gearing up for a general election, which must be held by February 2012.

    The National Solidarity Party has been targeting Jalan Besar, Jurong and Tampines GRCs and the MacPherson SMC. The Workers' Party has been active in Aljunied and East Coast GRCs, as well as Joo Chiat and Nee Soon East SMCs.

    The Singapore Democratic Alliance is contesting the Pasir Ris- Punggol GRC, while the Singapore Democratic Party is targeting the Bukit Panjang SMC.

    If the opposition parties' plans come to fruition, this election could be the most keenly contested since 1988, when 70 out of 81 seats were contested.

    Mr Kenneth Jeyaretnam told Berita Harian that the RP's prospective Malay candidates, to be named later, include professionals and former civil servants.

    The RP now has 100 members and as many volunteers. It announced six party candidates in May, and yesterday, Mr Jeyaretnam said there are six more seriously considering standing.

    He also told Berita Harian that 'we want to end the policy of treating Malays as a group that is suspicious, who cannot be trusted to hold key posts in security and defence'.

    'We don't want to treat or typecast anybody on the basis of race,' he added.

    Hong Kah GRC MP Zaqy Mohamad refuted this, saying: 'He is outdated. No such policy exists. There is a lot more progress that Malays can look forward to under the PAP and its policies.'
