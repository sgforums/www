    Cheap, world-class healthcare? You know that's not possible: Khaw
    by Hoe Yeen Nie
    04:46 AM May 06, 2011

     

     

    SINGAPORE - With the S$8-figure being bandied about by some of his opponents at election rallies, Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan (picture) clarified yesterday that the sum referred to his out-of-pocket expenses, not the total bill, for his heart-bypass last year.

    The bulk of his bill - S$25,000 for a week's stay in a Class A ward - was paid for with his MediShield and Medisave, he said.

    "I paid the normal bill, no special arrangement. So all those lies about, oh because he's a minister, he got special deals. It was cheap, only S$8! But why was it S$8, why was it so affordable? Because I used the 3Ms!" he told reporters before his walkabout in Woodlands yesterday morning.

    The 3Ms refer to Medisave, MediShield and Medifund, key components of Singapore's healthcare financing system.

    "So, yes, I paid S$8 out of pocket. That was what I said but they conveniently blocked out what I said, they said I paid S$8 for a bill. Minister special.

    "They did not say so but they were hinting that the minister abused the healthcare system for his own benefit. We never do. As ministers, we pay our own bills," said Mr Khaw, the anchor minister for the People's Action Party's (PAP) five-man team in Sembawang Group Representation Constituency (GRC).

    The team is facing a challenge from a Singapore Democratic Party (SDP) team led by Dr James Gomez.

    Mr Khaw also took aim at several of SDP's proposals on healthcare.

    He said there is no need to raise the health budget to at least S$10 billion a year, as the SDP proposes, up from the current S$4 billion.

    The experience in other countries has shown that spending more on healthcare does not lead to better outcomes, he added.

    We have achieved similar outcomes, and, in fact, in various indicators we are even better. We are among the best in the world and we achieve it with 4 per cent of GDP. So why do you want to move away from that?

    "Once you raise the spending level, that becomes the minimum level. It only grows from there, it doesn't drop from there," Mr Khaw said.

    While the minister emphatises with residents' concerns over rising costs, he said his aim is for healthcare to be affordable, not cheap.

    "How do you achieve world-class healthcare which is also cheap? You know that's not possible," Mr Khaw said.

    With Singapore having to spend more on healthcare as the population ages, his priority is to ensure long-term care is available in the community.

    "The ministries of Health all over the world are really ministries of Illness. That is wrong, we should go back to basics, which is, we are Ministry of Health," Mr Khaw said.

    Instead, he wants to build up community wellness programmes to encourage Singaporeans to stay healthy.

    The SDP had also proposed funding the increase in the health budget by cutting spending in defence and other areas.

    Mr Khaw said that doing so would directly impact the foundations of Singapore's prosperity.
