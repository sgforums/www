    OK, the other thread was a bit tangential. In direct answer to your first question, you will need to following to be a registered psychologist in Singapore:

    1) A Master's degree or Doctorate and;
    2) at least 400 hours of supervised practice and;
    3) full membership of the Singapore Psychological Society

    Those requirements are fairly relaxed. I'm registered in Australia, and I needed a minimum of 960 hours of supervised practice to qualify. I eventually got 1600 hours as part of my doctorate - the Master's students got 960. Also, the degree had to be vetted to ensure that it met the registration board's requirements.

    Singapore is not the most psychologically-aware country in the world, so you will probably find that pay scales are behind other countries. When I graduated as an organisational psychologist, I was worth A$72,000 (about S$90,000) a year - I seriously doubt you would find the same deal here. Also, pay varies according to the field of psychology in which you practice - as a comparison, a counselling psychologist with a doctorate would probably be worth A$40,000 on a good day.

    Ultimately, you will find that, pay wise, breaking out on your own serves you much better than being employed as a psychologist. Now that I'm running my own show, 72 grand a year is an unacceptable figure.
    As for what university to get in, I'm not sure about Singaporean universities - perhaps one of the other guys here might know the state of affairs in Singapore. What I can tell you about is the distinction between undergraduate and postgraduate courses. Some universities are better at one than the other - I did my undergrad at Swinburne University, which gave me a very good grounding in the basics but could not offer adequate academic support for postgrad research. On the other hand, I did my postgrad at Melbourne university, which provided great support for research but sucked at undergraduate training. The Honours students I tutored while I was a postgrad were very weak in their basic skills, and I had to start teaching them from the ground up.

    17 Feb 05, 22:08
    Okay, one thing to be aware of is the distinction between practicing as a psychologist and using psychology as part of your work.

    If you're going to practice as a psychologist, you have the following fields:

    Clinical psychologists
    These guys deal in mental health issues, usually in a hospital or clinic setting.

    Neuropsychologists
    Neuropsychologists deal with specific brain functions, especially brain related to injuries or neurological dysfunction, also in a hospital setting.

    Health psychologists
    These guys work with other health professionals in clinics and hospitals, to attend to the psychological aspects of treatment, e.g. getting patients to follow their treatment regimens.

    Counselling psychologists
    Counselling psychologists, um, well, counsel.

    Educational psychologists
    Educational psych deal in school-related psych issues, covering both learning and social/emotional problems. I'm not sure if you also need to be a qualified teacher to be an ed psych in Singapore.

    Social psychologists
    Social psychologists specialise in the interactions between people and their social environment.

    Organisational psychologists
    Org psychs deal with business-related psych issues. The vast majority of the work is related to HR, but it also covers things like industrial relations and organisational behaviour. Organisational psych is usually paired with industrial psych, which covers areas like ergonomics and human factors.

    Note that the above list is a very broad description of the fields. You can also see that the different fields can interact with each other - for example, counselling can be part of clinical practice, and social psych is part of organisational behaviour.

    The above are all fields where one practices under the title of "psychologist". On the other hand, you have jobs in which psychology comes in handy. The obvious examples are HR and advertising. If you're in HR or you're an advertiser, you can use what you've learned in psychology without having to practice as a psychologist.

    Hope that starts to clear things up.
