Reflections Of A BMT Instructor

  • Jason
    18 Jun 00, 21:19
    Today was the passing out parade of the batch of recruit whom i was with for the past 2 months. It marks the end of the Basic Military Training (BMT) phase of their National Service life. This is one of the few days the men actually get to bring their loved ones into and around the camp ( how is that so immensely attractive, i have no idea ). Also a time for the rest of us to wonder once again why is it that other people's girlfriends and sisters are always prettier, but that's another story.

    The day's events were divided into four parts - the contingent march pass and the speech by the obligatory VIP, a demonstration by the winning platoon of the drill competition, a bayonet fighting demonstration, and mock attacks mounted on two of our 4-storey living quaters.

    And so went the speech about how the drill competition fosters teamwork among the men and obedience to the commanders. Do what you are told and do it well ? So much for thinking soldiers..

    Teamwork ? Hardly. Men held together out of fear of being punished for the one wrong turn or step. Men getting paid wayang money to do wayang shit. Brainwashed into thinking what little they are taught in this army of ours in the two years they are stuck here will make a difference when the time of judgement comes.

    Bayonet fighting one-on-one. Bayonet fighting one-on-two. Parry. Thrust. Bash. Slash. Stab. Enemy makes feeble attempt at blocking attack. Enemy falls. Enemy dies. Audience claps. In the real life, the enemy fights back. In the real life, death is not very entertaining, not to the sane ones at least.

    Parents whose sons are plucked from the cradle, barely out of school, and taught how to kill, applauding their children's prowess at delivering death. It's a beautiful world we live in, isn't it ?

    Shortly after, sounds of gunfire and thunderflashes signalled the start of the mock attack. Charge up, charge in, fire some blanks, throw some flashes, declare the zone clear. Lots of loud noises. Lots of drama. Lots of applause. Lots of cheers. I wonder if these are the same people who laughed when they saw the one-armed soldier picking up his detached arm when SPR came to Singapore.

    And I thought I was mad.
