    Improving bus trips in the city

    Straits Times Review
    29 July 2006

    SOON enough, bus passengers can expect better service. Guidelines issued by the Public Transport Council, an independent body regulating bus services and public-transport fares, set 19 service standards that SBS Transit and SMRT Buses will need to meet - or face penalties. By six months from September, for instance, operators will have to ensure that peak-time buses are on average no more than 95 per cent full and 15 minutes apart. These are improvements that go to the heart of what passengers demand. Indeed, we are heartened by the fact that the Council's guidelines are based on customer feedback that capture actual travelling experience.
    Nevertheless, there will be the inevitable gripe that the guidelines don't go far enough. This is natural. But would-be complainers must realise that the Council can't be expected to accommodate every demand. Two things bear keeping in mind when considering service improvements. The first is that bus operators are private companies, and are justifiably entitled to make reasonable profits for the service they provide. So there must be a sensible trade-off between what commuters want and what they are willing to pay. Service can always be improved by piling on excess capacity, but does this make financial sense to both commuters (who will have to shoulder the cost) and operators (who will have to manage a far larger infrastructure)? Related to this is point No. 2: Singapore is spread out into far-flung suburban neighbourhoods, rather than concentrated into a few compact centres. Much can be done with far less in crowded Hong Kong and New York. But, for example, connecting the interiors of every neighbourhood here with buses means huge outlays against what are likely to be small riderships. Geography dictates the nature of service.

    And speaking of geography, a fair comparison for Singapore might be something like the vast landscape of greater Los Angeles. Now, consider how Singapore measures up. Certainly, things here can still be improved. But for this, commuters must appreciate the importance of focusing on what is possible. Our bottom line is that for the fares commuters pay, Singapore's bus - and train - services are probably as good as they can realistically get. Anything better will have to come at a price.

    PTC should pinpoint inaccuracies in bus fares
    Letter from danny chua

    It is good for the Public Transport Council (PTC) to set the service standards that SBS Transit and SMRT Bus must meet.

    When I take the SBS Transit bus No 156 from Clementi interchange to Holland Road (opposite the Cold Storage Jelita branch), the usual fare is 64 cents, but sometimes I get charged 85 cents.

    There is apparently an error rate of between 5 and 10 per cent, due to their Global Positioning System (GPS) being disrupted by overhead bridges, tall buildings, other stronger telecommunication signals and trees. (In Hong Kong, its bus system error rate is as high as 70 per cent due to its hilly terrain).

    It is also a hassle to call the hotline for a refund or to go to the nearest refund centre. It is not worth the effort, time and more money spent.

    There should be a billing accuracy standard based on the Six Sigma methodology (which emphasises the application of statistical tools to critical business processes); or an error rate below 1 per cent.

    Fine the bus operators if they do not meet it.

    Let's keep things clean
    Letter from graham mceune

    I applaud the proactive steps taken by the PTC to improve the standard of service for Singapore's commuters. But I was surprised that no mention was made of cleanliness.

    Compared to Singapore's taxis, which I rate the cleanest in the world, and the MRT and its stations Â— which, considering the volume of traffic, is excellent Â— some of the older buses appear unhygienic.

    I understand the logistics: The hours buses spend on the road and the number of passengers carried, and so on. I also understand the business sense of running buses into the ground rather than buying new ones, as well as the fiscal benefits of skimping on nonessential maintenance.

    But from whichever angle I look at it, I can see no reason for not at least keeping them clean.

    Surely the cost of mopping the floors with an antibacterial wash, wiping over the seats and bell pushes and handrails, and cleaning the grease and grime from the windows once a week, would not make the bus companies that much poorer.
