    SINGAPORE: The Public Transport Council has revised the service guidelines for public buses to help ensure they meet specific standards, and with recent changes to the law, it will, for the first time, have the teeth to enforce compliance.

    Service standards for buses have actually been in place since 1994.

    The problem was they were route specific and there were no penalties if they were not complied with.

    Bus commuters would identify with the main problems highlighted in a survey done by the Public Transport Council.

    Waiting for buses was top on the complaint list, followed by over-crowding and long travel times to complete a bus journey.

    All this changed in April this year when the PTC Act was amended, giving the council new powers to ensure compliance and impose fines if standards are not met.

    The standards cover six essential aspects of service -

    * Bus service reliability - for example, at least 85 percent of bus services should not deviate more than 5 minutes from the scheduled frequency
    * Loading - for example, weekday peak period bus loading shouldn't exceed 95 percent
    * Safety
    * Provision of information
    * Availability of bus services
    * Integration with other modes of public transport

    Designed to overcome the most common complaints, the standards will kick in once the two main bus operators - SBS Transit and SMRT - get their new bus licences in 2 months.

    PTC will then monitor these standards under actual operating conditions for half a year, tweaking them if necessary, by early next year, after which fines kick in for non-compliance.

    These can go up to $100,000 per contravention.

    "We can certainly strive and achieve continuous improvement and if each year, we can improve by a few percentage points, then, in time to come, it should reach a level not where no one will complain, but at least the majority of commuters will be satisfied with it," said PTC Chairman Gerard Ee.

    The PTC says it will audit the performance standards measures submitted by the operators.

    Singapore's two biggest bus operators, SBS Transit and SMRT, who'll be affected by the new service standards, say they welcome them.

    In a statement, SBS Transit said the new standards will act as a goal post, spurring not just them, but the whole industry.

    To meet the standards, SBS Transit says it will be stepping up efforts it has already made in the past few years, such as increasing the fleet and number of bus services, as well as revamping their website.

    Meanwhile, SMRT says they've also been continually pursuing improvements, like adjusting schedule & route to match demand, introducing new services as well as providing free services. - CNA /dt

    PTC Chairman says timing of new service standards for buses not linked to fare issue

    SINGAPORE: Public Transport Council Chairman Gerard Ee says the announcement of new service standards for buses was not meant as a precursor to any possible fare increases next month, and that the timing was merely coincidental.

    "We can choose to do whichever piece of the work first but there is a certain logic to it - some will interpret it as a sweetening of the pill - but imposing standards is a priority and the question of fares will come later on," said Mr Ee.

    He did add, however, that operational efficiencies, which include incidents like the recent NorthEast Line stoppage, will be taken into consideration when looking at any applications for fare increases. - CNA /dt
