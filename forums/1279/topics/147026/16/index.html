    Actually, I am not quite convinced that the slow down was caused by the computers reaching their capacities. This is because the second-link bridge is about 2 kilometers long. When it was full of cars on two lanes, the next car that joined the queues had about (2 lanes x 2000 meters / 4 meters per car = 1000 cars) in front of it. That car took about 2 hours to get to the other side of the checkpoint. This implies the checkpoint took about 2 hours to clear the 1000 cars in front of it, giving a clearance rate of about (1000 cars / 120 minutes = 8.33 cars per minute). If each car was packed with 5 people, then the load on the computers generated by the incoming car users at the Tuas checkpoint was only about (8.33 cars per minute x 5 people per car = 41.67 people per minute).

    It was reported elsewhere that car users at the Woodlands checkpoint also experienced wait times of a similar scale. So, we can make a simplistic assumption that both checkpoints had similar loads during that period. Then the total load on the immigration computers for car users was only about (41.67 x 4 = 166 people per minute). This load seems a little too low compared to the expected capacity of the immigration computer system.

    If we assume 600 people per minute mentioned earlier is a reasonable estimate of the combined car user clearance capacity of the two checkpoints, then the checkpoints were operating at only about 27.67 percent efficiency during that period.

    Therefore, the long wait time experienced was likely to be due to computer system outages, caused by humans intentionally, or because of poor human resource planning.

    There are two kinds of passports that Malaysians can use when traveling to Singapore. These are the restricted and international passports.

    The Malaysian immigration staff do not really care to check restricted passports, because the immigration department simply does not have the capacity to handle the volume. People usually use a blue cover for restricted passports, so this kind of passports is easily identifiable. However, it does mean foreigners who resemble locals can easily enter and leave Malaysia through the causeway and second-link checkpoints without being recorded. I think the staff also do random checks based on the age, attire and behavior of the passport holders. Legitimate frequent travelers are usually of a certain age group, wear certain attire and exhibit a certain behavior, making them identifiable to some extent. Anyway, this kind of passports will cease to be valid within the next couple of years due to the lack of relevant security features.

    New international passports have embedded chips. They usually have a red cover. But as far as I know, the readers are only installed at the autogates and counters. There have been concerns that personal data can be read unknowingly from quite a bit of distance away. But so far this effect does not seem to have been taken advantage of at the two checkpoints for security and clearance efficiency enhancements. The holders of this kind of passports need to be recorded by the computer system when going through the checkpoints, though stampings are no longer done. Stampings are done for old international passports, which do not have embedded chips.
