    We do run in the engines. If we do not run in, then our engines won't last. This is applicable to all engines regardless its a car or bus or motorcycle. Engines are very costly. I'm not sure whether there're any preservative chemicals as when I joined SBST, the buses were already in storage. But the CNG buses do not have fuel tanks for preservative chemicals like the Vappro anti-Corrosion Inhibitors which SAF is using. The fuel tanks are replaced by 8 gas tanks on the roof of the bus. These gas are ignited by spark plugs. So the engine is somewhat similar to a petrol engine. The CNG bus engine is 'boxer'styled like a BMW motorcycle as the engine is mounted lying down. (pistons move horizontally.) But it's also technically correct to say its an inline 6 cylinder turbo charged petrol/CNG engine. If pple were to say CNG buses are not safe as these gas will detonate if leak, it is not true. For the CNG to detonate, we need to have 10%-15% CNG and the rest normal atmospheric air, plus a condition of minimum 680 degress celcius, plus a mode of ignition (spark). Only 1 place in this world are we able to achieve such a condition. That is inside the combustion chamber of the engine where temperatures can hit as high as 2000 degree C. There is no way anyone other than God can create such a condition in the normal atmosphere. If there is indeed a leakage, then all that it will cause is a reduced mileage. But such problems will be tackled when the bus returns to the depot for PM servicing frequently. All workshop staffs will do our best to ensure only the best goes to the route groups which in turn are presented to passengers by our BC.

    I'm monitoring the performance and camouflaged problems of the CNG buses while its on the road. And will keep tabs on all technical problems that might create any possibilities to cause discomfort or danger.     MS, you seem to have some pretty good knowledge about the Volvo B10BLEs. What can you tell us about the fuel system of the Volvo GH10B engine? In particular, where abouts in the intake manifold are the injection valves located in relation to the butterfly valve?

    For comparison, the Mercedes-Benz M 447 hLAG engine (Citaro and OC 500 LE) has 12 Bosch gas injectors mounted on a metering block that injects fuel into a mixer located just above the butterfly valve. Injection quantities are closely monitored by the universal exhaust gas oxygen sensor, ensuring that variations in fuel quality are compensated for, right down to a minimum methane number of 68, and that the air/fuel mixture can be maintained close to a target setting, usually around Lambda=1.55, for the lowest possible emissions.

    Things that I do know about the GH10B engine are:

    1. it was replaced by the Volvo GH10C Euro 4 engine, which has now replaced by the current model Volvo G9A Euro 5 engine.
    2. it is a LEAN-BURN engine, running on a weak mixture of air and fuel.
    3. it is an OPEN-LOOP engine, meaning that the engine control unit has no provision for exhaust gas oxygen sensor feedback - critical for compensating for varying fuel quality as well as manufacturing tolerances and the usual wear and tear. The mechanics thus have the job of making sure that the engines are set correctly to match local fuel quality.
