    Engine

    Leyland - Cummins Engine. The most headache engine I have in hand. The Cyld head is in one whole block. That means if you want to replace 1 cyld head for weak valve springs or just 1 damaged valve, you have to replace all 6 cylinder head at one go. And you need a floor crane to do it. Waste time waste labour waste money.

    The FIP is a pressure timing pump. The only good point is that the FIP do not need to catch engine timing to replace. The bad points are that it relies on a dump valve to prevent fuel flow back. So as to maintain pressure to prevent air lock. So it helps to cut off the pressure. There is a solenoid fuel valve in front to help allow fuel in or cut off fuel. If the dump valve is not working, it will not allow ample pressurised fuel into the fuel valve to start operation. That means the bus has a max speed of <10KM/H. And this always happens. This month alone I had 2 cases. One of them on 9056 and the other on 9060. I know there're more than these 2 cases. And turbo chargers are very problematic. Last time there were quite a number of fire and smoke cases with Turbo chargers. To us, not even the slightest smoke is tolerable. With this kind of high expectations, my other colleague who's in charge of turbo chargers always keep high alert on such items. But for Volvo, we're more at ease.

    Volvo - 6 cyld Volvo engine. All 6 cyld are individual. That means you only need to replace one if need be during a top overhaul. Simple straight forward and easy to do. Saves on money, labour and down time. No need floorcrane. FIP is very straight forward. Though the FIP design is complicated like the MB0405, the breakdown rate on this is very rare.

    Mercedes Benz - The legendary MB0405 is so easy to fix. Likewise with Volvo, the cyld head is same in design. Except the engine is somewhat like a boxer engine. Horizontally lying down. Both Volvo and Mercedes need to catch engine timing. If the wrong timing is caught, the bus can crank but cannot start. But engine timing is basic maintenance skills of technicians. Not a problem at all.

    Front axle

    Leyland - The only insane design I have ever seen. Incorporated the front axle design of a small car for the use of a large heavy weight commercial bus. The most problematic @##$% axles I have come across. Leylands have more axle breakdowns than any other buses. The front axle wheel hub bearing is lubricated by grease!!!! What the?! Everybody else is using proper engine oil or GL5 to lubricate which helps to reduces heat and better permeability. But Leyland uses grease?! Grease melts around constant 120DegC to 150DegC. But Oils do not. When grease melts, it leaks out and also loses the properties of a grease. Then the wheel bearing once loses lubrication will be at risk of shattering. I have to upgrade the grease to an extremely high graded grease to prevent this problem from coming. Also periodically call back the bus and replace the grease off and on to prevent the wheel from flying off. God knows how much money I've wasted on this. Grease is talking about thousands per drum.

    Mercedes & Volvo - Wheel hub is lubricated by engine oil. No problem at all. Wheel hub oil seal can leak. But this only happens once in a blue moon. Until now I have only seen 1 time in my 1 year plus of work. Even when in the SAF with MAN trucks, I ave seen less than 5 times even with the crazy SAF styled driving over super terrains.

    There're many more I can explain which will cover the whole page. God saves Soon Lee with Volvos... Haha.... I hope the time when Leylands go to the scrap yard come quickly.[/u]
