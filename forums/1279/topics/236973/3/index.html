    Take the hammer for example. If you throw the hammer up into the air, the hammer head part, which is the heaviest part of the whole hammer, will always wants to fall first. However, if the hammer head is positioned in the middle (which is weird ) , the whole hammer may want to fall straight, in a level manner, meaning the whole body falls together, as compared with head first in the first explanation.

    Saw this explanation on Discovery channel of dunno what show, cannot rmbr, while explaining the problems of rear engine cars. This also applies to rear engined buses. If the bus speeds and turns, the heavier rear part with the engine, may cause the back of the bus to be "pushed" sideways, causing the bus to be out of control. However, if the engine is positioned in the middle, the weight is distributed evenly and the chances of the bus being "pushed" out is lessened.

    In other words, the heavier rear part of rear engined buses will have a greater inertia at the rear when turning, as compared with mid-engined buses.

    FYI, the engine of the O405 is also placed horizontally. Actually, the reason why horizontal engines are used on both B10M and O405 is not because of having a lower centre of gravity like what Subaru claims, instead since the engine is horizontal or "pancake", theres no need for extra space/room for underfloor equipment, which explains why B10M has a horizontal engine. If the B10M were to have a horizontal engine, the platform height would be so high due to the need for space for underfloor equipment that its platform height would almost same to those tourist coaches.

    Buses that are fully low floor, but uses vertical engines, has the engine placed in transverse position, in order to enable full low floor. An example is the N113CRB (altho not low floor), its engine was placed in transverse position, meaning engine is parallel to the axle. Another example of low floor buses with vertical engines placed in transverse position is the B9TL.
