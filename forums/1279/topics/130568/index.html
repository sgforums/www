  • The_Bus_Guide
    7 May 05, 11:54
    Albion was purchased by Leyland Motors Ltd. in 1951. After Leyland and Albion design teams co-operated with each other, Leyland engines were seen in Albion vehicles and Albion gearboxes and axles turned up in Leyland trucks and buses. After the British Leyland Motors Corp. was founded in 1968, Leyland removed the less popular Albion models. By 1970 only the Clydesdale and Viking models were available, and the engine options were confined to Leyland 401, 600 and 680 engines.

    The Allison automatic gearbox was installed later. This somewhat lightens the driver's workload. The Viking was a hardy and straight-forward vehicle. There's no power steering and the gear selector is stiff. I've heard that a driver once dislocated his shoulder driving one of the them. Despite these setbacks, I still love those loud, fast and bumpy Albion Vikings. Arang...arang...arang...hehehe.

    I recall the suspension ride comfort on the LP 1113 not as good as on OF 1413/1417, especially when seated @ the extreme rear ! More truck than bus like ! But found it still better than on Volvo B7F/B57 years later !!

    LP 1113 front overhang very short & came with the original M-B front body just like on their trucks of the time, complete with single piece 3 wiper windscreen & full dashboard with the '1113' in chrome characters like the '200' or '280 S' you also find in their cars of that time. M-B wanted 'consistency' i suppose whether on their cars, trucks or buses !

    Unfortunately the full dashboard was not preserved when SBS replaced the junky (non M-B) rest of the body with a completely new design from Soon Chow around 1979 & only the instrumentation was retained. They should have least retained the M-B original front part of the body as the quality & condition was superior to the rest of the (non M-B) bodywork used. More original character thus would have been preserved.

    So i don't think there was any 'recycling' of the (lousy junky local) bodywork which were in such bad state only fit for scrap. However the bus chassis part of the bus was still in excellent serviciable condition so it was still worth to overhaul & continue to use it but with a new body instead.

    As the OF 1413 is longer than the newer OF 1315 or OH 1318, not likely the body on the OF 1413 would fit onto the shorter newer chassi.

    24 Jun 05, 03:41
    I believe all the M-B using Marcopolo & Denning bodywork in Singapore are OH 1318. For Marcopolo i think Comfort has 'absorbed' some from previously independent private school bus owners (like the Caio ones) & for Denning i think AZ Bus operator (yellow & green colour body ?) have some. Of course the rest of these here are likely scattered over some independent private bus owners.
    There should be a few dozen units here in total maybe in Ang Mo Kio there's 1 Denning near the plan printing shop in Avenue 10 area.

    The Denning is less obvious to identify as if the bodywork was repainted the Denning name & C&C logo may not have been reproduced ! But the M-B original metallic-silver plastic coach wheel covers should still be there, unless stolen !
    The rear windscreen is quite usually large & curved towards the top, like that of the O 404. That i find is a major identifying feature of the Dennings here.

    Besides Marcopolo, Caio & Alan Denning, the OH 1318 (likely chassi used if not then OH 1418 or OH 1421 L possibly) here also can be seen using a look-alike body (non original M-B i'll say) of the O 404, complete with the O 404 rear lampset & front 3-element headlamps. But it doesn't have the 3-dimensional side panelling the O 404 is famous for & the glasswork is too vertical rather than curved like the real thing ! In Singapore they marketed this body as a school bus (!) instead of a luxury class coach which was what the O 404 was intended to be ! This lookalike O 404 body here was produced by a retired M-B engineer who i believe produced the real O 404 back in Germany. But the bodywork & completed bus with chassis was imported CBU to Singapore made outside Germany in South West Asia. Don't know the name of this coachbuilder & i think this could have been a 'one-off' job done more as a pastime/hobby than as some long term business.

    From what i remember, the O 309 here originally didn't have any auto passenger door & it was open all the time when the bus was used. Only much later on did they retrofit a pneumatic operated door but no stick was used instead the valve tap was like the swing type, like that used in laboratory gas supply taps.
