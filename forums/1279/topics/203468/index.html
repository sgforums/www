    A mentally disabled boy and danger on train 035/3351
    I took MRT train number 035/3351 (035 is the run number and 3351 is the carriage number, in this case its the first carriage) on 4th August 2006, from Clementi station to Outram Park station. I saw the driver wearing earphones when the train entered the station at Clementi.
    The driver sounded the horn several times when train entered Redhill station, after which a mentally disabled boy of around age 14 boarded the train shouting loudly. He even attempted to harass several passengers. In order not to disrupt regular service I decided not to press the emergency button, but to knock on the driver cab door in order to alert the driver of the situation. He did not reply until I alighted at Outram Park, when he came out from the side door, and I told him about the boy and danger it posed to passengers. He agreed to report it to his superiors and in that time the boy had already alighted and walking towards North East Line station.
    I feel very sad again, for the boy and for the passengers who got frightened in the scenario. However I am not questioning the existence of these people in our society, but rather if their actions are monitored and if they are well looked after.
    Plus, the driver of the train was listening to earphones, presumably a MP3 player at the time when he was driving. I saw drivers of SMRT trains reading newspapers and even playing handphone games and sms-ing while on the line of duty. What type of service is this? We definitely would not want another Clementi Station accident of 1993!
    On a side note, I was in Japan for the entire of December last year for vacation and I was impressed by their self-discipline. Train drivers do not read papers or even wander their eyes off the panel and tracks ahead. They are obliged to point at signals and acknowledge them, sound the horn before entering a station (this is very effective in lowering cases of suicide, hence I hope SMRT would adopt this measure). The accuracy of the train stopping at the designated spot is legendary, within centimetres.
    This comment would also be sent to SMRT for their reference, and I do hope that forumnites can comment on this incident and give your thoughts. Thank You.

    1993 train collision incident
    (Source: Wikipedia)

    A collision between two trains took place at around 0750 (SST) on 5 August 1993. It appeared that one train had stopped at the station for longer than usual after experiencing a technical fault, and then a second westward train hit the first. 156 commuters were injured, many of whom were on their way to work. Eight were admitted to three hospitals, while the rest received outpatient treatment.

    A four-member independent panel was subsequently formed by the Mass Rapid Transit Corporation (MRTC) to conduct an inquiry into the accident. The panel was chaired by Chua Koon Hoe, deputy director-general of the Public Works Department, with the members Chang Meng Teng, deputy chairman of the Public Transport Council, and Dr. Natarajan Varaprasad, principal of Temasek Polytechnic. Low Tien Sio, MRTC's general manager (project administration) served as the secretary.

    The results of the inquiry were announced by the MRTC on 19 October 1993. Shortly after the accident The Straits Times had reported that an oil spill on the tracks was responsible for the second train's inability to brake in time. Some 50 litres of oil had spilled from a locomotive doing maintenance work on the track in the pre-dawn hours of 5 August. SMRT staff had been alerted to the spill and had despatched a cleaning crew to the neighbouring Buona Vista station, but approval to access the tracks was delayed. Already ten trains had reported difficulty braking on the track that morning. The 11th train had had to use its emergency brakes upon reaching Clementi station, and while it was waiting for the brakes to be recharged so that it could move off again, the 12th approached the station and could not brake in time to avoid hitting the 11th train.

    The Communications Minister at that time, Mah Bow Tan, said that as the SMRT staff followed procedures and none were found to be negligent, so no staff would be punished. However SMRT promised to look into the problem of oil spillage from maintenance locomotives by replacing the hoses and oil seals on all locomotives, checking all locomotives for leaks upon their return to depots, and assigning a station master to inspect tracks for oil. Procedures were also amended so that trains would not move off from stations preceding the spill until it had been cleaned up.

    No similar incidents have occurred at Clementi or any other MRT station since.

    To my understanding, the operations of the trains are not fully automatic. I do agree that the drivers, or operators, do not need to make announcements and so on, but the operation of the train is definitely not automatic
    The operator controls the train using a control stick, from P4 which means power 4 to B4, brake 4 and ultimately the emergency brake. It is aligned vertically and against normal configuration, the brakes are at the lower end and power at the upper end.
    The train system follows the Japanese system of ATO, Automatic Train Operation. However, do not get fooled by the words, they are simply a monitoring tool to monitor maximum speeds, start and stop. If the train's ATO detector passes by a ATO emmiter which is on the tracks, and emmiting the 'Stop' signal, the alarm would sound. SMRT uses this system's design to install emergency stop buttons on platforms, and hence as the train departs the previous stop, the 'Stop' signal will stop the train. If the train is on its way, then speed limitations will be implemented, all the way to zero.
    There are green lights signalling the maximum speed, in increments of 20kph. Hence there is really no automation, it just cannot pass the maximum speed. How do I know that the trains are not fully automated? In very heavy rain, the train might overrun the platform. The driver would be at fault, not the systems then, as the train did not pass over the ATO emmiter, no alarm would be sounded.
    I am not comparing Japanese train systems, as their network is much larger and drivers work shorter shifts, but I am just giving a comparison. I did not want to argue in this topic, but just to give evryone a gauge as to things going along.
