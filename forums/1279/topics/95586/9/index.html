    Originally posted by CAIX:
    My question is : Which is the single deck pioneer in SBS. I though is the scania fleet.
    The Scania came in 1990, which made it 17 years too late to become the pioneer single deck of SBS. The pioneer buses were way back in 1973, when SBS was created. All of the buses were transferred over from Singapore Traction Company and the Chinese bus companies. Most of them date from the 1960s and were far from roadworthy conditions. The most common models include, ALbion Viking VK/EVK41L/UL, MB LP1113, OF1413 and Nissan 4R94/RX102.

    If you're talking about the first buses which were bought by SBS, then the honour went to Leyland with the Albion Viking EVK55CL/CUL (the CUL was the longer wheelbase version of the CL). This was the first large order of SBS with 255 buses delivered from 1975-1977. Following that was another big order from Leyland in the form of 144 Victories (known within SBS as the Guy Victory rather than Leyland Victory) delivered between 1975-1978.

    I'm inclined to believe why it seems no new public buses in Singapore lately is because the operators here are thinking of 'jumping' from their current Euro-2 vehicles here to Euro-4 & skipping Euro-3 instead. Anyway, i'm beginning to find it somewhat 'awkward' to see Malaysian operators (they 'supposed' to be more backward than the Singapore ones ?) already using new M-B O 500's with Euro-3 potential while even the big operators in Singapore still stuck @ Euro-2 levels on their buses here.

    I find the relative inefficientcy of CNG buses (to the diesel versions) remains a major deterrent to adopting CNG buses on any large scale. Therefore using Euro-4 diesel instead (of CNG) would still ultimately be THE more practical choice, especially with driving range & fuel utilisation efficientcy & efficacy taken into account. No need to bother with bulky CNG tanks which rob quite some space on the bus ! Overall exhaust emissions still low enough to meet Euro-4 standards & no need to be limited to using only low sulphur diesel, which is still more expensive & less readilly available in this part of the world.

    Of late. i notice in Germany they starting to use the Citaro with Euro-4 diesels.
    The new Ad-blue fuel additive on these Euro-4 diesel engines sounds an overall more practical space efficient alternative to having 'bulky' CNG buses. The Ad-blue tank is much smaller with only a small quantity needed to add to the diesel fuel & the technology certainly does seem very promising for large scale practical use eventhough costs go up somewhat due to the Ad-blue additive & the supporting infrastructure of Ad-blue refillng stations required to supply to the fleet operators.

    I believe if the big operators here like SBST were to invest a bit in setting up the Ad-blue supply infrastructure here, not only would it serve their own fleet but can also be offered to private commercial operators of buses, coaches or trucks here in future as they also upgrade to Euro-4 & Euro-5 diesels using this Ad-blue additive solution in time to come. With more demand likely lower bulk buying prices of the additive can then be negotiated with the fuel suppliers.
