    The Leyland Hydracyclic was created in 1975 and was initially used as a standard component on the Leyland Titan. It was derived from the Pneumocyclic and used fluid dynamics instead of air. The uniqueness of the Olympian chassis is that the engine is at the rear while the radiator is up front. This helps improve the enigine's cooling rate and is especially suitable for warm and humid climatic conditions. Proof of the Olympian's popularity was that over 30,000 units were produced and was the reason why Leyland kept on going despite troubled times. Note that the Olympian chassis was the only Leyland design which Volvo retained.

    The Olympian was offered with 2 engine options; the Leyland TL11 and the Gardner 6LXB. Gearbox, however, was restricted to the Hydracyclic. As for the semi-auto or full auto mode, its up to the operator's preferences. Considering the year of its development, it was considered one of the most advanced buses of its time.

I still keep that 2 full page Straits Times launch ad. put up by Cycle & Carriage which shows the left side view of the O 305. The dashboard was similar to that in the next 200 or so units to be ordered for delivery starting from 1984 to 86 & based on what i saw & also on the gear-change characteristics of the early unit i took on route 165, i'm fairly certain in early 80's era they already used the push-button type of full automatic by M-B for M-B.

    My thinking is that why Leyland used their own type of semi-automatics back then instead of full automatics like M-B, ZF or Allison etc. was due to the lower performance outputs of the largely backward Leyland engines (like those used on Atanteans) they probably figured with semi-automatic the vehicle performance might be slightly better with less 'drag' on their engines i.e. for their type of old engines semi-auto will do as they lack the performance levels of the more modern engines to make optimal use of a full automatic gearbox anyway ! Also in their favour is the possible lower costs of a semi-auto box.
    In other words, my belief is that if Leyland had better performing engines like the Germans, Swedes etc. then, they would have used the superior full automatics instead. By the way, any other makes of buses/bus-chassi used these Leyland semi-autos ? Very rare i'll say. I wonder if these Leyland boxes were really that fantastic, other makers would dump the more expensive types & queue up & buy from Leyland instead ?? Leyland then most surely would be most happy to sell since then they're not in too good form financially anyway ?
