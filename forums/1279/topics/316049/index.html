    MC=Demerit point 


    THIS list drove bus drivers up the wall. It showed how many medical certificates (MCs) they had submitted for sick leave.

    It was put up on the staff notice board at the Yio Chu Kang interchange

    And it was apparently meant to shame the 'MC kings'.

    Earlier this month, when The New Paper alerted SBS Transit about this 'shame list', its management ordered supervisors to remove the list immediately.

    Earlier this month, 34 bus drivers from various interchanges spoke to The New Paper individually. As their contracts forbid them from speaking to the media, their real names have been withheld.

    The drivers said they get points in various ways. It is eight demerit points if they drive a bus into the interchange five or more minutes early, and usually three merit points if they confiscate an invalid ez-link card.

    They usually earn half a merit point when they receive a passenger's commendation.

    SMRT, another major bus operator with some 1,700 drivers, said it also has a merit-demerit point system.

    But apparently it does not give demerit points to drivers who fall ill.

    Its spokesman said bus drivers 'may be given demerit points due to disciplinary issues such as being absent without a valid reason, being caught flouting traffic rules like speeding and beating the red light'.

    A pity not all drivers think the same way as you... Sometimes, some SOPs are there for a reason. It's a matter of in the course of abiding to them, whether you understand the reasoning behind such practices.

    1.  Stopping the bus at the bus stop to correct schedule is not being practiced because it will hold up traffic at the bus stop itself and affect the regular operation of other buses calling at the stop.  It is better for the bus to assimilate with the main traffic flow but adjust his travel speed accordingly (but not excessively).

    2.  I don't think the confiscation of cards should be viewed as an "anti-social" act... After all commuters must understand its part of the job to protect the interests of the commuting public at large in barring people from unauthorised misuse of concession passes.

    3.  There's a reason for OCC's existance.  Going off-service has implications on passengers waiting downstream of the route, as one bus is taken off and they will have to wait for the next one after you.  If BCs are given full autonomy to declare a bus off service, then we will have many fan pai buses on the roads and commuters will have to wait for very long before there will finally be one bus that will pick them up.  The OCC is equipped with the necessary monitoring devices to help them see a bigger picture of the whole service route, allowing them to make informed decisions in headway management for the entire service as a whole.

    4.  A BC who is reports to work while he is sick, is a potential danger to the commuters of his bus.  Apart from spreading his sickness to the passengers, a sick person is also unable to concentrate on his job well, and this will have disastrous safety considerations should he be allowed to continue working.  This is something which I believe the top management will not compromise on.

    I hope those pointers mentioned would help you understand more on the purpose of certain established practices, and why it is not always possible for BCs to "do their own thing". =)
