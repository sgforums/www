    origins

    Commuter.

    people started using the word commuter in realtiion to transport travel by the mid 90s.

    The word originated from a form of rather new fare payment scheme introduced in he UK in he early 1920s. it was the idea of seasonpasses.

    then people say that they commute (to commute a sum off money is to change it into another form of legal tender or piece of paper) their cash payment into a season pass card that is to be shown to he driver.

    of course using a season pass being bought from sales office at big prices for monthly travel, such people with the cards would want to off set their cost in getting a season pass. in the end they travel a lot on the bus. they used the word commuter to mean they travel a lot with the card.

    much later the word Commuter became fixed to regardless of whether u have season pass or pay cash.

    +++the word commute also means to change a prison sentence from X years to another YY years.

    fare coinbox

    why is our farebox locked like a security vault today? well according to research, the coinbox originated from America where it was rather unsafe to be a bus driver. then people rob buses of their money. the fare box was introduced with a security lock feature. this means u drop he coin down a hole into the vault n no one can get to it

    Later the practice crossed the Atlantic ocean over to he fair shores of Europe. the practice readily got adapted not for security reasons, but for the fact it speed up bus boarding. u juzz drop ur coin into a box. this was replacing the poor bus conductor then whom punched tickets using Bell punch (actual company providing the conductor tickets n punch) .. bell punch hole puncher n those coloured tickets with diff colour for diff cash denominations.

    so they said bye to the conductor. the practice then spread over to Singapore in 1970s.

    for those who wish the trace Bell Punch Systems if it ever existed today, its now absorbed into ...Almex.

    Almex ate up Bell Punch dunno when.

    how do they collect fare?

    the Bus conductor

    long gone from Singapore roads, the bus conductor once walked the planks of the old buses of STC n SBS times. but why bus conductor n how does he do his job?

    Why?
    the need to generate revenue when it comes to running a bus company means u need to collect money from ur customers. this can be done in many ways but things in the United Kingdom settled down to the one man who will do this job. the Bus conductor.

    howw?
    first a bus conductor will carry many things with him.

    1) waybill: a slip of paper accounting for the amount of money he collects. the Buss cconductor will need to fill in this paper with the numbers on his tickets he issued and count the differences between the numbers for each ticket. the Bus conductor recives his waybill before he starts his job at the start of the day and then hands it in at the depot.

    statisticians will then handle the waybill info to count the revenue out. in the early days ridership also can be found out from the waybills

    2) tickets of different color. for each cash denomination . the tickets will generally have a 4 digit counting number, some fare stages in numbers arranged in square boxes the size of a punch hole.

    3) hole puncher and bell ringer. this piece of equipment is exclusive to then bell punch . they made this bell punch. the conductor uses the punch in this way

    a passenger will tell him the fare he/she is paying. say 50 cents. So he takes out the next 50 cent ticket to pass to passenger. he punches the ticket at where the passenger boards the bus. the punched stub with the fare number falls into a recepticle below the puncher. a bell rings indicating a transaction is complete.

    at the end of the day, all the punched out ticket things 9preferably with the stage number clearly on them) will be sorted out by people n how people travel n travel patterns are determined from these small pieces of paper.

    they also will sort them by colour and then calculate and make sure the amount of money collected tally with the waybill's numbers.

    at th end of the day, the conductor will write down the last ticket number issued for each denomination into the waybill form and calculates he difference.

    4) a bag to hold the money collected.

    so basically this means the conductor has to steady himself on the bus as fares r being collected n he has to know who has paid and who has not paid. not a safe job if the conductor has bad sense of balance on the bus.

    the conductor system was deemed to cubersome,prone to robberies, not safe to walk on bus, n wage intensive and so bus conductor gave way to OMO. one man operation.

    SCANIA:
    chassis model codes, how they name them??

    due to the recent introduction of the new series, so called 5th series of bus from scania, they have changed the coding. however for those who wonder why there is such names like k95UB being used for K230Ub, this explains why some of us insist on using the old coding.

    SCANIA older style naming of chassis>>.

    Note: MOST Scania chassii have rear mounted engines, there are very few mid mounted underfloor examples)

    The prefix applies to how the engine is mounted.

    L = Vertically mounted engine, tilted 30 degrees to the left (not sure how it is tilted as such)
    K = Vertically mounted engine
    N = vertically mounted engine, horizontally opposed (east-west mounted)
    F = front engined, vertically mounted.
    I'm sure there are others, so please help.

    These are the digits which signify the engine capacity, and follow immediately after the prefix.

    9 = 9 litre engine (from 1st to 4th series)
    11 = 11 litre engine (from 1st to 3rd series)
    12 = 12 litre engine (from 4th series)

    The following digits, which follow the engine capacity indicate the series

    1 = 1st series
    2 = 2nd series
    3 = 3rd series
    4 = 4th series

    The suffixes inidicate the chassis type, and other extra details.

    CRB = standard/city floor height with stepped entry (1st to 3rd series)
    CRL = low floor chassis (1st to 3rd series)
    ARB = standard floor height articulated chassis (1st to 3rd series)
    ARL = low floor articulated chassis (1st to 3rd series)
    TRB = extended chassis (1st to 3rd series)

    For the above, the second letter indicates where the driving controls are situated.
    R = Right hand drive
    L = Left hand drive

    UB = low floor chassis (4th series)
    UA = low floor articulated (4th series)
    IB = "intercity" height chassis with stepped entry (4th series)
    EB = touring chassis? (4th series)

    These suffixes denotes what kind of floor height is "married" to the chassis, which is denoted by the prefix. It only applies to the 4th series of Scania chassii.

    Ok, now the examples.

    K94IB = vertically mounted, 9 litre motor, 4th series chassis, intercity floor chassis
    N113ARB = horizontally opposed vertically mounted 11 litre motor, articulated standard/city floor chassis
    L113TRB = vertically mounted 11 litre motor tilted 30 degrees to the left on an extended chassis

    so yeah some of us prefer this way of naming. however one shd use the new way of referring a scania chassis in official posts. I feel is Ok for us to mention the names as k95UB as long as we understand what it really means in new gen chassis names.

    berth types at bus interchanges

    in this post i discuss on soem terms describing bus interchange berths. these terms are used international and have been referred to in a book i am reading now.

    END ON BERTHS
    these are berths where u drive thhe bus into the berthh n end there. mmostly they have protective barricades between bus and the passengers. End on berths are used at HBF

    LAY-BY BERTHS
    These are berths found in most bus int of Singapore. the buses drive into these berths n pick passengers up and drive off. they do not park there for long periods. Laay bys design wise are like our bus stop lay bys all over the Islaand

    SAWTOOTH LAYOUT PATTERN
    when u have many lay-By berths in an inerchange, on the map of the interchange, it looks like a sawtooth pattern. hence Saw tooth pattern.

    This saw tooth phrase has been abuse to refer to lay-by berths as saw tooth berths . however the usage is not exactly correct.
