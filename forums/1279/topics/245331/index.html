    Natural gas engines produce similar engine-out emissions as diesel engines, only at different quantities (i.e. generally lower). No engine can function without some form of INTAKE and EXHAUST. A dedicated natural gas engine has an extensively modified intake system in order to allow for the delivery of the fuel outside of the combustion chambers (i.e. usually via single point injection like the Volvos). A gas engine also requires a butterfly (throttle) valve in the intake system in order to control airflow (and therefore fuel flow, which affects power) whereas a diesel engine relies entirely on fuel flow to control power output (i.e. they are throttle-less). On the exhaust side of things, a catalytic converter is installed in the exhaust manifold to lower emissions of carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons (two-way oxydising catalytic converter). Without a catalytic converter, the Volvo DH10A/B/C gas engines would not meet even Euro 1 due to extremely high levels of methane in the exhaust stream. The latest Volvo G9A gas engine features, aside from a more efficient combustion system, a three-way catalytic converter that also reduces emissions of nitrogen oxides (NOx) in addition to carbon monoxide and hydrocarbons to levels well below Euro 5.

    Sorry, I made a slight mistake in my original post. I meant that the GH10A/B/C require catalytic converters to meet Euro 2/3/4. Diesel engines also require catalytic converters for Euro 1 or stricter requirements.

    Regarding CNG engines, here's a brief description on how they work.
    1. Natural gas fuel is stored at high pressure (up to 200 bar) in cylinders usually mounted on the roof of a bus.
    2. Gas is delivered to the engine bay from the cylinders via a high pressure gas line.
    3. Prior to entering the intake stream, the gas is filtered for impurities and then depressurized to a set working pressure, typically anywhere from zero pressure to 10 bar. This is done in one or more pressure regulators, heated with coolant from the radiator. The pressure regulator(s) may have one or more stages.
    4. The gas is then mixed with incoming air. This can be done at a gas mixer (elementry carburettor) or via gas injectors.
    5. Airflow is controlled by a butterfly valve in the intake stream and in case of a turbocharged engine, a wastegate in the exhauast stream.
    6. When the air/fuel mixture reaches the engine, it is ignited by spark plugs that are connected to the engine's ignition control system.
