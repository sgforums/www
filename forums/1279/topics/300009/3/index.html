    haha... some of you really can talk and look professional.

    The bus crooked laterally yet going straight. Actually it's forced to go straight with a loosely mounted torsion rod.
    Worn mounting bush for torsion rod (aka Panhard rod).

    The bus slanted vertically to one side yet going straight.
    Corroded bellow plate so bellow not sitting properly.
    if there is air leak in one of the bellow, the bellow will still be unable to rise to the proper height. The 4 way protection valve will still feed the pressurized air to the air suspensions. In extreme cases, it can lead to insufficient pressure in the air system and causes brake binding. (by here, most BC will report the fault). Brake biding if ignored yet continued to drive can cause fire in the brake drums or tyre explosion (which happened before). However, during brake binding, the bus will show significant brake swing to one side during braking and lack of driving force. This is enough to poise most BC to report to OCC/CRS for help.

    These are very common problems found in the autmotive industries for older buses. I have already dealt with countless of these problems before. But still these problems are potential hazards on the road and must be dealt with. These are classified as Critical faults. The highest of the 3 classes.

    But you are right to say the above as in (1) and (2)
    You are also right to say that this fault can happen to both 2 and 3 axled buses.

    The bus with a healthy working air spring will be able to read the movement of load in the vehicle cabin. Then as the air spring of the same axle compresses more than the other, compressed air will be fed to the more compressed air spring to heighten up and allow the cabin to be levelled.

    Bus weight has distribution has been a challenge to vehicle chassis builders. When vehicles are going through the process of developement, the weight distribution must be one of the top priorities to be tackled. A vehicle with poor weight distribution can lose control easily.

    Also, you need to understand that vehicle cabin is suspended from the vehicle chassis. The weight distribution is governed by the effectiveness of the suspensions. And so, with pressurized air, the air springs (air bellows) suspends the vehicle cabin and the shock absorber dampens the oscilliation movement of the air springs to ensure comfort and road hugging capabilities.

    Bus advanced suspensions systems can even read terrain condition & driver's driving style to automatically mechatronically adjust the damping characteristics to optimize operational condition. The commands are received from accelerometers to the suspension ECU before returning to the shock absorbers for actual mechanical action. The speed of these adjustments can be as quick a fraction of a second. This system is also known as PDC (Pneumatic Damping Control)

    A bus going straight with a diagionally oriented body is due to a worn torsion bar mounting bush. If the bus still can be movable, the bar should not break. If the bar breaks, the axle will immediately go disoriented. these kind of incidences are rare.
    The axle is still considered to be in the 'forward' direction but has come disoriented from the vehicle chassis. But strictly speaking, the axle is in danger of breaking as differential units in the axle system will not be able to operate normally.
    Steerable axles are equipped with tie roads and mounted to the double wishbone with a king pin. if the torsion bar of the steerable axle is loose, chances of a broken axle is even more imminent. This is due to the dynamic movement of the axle in view of the steering capability.
