    It is true that the Leyland Olympians in SLWS have 2 steering axles with the front as a trailing steerable axle.

    However I have not maintained the Dennis before so I cannot comment. I will need to go back to office to search the offical TecDoc for clues. eg. with or without tie rod end supplied as parts to determine whether it is a steerable axle or not.

    You need to differenciate between the 3 types of axles:

    Steering - Axles which incorporates the presence of tie rod assemblies. Could also be driven or non-driven (trailing)

    Driven - axles with drive but no tie rod assemblies therefore unable to be steered. Driven axles still have a propeller shaft input into the differential axle.

    Trailing - without tie-rod assemblies and without drive. Does not have propeller shaft input. Cannot even be considered as axle. At most a driven wheel assembly generally with the wheels and lower/upper army assembly leverage and suspensions system, brakes systems, wheel bearings.

    I do not determine the bus is a steerable axle or driven axle etc by merely looking as a commuter on the ground. I need to go under the chassis and check for the presence of propeller shaft drive into the axle, axle design and presence of tie rods before I can make conclusions. If not, I will need to obtain the partnumber of the axle and check with TecDoc again. TecDoc is the internationally recognised automotive parts database which have been officially endorsed by manufacturers.

    Dear all, you all may want to check up the following key words which can help to improve your knowledge towards automotive chassis technology.

    - Tie Rod
    - Propellor Shaft
    - King Pin
    - Double wishbone
