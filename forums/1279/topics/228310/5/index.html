    Hello all,

    Just to clear a few things up...

    1. The Mercedes-Benz Citaro (O 530) is available with three Euro 4 engine options - the OM 906 hLA (6cyl, 6.374-litre, 286 PS), OM 457 hLA (6cyl, 11.967-litre, 299 PS or 354 PS), and the M 447 hLAG natural gas engine (6cyl, 11.967-litre, 252 PS or 326 PS). Of these engines, the OM 457 hLA and M 447 hLAG are also available on the OC 500 LE low-entry chassis. Mercedes-Benz has chosen to use Selective Catalytic Reduction to meet Euro 4 and Euro 5. The CNG engine has already been certified to emit less pollutants than specified in Euro 5.

    2. There are two way to meet Euro 4 currently, both with their own pros and cons. Exhaust Gas Recirculation takes a portion of the exhaust gases and recirculates it back into the intake to cool combustion. Selective Catalytic Reduction uses a special reduction catalyst with an injection system which directs a spray of urea solution at the exhaust gases, neutralising nitrogen oxides - one of the main pollutants caused by diesel engines. EGR places a high load on an engine's cooling system whereas SCR requires seperate urea solution refilling facilities at the depot. Both systems add significant amount of extra weight to a bus.

    3. Just because a 6-litre engine develops 280-odd horsepower doesn't mean it will outperform a 12-litre engine that develops less horsepower. A 12-litre engine relies on torque at low engine speeds to pull the vehicle along. With a higher displacement, a 12-litre engine also produces more power compared to a 6-litre engine at any given engine speed and therefore results in "effortless" performance. When driven properly, a 12-litre engine in a bus can actually consume less fuel than a 6-litre engine in the same bus.

    Regards,
    Dave
