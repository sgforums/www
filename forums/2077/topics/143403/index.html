    SINGAPORE - Singapore on Wednesday announced a special audit of the country's largest charity, and an entire new board of directors, after a rare public outcry over allegations that the group conspired to mislead the public and mask its assets.

    The National Kidney Foundation's new board has assigned accounting firm KPMG to conduct an independent audit of the foundation's finances, said Health Minister Khaw Boon Wan.

    The foundation - long a favourite charity for many Singaporeans - has reserves of $260 million (US$156 million).

    In Parliament on Wednesday, Mr Khaw said the foundation's board of directors has taken on a new chairman and seven new members, days after the former board quit.

    Mr Khaw said the new members 'need to specifically ask the questions which Singaporeans have been asking: Have there been lapses in judgement? Are there misleading claims? Are there errors?'

    He stressed that the foundation is an independent body, and said its board had asked him to intermediate in the crisis. He did not say whether the foundation's former board would face any criminal charges.

    Mr Khaw said the board is searching for a new chief executive to replace TT Durai, who resigned after his lavish pay and perks were revealed.

    The scandal came to light after Mr Durai sued Singapore's Straits Times newspaper for libel, claiming it had implied the foundation had misused funds when the paper reported last year that gold-plated water taps were installed in Mr Durai's office suite.

    The suit backfired. Singaporeans were outraged by revelations in court - including Mr Durai's $25,000 monthly salary, and his use of several Mercedes-Benz vehicles.

    The Straits Times' lawyer got Mr Durai to admit in court that the foundation had overstated the number of Singapore's kidney dialysis patients by a thousand, in an apparent effort to get more money for the foundation than it needed.

    Mr Durai said the error was unintentional.

    About 40,000 people lent their names to an Internet petition demanding Mr Durai's resignation. Vandals sprayed angry graffiti on the foundation's headquarters, and rotten eggs were hurled at one of its 21 dialysis centres.
