    Tycoon's teenager to be MP?

    Michael Palmer flanked by his dad Clive (left), Queensland's richest man, and a man with politics in his blood, US Senator Teddy Kennedy

    Billionaire Clive Palmer's teenage son to stand for parliament

    Patrick Lion

    January 07, 2009 11:00pm

    THE teenage son of Queensland's richest man Clive Palmer, and so-called ''sugar daddy" of the LNP, has won pre-selection at the next state election.

    The billionaire iron ore magnate's 18-year-old son Michael will contest the seat of Nudgee on Brisbane's northside.

    His rise at the age of only 18 comes after the Bligh Government has furiously attacked his father over the past year for allegedly "buying" the new party.

    LNP headquarters has refused to deny receipt of donations from Mr Palmer ahead of compulsory disclosures in coming months but confirmed the party had used his helicopter and luxury DC-9 jet.

    Opposition Leader Lawrence Springborg yesterday denied the teenager had been given preferential treatment because of his dad's generosity.

    "I'm not going to count him out because he is Clive's son," he said.

     "This is the politics of envy and personal character assassination from Labor."

    However, acting Premier Paul Lucas said the growing influence of Clive Palmer was "disturbing" and a reminder of the old Nationals under former premier Sir Joh Bjelke-Petersen.

    "The disturbing development is how one figure such as Mr Clive Palmer seemingly comes to dominate a party, from running and bankrolling conferences to supplying DC-9s," he said.

    "A DC-9 is a commercial passenger jet!"

    Michael Palmer was unavailable to comment yesterday but LNP state director Michael O'Dwyer said that the youngster won the seat uncontested through a normal selection process.

    The novice will battle Emergency Services Minister Neil Roberts, who holds the Nudgee seat by a margin of 18 per cent.##

    He is involved in his father's new A-League soccer club on the Gold Coast but also dabbled in politics as an intern for revered US Senator Ted     Kennedy during last year's election.

    Mr O'Dwyer yesterday confirmed the party had used the jet and a helicopter but would not say how often.

     He also refused to say whether or not Clive Palmer had donated any cash to the LNP.

    No larger donations have emerged under a new rule requiring donors of $100,000 or more to declare themselves within 14 days but other, smaller donations may surface under new half-yearly disclosures soon.

    "They are not details I am willing to divulge," Mr O'Dwyer said.

    "Clive has a back seat in all this."

    Mr Springborg insisted there was nothing wrong with using the chopper about 10 times to travel between Brisbane and his Darling Downs property.

    However, the Palmer connection has now claimed one scalp, with Mr Springborg admitting he had scrapped plans to use the jet during the election campaign because of Labor's focus on it.

    "It would have saved the taxpayers money but I don't want the campaign being hijacked," he said.    ----EOQ
