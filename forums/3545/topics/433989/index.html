One-time password renders card useless abroad

  • Clivebenss
    9 Jul 11, 06:24

    Jul 9, 2011 - ST Forum

    THE DBS Bank's one-time password (OTP) system aims to make transactions more secure without adding significant inconvenience to its users.

    However, my recent experience with it in Seoul proved otherwise.

    South Korea uses a different telephone system, so I could not use my SingTel line. This proved disastrous when I tried to use my DBS credit card to purchase air tickets for an upcoming overseas trip.

    I could not receive my OTP, which is delivered via SMS. When I contacted DBS customer service, I was told to update my overseas phone number on the DBS iBanking system and that the change would take effect a day later.

    It turned out to be a wrong advice. Two days later, I was told I had to print and send a document through snail mail to DBS before it could update my overseas mobile phone number in the system. This paper and stamp process would have taken a week.

    Such a requirement prevents credit card holders from completing a critical online transaction while overseas. I had to use my United States credit card and pay a higher international transaction fee, as well as less favourable exchange rates.

    What is the use of a credit/debit card if it is unable to allow card holders to make transactions conveniently?

    Banks in other countries allow many other flexible means of updates and verification instead of just snail mail documents, which can be forged.

    They include communicating through a verified e-mail account, and cross-checking recent transactions and personal details, among others.

    These strike the right balance between security and convenience.

    David Sng
