    Hmm start on $20 Aussie wines first, get used to the basic wines based on singular grape varietals like Carbanet Sauvs, Merlots, Shiraz, Chardonnay.
    Aus wines have purity of flavour, meaning it tastes like it should taste. But it gets boring cus they all taste the same, and taste so "fresh". But you learn to tell the subtle flavors apart.

    French and Italian wines have more character. Traditionally, european wines are sold according to Appellation(place of origin, aka Bordeaux, Burgundy), then Chateaus(name of the House producing the wine), but not grape varietals(they don't advertise what grapes they use). They usually blend their grapes, which can be anything from 2 to 5 varities. Also, quality is largely determinant on nature, while Aus wines depend more on technology to create consistent wines. Thus, old world wines may not taste as "good" or "standardized" as new world wines, but they've got their characteristics due to minimal processing.

    You can appreciate French wines better if you've drunk enuf Aus wines.

    Between New world and Old world, its not a matter of price, more of style (varietals vs blends). Its not a matter of better or worse, its a matter of difference(consistency vs character).

    The high price of old world wines is due to the limited quantities each estate can produce, vs the australian wine conglomerates which own hundreds of miles of vineyards all over australia, and can network and source them efficiently.

    Due to the old world wines mostly being blends, the flavors on individual grape varietals are more integrated together. A person new to wine can start out anywhere, but it would be better to start on new world wines sold by varietal format, so they can understand the taste of the main grapes, and hopefully pick them out in a blend such as a Bordeaux.

    Of course, that comes under wine appreciation already. You can drink wine like any other drink, but if Im paying for wine I would want to appreciate it more instead of gulping it down. The best way to enjoy wine is to half appreciate it and half drink for fun. Drinking for fun part is how much you like the wine no matter how much people say, and the appreciation part comes from understanding it. Good to have both, to see it from a dual perspective.

    In no where did I claim Old World wines are superior. The tasters in question were given unfair expectations in saying that one wine is superior, thus they perceive it to be. In a blind test, wines are picked based on characteristics percieved to be superior, thus new world wines win easily.

    Its like that "perfect guy" in class, who may be the most boring schmuck you ever know, vs that under-achiever with lots of personality.
