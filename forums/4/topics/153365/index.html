    For weddings, i think some thought should be put into what kind of wines you're going to get and the good thing is that some wine-shops do free tastings - try not to get too deep-thinking a wine - just get easy drinking ones since people going down for the wedding are just there to enjoy the occasion and not appreciate. Most wine-shops do tastings which you have to pay for though they will absorb the cost if you do decide to order in bulk from them.

    Cost-wise - if you get two bottles per table, for twenty tables, you're looking at 40 bottles already. try getting Chilean which are popular and easy drinking these days and the come up to about less than $20 per bottle - cheaper if you can bargain. Also look out for wine sales. Maxxium just had one and so did Magnum.

    Most importantly do check on corkage charges where you are hosting the dinner as well - no point in busting the budget if the corkage is too high and besides beer goes down with all generations of guests. Corkage can be neogtiated with the hotels though so be firm.

    I think now people drink more wine than beer at weddings.

    If you do not have a prefered variety - you can consider
    Pinot Noir - usually regarded as the most easy red wine on the palate and beginners...
    Cabernet Sauvignon - another easy going red wine wine
    Cabernet Shiraz - a little spicy...

    for the whites,
    Chardonnay - sweet and fruity
    Zinfandel - a bit more character

    of course there are also many others and prices range from mid teens to hundreds... so budget is something you also want to think about. Since it is for mass consumption, probably not a good idea to get exotic or expensive wines.

    Country wise - some of the Aussie wines are pretty good value, Chileans are pretty okay, the French tend to be a little overpriced, good US wines are a little on the high side too. South Africans could be considered.

    For reds, try to get at about 3-4 year olds... because at the wedding, and to manage corkage, you cannot afford to open too many to let them breathe... so something too young may not be ready for drinking when they reach your guests. If more matured, they cost more. For whites, 2 years is good.

    Most hotels already waive corkage for a limited number. Push for more and be nice to the wine captain that night... he or she will be the one reporting the number of bottles opened *wink*.

    lastly, is this for your wedding? congrats.
