    When we donate, we donate the whole blood (what the Americans call it packed cells)...

    We donate once every 6 months to allow the blood cells to recuperate in our body before we can go and donate again...

    The blood that we donated will be sent for centrifugation (a device that rotates rapidly and uses centrifugal force to separate substances of different densities) and divided into many other parts based on the components of the blood.

    Some may only contain a certain factor (Factor VIII for Hemophilia Type A patients) while some may contain many combined factors (IX, X, XI or XII - cyrosupernatant).

    The leukemic patients usually receive whole blood / packed cells.

    18 Nov 05, 13:41
    coolger03

    You may ask why the need to centrifuge and give the patients different components of the blood and why not give the whole since the patient can then get everything in one pack.

    The reason being is that the whole blood, even though screened, can have a small risk of transmission of Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C and HIV to the receiver. Screening can miss out these potential virus when it is in their window period (viral antigens not seen during these time, because of antibody suppression)...

    The other reason being is that some patients may only need one factor, and it is wise to give them what they only need to save cost and to reserve the others for those who are in need of them...

    The final reason is that those who only requires one component and not the whole blood will be safe from acquiring the whole thing, as it can lead to fluid overload in the patient...
