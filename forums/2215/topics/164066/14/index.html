    Say when you have a viral fever, the virus stimulates the release of various chemicals known as cytokines, such as interleukin-1 (IL-1) and tumour necrosis factor (TNF-alpha). They reset the thermostat in the hypothalamus of the brain to raise the body temperature.

    When you take panadol or when these chemicals tail off as you recover, the thermostat is brought back to the normal level. The human body's main mechanism for losing heat is by sweating, which comes into play as it does when you exercise. In exercise, the body temperature is raised by physical activity whilst in a viral infection, it is the virus that raised the body temperature. The way we lose heat to maintain normal temperature is the same: by sweating.

    Interleukins are cytokines (chemical messengers that serve as signalling molecules between cells). Interleukins are cytokines that mediate signals between ('inter') white blood cells (leukocytes) cytokines...; hence interleukins.

    There are many different interleukins, each given a different number and have different functions. For example, IL-1, IL-2 are produced by T cells and macrophages as part of a TH1 (T-helper 1) response whilst IL-4 and IL-5 are part of a TH2 response. The type of T cell response leads to a humoral versus cellular type of immune reaction. They have different effects: IL1 produces fever, IL2 leads to proliferation of T cells, IL6 leads to proliferation of plasma cells etc...
