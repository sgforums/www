    More needy patients seeking help for “subsidized” healthcare

    January 8, 2010

    More and more needy Singaporeans are seeking help with their medical bills last year even though they are already heavily “subsidized” by the government.

    About 370,000 patients received “help” from Medifund in the year ended March 2009, $26,000 more in the previous year.

    “C” class patients can have their entire medical bill footed for by Medifund and they form the majority of the hospital inpatients who received help directly from Medifund last year.

    Medifund was set up to provide financial assistance to poor patients who are unable to pay for public healthcare.

    The exact criteria is unknown, but one must have depleted one’s medisave as well as that of relatives in order to qualify for it.

    Needy Singaporeans who have difficulty paying subsidised hospital bills must undergo means testing and assessment by a medical social worker to confirm that they are truly in need.

    Singaporeans are allowed to tap on their medisave, part of their CPF savings to foot for hospitalization bills and outpatient bills for chronic disease like diabetes and hypertension.

    If they do not have sufficient medisave to pay for their medical expenses, their children’s medisave will be used.

    Mr Gerald Ee, the Chairman of the Medifund advisory council reiterated MOH’s stanace that no Singaporean will be denied access to good basic healthcare because of inability to pay.

    Though Singapore is a first world country, its citizens enjoy few social welfare benefits compared to other developed nations.

    One must be really poor and destitute to qualify for meager “aid” from the government.

    The “sandwiched” class of Singaporeans are especially hit because they are not “poor enough” to receive assistance from the government and yet not “rich enough” to afford public healthcare in Singapore whose costs have increased in recent years.

    A typical 7 day in a public hospital can easily incur bills for more than tens of thousand dollars especially if one is warded in the ICU.

    There is a common saying among Singaporeans that “one can afford to die in Singapore, but not fall sick.”

    Despite public sentiments showing otherwise, the ruling party continues to insist that public healthcare is “affordable” to ordinary Singaporeans.

    There is no opposition in Singapore’s parliament to check on the ruling party which has governed uninterrupted for 50 years continuously since 1959.
