    Notifiable Disease #8

    Allergic Rhinitis

    Seeing that many in my clinic complains about having nose block and watery discharge from their nose accompanied with fever and the common flu, I'd like to dedicate this post to subarugal, huiz, starblue and any of those who have this condition.

    Allergic rhinitis is by itself self explanatory. The main cause is allergy, and the main triggering factors are the omnipresent dust mites (Dermatophagoides pterrynosinus), house dust, animal dander and feathers. The food we eat may have connection to this condition, but is rarely the compounding factors to the complains.

    There are two main subcategory for allergic rhinitis, that is seasonal and perennial. Seasonal is episodic, occuring only at a certain season and hence is more common to the countries with 4 seasons. Perennial occurs all year round, and is more relevant to the tropical and subtropical countries, especially Singapore and Malaysia.

    The common triad symptoms or complains by patients with allergic rhinitis would be nose block, watery discharge and episodic sneezing. This condition gets particularly worse when the patient suffers from a viral cold or predisposed to a cold weather or environment (based on normal physiological changes which may exacerbate the symptoms).

    The problem is after all troublesome, as it not only affects the nose, but also the eyes, ears and throat because of the proximity of such organs to the affected site. Itchiness of the eyes, mild hearing loss in the ears and itchy throat (postnasal drips) are among the several complains attained from patients.

    Patients who are proned to such rhinitis condition will also present with sinusitis as both the mucosal lining for the nose and the sinuses are similar in question and hence favors the spread of inflammation throughout the close proximity and exaggerate the problem (triad symptoms).

    How Allergy Occurs

    Type 1 Hypersensitivity

    You have to understand that allergy to a certain allergen that trigger your "blocked nose" in the first place happens throughout your life (whenever you are exposed to it).

    Steroidal medications (they are safe) are for symptomatic relief (they treat the symptoms, they don't cure the disease). Hence once you are relieved symptomatically, there is an inevitable return of such symptoms once exposed to the things that you are allergic to. The body has these cells known as mast cells that release histamines in huge amount to trigger off such allergic symptoms. The thing is that the antibody IgE (which is the key to opening the door for the release of histamines by mast cells) is attached to mast cells after the first exposure to such allergens. Such attachment increases the rapidity of histamine release because they are already attached to the lock. What it needs is an allergen to sit on top of them (ref IgE) and turn them to open the door. Hence the analogy is that the mast cells are the door that contains histamine in the inside, the IgE is the key and the allergens are the hands that turn the key to open the door. (Note that none of the medications used can remove these IgE, antihistamines work by inhibiting the histamine chemical's effects on the receptors. Histamines once released will find their receptors and sit on them leading to allergic symptoms. Your loratidine works in such a way that it sits on these receptors first, but the magic is that they do not cause an effect. The histamines in the body would then be unable to sit on the receptors for they have been occupied. Steroids on the other hand works by easing up the bronchial muscles, reducing the inflammation and clearing the inflammatory chemicals from the site of the allergy, hence helping to reduce the symptoms).

    The best way or the first line treatment is to avoid the things that you are allergic to. Remove your carpet if you have one in your bedroom or your house, keep your curtains clean (wash them weekly), reduce the number of pillows (hence the covering) or mattresses in your room, if you have pets and if you are allergic to their danders, its time to consider letting them out rather than in the house (if they are house pets), etc.

    The severity of your blocked nose dictates the course and frequency of medication you will be put on. Your's is a type of allergy known as perrenial allergic rhinitis (happening throughout the year when exposed to certain chemicals or dust that you are allergic to). There is another one known as seasonal allergic rhinitis (but this happens more often in countries that have four seasons, and their blocked and irritatations of the nose comes only in particularly one specific season repeated annually). As yours is yearly, you would often face these symptoms frequently.

    Heed your ENT specialist advice on your medications intake and you will be fine.

    Potential Treatment or Management

    Find out the underlying cause
    Rid the underlying cause
    Avoid if possible
    Get treated with medications
    Follow-up with your doctor

    Other remedial options are best avoided, but if you feel the necessity, consult the particular source provider and tally with your ENT physician / surgeon.

    Drugs used can be topical (cream / oil based solution) and systemic (oral medications).

    For purpose of medication, understand the text posted above and the picture pasted.

    Antihistamine - Loratidine
    Mast Cell stabilizers - Na Cromoglycate
    Nasal Decongestants - Ephedrine
    Steroid sprays - Beclomethasone (Beconase) or the newer Fluticasone (Flixonase)

    There are no useful surgical approach to remove the problem.
