Therefore in order to find the pH of the buffer solution, we must use the Handerson-Hasselbach equation using the newly found concentration of the weak acid and its salt, which is derived from dividing the amount of salt/acid (mols) with the total volume of the solution which is 200cm^3 in this case.     2 points to note :

    1stly, for Cambridge A levels, as long as your final answer is correct, you'll get full marks, regardless of whether your working is the same as that given in the mark scheme (unless your working is obviously based on erroneous concepts or assumptions).

    2ndly, for this question, because Cambridge (too kindly) used the same volumes for both members of the conjugate acid-base pair, hence upon dilution (ie. mixing both volumes together), the new ratio of molarities will still be the same as the original ratio of molarities.

    Hence, Cambridge simply used the original molarities, plugged into the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation, instead of calculating the new molarities (as you've correctly done, no worries). Final answer will still be the same.

  • UltimaOnline
    21 Sep 15, 04:09
    Yes, correct.

    Do an ICF (not ICE) table in moles.

    Final moles of propanoic acid = 0.05 - 0.03 = 0.02 mol

    Final moles of propanoate ion = 0 + 0.03 = 0.03 mol

    Final molarity of propanoic acid = (0.02 / (100/1000)) = 0.2 M

    Final molarity of propanoate ion = (0.03 / (100/1000)) = 0.3 M

    To calculate pH, you can plug either the ratio of molarities, or simply the ratio of moles (since the same volume in both numerator and denominator will cancel out), of [conjugate base] / [conjugate acid] into the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.
