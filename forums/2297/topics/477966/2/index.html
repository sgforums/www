    • If your L1R5 is below 21, and if you want to get A level certificate, you should go to JC. Even the 20 points ones are better than Millennia Institute.
    • If your L1R4 is below 21 but you cannot go to JC, you should go to Polytechnic. 

    I strongly discourage students to go to Millennia Institute. 

    • Firstly, among the JAE applicants who go into Millennia Institute, 10% are those who are eligible for Junior College. They choose Millennia Institute because Millennia Institute is the only government school that offers Business courses at A level, and/or they stay in Bukit Batok and they do not want to travel so "far" to the Junior Colleges. The 10% are the big fishes who would come in and occupy the small pound at Millennia Institute, while the 90% are the small fishes who have to struggle and fight for their share at Millennia Institute. If you are already not eligible to go to Junior College, it is going to be difficult for you to fight for a "place" for yourself in Millennia Institute, as you are competing with those who actually can go to Junior College.
    • Secondly, statistics have shown that among the Year 3 cohort of Millennia Institute - who are the residue after 2 years of "filtering" (promotion) -  only 30% of them can qualify for Local University with their A level certificate. As I have mentioned, 10% of the Year 1 cohort are JC-elligibles and the remaining 90% are not. That 10% of JC-elligibles already form part of the 30% who would go to Local University, if they keep up their effort. Considering that there are students who would retain or drop-out along the way, if you are the remaining 90% who are not eligible for Junior College, your chances of going to Local University is actually less than 20%, if you go to Millennia Institute.
    • In addition to the second point, students of Millennia Institute are fighting with students of the Junior Colleges (which includes National Junior College and Hwa Chong Institution), except for two A level subjects that are only offered in Millennia Institute, but there must be a reason why they were phased out of Junior College in the past (there used to be Business stream in Junior College), such as it no longer being a requirement in many University courses, etc. In reality, Millennia Institute students are actually just there to lower the A level bell curve.
    • Thirdly, Millennia Institute does not really have a cut-off point. They admit students, regardless of their L1R4, just to fill up their vacancies. As such, there are those whose L1R4 is in the 30+ range at Millennia Institute, although logically it is better for them if they go to the Institute of Technical Education. As my secondary 3 chemistry teacher told me when I was misbehaving, "your attitude translate to your results", those with very high L1R4 points are those with bad study attitude. So, regarding your question on the environment at Millennia Institute, there are many discipline cases here, as many students here do not come here to study but to create nuisance. Even those who are eligible to go to Junior College can be affected negatively by them and deteriorate. In short, the environment here is bad.
    • Adding on to the third point, at Millennia Institute, there is no such thing as "counselled to exit". Those who are "counselled to exit"(asked to leave) are retained instead and are in Year 1 together with those who are "retained" or "advanced"(but want to change subjects or feel that they need another year). This is how bad the environment is in Millennia Institute.
    • Fourth, in case you do not already know, the lessons at Millennia Institute are conducted in classrooms, unlike at Junior College where lessons are conducted in lecture theatres. Hence, the classroom conditions are very hard to control as there is only 1 teacher to 30 students in a class, unlike in Junior College, where there are more than 1 teacher to many students in a lecture theatre. Besides, it is harder to disturb the class or even dose off in lectures as the teacher conducting the lecture in front can easily see every student in the lecture theatre from the stage. Secondly, unlike in Primary School and Secondary School, there is no seating arrangement at Millennia Institute; The taller ones may sit in front and block the shorter ones behind of their view of the whiteboard. Meanwhile, at Junior College, as lessons are in lecture theatres where the seats are in rows of different heights, the taller ones in front would not block the shorter ones behind. In short, the environment in the classrooms/lecture rooms of Millennia Institute is bad and hard to control.
    • Fifth, the notes produced by Millennia Institute are (usually) inferior to the notes produced by any Junior College. The notes by Millennia Institute are (usually) brief in content or even have crucial information missed out. You would have to resort to buying notes (illegally) from the "black market" or spend huge amounts of money buying guide books that are very expensive (some are beyond $50), otherwise you would tend to lose out from those who have such resources.

    Regarding MI for those who couldn't make it to JC.

    As long as you're sure that A levels to University is the route you wanna take (eg. if you decide you're unsuitable for any of the Poly courses), then regardless of JC, MI or Private Candidacy, it's negotiable (ie. no option is all that bad) and you can still succeed based on your personal will.

    Of course, if we're talking about elite courses (eg. Medicine), then you have no choice but to go JC (and specifically, the top few elite JCs). But if you're interested in a reasonably decent Uni course (in NUS, NTU, SMU, SUTD, etc), even MI students or private candidates can succeed.

    So if you can't make it to JC, go ahead and try MI. If you're dissatisfied with MI after a year or two, you can always register as a private candidate. Project Work is not a deal breaker for private candidates, as far as non-elite Uni courses are concerned (depending on the Uni, they may either simply not consider your PW component in calculating your overall score, or they may replace it with another H1 subject, or in the worst case scenario, you might be at a slight disadvantage, but it's no deal breaker as far as non-elite Uni courses are concerned, and as long as you've done well in all your other subjects).
