    Engineering need to have good (strong) foundation on O level Advanced Mathematics. I have taken Engineering Mathematics 1 and I am now taking Engineering Mathematics 2B. There is a lot of Trigonometry, Exponential, Logarithm, ... in both Engineering Mathematics modules. 

    It would be good if you have experience in doing soldering. When I just started my course last year April/May, I struggled through and made lots and lots of mistakes while doing soldering. Have to re-do and re-do again. Why? Because this was my first time doing soldering. Why? Because I did not take DnT at O level. Well, mine is in the school of Electrical and Electronic Engineering. All EEE students in my Polytechnic have to do soldering work once in a while (eg. every week). I am not sure if Aerospace Engineering students have to do soldering work or not.

    Whereas for Project work, in my nine months in Polytechnic so far, I have done many many many.. projects, in the name of "project", "assignment", "assessment", etc. basically, it's either individual or group. I know in this context you are referring to the group project. Well, just do your work in the project and make sure everything is recorded (eg. recorded in WhatsApp group, email, sms, minutes, notes-taking, etc.). If there are any disuputes, just show the evidence.

    One advice: Polytechnic life is about consistency. You have to work hard throughout. The moment you become complacent, that's it. It is tough, unless it becomes a habit to work hard. Your attitude has to be there right from the start and must maintain right all the way until you graduate three years later. 

    That said, the key thing is how you prepare for the tests and examinations, and everything that is graded. Polytechnic is not always stressful. You have to do things at the right time. When it's time to study, you study. When it's time to play, you play. You got to set your priorities right. Spend at least some hours studying concepts on a regular day. On days when exams/tests are nearing, spend more/all hours on studying, do past tests/exams papers and clear all your doubts.\

    Bell curve? I am not sure about it. I do not think there is. You may want to ask the lecturers (in polytechnic, we call teachers 'lecturers') at your polytechnic about it. Personally, I believe everything is fair and meritocratic. Whether majority do well or majority do badly, the passing is always at 50%.

    Whereas for work, I would recommend administrative/office work because it may cultivate the habit of sorting things in order and submitting work of quality and on time. For example, a clinic assistant in a clinic, doing paper work in an office, etc. Doing administrative/office work may be a headstart because it may give a sense of how Polytechnic life may be like: meeting deadlines, meeting expectations, doing team projects, balancing work, study, life and play. Besides, admistrative/office work their pay is high (at least $6 per hour).

    Like I have said, you have to set your priorities right. If along the way you intend to seek employment, 

    • - have at least one entire day in a weekend (saturday and sunday) free to do homework, do revision, etc.
    • - totally do not work when exams/tests are near (within one or two weeks).
    • - if you have school the next day, have at least two hours to study the day before.

    Another advice: Have six to eight hours of sleep everyday. Drink two to three litres of plain/mineral water everyday.

    Then the other thing is clothes. To be honest, no one really cares what clothes you wear. Of course, if you are boy, do not wear girls' clothes. If you are girl, do not wear boy's clothes. Every polytechnic have their own T-shirt (with polytechnic name on it), but many people prefer to wear other T-shirts. Basically, the outfit most people wear is T-shirt and jeans. Normal ones will do. What matters is comfort.

    Still got what else... mmm..... oh yea. The food. I have visited TP before. TP canteen food is very cheap. Then still got what... the internet. SP students have free wifi (SPstudent) that usually runs at 65mbps. NP students have free wifi (NPwireless). I am sure TP has free wifi for students.

    Other than that... not sure if TP have or not, in SP, we have this e-learning portal where students can go online to download powerpoint slides and other lecture materials to do self-study. If need to contact lecturer, their email can be found at the e-learning portal and the student portal. 

    Oh yea, speaking of which, if you need/want to contact (send email, send powerpoint slides, send word document, etc.) someone but you do not have their email, you can search for his email at the student portal and e-learning portal (if he is in the same class as you).

    Well, I'm not sure what else to write. 

    Okay la. One more I can think of... the computer lab, IE lab, DE lab, etc... have free access, which means after school if you need to do lab work, you can go to labs with free access to do work.

    Anyway, all the best!

    I highly doubt that there is a bell curve system in poly. The gpa is arranged according to performance scores. If you want 4.0 GPA,target for 80 and above.(highly possible for maths) Moderation may exist if the paper is too tough,then perhaps the bell curve may come into play.

    Page70 http://www.tp.edu.sg/staticfiles/TP/files/studenthandbook/student_organizer_ay13.14___merged__.pdf

    Backstabbers>1.Try to not gossip about others and try to listen more and talk less.

    2.Build good relationship with others and not just specific groups of people. Even if someone turns on you,others will side with you.

    3.Identify the backstabber>False rumors reach you about what you've supposedly done or said.

    You said something in private, and now everyone knows you said it.

    People stop entrusting you with information, assigning you tasks at work, or asking you to events that they once did.

    People act cold or unfriendly toward you for no clear reason.

    The suspect treats you with disrespect, ignoring your opinion or failing to change her behavior when you ask her to stop.

    If this happens>ask to speak to the backstabber and listen to what the backstabber has to say.>>Apologise if possible and forgive the other party.

    Slackers>1.attack the person based on facts of slacking>how many times he/she is late and miss so many deadlines.

    2.Set goals,expectations and objectives to pressure the slacker into working.

    3.Set up a reward system for the team.

    4.Show them that their behaviour is pulling down the team

    5.Help the slacker not to feel overwhelmed by his/her role>break down the task assigned to him/her

    6.Find out what makes them slack and try to get rid of the causes,or work with them to prevent them from slacking.

    7.If they are still slacking,inform your project teacher about his/her unchanging behavior.

    Other advices:

    Take up a CCA.>active way of destressing and relieving yourself,as opposed to staying at home and partaking at your own activities.

    >A good opportunity to interact with others and work as a team(teamwork-based CCAs)

    Don't take up too many CCAs or time-draining CCAs.

    e.g. Dragon Boat> 3 training sessions a week,which takes up the entire afternoon till early evening.By the time you go back home,you are too tired to do your assignments. One of the training days,may also require you to come back on weekends.

    Don't just be a loner and study all day long> Make some friends and hang out with them from time to time. You want a balanced lifestyle,not one that is tilted towards solely on academics

    Set deadlines for your assignments> Don't do it last-minute,start immediately! If anything urgent happens,you will be able to complete your assignment on time.

    Study with a group of motivated friends during exam periods(depends on individual)>Research shows that one can retain 90% of what he/she learns by teaching others. So,you want to be able to interact and learn from each other to speed up your revision time.

    Adding on to NanaHou Network comment on core foundations> Don't throw away everything you learnt into the drain after each module. You can revisit them during holidays,etc. Some of them are very important for your future modules.

    Learn to say no > You don't want to be easily pushed around by peer pressure. Some times, peer pressure can be bad for you. That's when you have to say no!

    Don't burn out>Have a day off every week and relax. Keep calm for that day and don't think about studies.

    All the best for your poly years! Have fun~

    1. Brush up on your A maths (if you have), be very very familiar with mathematical manipulations, like log, partial fractions, trigonometry(how to play the tricks to simplify equation), differetiation and integration(chain rule, product rule, integration by substitution, integration by partial fraction), algebra manipulations, probabilty and stats, matrices(inverse matric, determinant, cramer rules etc). Engineering maths will build on some of these.

    2. A simple way will be to think as if you are an online store selling many products. Your store's overall rating will be called the GPA. Each product you sell will get a rating, how well the product get rated will determine on how well you did on your exam. How well the rating of this product affect your shop's overall rating will be determined by the "academic unit", the more the academic unit, the more it will affect.

    So if you your shop sell 5 products, Product A,B,C,D,E. Product A,B,C have a high weightage while D and E have little weightage. If the rating for product A,B,C is very bad, your shop's overal rating will still drop even if product D and E gets very good rating.
