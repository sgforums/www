    If the question allows condensed formula for optical isomers, then it's true you cannot write "H3CH2C-" but you must still write as "CH3CH2-". But if the Cambridge question requires "displayed structural formula" meaning you must show all the individual bonds, then you should really draw out both optical isomers exactly as if they were mirror images, eg. if LHS optical isomer shows -O-H, the RHS optical isomer should show H-O-.

    Cambridge accepts either formal charges on the individual atoms, or overall ionic charge outside the square brackets (which represents "summation" of individual formal charges). But for dot-and-cross diagram, don't show formal charges, just only show the overall ionic charge outside the square brackets, as the positive formal charge looks too similar to a cross.

    Yes, you should get full marks for drawing 4 isomers (either two sets of optical isomers, or 1 set optical isomer and 1 set geometrical isomers).



    - Tertiary carbocation more stable (since alkyl groups are electron donating by induction).

    - H2O is the competing nucleophile (present in higher molarity than Br-)

    - After H2O nucleophile adds onto carbocation intermediate, deprotonation generates the final product of bromoalcohol.



    The immediate product is phenylammonium chloride, which is ionic and hence difficult to seprate. Hence deprotonation is required to separate the molecular phenylamine via steam distillation.
