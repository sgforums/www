    There is Chinese B for A Levels too. It is for those who took Chinese B in secondary schools. However, Chinese B for A Levels is not a H1/H2 subject and thus cannot be used for your university admission score. Passing CLB at A level would meet the university MTL requirement though. Performance in CLB is just indicated as "Merit, Pass or Ungraded."

    University admission score (UAS) is generally as follow:

    A) 3 H2 subjects - 60 points

    B) H1 General Paper (GP) - 10 points

    C) H1 Project Work (PW) - 10 points

    D) 1 H1 subject - 10 points

    Total (A to D): 90 points

    E) H1 Mother Tongue (MTL) (optional for UAS) - 10 points

    Total (A to E): 100 points* (will be rebased to 90 points)

     

    MTL will only be included in the computation of the UAS if it raises your score.

    For those who perform badly in other subjects but perform well in MTL, MTL will help them by pulling up their UAS. But for example if you manage to get straight A's for all your other A Level subjects, then MTL does not matter for your university admission score as you would already have a perfect 90/90 UAS (you just need to get at least 'S' grade for H1 MTL or pass MTL 'B' at A Level to meet the general admission requirements)

    Take note of the different score weightage for MTL and MTL 'B' :

    The 'B' version of the syllabus has a higher weightage on the Oral/Aural (aural = listening compre) components at 50%. This means that if you can brush up on your CL speaking skills and also do well for listening compre, it is not that difficult to pass CL 'B'. Another key difference is CL 'B' also requires you to write less words for compo.

    O Level

     

    CL

    Composition - 30%

    Language Use & Comprehension - 35%

    Oral/Aural - 35%

     

    CL 'B'

    Composition 20%

    Language Use & Comprehension - 30%

    Oral/Aural - 50%

     

    A Level

     

    H1 CL

    Composition - 30%

    Language Use & Comprehension - 40%

    Oral/Aural - 30%

     

    CL 'B'

    Practical Writing - 20%

    Language Use & Comprehension - 30%

    Oral/Aural - 50%
