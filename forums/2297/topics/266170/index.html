    anyway, i find that for o levels, emaths is a subject that you do not need to study for and yet get A1. in fact , my maths teacher who taught both e and a maths used to say if you dont get at least a2 for emaths , you're just dumb.

    anyway, for combined humanities, consistent reading and memorising and evaluation of the facts are very very important. you can spot, but have a large margin of error, i.e. study at least 4/5 chapters. you do not want to, on the eve of the exam, to be cramming until 4 am and waking up at 8 for your exam.(that was the mistake i made last yr- i did not consistently read ss and history, and had to cram the eve of the exams(i did not sleep AT ALL before the comb. humans exam). luckily i got an a1.

    for the o level pure sciences and amaths, simple. practice, practice, practice. if you notice, in the tys, many questions are repeated. therefore, its likely some of the tys questions may come out word for word or slightly modified. what i did last yr was do alot of test papers (at least 15(no kidding) past school prelim papers before o levels. when you dont know the solution and cant get answer right, check textbook.) studying concepts is useless if u simply read text book. doing it is crucial to an A.yes, i got A's for my pure sciences and a maths.

    anyone comment on english / chinese / pure humanities. my study methods for these subjects are erm... atrocious.

    Paying attention during lesson time can replace more than half of your studying time.

    The trick is to study/mug effectively. Don't equate time with quality. Study when your concentration is high. If it's waning, rest for a few minutes, get some snacks then come back and continue. Tired? Sleep.

    Nevertheless, an hour spent paying attention during lesson is better than two spent studying/mugging.
