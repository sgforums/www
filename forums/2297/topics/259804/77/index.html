    Molarity certainly affects redox potentials. The values given in the data booklet are valid under standard conditions, ie. equal molarities of aqueous species on both sides of the equation, including stated stoichiometries.

    Indeed, in a neutral pH solution, as long as the molarity of Pb2+ is sufficiently high, Pb2+ will be reduced to Pb, instead of H+ reduced to H2, or H2O reduced to H2 (since the molarity of H+ is too low in a neutral pH solution, and since the reduction potential of H2O to H2 is significantly more negative than the reduction potential of Pb2+ to Pb).

    For H+ to be reduced to H2(g) instead of Pb2+ reduced to Pb(s) at the cathode, the molarity of H+ must be significantly greater than the molarity of Pb2+, ie. a strongly acidic solution. If the pH is neutral, then Pb2+ is assumed to be present in significantly larger molarity (compared to H+), and hence Pb2+ is reduced to Pb at the cathode.
