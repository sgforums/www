  • UltimaOnline
    26 Nov 09, 16:09
    The meaning of coordination number depends on context, and is somewhat calculated differently in crystal lattice structures vs complex ions. For a crystal lattice (eg. ionic lattice), coordination numer refers to number of closest neighbouring atoms touching a given (central) atom, and not "no. of ionic bonds" (to what extent you'll be penalized for it depends on how strict the mark scheme is). The usage of the term "bonds" mostly refer to covalent (dative or non) bonds; the so-called "ionic bonds" are really electrostatic forces of attraction between oppositely charged ions. This wiki page explains clearly everything relevant you need to know about coordination number. http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_number

    Simply speaking, (I haven't personally seen the question, but assuming that no stoichiometric info is given, then) this is an unfair question. But it's not beyond Cambridge to give unfair questions at 'A' levels (eg. see 2008 paper, and now 2009 as well). The chemistry of complex ions is actually far more complex than can be handled at 'A' levels. Which means at 'A' levels, other than a list of common complex ions which you can use to memorize their coordination numbers and geometries (eg. tetraaminediaquocopper(II) ion [Cu(NH3)4(H2O)2]2+), for relatively unknown ions, the question must provide the relevant info for you to deduce the coordination number and geometry. As to whether they will accept or penalize your erroneous answer, it depends on how strict Cambridge sets their mark scheme for this paper. Sometimes (ie. in some papers for some years) they accept, sometimes they partially penalize, sometimes they totally penalize.

  • UltimaOnline
    27 Nov 09, 14:03
    Whenever the question doesn't is ambiguous or doesn't specify conditions, it is the duty of the exam-smart student to take the initiative to specify conditions and consequent products. Hence, the exam-smart student would give BOTH answers : In non-polar solvent such as CCl4, we obtain a mixture of 2-bromophenol and 4-bromophenol. In aqueous solvent, we obtain 2,4,6-tribromophenol. That said, less exam-smart students who only give either answer (but solvent MUST be named/identified together with product) should score full marks, but this does depend on the particular mark scheme. If only one answer is to be given (eg. if this is indicated in the question), the more appropriate answer would be the one in which the reaction proceeds further, ie. aqueous solvent and 2,4,6-tribromophenol.
