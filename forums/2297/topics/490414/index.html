    I am sorry, but I think the correct answer should be that both carbon dioxide and oxygen react in the blast furnace.

    Let me explain. (You may want to Google for images and videos of furnace, to see how a blast furnace looks like)

    Say we have an iron ore (haematite) and we want to extract iron from it.

    Haematite contains iron(III) oxide and impurities, such as sand and clay (limestone).

    To extract the iron, we add the haematite, limestone and coke (carbon) from the top of the blast furnace.

    Next, we blow hot air into the furnace near the bottom.

    The oxygen in the hot air reacts with coke (carbon) to form carbon dioxide.

    The carbon dioxide in the hot air, as well as the carbon dioxide formed, reacts with more coke (carbon) to form carbon monoxide.

    This carbon monoxide reacts with iron(III) oxide to form iron and carbon dioxide.

    The carbon dioxide formed (and not used at all) escapes through the top of the furnace, along with carbon monoxide (either did not react or not used at all) and nitrogen, as waste gases.

    The iron is formed in molten state and it runs to the bottom of the furnace.

    As mentioned earlier, the haematite contains impurities such as sand and clay (limestone). When iron is formed in molten state, it is mixed with the impurities.

    The heat decomposes the limestone (calcium carbonate) into quicklime (calcium oxide) and carbon dioxide.

    This quicklime (calcium oxide) then reacts with acidic impurities such as silicon(IV) oxide (sand) to form a liquid called slag.

    This slag floats on top of the molten iron and they run off separately at the bottom of the furnace. Slag can be sold for use as a constituent of cement.

    With that, we obtain the molten iron.

    Nitrogen: 79%
    Oxygen: 20%
    Carbon Dioxide: 0.03%
    Water vapour: 0-5%
    Noble gases: Argon: 0.9%
    Noble gases: Neon and Helium: 0.002%

    Oxides of Nitrogen (nitrogen monoxide and nitrogen dioxide) are formed from the following sources, when the temperature is very high:
    - lightning activity during thunderstorms
    - internal combustion engines

    Again, it's Cambridge's fault for setting ambiguous qns like these (every year there'll be a couple of such qns, in which different school teachers will disagree with each other and/or with Cambridge, and teach their students different answers) at both O levels and (especially) A levels.

    The most correct answer (regardless of whether it's the answer Cambridge wanted, which is always debatable for such qns), is that all 3 gases do indeed react at the high temperatures present in blast furnances (whether for extraction of iron from its ore, or other purposes).

    As nitrogen (the most abundant gas in the atmosphere) is inevitably present in the air used by blast furnaces (air is afterall, the cheapest source of oxygen, and obviously all industries are profit-oriented and want to make as much money as possible by reducing costs), at such high temperatures of blast furnaces, it's inevitable that various oxides of nitrogen (NOx) are generated. Such oxides of nitrogen are toxic to humans, and are known to be a industrial medical health hazard for blast furnace workers.

    While the O level syllabus doesn't specifically include mention of this health hazard suffered by blast furnace workers, nonetheless (assuming you believe Cambridge wants you to including nitrogen as the correct answer) at O levels and A levels, students are arguably expected to put 2 and 2 together (presence of nitrogen and oxygen, and at high temperatures) and conclude oxides of nitrogen may (and are) being produced in blast furnaces, albeit as undesirable (and medically hazardous) side-products.
