    The God
    By Chris Wood

    Pagan Witches work with many Gods and Goddesses, but one God and one Goddess in particular feature in our invocations. To some of us, all the other Gods are aspects of Him, and all the other Goddesses are aspects of Her. I will talk about the Witches' God.

    His is the power of the stag and the stillness of the heron; the wisdom of the serpent and the bounty of the oak; the fear of the hunted and the tension in the hunter's bow. He is Robin Hood of the summer-green glades, and the Horned One of winter woods, leading the Wild Hunt across the sky. He is the spirit of the grain, self-sacrificed at Lammastide, and the Green Man, laughing in the leaves. His is the throb of the express train, and the gentleness of the dewy dawn. In Him are the grace of the fox and the strength of the plough-horse.

    The God is the Divine Masculine, son and lover to the Divine Feminine, seen in the Goddess. He is also the model of human spiritual striving, of transcendence, of walking between the worlds. That is why He is often seen as twins: one of the Underworld, one of the Upperworld.

    The powers of Light and Dark, of generation and of rest and decay, are more distinct in the Masculine side of divinity. Light and Dark Goddesses are much more closely linked than are Light and Dark Gods. So, the God is the complementary pole to the Goddess, but He is also the very embodiment of creative polarity in His very nature.

    He is the Lord of the Sun and of the regenerative Dark of the Earth; the Creator and Destroyer; First and Last; Father and Son, co-existent, sharing Creation with the Goddess. He is trickster and law-giver, creator and created: the Divine Artificer.

    He is the Summer King and the Winter King: the Oak and the Holly brought together by the Goddess, just as the Morning Star and the Evening Star are united in the Lady Venus. It is the monotheist religions that have separated Him out into unstable figures of absolute good and absolute evil. As the poet Robert Graves wrote:

    "The War between Good and Evil has been waged in so indecent and painful a way during the past two millennia because the theologians, not being poets, have forbidden the Goddess to umpire it, and made God impose on the Devil impossible terms of unconditional surrender." (The White Goddess, p.446)

    We have no Satan, we do not give evil power by making it into a god. We honour the Light Bearer and the Night Bringer, knowing we need them both in their season.
