    UIWANG, Kyonggi-do, South Korea--

    They have not seen a miracle yet, such as the healing effects of Lourdes. But for monks and people visiting Chonggye-sa Temple in this suburb of Seoul, it is an unprecedented mystery on the shining gilded statue of the Buddhist deity, often compared to the Madonna in Christianity.

    The enigma is that more than a score of tiny white flowers are blooming on the forehead of Kuanyin Bodhisattva, enshrined in this age-old temple perched on Mt. Chonggye in South Korea.

    According to Buddhist literature, the legendary flower called "`Udambara'' (Udumbara-pupsa in Sanskrit) blooms once in 3,000 years, as told by the Lotus Sutra. Religious Indians believe the blooming takes place when the Sage King of Future comes to the world.

    "It is a sheer delight that gives you a feeling beyond description,'' said the Rev. Chongsang, chief abbot of Chongye-sa.

    He added, "I hope this joyful experience of `hwanhisim' be shared by most people, including those who have suffered.''

    "This an auspicious sign and a wake up call for us to undertake our responsibility of turning this country into a pure land,'' said the senior Chogye Order priest, who is also managing Sokkuram Grotto and serves as deputy chief of the Silla temple Pulguk-sa, a Unesco-registered World Heritage Site.

    The discovery on the tip of the eyebrow of the Buddha-to-be "Kwanseum-bosal'' was made on Oct. 6 by a laywoman believer, Lee Kyong-sun, 40 while praying at the main Buddha hall.

    According to monks, it now grows in upright arrays of 21 thread-like twigs that each are one centimeter in length, with a tiny white flower no bigger than the tip of a ball-point pen.

    People say it is up to each individual to interpret its true meaning.

    Some even try to make its association with President Kim Dae-jung's winning of the Nobel Peace Prize last week. Couldn't it also be a good omen for the success of of Seoul's ASEM summit? Ranging from political prospects for such issues as national unification to personal wishes, people seem to be very hopeful before the statute with its extraordinary appearance.

    Does the image have any healing power? So far, nobody has made that kind of claim, yet many hope for exactly that kind of occurrence.

    The Rev. Chongsang said, "You may feel a difference between here and other places of meditation, as you sit before the Kuanyin statue.'' He explained that he himself feels ki, or energy, in his body, as well as more depth and concentration in his mind.

    Does this make it possible to make a comparison to the Grotto of Massabielle, in Lourdes of Southern France, where a poor 14-year-old girl, Bernadette Soubirous, met the Virgin Mary during the 18 apparitions that began in 1858.

    More than five million people, many of whom are sick and handicapped, come to Lourdes from every country of the world. Lourdes today is called a town of friendship, world center of pilgrimage and a special place of meeting, with God and people.

Due to the traffic jams on the access road, some people park their cars and walk to the temple. The 3-kilometer entrance will be expanded and paved next year, with construction work already underway.

    Many lay visitors and priests have made the pilgrimage from far away. Former president Chun Doo-hwan, who had spent the days after his resignation at Mt. Sorak's Paekdam-sa Temple, visited the temple on Wednesday.

    The temple (phone:031-426-2348, fax:031-426-3666, address:#San 11, Chonggye-dong, Uiwang City) is accessible from Uiwang via Kwachon and Pangyo, the southern portal cities of Seoul. The temple belongs to the Chogye diocese supervised by Yongju-sa Temple in Suwon.

    It is on the southern slope of Mt. Chonggye, a 618-meter-hill whose eastern side faces the Seoul-Pusan Expressway, and the northwestern foothills are the location of the National Contemporary Art Museum, Seoul Grand park and the horseracing track.

    Chonggye-sa Temple was founded in the Silla period, but its reached its greatest expansion in 1284, during the reign of Koryo King Chungnyol by Cho In-gyu, a rich layman. At that time, more than 100 monks resided while it was developed under the patronage of Cho. In 1407, during the early Choson peri d, the dynasty's government designated it as a "very fortunate temple.'' During the rule of Yonsan-gun, when the king banned temples in the walled capital, the temple came to assume the role of Zen monastery, instead of Pongun-sa at the south of the Han River.

    "Its tradition as a Zen monastery has continued at Chongye-sa until modern times,'' said the Rev. Chongsang, citing such great Buddhist masters as Vens. Kyongho, Mangong and Kumo.

    For example, Ven. Kyongho, of the early last century, is legendary for his extraordinary life and teaching as a Zen master.

    Many of their disciples are leading figures in the monks' Chogye Order, the nation's largest Buddhist sect.

    "I believe the phenomenon of our temple's statue has a connection to the great teachers of the past,'' said the chief abbot.

    The temple is going to hold a series of masters' sermon meetings, with high-ranking monks invited. The number of teachers, 21, corresponds with the number of the flowers in bloom. It will last for more than 100 days, with weekly shifts of the sermon speakers.

    They believe that 21 flowers will bloom on each of the 21 twigs. The chief monk said, "We will wait and see.''

    In an act of acknowledging the mysterious occurrence, Ven. Chongdae, chief executive of the Chogye Order, attended the ceremony Wednesday to initiate the 108-day Buddhist meeting and sighting of the flowers. It will last until Feb. 1 of next year. Already some 20 persons have kept vigils at the the Buddha Hall.

    The temple and the municipality of Uiwang look forward to the increase of visitors, including Buddhist pilgrims from across the country and possibly from abroad.

    Gov. Kim Chang-yuel of Kyonggi-do Province reminds people that Korean Buddhism has kept the tradition of national safeguarding in times of moral chaos, economic difficulty and foreign invasion, according to history.

    "The appearance of Udambara is auspicious sign by the Buddhist tradition and it is a happy incident to all nation as President Kim Dae-jung's winning of the Nobel Peace Prize is so to all 70 million Korean people,'' said Lim in a speech to congratulate the apparition at Choggye-sa Temple.

    It is the first time that as many as 21 flowers of Udambara appear though the similar kind of phenomenon took place twice recently at temples in Korea recently, at at a temple in Kwangju, Kyonggi-do, in 1997 and at Kwangsu-sa in Taejon in July this year.

    The Rev. Chongsang, maintained it is almost unthinkable that the 500 year-old Buddhist statute, which might have been gilded every two to three years over the woodwork, could produce the flower-like growing things.

    Botanically the flower may be in category of Ficus (glomerata), or fig. However, botanists regard the appearance at Chogye-sa can be hardly related with the fruitful plant.

    In India it is believed as a divine plant along with the bo tree.

    Buddhist daily Popbo Sinmun editor Lee Hak-jong said the floral appearance is associated with the sage king in India and the best model of such leader is King Ashoka, the great ancient patron of Buddhism.

    The Lotus Sutra decribed the sighting of the udambara flower is like seeing the the birth of Buddha and the audience of Him.

    This is the most famous of all Chinese oolongs. Guanyin, sometimes called the Goddess of Mercy, is actually more like the Buddhist equivalent of the Madonna. She is a bodhisattva, one who is qualified to enter nirvana, but chose to remain on earth to bring all to enlightenment. Statues of her stand in many Buddhist temples, and a woman who wants a child may pray to her. A legend gives one version of why a tea bears her name.



    An iron statue of Guanyin stood in a rundown temple in central Fujian's Shaxian (Sand) province. The temple's condition aroused the concern of a tea grower who passed it daily. Financially uanble to repair it, he thought that the least he could do was to burn incense and clean the place twice a month.

    One night Guanyin appeared to him in a dream and told him to look in the cave behind the temple for a treasure. He was to take it for himself but also to share it with others. There he found a single tea shoot which he planted and cultivated into a bush with leaves that produced a singularly fine drink. He began selling it under the Guanyin name, and gave many cuttings to his neighbors. All prospered, and eventually the temple was repaired.

    A book from contemporary China gives another version: this tea is so named for the appearance of its processed leaves -- dark as iron and heavier than other teas, but with a quality as pure and beautiful as Guanyin.
