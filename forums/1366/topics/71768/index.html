    The Lesson of the Indigo Child

    I first heard of the Indigo Children in the early 1980's. The Indigo Children were the New People, the Children of the Earth; those who would retrace the steps of Mankind to find what we had lost. They would live the philosophy of the Earth, balanced in Spirit, and they would purge Man of destructive thinking. The Elders spoke of the arrival of the Indigoes in hushed voices that underscored both their hope that the Indigo Children would bring peace and harmony in the Fifth World, and their fear that things had gone too far for Mankind to survive.

    Like many professionals who work with children, I began to see a distinct pattern emerging as the Indigoes arrived in force and as I began working with them more frequently. Articles began appearing in psychology, social work, medical, psychiatric and education journals on the Indigo Children. Most of these articles discussed the increase in gifted children who were behavior problems at home and at school. Many of these children were also hyperactive. It became increasingly clear that the Indigoes were considered by professionals to be difficult children who did not respond well to many behavior management techniques or to most medications. Many alternative strategies were proposed to assist parents and professionals in "norming" or socializing these children, including experimental medication, behavior management programs, intensive psychotherapy and institutionalization.

    At the same time, references to the Indigo Children began appearing in New Age articles and books, and these writers spoke enthusiastically about the skill and insight of the Indigo. These children were special in their ability to understand and work with energy on an instinctive level, they were intelligent and insightful, and they had confidence in both their knowledge and their skill. Many were psychic and many had extraordinary healing power. But these writers made no mention of the difficulties the Indigoes faced in daily living. I saw the Indigo Children struggling to maintain their spirit in a society that does not value spirituality, does not recognize knowledge or wisdom that is not taught, and which claims to value individuality but does not accept individual differences.

    The first significant published work that opened the door to serious discussion of the Indigoes plight in relation to their unique skills and gifts was The Indigo Children: The New Kids Have Arrived by Jan Tober and Lee Carroll, published in 1999. In this work the Indigo Child is defined as "one who displays a new and unusual set of psychological attributes and shows a pattern of behavior generally undocumented before." Tober and Carroll caution: "(the) pattern has common unique factors that suggest that those who interact with them (parents, in particular) change their treatment and upbringing of them in order to achieve balance. To ignore these new patterns is to potentially create imbalance and frustration in the mind of this precious new life."

    Indigo Children are notorious for their unwillingness to accept authority at face value or to follow instructions or routines that do not make sense to them. Our society demands that people, especially children, earn respect through demonstrated expertise or accomplishment. Position and authority is granted when respect is earned, but minorities (racial, age, gender or religious) continue to find that they are denied position and authority in the hierarchies for reasons that cannot be justified. Society demands that those granted position and authority be followed without discussion or question. This expectation is particularly difficult for the Indigo Child. Tober and Carroll describe them as coming into the world with a "feeling of royalty" and although many parents and professionals agree wholeheartedly in this assessment, I challenge this perception. I see the Indigo Child as an evolved spiritual being who is a "self-actualizing person" as described in the work of the well-respected psychologist, Abraham H. Maslow. Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs model for human behavior proposes that human beings require a series of needs be met in order to develop to their full potential.

    I believe that the Indigo Children are true egalitarians, they have a developed and healthy sense of their connectedness to the Universe and they understand their place within it. They will not allow this to be challenged. Those people who are still attempting to meet their own Esteem needs will view the Indigo as behaving in a superior and condescending way. Thus, the Indigo Child can appear to be arrogant when they are self-confident, defiant when they are self-regulated, condescending when they have no sense of inequality or inattentive when they are prevented from pursuing their potential.

    The Indigo Children are currently struggling with societal norms; norms that are based upon a hierarchical structure that is contrary to the values that Pagans and Universal Beings value and respect. Maslow recognized that our social structures, like schools, routinely undermine self-actualizing people, especially in the areas of Social Needs with acceptance and Ego/Esteem Needs with respect. All of the research that I have read, and all the experiences that I have had in working with Indigo Children follow the advice that Maslow gave educators in 1968:

    [We should teach people to be authentic; to be aware of their inner selves and to hear their inner-feeling voices.

    We should teach people to transcend their own cultural conditioning, and become world citizens.

    We should help people discover their vocation in life, their calling, fate or destiny. This is especially focused upon finding the right career and the right mate.

    We should teach people that life is precious, that there is joy to be experienced in life, and if people are open to seeing the good and joyous in all kinds of situations, it makes life worth living.

    We must accept the person and help him or her learn their inner nature. From real knowledge of aptitudes and limitations we can know what to build upon, what potentials are really there.

    We must see that the person's basic needs are satisfied. That includes safety, belongingness and esteem needs.

    We should refreshen consciousness, teaching the person to appreciate beauty and the other good things in nature and in living.

    We should teach people that controls are good, and complete abandon is bad. It takes control to improve the quality of life in all areas.

    We should teach people to transcend the trifling problems and grapple with the serious problems in life. These include the problems of injustice, of pain, suffering and death.

    We must teach people to be good choosers. They must be given practice in making choices, first between one good and another; later between one god and another.

    Maslow's advice will help any parent or professional in living or working with Indigo Children. More importantly, these should be the norms for our society. We must take this advice to heart and advocate for our society to change so that all people will be encouraged and supported to become World Citizens or Universal Beings. We can demand nothing less for our children and ourselves.

    Many spiritualists acknowledge the emergence of the Fifth World. Many theorize that the World and Mankind is evolving to a new level. Much of the Indigo Children literature speaks about the spiritual and biological impact that this evolution will have on the World, but there has not been much discussion about what the impact will be on the psychological functioning of individuals and the impact the change will have on our society.

    Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs is an accurate reflection of the natural evolution of the human spirit and psyche. The First World required people to learn how to get their physical needs met, leading them to discover how to hunt, what plants were edible, and how to find shelter. The Second World required people to learn how to keep themselves safe, leading them to learn how to make and use weapons, how to build shelters that could be defended and withstand natural forces, and how to outwit predators and enemies. The Third World required people to learn how to meet their social needs, civilizations were born and rules and protocols for social interaction were developed. The Fourth World prompted people to meet their esteem needs, to learn about themselves in relation to the world, to question their place in it, and to explore and to learn everything they could. The Fifth World would prompt people to be self-actualizing, to be involved in a cause outside their own skin, to act on their potential and to seek moral and spiritual fulfillment.

    For spiritualists the Indigo Children herald the coming of the Fifth World. For psychologists, educators and parents they herald the emergence of a new and unique set of behavioral problems that must be "solved". Both perceptions are valid. Both perceptions lead us to the same solution: the lesson that the Indigo Children are teaching us through the pain and anguish that they experience in schools, in communities, and in some homes is that our society needs to evolve along with our spirit
