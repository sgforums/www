    Former top referee warns at inaction on complaints

    Some 7,000 officials leave the game every season

    More referees will follow Graham Poll out of the game unless they receive significant backing from the Football Association, a former Premiership referee warned yesterday. Poll revealed that he retired from officiating early because the FA failed to support him after he dismissed John Terry last November and there are fears many of his colleagues are also disillusioned.

    "I have heard moans and groans from many referees," said the former referee, who is still involved at all levels of the game. "They are getting uptight because they're asked to report things and nothing seems to be done about it. The FA has got to do something regarding what referees are saying because there are a lot of referees that are going to go out of the game. It has been happening for years at local level. Out of all those that pass the referees' course, less than 50% are refereeing within 10 weeks."

    There are 7,000 referees trained every season but the same number give up during that period, currently leaving an 8,000 shortfall. Among those officiating at the top of the game, there is a belief that power has been eroded. "Referees have lost some of their authority and I don't think there is any doubt about that," continued the source. "Andy D'Urso in one game issued two cards to a player and didn't send him off and got demoted from the Premier League to the Football League. But when he was surrounded by Manchester United players [in a previous match], what happened to the players and the club?

    "This is a current classic case. If you referee in Europe and the benches do not adhere to the regulations, for example players stand up or the manager goes out of the technical area, you can be fined. In England every week, there are benches and managers being reported for abusive behaviour and not conforming with regulations but nothing seems to be done about it. I think that is part of what Graham is talking about and it's the same on the field of play." The FA did not respond to the retired referee's comments, referring only to remarks made by Neale Barry, the FA's head of senior referee development, earlier in the day.

    Graeme Murty, the Reading captain, believes the problems start at grassroots level. "It comes down to what you see in junior football," said the Scotland international. "Referees in kids' football get a hell of a raw deal. They get stick from everywhere and I'm surprised that some of the matches go on because of the level of abuse they get from players, fans, parents and coaches. In fact, it surprises me that people are willing to put themselves through that situation."
