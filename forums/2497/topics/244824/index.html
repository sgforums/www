    McCLAREN PREPARED TO MAKE CHANGES



    Under-pressure England coach Steve McClaren insists he is bold enough to drop one of his big-name stars - but says it is too early to decide whether he will swing the axe for Wednesday's Euro 2008 qualifier against Andorra.

    England's failure to make their dominance count against Israel has placed a major question mark over their ability to reach next summer's European Championship finals.

    Although McClaren continues to insist his side will meet their ultimate objective, it is becoming increasingly apparent a major decision will have to be made in midfield, where Frank Lampard is doing little in the middle to justify Steven Gerrard being shunted onto the right.

    Gerrard's positioning also means Aaron Lennon, who excelled in the first half of the goalless draw at the Ramat Gan Stadium, does not get the chance to utilise his extreme pace on his favoured right side.

    So, the murmurings once again are that Lampard should be axed, a move McClaren has always resisted because of his superb record at the highest level for Chelsea.

    But, mindful of the criticism his predecessor Sven-Goran Eriksson received for effectively turning former skipper David Beckham into an untouchable, McClaren emphasised his belief no-one is beyond being dumped.

    Whipping boys offer England chance of temporary relief

    BARCELONA - Whatever has gone wrong with England over the last six months, Steve McClaren knows he cannot put it right against Andorra here on Wednesday. Even if England were to hit double figures against the tiny principality, the pressure on their manager would ease off only a fraction between now and his squad's next genuine test in their Euro 2008 qualifying campaign, an end-of-season trip to Estonia.

    Failure to beat Andorra, against whom England scored five without reply in McClaren's first match in charge last year, is so unthinkable that the manager will be able to claim no credit from ending a winless run of five games in which his side have produced a solitary goal.

    A bit of target practice at Barcelona's Olympic stadium could nonethless serve a valuable purpose for McClaren as he seeks to rebuild the self-belief of a squad in which morale appears to be disintegrating.

    In the build-up to the match, McClaren could have done without having to deny suggestions that a rift has developed between him and Wayne Rooney, whose current difficulties in recapturing his best form in an England shirt is one of the main reasons why the team's supply of goals has dried up.

    McClaren dismissed the claims as "an absolute load of rubbish," insisting that Rooney is one of the players to whom he is closest. But it was telling that the head coach did not deny that things had got heated during the dressing room inquest into Saturday's disappointing goalless draw in Israel, a reality which had in any case already been confirmed by his captain, John Terry.

    Instead, McClaren implied that frank exchanges reflected a genuine willingness to address the mystery of why the likes of Rooney, Frank Lampard and Steven Gerrard have, of late, rarely transformed their club form to the international stage.

    "Everybody is together and knows what we have to do and we have got to start doing it," McClaren said.

    SUN GOES DOWN ON MCCLOWN

    Steve McClaren's unscheduled appearance in front of a baying press pack on Monday has been described as 'a public relations disaster as great as any perpetrated by any recent England boss'.

    Ridiculed as 'Mad Mac' on the back page of The Daily Mirror, and described as 'deluded' by The Sun and Daily Mail, McClaren's decision to face his detractors has backfired spectacularly after he told scornful hacks: "Someone who has watched a lot of international football called me and said it [England's display in Israel] was as good a display of football as they had seen over the entire weekend."

    It is perhaps just as well that McClaren declined to identify the perpetrator of that fanciful claim.

    Yet if the refusal of McClaren to stay below the firing line was laudable, his comments have failed to inspire confidence that he is capable of reversing England's declining fortunes.

    There will be more fevered anguish at his reluctance to accept the evidence of the previous two years and conclude that Steven Gerrard and Frank Lampard are incompatible. "Can Lampard and Gerrard play together? They can. They can. They can," the England manager responded with a mixture of desperation and playground histronics.

    Nor was he convincing in dismissing reports of a dressing-room row with Wayne Rooney. "Whatever happened after the game, there is no rift between me and Rooney," he insisted, before stretching credibility by declaring: "Me and Wayne Rooney have one of the best relationships between a player and coach."

    Meanwhile, Andy Johnson, rather than Frank Lampard, is expected to become the fall-guy for the failure to beat Israel...

    Lampard is Fit Despite Rooney's Best Efforts

    England midfielder Frank Lampard was fit enough to join in with his team-mates for their final training session ahead of Wednesday night's European Championship qualifier with Andorra in Barcelona.

    The Chelsea midfielder had strapping on his right wrist after being struck by a shot from Wayne Rooney in Monday's training session. But Lampard sustained no serious injury and is likely to be fit for the game England have to win to maintain any hopes of qualifying for the 2008 finals.

    There has been intense speculation Lampard could be dropped after an ineffective performance against Israel in Tel Aviv on Saturday. But England coach Steve McClaren would only say "Wait and see" when questioned about his possible line-up against Andorra, who were beaten 5-0 at Old Trafford in September.

    The entire 23-man squad were put through their paces by McClaren and his staff as he looked for a positive response following the savage criticism of the performance in Israel.
