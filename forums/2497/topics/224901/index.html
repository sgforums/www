    Chelsea have moved to tie their key stars to long-term deals as fears mount that Jose Mourinho will leave the club at the end of the season.

    Although chief exectuive Peter Kenyon has denied newspaper reports that this will be Mourinho's third and final year in west London, negotiations are already underway to tie a host of Stamford Bridge players to 'golden handcuffs' deals to stop them leaving with the Portuguese next summer.

    "My question is 'Why would he want to go?', and I don't think he does," Kenyon protested when asked about Mourinho's future. "We are chasing ghosts thinking he is going to ride off into the sunset. Where else is he going to go?"

    Such public confidence belies private fears that, whatever Chelsea's wishes, Mourinho will not be at Stamford Bridge next season. And that has manifested itself in Chelsea's latest attempts to extend the contracts of their key personnel, with the club reduced to stating during negotiations that they 'hoped' the manager would be in place next August, but 'they were not in a position to guarantee that.'

    Worried that Mourinho will attempt to take his players with him if he does leave for pastures new in the summer, Chelsea have been swift to offer massively improved contracts to their biggest names.

    Despite having over a year and a half remaining on his deal, Didier Drogba has seen his wages more than double to over Â£90,000-a-week in exchange for his commitment to the club until 2010.

    'Also elbowing for space at a crowded west London negotiating table are Chelsea's two English figureheads. John Terry and Frank Lampard are set to be upgraded to the same Â£121,000-a-week terms that brought Michael Ballack and Andriy Shevchenko to the club in the last transfer window,' reports The Sunday Times.

    Michael Essien is also in line for a massive pay-rise - despite already being on one of the longest contracts at the club. Chelsea have indicated that his wages will also exceed the Â£90,000-a-week barrier.

    While such deals seem unnecessary with so long remaining on the players' contracts, and while they further embarrass Kenyon's prediction that the club would be able to survive without vast handouts from Roman Abramovich by 2010, the Chelsea hierarchy feel they are necessary to prevent bids for those players from whichever new club Mourinho finds himself at next summer.

    Such deals are in stark contrast to those offered to William Gallas. Mourinho complained this week that "I did my best to try and keep him at the club," - an admission seen in some quarters as a criticism of Kenyon's tough bargaining.

    Mourinho also spoke for the first time about an apparent willingness to leave the club if his contract was paid off, saying of Abramovich: "The day he doesn't respect me, just give me the cheque and send me home."

    The Portuguese has threatened to resign twice during his tenure at Stamford Bridge. The first time, in April 2005, he was angry that the club had failed to support him in his disciplinary dispute with UEFA. Then, at the end of last season, Mourinho admitted "a couple of times during the season, I thought about closing the door and saying goodbye."

    Chelsea's solution then was to offer their manager a Â£5.2m-a-year contract extension - a policy they are repeating with his players as fears over the inevitability of his departure grow.
