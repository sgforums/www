    Are Chelsea everyone's second favourite team? Sportsmail looks at if we can love the Blues

     

     

     

    Good times at the Bridge: Chelsea have made a 100 per cent start to the season

     

     

     

    Twenty-five goals in six games, 100 per cent start to the season and playing attractive attacking football - Chelsea are the team on everyone's lips at the moment.

    Long gone are the defensive - but successful - methods of a certain Special One and instead the Stamford Bridge faithful are being treated to Carlo Ancelotti's brand of Italian style.

    But can we ever learn to love Chelsea?

     

     

     

     

    SPORTSMAIL SAYS...

     

    JAMIE REDKNAPP

    I’m loving watching Chelsea at the moment — they’re electrifying.

    They’re mirroring the image of Ancelotti, who was a top player, is a top coach and a top man.

    Some players’ on and off-field behaviour put a lot of people off. Let’s not get carried away, though, their season starts now with Man City, Marseille and Arsenal up next.

     

     

     

     

    MATT LAWTON

     

    Love might be too strong a word but we can admire them.

    They were the ‘enemies of football’ because of Mourinho’s rows but Abramovich deserves credit for the way he has tackled that image problem.

    Ancelotti is far more charming and the football his team play is more adventurous, too.

    And if we are jealous of clubs for their wealth, hasn’t the focus now shifted to Manchester City? 

     

     

    SIMON CASS
     

    Chelsea are certainly more loveable than during the Jose Mourinho era.

    The Special One provided great soundbites but his arrogance and workmanlike tactics won few admirers.

    Ancelotti’s amenable personality and attractive football have changed attitudes.

    Outsiders may never grow to love them but there’s no reason to loathe them anymore.

     

     

     

    MATT BARLOW

    Reasons to warm to Chelsea are emerging: enduring stars, lots of goals, homegrown starlets, Carlo Ancelotti.

    Reasons to dislike them are disappearing: unnecessary clumsy boasts are down, Peter Kenyon has gone and Man City have seized their mantle as transfer market bullies. 

    But love them? That’s a big commitment. Who truly loves two clubs?

     


     

    IAN LADYMAN

    It is hard not to admire a team that can roll sides over as convincingly as Chelsea.

    Personally, I never warmed to them under Jose Mourinho and Peter Kenyon. They had a strut back then that they hadn’t earned. Recently, though, I have softened.

    They are not the only team in Europe to spend big on players. I will never love them. Why would I? But they do deserve our respect.

     

     

    JOHN EDWARDS

    Probably not, but I could develop a soft spot for them, if they keep playing the way they are.

    The old stifling efficiency under Mourinho has given way to flair and freedom.

    As a lifelong devotee of non-League Altrincham, where you pay £13 and feel part of the proceedings, I should have a natural aversion to any big-time Charlies from London.

    But anyone who enjoys football played with pace and power would have to warm to this Chelsea team.

     


     

    CHRIS WHEELER

    Unlikely. You can admire the ruthless efficiency, as we have in other sports, but that’s as far as it goes.

    The secretive Abramovich, the scheming Kenyon and brooding Mourinho gave Chelsea a slightly sinister air which has lingered despite the changes in personnel.

    Cole, Terry and Drogba may be fine players but they are unlikely to win many popularity contests.
