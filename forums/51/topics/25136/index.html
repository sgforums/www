    One of the unique points of bowling as a competitive sport is that every competitor has, in effect, only one person to beat - oneself. Few other sports (billiards, golf) offer this. In most sports, one has to beat the opponent directly, such as in tennis. But in bowling, no matter how good or bad your opponent's shot-making is, you still have to play better than he to win. Hence the theory stated by many above that its' better to concentrate on one's own game.

    That said, however, there are strategies to *ahem* throw an opponent off balance. As what Ian has mentioned, everyone has a "Zone" in which they are practically invincible. The key is to make staying there as hard as possible.
    One example is: Some people like to play with a higher tempo, whilst others take their time. One can use this observation to affect the time each frame takes. Taking one's time to "break the opponents' routine"; vice-versa.

    Personally, I do not object to these tactics. They may not be sporting, but it is part and parcel of gamesmanship. After all, if one wants to succeed, one has to have a strong mental game as well as a physical and technical one. After all, what's the difference of being distracted by say, a noise in the crowd or by one's opponent? No excuse for either, in my opinion.

    It's all part of the game.
    (Assuming one is good enough to pull all this off without self-destructing)

    Another way to foil the opponent's game is to ruin their line while keeping your own to the pocket. Spinners excel at this, for obvious reasons. This is a very shrewd tactic utilised by some of the more knowlegeble coaches while selecting team line-ups especially.

    In the end, bowling is just a sport where to win, one has to be better than the rest. Unless the opponent physically hinders your game, the better man or woman should win.

    Well, there are always two faces to every puzzle. In this case, you can do anything you wish, but it will only affect the opponent if he/she is the type to be affected by your actions in the first place.

    For instance, some people cannot play well against expressive opponents, while others prefer to play such types. Just like Deamon's mention of Pete Webber (who, incidentally, is one of my favourite players ! ) - whose combination of verbal expressions/body language is considered unsporting by some. Some people may actually excel when playing "high energy" people like him. Michael Haugen Jr. shot 279 in a final match against Pete to lose to a 289.
    So, your game has to match the opponent's.

    Personally, I don't think there's anything wrong to express disgust at a brooklyn strike, especially if you know where you made the mistake. Some people may seize upon it as a weakness, yet others may be intimidated because it hints that you can only play better.

    I train with two people who have collected money from 300 games. One has a very showy demeanor, the other is so humble, you wouldn't think more of him. But both are devastating even while playing leisurely.

    Bottomline: Whatever you do, I think it's at best a measure you take to psycho yourself into thinking you're putting pressure on your opponent. A security blanket, if you will. The more important thing is to play as you are comfortable.
    Only in that fashion can you play to your own strengths, and that's the best insurance for a win.
