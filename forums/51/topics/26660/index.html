    There is one main reason why people choose to bowl without inserts:
    the fit. Many serious and frequent bowlers will tell you that with inserts, a very, VERY big problem is that over a period of time the rubber/plastic inserts will wear out, unlike that of the thumb, which never does. This means that everytime you throw a shot, even between frames, the equipment isn't reliably consistent. That's the foundation of bad shot-making. And depending on how much you bowl and how your release is like, inserts (the rubber ones, especially) wear out slowly but surely. When I was still using rubber inserts, I had to change my inserts about once every 2-3 months to keep the same approximate "feel" in the grip. Not to mention expensive.

    I know there are people who consider inserts as an aid to increase revolutions. I used to think so, but not anymore. At most, the inclusion of inserts gains a person perhaps 1-2 revolutions. And this is a very good bowler. If you're a person with little hand, do not expect the inserts to give you even 2 revs over the lane. Many bowlers in Singapore do not know how to calculate revolutions.

    The proper way is to use a video camera (not possible otherwise), and count how many revolutions a person has from his/her point of release to the arrows. Multiply this number by 4 to get the revolutions for the entire lane. A lot of people count all the way to the pindeck and its wrong. Most balls rev up more in the drier mid-lane and after the turn. Counting to the arrows is accurately the amount of revs off the bowlers' hand - the true measurement of revolutions.

    Think inserts can give you so much more before the arrows?? Think again.

    I thought I was pretty clear on what I was saying. Oh well.

    Prata is right - a low RG ball spools up quicker thru simple physics. But because the amount of energy imparted is the same for a bowler, regardless of which ball they use, the ball's RG distribution determines when the energy is expended.
    A high RG ball has an over/under tendency because it rarely rolls heavily enough in the midlane for enough friction to occur. Enough friction for what?? Well, if a ball has enough friction in the midlane, one can observe the ball reaction.

    A trained eye can tell if a ball is biting too early, too late or just nice. This is called reading the ball. And because the ball is the only thing that is in contact with the lanes, reading the ball reaction is essential to reading and mapping lanes.

    Therefore a high RG ball will more easily glide past the breakpoint and will give less "read" because it doesn't bite enough. Note: This is under the assumption that one is using a high RG ball on a condition better suited for low RG balls. If high RG balls are properly matched to a lane and bowler, then it can be read as well as a low RG ball. Conversely, a low RG ball on too dry a lane won't be readable because it'll roll or hook out waaaay before the proper midlane or breakpoint area.

    So Airgrinder, you aren't wrong about which balls are better suited to which lanes. It's just that you didn't know the rationale.

    -------------------------------------

    I take it that you know what flare is, so I'll just explain the relationship between this and differential.

    Simply put: RG determines how early a ball rolls. RG differential determines how much a ball flares.
    Imagine a see-saw with a very heavy person on one end, and a much lighter person on the other. Compare this with one with equal weights on both ends. Which one will tilt more forcefully?? This illustration is somewhat similar to that of RG differential.

    RG diff. refers to the difference between the highest and lowest RG values in a core. In our playground example, a big RG diff. means a big difference in weights of the two persons. Imagine the core as an off-balanced see-saw. The heavier end will want to drop down with gravity. The larger the RG diff., the faster the drop. Thus, the ball will "tilt" more, creating more flare.

    Lastly, flare has nothing to do with the shape of the breakpoint. I.e. snap, arc, heavy arc. Flare only determines the amount of hook area a ball can create.

    I've seen you bowl in the last , and I think you need to speed up your shots anyway. Even if you are planning on expanding your arsenal, increase your ballspeed. Today's equipment dictates the way we have to bowl. Even the pros have to change their styles to play today's gear.     Prata is correct - do not think too much about the numbers. They are there to help those who can read them. One can still be a great bowler while not being able to understand any of them.

    In any case, here is how RG diff. works. Imagine two open doors you are going to slam shut. One door has an edge that is 30cm from the hinge, the other is long, measuring 1m from edge to hinge. The longer distance would mean that door gets slammed with more force, right? That is the RG diff.

    The edge of the door is one end of the weightblock in the core (either top or bottom) and the hinge is the other end. The greater this difference, the harder the core will rotate. Do you follow?

    So when the core rotates, the ball flares. Thus a ball with a big RG diff. will flare more than one with a small RG diff. Understand?

    Now, this is RG: Assume there are two cellphones in front of you. Both are the same size as a Nokia 8210, ok? Now imagine you have to spin them on the spot on their backs. I know some people do this, hopefully you are one of them. One of the phones has it's weight in the centre so it spins very easily and quickly, yah?? But the other has its weight distributed very oddly, with it heavier at the top than in the centre. This makes it spin very slowly, even though you spun them with the same force. This is RG determining how easily a ball is to turn, can you see it?
