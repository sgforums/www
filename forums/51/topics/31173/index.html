    A Taipei helicopter spinner ball will move forward as near the target (pin 1) it will back up into the 1,3 pocket. Western spinners (rare or extinct?) used a heavier ball of up to 16lb, whereas 90% Taipei spinners used no heavier than 12lb. If you paste a tracer sticker on the ball you will be able to observe the differences in axis between the two techniques and why on ball backup while another hook even though both spins.     The term "western spinner" is an oxymoron.

    As pointed out before, this is simply a name the author uses to denote a bowler with a very low track. Before the Urethane era of bowling balls, the extremely rare number of players who could hook rubber balls had to impart tremendous amounts of torque to the balls combined with a very low track to create the area we know as "hook". With today's equipment, a low track is actually not necessary. It simply looks like the ball is spinning.

    I won't even compare the two styles because they are two different forms of technique. It's hook versus spin. If only because of the terminology used by a certain author causes some perceived similarity, but that's deceiving.

    I don't know if there is a name for a low-track bowler. But a tweener refers to someone who is half-strokes and half-cranks the ball.

    Hook bowler progression in terms of power:
    Stroker --> Tweener --> Power player (crank)
