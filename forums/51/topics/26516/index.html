    I choose option (3).

    Walking toward the target is always the best way to go. Often, it is not possible to walk toward the target throughout the approach, such as in Ian's case. In cases such as this, and indeed in most cases, the teaching is that bowlers should walk straight on, and only changing direction to angle inward/outward toward the target at the final 2 steps.

    This way, the first few steps avoid the inconsistency of drift and take up a small area, while from the 2nd last step onward, the bowler's momentum is positioned to thrust toward the target, hence relieving the arm of a lot of stress and tension, which is necessary for a clean release.

    Make no mistake about this. I only sounds easy - even the pros find this a very advanced technique to master. And this is also the reason why a lot of amatuers (like us) cannot play the deep inside line properly. Most people simply stand deep in, and try to "swing" the ball outwards. This, as they have found out, is next to impossible to execute while at the same time keeping the same amount of power and push as their regular, tighter release.

    airgrinder

    walk straight to the right, hence blocking the downswing of the ball
    If you're trying to swing the ball to the right, the swingplane for your arm should be angled inward-out anyway, so the lower body does not get in the way. If you drift, it's even worse because there is more area of the lower body exposed. AND you lose power because to avoid the sliding ankle, many people swing so far out, their elbow is not next to the body, and ball is too far away from the ankle. All this loses power.

    Actually, the theory is that one does walk to the right in the final 1Â½ step. In the final step, pushing into the slide, the sliding foot should be pointing straight (not to the pindeck, but to the right) and not pointing at the pindeck, but sliding to the right. It looks confusing as I type it... oh dear. But I hope someone understands, if no one does, it's ok.
