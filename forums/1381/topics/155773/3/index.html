    Parents, principals against evangelising in schools

    AT LEAST three teachers have been hauled up at St Joseph's Institution (SJI) in the past 10 years - and each reprimanded for evangelising their students.

    The three teachers - all women in their 30s and 40s - were ordered to stop inviting their students to church events and camps.

    The school said it had been receiving complaints about their activities from other teachers, parents and students.

    After the warning, all three stopped doing it.

    But their cases underscore the importance of an issue recently thrown into the spotlight: What kind of latitude should teachers be given when discussing religion with their students?

    A letter to The Straits Times Forum page last Saturday told the story of a Primary 6 pupil who was urged to attend a church service by her teacher. When the 12-year-old said her Buddhist parents would not allow it, she was apparently told that it was not up to them to decide.

    Naturally, it left her parents fuming.

    The pupil's mother, housewife Shirley Ng, 40, said: 'It is not within the teacher's role to persuade my daughter to go to church. How could he tell her that it was not up to us to decide? And that she was free to make up her own mind? She is only 12 and still under my charge.'

    The letter has sparked a flurry of other letters to the ST Forum page - some complaining of similar instances, others simply eager to air their views on the issue.

    The Ministry of Education (MOE) said in a response published yesterday that it was opposed to teachers converting those in their charge.

    'Our schools are secular, and teachers should not be engaged in proselytising their students,' it said.

    The episode has touched a nerve among parents, schools, teachers and religious organisations. Most share the MOE's view.

    Religious organisations such as the Singapore Islamic Scholars and Religious Teachers Association (Pergas), the Catholic Church and the National Council of Churches in Singapore unani- mously rejected proselytising in school.

    While all the 33 schools polled by The Sunday Times frowned on it, four said they had no objections if done informally outside school.

    The four are Bedok North Secondary School, Anderson Junior College, Methodist Girls' School (Secondary) and CHIJ Secondary (Toa Payoh).

    But they stressed that it is a 'yes' that comes with many qualifications.

    Said Madam Chou Weng Ha, principal of Bedok North Secondary: 'If it helps delinquents and students' moral development, if it's done in the teacher's personal capacity outside the school context, if the teacher is sensitive to the student's family background and religion - then I don't have major objections.' Other schools do, however.

    At the heart of their argument is the belief that a teacher's role is to teach, not preach.

    Said Meridian Junior College principal Esther Lai: 'Whatever we do, we do it in a secular way, because we are a secular college and our students have different faiths. We are not here to propagate religion, we are here to teach.'

    Brother Michael Broughton, deputy principal (student development) at St Joseph's Institution, agreed and added that the school had an obligation not to do it.

    'The parents trust us. It's against the integrity of the children confided to your care to do something to them that might turn them against their parents' value system or culture.'

    Many were also quick to point out that parents who send their children to schools that are partly funded by a religious organisation must expect some degree of religious influence. Methodist Girls' School (Secondary) for example, has all its students - Christian or non-Christian - attend the school's chapel services, but those who are not comfortable can bring it up with the principal.

    Manjusri Secondary, a Buddhist school, has daily morning prayers and Vesak Day celebrations that are attended by all its students. The non-Buddhists at these events would watch and are not required to do what their Buddhist schoolmates do.

    Other government-aided schools do not even go that far.

    At St Joseph's Institution and Catholic Junior College (CJC), religious services are optional for non-Catholics and daily prayers are made as inclusive as possible.

    At some events like National Day, the schools have prayers done by representatives of every religion. Last Wednesday, the morning prayer at CJC included a mention of its Muslim students who had started fasting.

    In all the schools, the policy against proselytising is an unwritten rule. Apart from that, teachers are reminded verbally from time to time.

    Still, some choose to defy it.

    A 31-year-old English language teacher at a secondary school admitted to The Sunday Times that she has invited about 10 students to church events even though her principal had warned her against it. Half of them went and one converted to Christianity.

    Another teacher in her thirties, who teaches science in a junior college, is more careful.

    She would wait until her students have completed their studies and left the school before approaching them. Over eight years, she has invited about 10 former students to her church. Only three went.

    Students say these incidents - though rare - have been going on for a long time.

    National University of Singapore undergraduate Lin Jingyi, 19, remembers her primary school teacher inviting her classmates to church. Huang Peijun, 15, a Secondary 3 student at Peicai, also said her primary school teacher used to tell religious stories at the end of lessons.

    While all this has parents worried, most are willing to give teachers a little leeway outside school.

    Housewife Urimila Misir, 48, who has three children in Christian schools, said: 'It is all right for the teacher to ask the student to go to church, just to have a look. But the teacher shouldn't repeatedly ask them to go and get more involved in the religion.'

    Taxi driver Cheng Ah Huat, 57, agreed: 'If it's outside of school, if the teacher is just informally socialising with the students, then it's okay for the teacher to talk to the students about his or her own religion. But not in the classroom.'

    A spokesman from the Inter-Religious Organisation summed it up.

    'The teachers have to draw the boundaries. When the student is still under their charge, there is an unequal relationship as the child looks up to them. They can't take advantage of that to spread their beliefs.

    'In this situation, to proselytise is a real problem. As we are a secular state and a multi-religious society, we must create and respect the space for all religions.'

    Additional reporting by Sarah Ng and Liaw Wy-Cin

    Today's ST Forum

    No proselytising allowed in schools

    A RECENT Insight article ('Say aaah... men'; ST, Oct 15) discussed the issue of proselytising.

    Any form of proselytising to students is strictly not allowed in our schools, including both government and mission schools. Schools will take action against any teacher found to have engaged in proselytising.

    The article cited the case of a National Junior College physics lecturer who invited his class to a Christmas party and prayed over them, and who attached Christian sayings to his lecture notes. The principal has warned the lecturer and counselled him. The lecturer is remorseful about his actions.

    If parents have any concerns regarding any action involving their children being encouraged to join a religion other than their own, I encourage them to approach the school.

    Ms Geraldine Chay Mei Fong ('Not true that all schools are secular'; ST, Oct 14) pointed out that religious values are imparted in mission schools. She supported this practice, and felt that parents who want a secular education for their children should send them to government schools.

    Mission schools follow clear rules. While they can conduct prayers, religious classes and chapel services or mass, these must be optional. They cannot compel any student to participate in any religious activity against the student's wish.

    Students are allowed to withdraw from any such activity if they are uncomfortable with participating in it, or if requested by their parents.

    Further, attendance at any such activity cannot be a condition for students to be admitted to the school. The time used for these activities must also be in addition to that required for the schools to cover the subjects in the regular Ministry of Education curriculum.

    It is in school that children of different backgrounds build bonds and develop shared aspirations as Singaporeans. We encourage parents and schools to work together to ensure that we sustain the strong social cohesion that we have built so far.

    Wong Siew Hoong Director of Schools Ministry of Education
