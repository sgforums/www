    Please bear with my long post.

    Interesting you should mention Freud, but I wonder if you have considered the implications of the stance you are taking.

    Firstly Freud often argues from a position that is not unlike the religious point of view, the difference is that he offers a materialist alternative to many of the things that humans might consider to be desires placed in us by something beyond. Let's admit for once that he has built up a very convincing case. But there is a large problem in wanting to adopt this view:

    In Freud's point of view everything we do (including me typing this reply now) is simply a suppressed and edited version of the sex drive (all actions are done for the ultimate sake of sex), indeed as you have quoted Freud arguing that even such things that gives a person a 'moral' compass all simply come from the superego... then indeed morality and guilt are just things that are totally naturalistic and can (and should) be controlled by the application of neurological suggestion and science.

    Freud of course, is arguing from a position that there is no such thing as traditional morality, they are all simply guises of the ego and sex drive. The crux of his argument rests totally on the notion that things we consider above us like conscience and morality have nothing else but natualistic and nonrational origins, and hence we cannot consider them to be rational or worth listening to at all.

    Just as one considers another person's pacifism to be irrational if it is simply based on cowardice, or another person's argument for the value of properity invalid because he is simply loves his riches to an irrational extent. Seemingly rational causes are rejected if they come from irrational causes. Freud attacks traditional morality on these grounds (that they come from irrational causes), with wide success.

    Now the problem is this, if our conscience is really a product of the superego and simply a perverted sex drive kind of thing... why should we listen to it at all? It is not any more rational then my liking for chee chong fun or some other kind of food. If Freud's argument was indeed correct, then the implication is that there was no need to listen to the conscience at all, since it is a naturalistic process, another kind of raw material subject to our desires to change it... rather then us being a slave to it and the guilt it produces.

    Firstly I'm not sure you might be tempted to buy such an implication, because while it may be tempting to think of conscience as naturalistic... yet we insist on not putting naturalistic conclusions to it. If I kill another person's family and my conscience pricks me, I could just dismiss it as some trick of the mind that my parents put in me when I was a child. Indeed in Freud's universe there is no right and wrong, and I'm not sure you would buy it. Being classified as intelligent apes is one thing, but considering ourselves as nothing but intelligent apes is yet another. The distinction is important to make.

    Now it is obvious that we are creatures of flesh and blood, and obviously to work in us such a thing as a conscience has to be applied in the physical mind in some areas or another (some areas of the mind have been mapped to handle our notion of right and wrong), and of course the physical mind has probably to be taught in some ways to exercise this notion as well. But one suspects it might be folly to consider that the sense of conscience is nothing but these activations and movements of atoms in the brain and the nursery. One might as well denounce maths as activations of the brain, or running as no more meaingful then the moving of limbs. Obviously they are not!

    It seems to me that in the activation of conscience we are grasping (or trying to grasp) some higher universal law. And the important thing is that we seem to, and in fact must believe that this higher universal law is absolute, and applies to all beings... and somehow we must listen to it.

    C.S. Lewis noted that a lot of dogs don't understand pointing. They may see the finger pointing but don't get what it is about. They may sniff the finger or lick it. But the dog sees and experience the same facts as we do... in fact the dog's world is often all facts and very little understand of how they work (except in doggy notions). Now his concern was that we were encouraging such a doglike mind in regarding science, having all the facts but never really considering how to put them together, except in a very doglike way. Freud might have sucessfully decoded (though many scientists disagree with him on the idea that ego and sex are responsible for all behaviour) the physcial facts behind the conscience... but he might have very well missed much of what it's about.

    I hope you consider this.
