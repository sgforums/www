    Actually a case of mistranslation has occurred here. Another one of those inaccuracies in translation.

    If you noticed the underlined words above, both are in the future tense: Â“will be bound in heaven and will be loosed in heavenÂ” in some of our English versions.

    But notice a revision in the Modern King James Version about this verse:

    Mt 18:18 Truly I say to you, Whatever you shall bind on earth shall occur, having been bound in Heaven; and whatever you shall loose on earth shall occur, having been loosed in Heaven.

    Why the revision? Which is a correct translation of the original Greek? Actually, in the original Greek, the words Â‘will be bound in heavenÂ” and Â“will be loosed in heavenÂ” are both in the perfect tense not in the future tense. Perfect tenses in Greek signify an action that is begun in the past reaching a point of culmination or completion and its effects linger to the present.

    Thus to translate the original Greek as future tenses is inaccurate like in the case of the New King James and the NIV.

    If i were to paraphrse this verse based on the tense of the original it would be something like this:

    "whatever you bind on earth, make it sure that that which you bind is what is already bound in heaven, and whatever you loose, make it sure that that which you loose is what is already loosed in heaven."

    This fact is attested by Greek scholars:

    Kenneth Wuest, an eminent Greek scholar, rendered this verse as: Â“And whatever you bind on earth, shall have been already bound in heaven; and whatever you loose on earth shall have already been loosed in heaven.Â”
