    Sorry I made a mistake by saying most Taiwanese are Yi Kuan Dao followers.

    Yi Kuan Dao is like a "new age of eastern religions/philosophies".

    I-Kuan Tao 一貫道

    ((I-Kuan Tao))I-Kuan Tao (also spelled Yiguan Dao) could be translated into English as "Religion of One Unity." Many regard it as a spin-off from Taoism, but I-Kuan Tao followers claim their philosopy unifies common truths underlying Taoism, Buddhism, Confucianism, Christianity, Islam, Judaism, and Hinduism. Unlike classical Taoism, they celebrate one god that is is higher than all the others, Mingming Shangdi (God of Clarity).

    Buddhist elements are often incorporated in I-Kuan Tao temples, especially the Maitreya Buddha (often erroneously nicknamed the "Fat Buddha" or "Happy Buddha").

    I-Kuan Tao was first established in 1930 in Ji'nan, Shandong province (in mainland China) by two men, Shi Zuang and Shi Mu. In just 15 years, I-Kuan Tao spread to all the provinces of China. However, the Communist takeover of mainland China in 1949 caused many I-Kuan Tao followers to flee to Taiwan and other countries in Southeast Asia. Today, more than 2/3 of all I-Kuan Tao disciples are believed to live in Taiwan, while others are scattered through Southeast Asia, Hong Kong, Korea, Japan and overseas Chinese communities in Western countries. Today are approximately 200 established I-Kuan Temples in Taiwan, plus at least 30,000 I-Kuan Tao family shrines in people's homes.

    I-Kuan Tao believers are expected to follow a vegetarian diet. It's estimated that half of the vegetarian restaurants in Taiwan are operated by I-Kuan Tao adherents. Because they are permitted to eat eggs, it was once popular to call I-Kuan Tao the "Duck-Egg Religion," a term which is considered impolite.

    I-Kuan Tao is involved in various community welfare projects, operating a number of kindergartens, hospitals, clinics and retirement homes.
