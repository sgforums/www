    New Paper journalist Joanne Soh got rapped for remark that “All men are pigs”

    December 6, 2009 by admin  
    Filed under Headlines

    Leave a comment

    New Paper journalist Joanne Soh was rapped for her sexist remarks made in a recent article on 4 December entitled – “My mum is right: All men are pigs.” as part of series discussing why men still cheat even when they have a beautiful wife like Tiger Wood’s.

    Joanne began her article with a typical stereotype of men:

    “My mother once offered sage advice: We must always be aware of our husbands’ activities, regardess of how devoted and loving they are. Her rationale? Men are born with roving eyes, een the good and the well-brought up ones.”

    She subsequently launched a tirade against men by laying the blame for cheating on spouses solely on them.

    Her article brought a torrent of criticisms from readers the very next day, expectedly from men which were published in today’s New Paper.

    Jimmy Ho felt that marriage breakdowns are sometimes contributed by women:

    “If men are pigs….then who are the asses that married us? The comment is at best a sexist piece. It is extremely judgmental and myopic to say the least…..Marriage breaks apart for various reaasons, the roving eye of a man is obviously one of them, but does that exclude the problems that may have been caused by the woman?”

    Yeh Siang Hui wrote that infidelity is not gender-specific:

    “What was most offensive, of course, was her declaration – “Men are pigs”. This is unfair, unreasonable, unprofessional, and uncalled-for. The writer has conveniently forgotten that if men cheat, so do women. Just as there are wives with cheating husbands, there are husbands with cheating wives too. Just as there are men with roving eyes, so too, women with roving eyes.”

    His views were shared by Lawrence Soh:

    “I have friends who were dumped when their wives found more lucrative relationships. Women generally worship wealth, status, good life and power, and if they cannot attain these on their own steam, they search for these through relationships, legitimate or otherwise. We see this everywhere. Unfortunately, the Women’s Charter does not give men this advantage. Divorce is a very expensive affair for men.”

    Others simply brush aside Joanne’s arguments and throw brickbats at her “professionalism” as a journalist.

    Ambrose Sim wrote:

    “Your article is highly offensive and unreasonable. It seriously lacks objectivity and is unprofessional from a journalistic point of view.”

    Javert Ong added:

    “Not all men are pigs, and it is extremely rude and unpleasant to call men names. Can we call women bitches, sluts, crows if they commit adultery?”

    Not surprisingly, the women-folk are supportive of Joanne and jumped to her defence.

    Karina Wong even praised Joanne’s article:

    “Just wanted to compliment you on a well written article. Simply love it! Even if these men deny their affairs, it is a fact that they are born with roving eyes and when it comes to women, they are never rational.”

    Christina Teo thanked Joanne for revealing the truth that men are indeed “pigs”:

    “Men are pigs. Thanks to your columnist Joanne Soh for writing it.”

    Judging from the wide disparity between the opinions of both sexes, it does appear that the adage – “Men are from Mars and Women are from Venus” holds some truth after all.

    But how does this explain the rising divorce rates in Singapore due to adultery, which is the commonest cited cause? Why are our children more likely to stray than our parents?

    According to a New Paper report published on the same day, one lawyer said that three out of every 10 adultery cases she sees nowadays involve the wife. A decade ago, it was only one out of 10.

    A private investigator said he has seen the number of adultery cases involving cheating wives rise from 30 per cent of all cases 10 years ago to 50 per cent.

    How does Joanne Soh feel sleeping besides a “pig”? And what are her husband’s thoughts on her unwarranted outburst against men? Perhaps it is nothing more than a gimmick to attract readers to the New Paper which has been suffering from declining readership.

    Sensitive topics like race, religion and politics are considered taboo in Singapore. Bashing men, however, is one sure way of driving up one’s readership without attracting unwanted attention from the authorities.
