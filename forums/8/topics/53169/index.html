    Video pirates' new ruse: parallel imports     They submit these fake discs - mostly Japanese dramas - to censors and then pass them off as Real McCoy; new anti-video piracy group out to fight scam

    By K. C. Vijayan

    NEW economy video pirates are resorting to parallel imports to get fake discs of movies and TV shows past the censors, and retailing them at market prices, well before their official release date.

    PIRATED VCD/DVD

    Despite repeated attempts to rid Singapore of video piracy, unlicensed copies of top movies are still hitting the streets -- some even before the shows are screened in the cinemas.

    Click here to poll

    For instance, industry detectives uncovered Lord Of The Rings - The Twin Towers in shops early this month, even though its worldwide video release date is Aug 26.

    The fakes were imported directly from an overseas supplier and copies were submitted to the censors, then passed off as genuine products in legitimate retail shops.

    The film's authorised distributors Alliance Entertainment took out a High Court order three weeks ago against three companies for copyright infringement.

    Alliance chief executive Tan Poh Lam explained that the video pirates look to parallel importing to order their goods directly from suppliers overseas, who are not licensed to make copies.

    'You can't bring in a fake Mercedes-Benz and pass it off here as the genuine product,' he said. 'But it's possible to get away with a fake disc.'

    Hardest hit here are the Japanese dramas. According to a study done last year by the Anti-Video Piracy Association of Singapore, 90 per cent of the VCDs of these shows sold here are pirated.

    It said there were 355 pirated Japanese drama titles on VCD on sale here then; a third of these were in packaging different from the original. It also said there were about 510 pirated Japanese animated DVD and VCD titles being offered.

    These add up to a $1.7 million loss in revenue for the Government, besides hitting the bona fide owners, said the study.

    Industry players want to clean up the situation, and yesterday, 19 companies, including major overseas players such as Fuji Television Networks, Nippon TV Network Corporation as well as local firms, came together to officially launch the association.

    Lawyer Toh See Kiat, who was a former MP as well the anti-piracy group's president, remarked then: 'Singapore is fast becoming the headquarters for pirates of Japanese anime and serials.'

    The group already has a five-year battle plan. This includes helping the public identify pirated products and working with the authorities to promote stricter controls.

    Lauding the moves, Senior Minister of State for Law and Home Affairs Ho Peng Kee, who officiated at the launch, said the Government will strengthen the laws to include protecting copyright over a longer period and swifter access to damages arising from copyright infringements.

    He noted that since the police formed the Intellectual Property Rights Branch three years ago, about $45 million worth of pirated items have been seized in more than 2,000 raids.
