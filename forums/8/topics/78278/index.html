    Most aerosol cans carry a warning not to incinerate because the can may explode. The reason is that all aerosol cans use a gas (CO2 or sometimes a hydrocarbon)as the propellant. When any gas is heated it will either expand and/or cause pressure increase within the container if it cannot expand. The increase in pressure eventually reaches the point where the structural integrity of the can is breached. The sudden expansion of gas, commonly combined with the metal schrapnel is a powerful explosion. If the gas is a flammable hydrocarbon, the explosion will be a powerful fireball.

    why?

    anyway, an aerosol can is pressurised... so that it can spray out stuff... high pressure moves into low pressure.. basic physics...

    so, when you heat a can, think laws of thermodynamics... holding volume constant...

    P1/T1 = P2/T2

    temp goes up, pressure must go up to remain at the mathematical constant of P1/T1

    so when T2 is, say, 4 times the temp of T1, P2 must be 4 times of P1

    if P2 is greater than what the metal can will stand, a rapid deterioration of its integrity will result.. which is the explosion.. akin to a balloon bursting...

    okay?
