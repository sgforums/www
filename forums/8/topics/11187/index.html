    1 Apr 01, 13:53
    IF THE so-called Reds renaissance under Gerard Houllier is to become a long-term reality, then Liverpool fans could argue justifiably that it began at Anfield last night.

    Forget the half-hearted League Cup triumph over inferior opposition. The Premiership is the real yardstick and Manchester United is the team to beat.

    For the first time in seven years at Anfield, Liverpool achieved that objective, defeating the most dominant side of the 90s 2-0.

    Having won at Old Trafford earlier in the season, this was the Reds' first League double over their old rivals for 22 years.

    But these statistics may pale into insignificance.

    Ultimately, soccer historians may claim that this contest, coupled with Liverpool's Old Trafford win, marked the slow reversal of fortunes for both sides - the beginning of the end for United and just the beginning for Liverpool.

    Make no mistake, United came off second-best to Liverpool in every department.

    Considering Liverpool has a fixture pile-up that surpasses even United's, it attacked with conviction.

    Handling the treacherous rain better, Steven Gerrard and Danny Murphy shackled Nicky Butt and Ryan Giggs effectively.

    Having returned from injury, Giggs was a spectator for most of the match.

    But United suffered the most damage before the match when Alex Ferguson decided to rest Jaap Stam.

    His absence allowed the Reds to penetrate United's defence at will.

    In the third minute, Robbie Fowler's long-range effort curled wide and Sami Hyypia saw two headers saved by Fabien Barthez.

    A somewhat-subdued United had a couple of chances, but Dwight Yorke's header bounced over and a Giggs drive was saved by Sander Westerveld.

    It soon became obvious that the deadlock would be broken by the home side.

    And what a stunning strike it was from one of England's most exciting young players.

    Gerrard's troublesome back forced his withdrawal from England's squad against Albania last week.

    But England's loss was certainly Liverpool's gain when the rejuvenated midfielder almost broke the net in the 16th minute.

    Collecting a lay-off from Robbie Fowler some 25 metres from goal, he unleashed a right-footed shot with such ferocity that Barthez was left grasping at thin air.

    A second goal looked inevitable and should have arrived on the half-hour mark, but Emile Heskey's low shot was saved by Barthez.

    It was left to the Reds' captain to remind Eriksson that he should still occupy that coveted striking role alongside Michael Owen.

    In the 40th minute, Gerrard, who else, sent in a deep cross, which Gary Neville looked set to clear.

    But the slippery conditions contributed to an embarrassing slip, leaving Fowler time to trap the ball and smash it past the stranded Barthez with his customary aplomb.

    That goal killed the contest as a viewing spectacle.

    Considering Liverpool has lost only once at Anfield in the Premiership this season, United was left with a huge mountain to climb.

    And the obstacle came in the form of Finnish international Hyypia.

    He was magnificent at the back, covering every blade of grass in and around his penalty box and ensured that Yorke had a comparatively quiet afternoon.

    The great Liverpool sides of the 70s and 80s were renowned for their ability to 'close shop' at Anfield and now, finally, Houllier has built a side that displays that defensive hallmark.

    The tireless midfield work of Gerrard and Murphy was soured slightly when the latter was sent off in the 69th minute for two late tackles.

    Surely now, United, famed for its comebacks, would produce another?

    Yorke had two late efforts saved, but it was not to be the Red Devils' day.

    Whenever they play, there is always a confident side on display, which defends its lead with little difficulty - only this time, it was their opponent, the old enemy. And that will hurt.

    When both managers left at the final whistle, Ferguson walked off hurriedly, ahead of a jubilant Houllier, who was just behind.

    He had no choice. The pragmatic Scot knows that the quiet Frenchman is getting closer all the time.
