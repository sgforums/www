    THE runway markings and lighting are now the focus.

    Almost a week after the crash of the ill-fated SIA Boeing 747-400, questions are being asked about them.

    So far, the focus has been on pilot error as initial investigations have revealed that the plane had taken off from the wrong runway.

    The pilots had turned into the wrong runway. But did he make that mistake because of the way the airport is managed?

    Two pilots interviewed by the International Herald Tribune say that for years, pilots flying into Taiwan had complained about lighting and markings on the runways at Chiang Kai-shek airport.

    COMPLAINTS

    The paper did not name the pilots, but said that a Western pilot had told it that for nine years pilots have been complaining.

    He said that since the construction of a large parking apron for cargo planes at one end of runway 05R, as well as an adjacent cargo terminal, pilots had been warning that its continued lighting and marking as a runway made the taxiway a potential hazard.

    The 05R runway is parallel to the main airport runway (05L), from which the crashed plane was supposed to have taken off.

    The pilot added that for many years pilots using the airport had recommended that this runway be marked and lit as a taxiway.

    Said the pilot: "To fail to do so, we argued, was setting a trap for a dark and stormy night."

    And it was a dark and stormy night last Tuesday.

    An Australian pilot interviewed also confirmed that there had been complaints about the marking and lighting at the airport.

    He said that there were complaints that the runways and taxiways were not properly differentiated.

    Mr Yong Kay, head of Taiwan's Aviation Safety Council, said he was looking into whether pilots had been complaining for years.

    The Aviation Safety Council is leading the official investigation into the crash.

    Mr Yong said that in Taiwan, runways were normally lit at night by white centre and edge lights, while taxiways had a green centre line of lights, but no edge lights.

    However, there are doubts on whether the edge lights were on.

    According to The Asian Wall Street Journal, flight attendants interviewed by Mr Yong's team say lights on the side of the wrong runway were lit.

    STILL USED AS TAXIWAY

    But Mr Yong said the staff at the control tower say the switch for the runway sidelights was off. He is still hoping to interview third parties on this issue.

    The airport had notified all airlines using the airport on Aug 31 that the wrong runway was under repair and closed for flights.

    But it did not close the beginning of the runway because it was being used as a taxiway.

    Experts retrace last moments

    AN SIA cargo plane (above) was used to simulate SQ006 and retrace the crucial last few minutes after it turned into the wrong runway and crashed.

    On board were 15 investigators - including experts from Taiwan's Aviation Safety Council, Singapore, the US and Australia - Taiwanese officials said yesterday.

    The simulation was carried out at 3.30am yesterday to match conditions when SQ006 crashed at 11.17pm last Tuesday.

    The plane followed the exact route taken by SQ006 before turning onto the top of runway 05R.

    Investigators also tested the radio systems of the control tower and the plane as well as the lighting systems of both runways 05R and 05L.

    The three pilots involved in the crash were not asked to take part.

    They are not allowed to leave Taiwan until investigations are completed.
