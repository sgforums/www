    Price fight
    Cellphone customers will have plenty to smile about as telcos, worried about rule on number portability, slug it out

    By Kelvin Wong

    AN ALL-OUT price war has broken out among Singapore's three mobile phone operators, with StarHub making an unprecedented offer.

    Customers benefit from the ruling that lets them keep their numbers when switching phone operators.
    From tomorrow, its customers can make free international calls on its 018 IDD economy service to the United States, China and Hong Kong, three of the most popular destinations.

    However, they have to pay the local toll charges, as they now do on the telco's existing offer of free calls to Malaysia.

    StarHub 's move prompted SingTel to announce late last night that from next Friday, discounts of up to 69 per cent will be offered for eight destinations through its v019 non-premium service.

    These destinations are Australia, China, Hong Kong, India, Japan, Malaysia, South Korea and Taiwan.

    The ante was raised beyond free incoming calls after a day of one-upmanship yesterday, which coincided with the introduction of a new ruling.

    The ruling prompted SingTel, the dominant player, to fire the first salvo in a bid to keep customers, since the ruling lets cell-phone users hold on to the same number when they switch operators.

    All they pay is a one-off administrative fee when previously, they were charged a monthly fee.

    To coax customers not to defect, or 'churn', SingTel offered free incoming calls for the first time yesterday, copying what StarHub has been doing for three years since it entered the market.

    M1 will do the same from today but with even more free talk time.

    These moves led StarHub to announce its free IDD 018 service, which will be given to pre-paid customers too. At the moment, its customers pay eight cents a minute to the US, 18 cents to China and 28 cents to HK.

    'The new offer will be open-ended with no deadline in mind,' said its spokesman last night.

    Market watchers do not expect the war to go on for long because, at some point, one of the telcos will say, 'enough'.

    'Otherwise, their costs will keep going up and they will just bleed,' said JP Morgan telecom analyst Kelvin Tay.

    The current war recalls a similar campaign in 1999, when the entry of M1 sparked several rounds of price cuts. As a result, customers' bills halved.

    SingTel , in its full-page ads in The Straits Times yesterday, offered four new packages, with two of them giving free incoming calls.

    The two plans, iOne and iTwo, also offer more free outgoing talk time each month. For $28, iOne gives 80 minutes and iTwo, at $88, give 680 minutes.

    Not to be outdone, M1 introduced two new plans too. Both also gave free incoming calls but 100 minutes and 700 minutes of free outgoing talk time.

    It threw in an extra: calls to three pre-determined M1 numbers will be at half price.

    Both SingTel and M1 also said their existing customers can switch to the new plans.

    SingTel Mobile chief executive Lucas Chow was candid yesterday about the motivation for the new mobile plans.

    'The key reason is mobile number portability. If existing customers want to 'churn', they need not look further since we also offer the same thing,' he said, referring to StarHub's existing Power Play and Power Talk plans.

    Analysts interviewed said the latest offering might bring some new subscribers into the mobile phone market but they did not see many customers switching operators in the near term, being locked in by their one- or two-year contracts.

    'But in the long term, operators are going to lose subscribers to each other and are going to face higher costs,' said JP Morgan's Mr Tay.

    Mr Melvin Tay, a 23-year-old student with a StarHub account, said he would look into 'churning' when his contract expired next year.

    'Since all three now offer free incoming calls, I can choose the one that gives me the best reception in Serangoon, where I live.'
