    A BUDDHIST monk stood on Siloso beach yesterday afternoon, chanting prayers.

    Barely 24 hours earlier, a few yards from where the monk was praying, 17-year-old Steven Sim Jian Feng had disappeared under the water while swimming with a group of friends.

    Beachgoers looked curiously as the monk, flanked by Steven's distraught family members, prayed for the boy. Many did not even know of the drowning. Most had no idea that this was the fourth drowning at Siloso beach this year.

    And here's one startling fact that very few will know: So far this year, beach patrol officers have saved almost 70 persons from drowning at Sentosa.

    And last year, when two people drowned off Siloso, nearly 100 were saved from drowning at Sentosa.

    The truth is that the fine, white sand and clear blue waters spell danger for the unwary Â— more so for those who don't know their limits and even more for brash teens.

    Mr Adam Nathershah, who swims and tans at the beach twice a month, said inexperienced swimmers could run into trouble with strong underwater currents.

    "Sometimes, I can feel it sweeping me in opposite directions," said Mr Nathershah, 22, who said the slope of the lagoon shore was "steep". The water in the lagoon can be 4.5m deep during high tide.

    He suggested putting floating platforms, or pontoons, in the lagoon.

    "At least that way, people can cling on to them if they are tired from swimming," he said.

    Pontoons are put up at Palawan beach, another of Sentosa's three beaches.

    Sentosa's senior beach patrol officer Julian Ong doesn't buy the "currents" theory as three man-made islets Â— acting as breakwaters Â— protect the lagoon.

    "Most strong currents from the open sea are sheltered by the islets across the shore," added Sentosa spokeswoman Eileen Lee.

    "But some current is inevitable as our beach is open," she added.

    Mr Ong's explanation is more straightforward. He has spoken to those who were rescued and said most of those who get into trouble are in their teens or in their early 20s.

    Succumbing to "peer pressure", these swimmers ignore their safety and jump into the sea to join their friends, even if they are unable to swim or are bad swimmers, said Mr Ong.

    "How can a weak swimmer help a poor swimmer?" he asked.

    "If you are not a swimmer, don't swim."
