    The fundamental definition of elitist is simply someone considered superior by society. In a society like Singapore where meritocracy is the premier ideology subscribed by everyone and accepted by most, elitism is to be expected – the belief that elites should rule the nation. There has been plenty of issues raised to challenge this system of meritocracy and I’m not in the mood for that discussion having talked so much about that during the academic year. What’s more interesting at hand is how increasingly, elitist and elitism are accumulating and acquiring negative connotations. In a brief summary, the notion of elitist is becoming more and more synonymous with snobbery. Going as far back as 2006, we have evidence of promising elites such as a certain Wee Shu Min (then, an 18-year-old scholarship recipient) who came into the limelight for criticizing older workers:
    i am inclined – too much, perhaps – to dismiss such people as crackpots. stupid crackpots. the sadder class … we are a tyranny of the capable and the clever, and the only other class is the complement … if you’re not good enough, life will kick you in the balls … my future isn’t certain but i guess right now it’s a lot brighter than most people’s. derek will read this and brand me as an 18-year old elite, one of the sinners who will inherit the country and run his stock to the gutter. go ahead. the world is about winners and losers … dear derek is one of many wretched, undermotivated, overassuming leeches in our country, and in this world. one of those who would prefer to be unemployed and wax lyrical about how his myriad talents are being abandoned for the foreigner’s… please, get out of my elite uncaring face.

    The new definition for Elitism
    Taken from: http://intelligentsingaporean.wordpress.com/wee-shu-min/

    This situation doesn’t seem to be improving and why should it? We are a society which grants unchallenged respect to educated individuals with paper qualifications; we are a society who rewards the most academically gifted scholarships and special programs which grant them a deep sense of importance; and we are a society which embraced meritocracy and have bought so deeply into this “fair and equal” system. The product is a massive group of educated young citizens who feel too full of themselves.

    Meritocracy
    Taken from: https://geepeeland.wordpress.com/2011/09/14/

    A society of elites is perfectly fine so long as they never forget their roots and give respect where respect is due; not solely based on academic excellence; but experience, efficiency, attitude and way of life. I have come across too many incidents of young graduates being disrespectful to under-educated cleaners; young graduate managers showing no respect for older, experienced technician and manual workers under them; and even blatant outbursts of elites against a world that just isn’t good enough for them (the likes of the incident in 2006).

    As far as I’m concerned, being educated means nothing if one does not live by a certain principle and moral code. Respect is earned through day-to-day attitude and behaviour and not through the scholarship that you were given or awards that you have won. It is the way you treat others that will determine the kind of person you are. Many of us fall into the trap of knowing too much information thanks to Mr. Google and Mr. Wiki and become impatient in the face of ignorant elders who lack so much of the common knowledge that is so readily available to us. All this knowledge act as a ingredients to justify talking down to others who seem to know so little. Unfortunately, it is also this knowledge that empower us to adopt a snobbish attitude.

    Of course, this is not addressing the entire population. Many of us still understand the value of staying grounded and showing respect where respect is due. I’m writing here as a reminder to myself and whoever reads this of the importance of re-redefining the notion of elitism into something that ONLY means a trust-worthy group of educated elites without the connotation of snobbery and disrespect. Education has empowered us and this power can be used in any way one desires. Instead of adopting the “elitist” mentality that looks down upon all academically inferior others, why not adopt the elitist mentality that stems from sharing, respecting and appreciating all others; for what others lack in knowledge, they may well outshine you in passion, motivation, experience, ability, and so on.
