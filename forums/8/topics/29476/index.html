    Speaking loosely without technical terms such as Hausdorff-Besicovitch dimension, a fractal (coined by Mandelbrot in 1975) is a geometric pattern that is self-similar, i.e., if we zoom in on a small portion of the pattern we find a figure that is similar to a larger portion of the original image.

    Each of the fractals we consider here is generated by a single equation and they may be classified into three categories, Mandelbrot, Julia, and Newton patterns, to be discussed one at a time. The prerequisite for the subjects is to know (1) fundamental algebra and geometry of complex numbers, (2) beginning calculus, and (3) basic computer programming in such language as Pascal in a graphics mode. (1) includes the practice of writing a complex number z as a point (x, y) in the xy-plane as well as the standard algebraic expression z = x + yi. At the outset of fractal plotting, we always choose, as we shall see, a generating equation and a rectangular region R in the xy-plane bounded by four real numbers called xmin, xmax, ymin, ymax, and consisting of points, er complex numbers (x, y). Then using a simple change of coordinates (a basic routine in (3) usually explained in a programming manual), we determine the size of a canvas or a window (a rectangular area) that will appear in the computer's display screen, and identify the picture elements, a.k.a. pixels, of the canvas with complex numbers in the region R. Each pixel is itself a rectangle (albeit tiny) and canvas most frequently comprises 800 x 600 = 480,000 of them. The computer will be programmed to light up each pixel of the canvas with a color determined by a simple interaction between the generating equation and the underlying complex value, and eventually display a fractal image on the screen.
