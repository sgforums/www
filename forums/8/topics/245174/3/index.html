    One misperception that needs to be cleared up here is that the idea of university is not to know what to think - the point is to develop the skills to know how to think.

    An unfortunate aspect of undergrad life is that the majority of undergrads simply aren't taken seriously by the teaching staff. This majority is by and large perceived to be there to jump through hoops and get the paper, and that perception's often not far off the mark. Tertiary education has generally become an education-for-sale system and in doing so has fostered the jumping-through-hoops mentality.

    There are a few out there that do get the point, and those are genuinely intelligent enough to go out into the real world and make it with or without a degree. If the system of separating the goats from the sheep is set up right, these are the ones who get past undergrad and get to the real fun stuff.

    That said, the system's not perfect. At university and now at work, I still come across postgrads who made it by sheer perspiration and no inspiration. All kudos to them for having made it that far through sheer brute force, but they're generally people I tend to avoid working with because they don't bring aboard what's expected of people of their qualifications and I end up having to carry them.
