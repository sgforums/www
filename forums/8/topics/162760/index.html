Why no Tamil signages in Changi Airport?

  • Cindyfeh
    13 Nov 05, 10:42
    http://straitstimes.asia1.com.sg/forum/story/0,5562,351985,00.html?

    After a gap of five years, I had the pleasant experience of using the services of Singapore Changi Airport when I went abroad last week.

    Being continuously voted as the best airport in the world, no one can easily find fault with the facilities afforded by our airport.

    Neverthless, there was a sore point pertaining to the signboards at the airport. I was very upset that there were no signs in the Tamil language, which is one of our official tongues.

    The main sign is in English followed by smaller signs in Malay, Chinese and Japanese. Logically, it should be in Malay, Chinese and Tamil.

    It has been the norm that major signboards in all government buildings are in all the four official languages. This includes Tamil. Hospitals, MRT stations, schools etc are examples where Tamil signboards can be found. There shouldn't be any doubt that our airport too is a public place. Where are the Tamil signboards?

    I can see the reason for including Japanese in the signboards. In fact, I strongly support having signboards in other prominent foreign languages, but this should not be done at the expense of our local languages.

    If you choose to have signs in Japanese, what about French, German, Korean, Hindi, Russian etc. Why the special treatment for the Japanese tourists? In fact, most Japanese can read and understand Chinese characters. So why the need for Japanese signs?

    Indian tourists especially from southern Tamil Nadu are coming in increasing numbers and are emerging as big spenders in Singapore.

    This implies that it makes good commercial sense to have signboards in Tamil. Although Tamil is only spoken by five per cent of our population, in this present era of globalisation it is fast becoming our gateway to the lucrative South Indian markets.

    In Chennai and Colombo airports, signboards carry Tamil translations in recognition of its status as an official language in those places.

    Not having Tamil signboards could also be construed as unconstituitional or against the spirit of our Constitution which has granted equal status to all our local languages.

    I hope the airport authorities take note of this and rectify the shortcoming as soon as possible.

    K. Sabesan
