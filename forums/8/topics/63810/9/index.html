    S'pore to see lowest birth rate in 26 years
    Barring a late surge in baby-making this year, the country will again fall short of the 50,000 newborns it needs

    By Crystal Chan

    IT IS clear now that Singapore is headed for its lowest birth rate in 26 years, unless there was a massive baby boom last month and this month.

    There were 31,171 births between January and October this year, well below the 33,618 in the same period last year. February was the least productive month, with just 2,772 babies born.

    The birth rate has been sinking in the past few years, with just 41,451 births in 2001 and 40,864 last year, despite the baby bonus kicking in in April last year.

    Only in 2000, the auspicious Dragon year, did the numbers jump to 46,997. All races contributed to the surge, with Chinese births jumping 10 per cent compared to the previous year.

    Given that the last two months of last year yielded just over 7,000 babies, it is unlikely that there will be 9,700 babies born in the same period this year, which is the shortfall needed to meet last year's total.

    Singapore needs at least 50,000 live births a year to sustain economic, defence and other manpower needs.

    A statement from the Department of Statistics attributed the downward trend to a number of factors, such as people putting off marriage and having children, and wanting smaller families.

    Sociologists told The Straits Times this is unlikely to reverse any time soon.

    The poor economy this year probably contributed to the drought, as couples tend to put off having children until they think they can afford to.

    As Ms Tan Yen Nee, 24, a copywriter, put it: 'We can't expect a decent standard of living with a child. We've got our housing loan and we are saving up for a car.'

    Mr Lim Hng Kiang, Minister in the Prime Minister's Office and Second Minister for Finance, hinted on Sunday at a scheme to help parents pay to have infants in care centres. He identified this as a gap in Singapore's baby-making incentives.

    Checks with various bodies show that there is a slew of schemes to help couples cope with the cost of having children.

    The baby bonus gives a yearly allowance of $500 for the second child and $1,000 for the third child until he or she is six years old. There is also paid maternity leave for the third child.

    Working parents also get a subsidy for children in childcare centres. Mothers who do not work get half the subsidy.

    A working mum also gets a special tax rebate for the second child, which can be offset against her or her husband's taxes for nine years.

    There is a further 15 per cent tax rebate for working mums with their third or fourth child.

    Mr Terence Ng, a 40-year-old sales manager with three children below the age of nine, said: 'I don't expect much from the Government where childbirth is concerned, because having children is a personal decision.

    'But I would say the tax reliefs help to minimise the cost of raising children.'

    Mrs Josephine Teo, a 29-year-old pastoral supervisor with a two-year-old girl, said she would have a second child in due course. She, too, said that tax reliefs and childcare subsidies would help lighten the financial burden.

    But married couples had doubts about putting babies in childcare centres.

    Madam Sarimah Salamon, a 30-year-old communications manager with a two-year-old son, said: 'The childcare subsidy wouldn't help because we prefer a babysitter.

    'The babysitter gives our child her full attention, whereas in childcare centres, the staff have to tend to a few children.'

    Looking for a 5-year-old car? It'll be tough
    Cars registered during 'high COE' period 3 to 10 years ago are not economically viable, so they are scrapped or exported

    By Christopher Tan

    IF YOU are in the market for a used car, chances are that you'll only be able to find cars less than three years old - or over 10 years old.

    Everything in between has either gone to the scrap yard or been exported.

    A check with The Straits Times Classifieds section found that more than 70 per cent of used-car advertisements placed were for models registered between 2000 and this year, the bulk of them in 2001 or 2002.

    At the other end of the spectrum, there were ads for cars more than 10 years old, but they were far fewer than those for 2000-2003 vehicles.

    Models in between, cars four to nine years old, were distinctly in the minority.

    Singapore Secondhand Motor Vehicle Dealers' Association committee member Jerry Low said the phenomenon has a simple explanation:

    Cars registered during years of 'high COE' - generally considered to be anything above $35,000 - were not economically viable here.

    'The simple fact is that if the annual depreciation for a used car is high, like $6,000 to $7,000, then you might as well buy a new car,' Mr Low said.

    Take the best-selling Toyota Corolla, for instance. A new 1.6-litre automatic is priced just above $70,000. After deducting its scrap rebate value in its 10th year, its annual cost - or depreciation - works out to be $6,000.

    A Corolla 1.6 registered in January 2001 and going for about $63,000 will have a depreciation of $7,000.

    Certificate of Entitlement premiums have come down from between $40,000 and $60,000 to about $25,000 now, and new car prices have also fallen to 13-year lows because vehicular taxes such as the additional registration fee (ARF) and Customs duty have been cut in recent years.

    So, a brand new two-litre Nissan Cefiro goes for less than $110,000 today. In 2000, it cost between $130,000 and $150,000.

    'The capital outlay for a new car has become lower since the change in financing rules,' said Mr Low.

    Most new cars are now sold with 90 to 95 per cent financing. Some dealers have also been advertising $1 down payments.

    The boon for the new car trade has been a bane to used-car dealers.

    Mr Thomas Teo of Valley Trading said monthly sales had fallen to '20-something' a month, half of the volume a few years ago.

    Business is very slow, he said, and he doesn't know when things will change.

    While buyers enjoy low prices for new cars, industry players reckon that these buyers will also have to wait far longer to sell the cars, because of the loans they took.

    Said one dealer: 'If they try to sell too early, they will not have enough to repay their outstanding loan.'

    Scrapping the car early to recover taxes - a trend in the past four years - would not be an option either.

    Last year, the Government changed the scrap rebate formula to make refunds less generous.

    Instead of 80 to 130 per cent of a car's open-market value, rebates are now 50 to 75 per cent of its ARF. For a car like the Nissan Sunny, the difference can be $5,000.
