Experts fear escape of 1918 flu virus

    Experts fear scientists who are reviving the virus which caused the 1918 flu pandemic are putting millions of lives at risk.

    Researchers in the US are working with reconstructed versions of the virus at less than the maximum level of containment, reports the New Scientist.

    "The potential implications of an infected lab worker - and spread beyond the lab - are terrifying," says leading biosecurity expert D A Henderson of the University of Pittsburgh.

    And Ingegerd Kallings, who helped set laboratory safety standards for the World Health Organisation, says: "All the virologists I have spoken to have concerns."

    The researchers working on the 1918 virus, which killed 40 million people worldwide, say their work is vital to understand what changes make flu viruses dangerous.

    They started the work at the highest level of containment, BSL-4, at Canada's National Microbiology Laboratory.

    But then scientists decided the viruses were safe enough to handle at the next level down, and did the rest of the work in a BSL-3Ag lab in Madison.

    The main difference is that precautions to ensure staff do not get infected are less stringent. They do not have to wear fully enclosed body suits.

    Project leader Yoshihiro Kawaoka told New Scientist that the decision was taken only after experiments with antiviral drug oseltamivir prevented mice from falling ill.

    However, the US Department of Agriculture's poultry research lab found that mice given oseltamivir still got sick and one in 10 died.
