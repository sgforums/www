    Gameplan is to lift worldwide market share from 16.5% to 40%
    By Goh Eng Yeow

    CREATIVE Technology's Sim Wong Hoo plans to escalate the MP3 digital music player war between his homegrown Zen and Apple's iconic iPod further.

    At the company's briefing on its second quarter results yesterday, Singapore's famous entrepreneur spelt out Creative's gameplan to lift its share of the MP3 market worldwide from 16.5 per cent to 40 per cent.

    It's a formidable task as Apple has the biggest bite of the fast-growing market - about 54 per cent, as at last November.

    But MP3 players have become the latest 'must-have' in consumer digital entertainment, so it's crunch-time.

    Zen, iPod and the like are palm-sized, portable gizmos that store your favourite songs copied from CDs or downloaded from the Net.

    On why Creative founder and chief executive Mr Sim reckons his Zen is set to rock harder in the MP3 market, he pointed to stellar figures for the three months ended December - Creative shipped two million MP3 players, which boosted its sales revenues by 50 per cent to US$375 million (S$615 million).

    This shows a sudden jump in demand for the hot little players, as before that quarter - since Creative entered the MP3 market in 2002 - it had sold a total of three million of them.

    However, consumers didn't just lust after the Zen in that period; they snapped up its rival too. Over the same three-month period, they grabbed more iPods - a far higher 4.6 million units.

    Mr Sim said he expects global sales of all MP3 players to double to 60 million this calendar year alone.

    To get 40 per cent of that pie, he has announced a US$100 million marketing blitz and deployed over 1,300 engineers in research and development just to fight the 'MP3 player war'.

    Mr Sim also plans to grow his market share through big United States retail players such as supermarket giant WalMart and on-line giant Amazon.com.

    Another strategy is to team up with major original equipment manufacturers, giving them access to Creative's design, technology and manufacturing capabilities, and either branding the products as their own, or co-branding with Creative's own brand-name.

    'We have successfully migrated ourselves from the old world (producing sound cards) to the new world (making MP3 players),' said Mr Sim, who described the current challenges as '100 times more exciting than the Soundblaster days'.

    Creative made its name with Soundblaster personal computer audio systems.

    The personal digital entertainment segment, which includes MP3 players, now makes up 64 per cent of Creative's revenues, he added.

    Creative is now poised to take on the big consumer electronics giants. 'We are now in that league,' he said.

    As for future trends, Mr Sim said it is just a matter of time before MP3 players are equipped with mobile phone capability.

    'The moment that the cell phone and MP3 markets converge, we will enter the market with full credibility. I have good audio. I will have a strong MP3 position. I will also have tens of millions of customers using my products. I will be a tough nut to crack,' he said.

    Rather than compete with him, the electronics giants might want to team up with him given the value-added he can offer, said Mr Sim.

    Creative also announced yesterday that net profit for the second quarter fell 71.4 per cent to US$11.8 million after it wrote off US$65.2 million in acquiring a computer graphics maker in 2002. But for the current quarter, Creative is targeting a 50-per-cent jump in revenues.
