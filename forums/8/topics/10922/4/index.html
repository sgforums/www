    4) Questions About Evil

    a) What is evil?
    A lack of something that should be there in the relationship between good things.

    b) Where did evil come from?
    God made everything perfect. One of the perfect things God made was free creature. Free will is the cause of evil. So, imperfection (evil) can arise from perfection (not directly, but indirectly through freedom)

    c) Why canÂ’t evil be stopped?
    Evil canÂ’t be destroyed without destroying freedom. Free beings are the cause of evil, and freedom was given to us so that we could love. Love is the greatest good for all free creatures, but love is impossible without freedom. Hence, to destroy evil would actually be evil. If evil is to be overcome, we need to talk about it being defeated, not destroyed.

    d) What is the purpose of evil?
    We need to make a distinction: there is a difference between our knowing the purpose for evil and God having a purpose for it. We canÂ’t assume that there is no good purpose for something just because we donÂ’t know what it could be.

    For instance, God sometimes uses evil to warn us of greater evils, keeping us from self-destruction (the gift of pain). While it may seem a high price to pay, some evil helps to bring about greater good. Finally, permitting some evil actually helps defeat evil. In some sense, we need pain so that we are not overcome by the evil that we would choose were it painless. God alerts us to the fact that there are better things than misery.

    e) Does there have to be so much evil?
    The extent of evil poses a problem. Surely there doesnÂ’t have to be this much evil to fulfil GodÂ’s purposes. This can even be taken to the extreme case: What about hell? WouldnÂ’t it be better to have one less person in hell?

    1. The greatest good is to save all men.
    2. Even one person in hell would be less than the greatest good.
    3. Therefore, God cannot send anyone to hell.

Now, God canÂ’t force anyone to love Him. Forced love is a contraction in terms. Love must be free: it is a free choice. All who go to hell do so because of their free choice.     They make the decision to reject God, even though they donÂ’t desire punishment. People donÂ’t go to hell because god sends them; they choose it and God respects their freedom.

    Now if that is how eternal destiny is decided, then it is not person in hell that is evil; it is one more than is really necessary (i.e. one who did choose God but was sent to hell anyway). Granted, a world in which some men go to hell is not the best of all conceivable worlds, but it may be the best of all achievable worlds if free will is to be maintained.
