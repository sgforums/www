    SPH does have a right to be given reasonable doubt. Reason? I don't know what's their intention for writing such an article.

    The use of such words, is not merely an escape clause for the journalists. It's at the very least, responsible journalism. If something could be true or untrue, the word "allegedly" is used. It's used most commonly in reporting crimes, if a person is being suspected, but no confirmations yet. For example, Mr X allegedly stole something. He may or may not have stolen anything. Everything will be confirmed once investigations are completed. If the newspapers wrote Mr X stole something when he's only a suspect, the man do have a right to sue him, because it is deemed as a fact.

    Same goes for this case, SPH could be trying to encourage a debate on it, but have stirred up the wrong emotions instead.

    I definitely don't support the use of such words as a way to escape responsibility, since its main purpose is about responsible writing...
