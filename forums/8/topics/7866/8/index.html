    Dear Prodigal,

    an interesting concept that goes into this area is Cooley's "Looking Glass Self". We see ourselves based on an understanding of how others look at us. In the Internet realm, where anonymity acts as a protective shield that allows us to be anything we want to be, we project ourselves according to how we think others will react to that image. If I think that guys will flirt outrageously with an tough talking seial monogamist, then it's she I'll become.

    Of course, the "Looking Glass Self" is not within our total control in the Internet. As you have noted earlier, there are certain characteristics that will slip out. If a person is hot tempered in real life, he'll probably jump at all provocations, real or imagined.

    However, I would disagree that a person who is rude and defiant in the Net would definitely be so in real life as well. This has to do with social control. How we fit in- whether we want to fit in at all in the first place- is explained by Mead's "I and the Me". "I" represents our immediate knee-jerk response while "me" represents the generalized attitudes of others (i.e. society) that we assume. It is through "Me" that society dominates. Of course, "I" and the "Me" often conflict internally before we engage in any form of social interaction.

    How this concept applies is the sense of belonging a person has in the forum. If he feels he doesn't fit in or like the people in the community, the "Me" is low in his priority becos he simply does not care how others perceive him. Moreover, he knows he can get away with little or no consequence if he were to behave badly. This is unlike real life, where interaction is mainly face to face.

    Note that I am only talking about behaviour related to social interaction (e.g. defiance, inconsideration to others, manipulation), not personal characteristics (e.g. hot temper, cruelty). Of course, it can be argued that personal characteristics prediposes us to certain social behaviour. Again, I'll like to bring up the role of social control (as discussed earlier) as an intermediary, a bridge between the two.

    I'll like to end by saying that I used to moderate a forum. There used to be this really sarcastic and unpleasant person who liked to pick on the amateurs. Needless to say, I couldn't stand him. One day, I dropped by Malaysia and decided to pay him a visit and was very surprised that he was actually a very nice and gracious person. And because we've established some kind of social connection, we've come to respect each other as real human beings. Nowadays, he gives me more face as a moderator whenever he's involved in an argument. I am also less harsh on him.

    Regards,
    purr
