    The real deal on deals

    Paying more and getting less. And paying more for stuff I do not want in the first place. What's the deal?

    By Sharon Loh

    ALL I wanted was a large bag of popcorn, sweet and salty mixed.

    'I can't sell it to you, ma'am,' said the sales assistant behind the concession counter. She pointed to the combo being advertised. 'The large popcorn comes with two drinks.'

    I had taken the children to Great World City to catch a movie. They love going to the cinema, and part of the fun is scarfing a huge bag of popcorn in the middle of the day.

    This was the first time anyone had refused to sell me just the snack. And apparently, the regular-sized bag also had to come with a drink.

    But I didn't want a combo, and I sure didn't want the sweet fizzy drinks that went with it.

    Can I at least trade the sweet drinks for bottled water, I asked? The seconds ticked by towards the start of the movie.

    She looked at me sternly. 'We can't do that.'

    I glared back as I handed over $8 for a bag of popcorn and two diet Cokes, and made a mental note to myself: Next time bring own bag of microwaved popcorn.

    I don't know if all Golden Village concessions are equally inflexible but purveyors of goods and services should take care not to lose the plot.

    Not all customers will appreciate being forced into buying packages that claim to be value for money but actually just make people pay more.

    The new memberships to Wildlife Reserves parks are another example.

    We are Friends of the Zoo and Friends of the Birds, which means that for a flat fee paid every year, we get unlimited entry to the Zoo and Jurong BirdPark.

    This is very useful when you have small children who constantly want to go there. Sometimes we go just to enjoy their very nice playgrounds.

    But last week I received a flyer informing me that membership fees had gone up to $100 for each park, for two adults and three children.

    Price increases are part and parcel of life, but my eyes popped at this one.

    The old charges were $87 and $81 for the zoo and bird park respectively.

    Overall, it is a 20 per cent increase. Rather hefty, no?

    Wildlife Reserves says that it has to raise membership fees, which have not gone up since - wait for this - 1974.

    Wow. It must have cost a small fortune to be a friend of the zoo 30 years ago. Is it now making up for lost time?

    Price rise aside, I took umbrage at being cheerily informed by the flyer that I would 'save $110' by signing up as a member of the zoo, bird park and Night Safari - for $240.

    The company is starting a membership scheme for the Night Safari for $150. You save only if you subscribe to all three attractions individually, which you can't do anyway, since the package would automatically kick in.

    And since my membership to two attractions cost me $168 in total, I would have to spend nearly $80 more to get all three, and at least $32 just to maintain my current status.

    What am I, stupid?

    I called the BirdPark to complain. My $81 membership now grants entry to my family of four, plus two grandparents.

    The new package excludes grandparents altogether. So, you want me to pay more for less, I asked? Why would I do that?

    The girl who took my call was very apologetic.

    'Write in, ma'am,' she said, fervently. 'I agree with you.'

    I'm still trying to decide if I should renew my membership or wean the children off the parks.

    I know $200 is not going to break the bank, but I don't relish feeling sold, when I am a faithful customer. We go often, and each time we spend freely on food, drink, animal rides and train and tram rides.

    Wildlife Reserves may have good reasons for raising prices by 20 per cent - for instance so there is a more equitable distribution of charges among regulars and casual visitors.

    If so, I would welcome an honest justification over the attempt to sell me a 'deal' that is no deal given what I now pay.

    Marketing is a mystery to me, and I'm sure that price formulas are complicated, taking into account a myriad of factors.

    But from a consumer's point of view, more flexibility is preferable to less. Even at snack concessions.

    That was the approach taken by the new Sentosa management which seems to be paying off. They dropped the entrance fee to a nominal sum and let visitors choose what to spend their money on instead of bundling unwanted attractions into a fixed package.

    Last week we were informed by StarHub that the Disney Channel, which we subscribe to, will migrate its preschool programming to a different channel, Playhouse Disney, in June.

    The main Disney Channel, which is aimed at the whole family, will be included in the kids' tier with Cartoon Network and Nickelodeon.

    Since we don't subscribe to the kids' tier, we now have to, if we want to keep the Disney Channel. Or I can drop Playhouse Disney if I don't want to have to pay more.

    Now, here again was a choice being foisted on me that I didn't ask for. But I can appreciate that there may be people who want only the preschool channel, and now can opt just for that. And vice versa.

    Either way, it gives people choice, and consumers appreciate more flexibility and choice, not less.

    In the meantime, I have been advised to invest in a good home entertainment system.

    That way, there will be no need to go to the cinema. You can easily recreate the experience at home, with a good DVD and microwaved popcorn.

    Hey, and Coca Cola which doesn't cost $8. The kids could invite their friends over. We'd have a great night in.

    MAY 22, 2004
    New deal offers unlimited visits to 3 wildlife parks
    WILDLIFE Reserves, the company that manages the Singapore Zoo, the Jurong BirdPark and the Night Safari, is looking to boost the number of visitors to these attractions.

    It has launched a new membership package called Wildlife Unlimited, which covers all the three parks, and those who are already members of one of its annual plans can upgrade to it.

    It hopes to sign up one million members in the next three months.

    For an annual fee of $240, two adults and three children under 12 years old can get access to all the three attractions any number of times.

    The one-time entry fee to the zoo and bird park is $14 for adults and $7 for children. For the Night Safari, it is $18 for adults and $9 for children.

    Each year, the zoo has an average of 1.5 million visitors, the bird park, 900,000, and the Night Safari, 850,000. They bring in an estimated $60 million in revenue.

    Wildlife Reserves has increased the family membership fees of its existing Friends of the Zoo and Friends of the Birds packages from $87 and $81 a year to $100 each. A new Friends of Night Safari package has also been launched, which costs $150.

    The hike is the first since the Friends of the Zoo and Friends of the Birds family packages were launched in 1974 and 1978 respectively.

    Wildlife Reserves is offering a six-month PacNet dial-up usage package and a two-month extension to each annual membership that is upgraded to Wildlife Unlimited by July.

    Ms Fanny Lai, the company's director of marketing and communications, attributed the increases to the cost of upgrading and introducing new facilities at the parks.
