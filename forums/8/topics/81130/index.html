    Ties rubber band so tightly on cat's neck, it eats into flesh

    Report and picture
    By Teh Jen Lee

    FROM far it looks like a red collar around the cat's neck. But you're actually looking at her flesh.

    Somebody was cruel enough to wind four rubber bands tight around her neck.

    For days, the bands ate into her skin, cutting off blood circulation. Left alone, she would have died a slow and agonising death.

    But Mr Suppiah G, 70, a pensioner who regularly feeds strays in his neighbourhood, found her near the Marsiling estate market on Tuesday night. He recognised the cat because he used to feed her often. She was lying on her side, weak with hunger.

    He said: 'She had gone missing for a few days. She probably couldn't eat the whole time because it was too painful to swallow.'

    With the help of another man, he removed four rubber bands and hoped there were no more embedded in the flesh.

    'The rubber bands were red so it was hard to see. Also they were very tight. It's a very sad thing, people shouldn't do this to these poor creatures,' he said.

    FOURTH CAT TO SUFFER THIS ABUSE

    He has seen three other cats suffering in the same way over the past two years.

    Two of them let him remove the rubber bands, but they still died, probably from infections.

    'I know the cleaners so they told me they found the bodies. What to do, I couldn't afford to take them to a vet,' said the former safety officer.

    He couldn't take them home either because he and his wife have health problems.

    As for animal welfare groups, he didn't want to burden them again because they had helped him treat or adopt strays in the past.

    He said: 'I know they are also stretched for resources so I feel bad if I keep going to them. I thought I would just do what I could on my own.'

    But when he found the latest victim, he decided to speak up. He called The New Paper, so that more people would be aware of animal cruelty.

    When contacted, the Society for Prevention of Cruelty to Animals (SPCA) sent its inspector to find the cat with Mr Suppiah.

    She was taken to SPCA's clinic, where a vet examined her. With the help of medicated cream and antibiotics, she is expected to recover in about a week.

    SPCA will work with the Animal Lovers League to get the cat adopted.

    Said Mrs Cathy Strong, the league's president: 'In case we can't find a home, we'll get sponsors for her upkeep. It's only $30 a month, it's not much.'

    SPCA executive officer Deirdre Moss said: 'We want to thank Mr Suppiah for coming forward. Because he took steps to prevent further injury by removing the rubber bands, we were able to treat the cat.

    'In view of previous incidents, it's not an option to release her back into the same area so we will make every effort to rehome her.'

    Usually, friendly or tame stray cats such as this one are more likely to be victims of extreme animal abuse, she added.

    Could the feeding of strays make them more trusting of humans and therefore more vulnerable to abuse?

    Ms Moss said it's hard to generalise because many factors, such as genetics, make up an animal's temperament.

    She urged people to be more vigilant against animal cruelty, report the crime immediately and inform the SPCA if they see anything.

    She said: 'As long as there's a witness, these people can be charged...

    'We'll follow up with the police and see what we can do for the animal. Early treatment can save its life.'

    Under the Animals and Birds Act, anyone found guilty of cruelty to animals or of abandoning their pets faces a fine of up to $10,000 and up to a year in jail.

    Source: NP
