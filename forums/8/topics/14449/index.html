    Small plane slams into Milan skyscraper

    A small tourist plane has hit the Pirelli skyscraper in central Milan on Thursday, setting the top floors of the 30-storey building on fire, a police official said.

    Eyewitnesses reported hearing a loud explosion from the office block and smoke was seen wafting from the building.

    The 127 metres-high building however managed to withstand the impact.

    Officials said that at least two people were killed and 15 others wounded.

    Among those killed was the pilot of the plane, they said.

    Local air transport authorities said the plane had reported problems with its undercarriage and was circling the city ahead of trying to land at a local airport.

    Italy's Interior Minister Claudio Scajola said the crash was "probably an accident".

    A witness told AFP that the building was being renovated from the 24th floor to the top and that the area struck by the aircraft was vacant.

    The first nine floors of the tower had been evacuated without incident.

    Police were trying to seal off the streets around the building, and ambulances were at the scene.

    The plane is believed to have taken off from the Swiss town of Lucarno.

    Milan's stock exchange suspended share trading after the incident.

    Meanwhile in New York, the news of the crash sent the Dow Jones industrials index tumbling more than 50 points within minutes of the report.

    The tower, which sits next to the city's central train station, houses the administrative offices of the local Lombardy region.

    It is one of the world's highest concrete skyscrapers and also a symbol of Italy's financial capital.

    (((Small plane 'accidentally' slams into Milan tower, 3 dead)))

    A small plane crashed into a Milan skyscraper and killed at least three people, triggering fears of a repeat of the September 11 attacks.

    Italian Interior Minister Claudio Scajola said the crash was "probably an accident" amid reports that the pilot had sent a distress message to the control tower of Milan's Linate airport shortly before the crash of the four-seat Commander 112 light plane into the 30-floor Pirelli office block.

    The pilot reportedly had problems with the landing gear shortly before ramming into the top floors of Milan's landmark tower.

    Three people including the pilot were killed and some 60 were injured, police said late Thursday.

    But the majority of the 300 people working in the building were unscathed, if badly shaken.

    The top floors were largely empty at the time due to renovation works.

    The crash also took place in the evening, when many people working in the building had already left.

    A Milan councillor had earlier said five people had been killed.

    Despite Scajola's public reassurances, Italy's air force was put on full alert "until the situation becomes clear," the crisis unit in Prime Minister Silvio Berlusconi's office said.

    Transport Minister Pietro Lunardi said late Thursday it was unclear why the 68-year-old pilot had veered into Milan's tallest building, but that investigators believed he either had a heart attack or was overcome by fumes from a fire on board.
