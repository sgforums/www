    Quote Audrey Heng Aik Choo (Ms) on Strait Times on 13 March 07

    " Bid to enter polytechnic failed by just one point: Appeals process too rigid

    I REFER to the article, 'Poly route shut for average students' (ST, March , and would like to share my experience regarding my daughter's application to the polytechnics after her O level results.
    My daughter had an aggregate score of 14 that allowed her eligibility to many courses as listed in the result slips. Having attended a number of open houses organised by the various polytechnics and with the understanding that an aggregate score of below 16 is likely to meet the entry requirement for Accounting, the subject she is interested in, she listed Accounting in a polytechnic, which is within walking distance from our residence, as her first choice of selection.

    When we received her enrolment results, she was posted to another polytechnic to do the course of Business Administration that she had put as her fourth choice of her selection.

    We also found out that she missed the entry score of her first choice subject and polytechnic by a mere one point.

    As my daughter had A2s respectively for her Principles of Accounting and Maths in her O level, I decided to make an appeal at the polytechnic of her first choice.

    However, I was informed by the officer at that polytechnic that entry is based strictly on merit and that as long as one aggregate score is above the cut-off point, it would be a waste of time making an appeal. I am disappointed that our educational system is adopting the mindset of 'No target score, No talk!'

    With the change in trend and the focus shifted towards polytechnic education by many O level students, I fully agree with Ms K. Mathiyazhaki that the ministry should look into increasing the intake of the polytechnics to allow average achievers the opportunity to pursue their education in this route.

    Looking at the low entry points of the various courses published by the polytechnics in Singapore, which were released only at the time of enrolment results, it appears that many students with good GCE O level results have decided to enrol in the polytechnic instead of the junior college. If this increase in enrolment is not matched by an increased intake for various courses offered, then the average achievers who wish to pursue courses of their choice will be displaced as they are left with no other choice but to accept a posting to courses that they may have lesser aptitude or interest in.

    Perhaps the appeal process can be further fine-tuned to consider students who have achieved reasonably good results in their relevant subjects but have missed the aggregate cut-off, which is based on all subjects regardless of their relevance to the courses of pursuit.

    I would also wish to appeal to the relevant polytechnics to publish the entry cut-off points of the previous year's intake in their open house brochures so that students can make informed choices of the courses they like to pursue and within their capability. "
