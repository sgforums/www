    In brief summary at the risk of oversimplification :

    The parathyroids (4 of them, located around the thyroid gland) has the function of regulating the concentration of calcium in the blood. As some of you have studied, calcium is an important metabolite that participates in many vital biochemical processes everywhere in the human body, eg. nervous and muscular activity.

    When calcium levels fall below normal, the parathyroids increase their secretion of the ParaThyroid Hormone (PTH), which causes calcium to leech out from bones (bones function as a store for calcium). When calcium levels are normal or above normal, the parathyroids should decrease their secretion of PTH.

    In some individuals, the parathyroids, due to a variety of possible reasons (eg. tumour, hyperplasia, etc), secrete excessive amounts of PTH uncontrollably. As a result, excessive calcium is continuously leached from the bones, blood calcium level is abnormally high, and we have the following disastrous consequences and symptoms (only a few a listed below) :

    Osteoporosis, weakened and brittle (more easily broken) bones, high blood pressure, headaches & migraines, nausea and vomitting, kidney stones (causing blood in urine), calcified kidneys, kidney failure, shortened life span and death.

    Most individuals suffering from hyperparathyroidism (doctors estimate every 1 in 5000 people have this disease, but most) are not aware they have the disease, and live for 10-20 years (while the damage is being done) without knowing it, until they are crippled with pain from the kidney stones and kidney failure. Then, a variety of blood and urine tests will indicate their hyperparathyroid condition (there are two forms of the disease - primary hyperparathyroidism and secondary hyperparathyroidism).

    Thus, hyperparathyroidism is one of the leading causes of kidney stones, kidney failure, as well as osteoporosis. The sad thing, is that for *many* people with these diseases, their doctors or hospitals may not do the right tests, and sometimes fail to identify hyperparathyroidism as the cause for their symptoms (because these same symptoms can have many different causes).

    Currently, medical science has no way of healing the diseased parathyroid glands. The only option is surgery to remove the parathyroids. This is not without risks either - during the surgery the nearby vocal nerves may be damaged, leaving the person unable to speak (or only able to speak hoarsely) for the rest of his/her life. Another, perhaps worse, complication, is that all 4 of the parathyroids are either accidentally removed (usually only 1 of the 4 are problematic and responsible for the excessive secretion of PTH), or are accidentally killed during the surgery.

    This (destruction or removal of healthy parathyroid glands, while accidental, is quite common during surgery) results in hypOparathyroidism, in which there is not enough (or sometimes nothing left at all!) healthy parathyroid tissue left to produce PTH, and the result is hypolcalcemia (abnormally low calcium levels in the blood), which causes a whole range of other (and equally delibitating) symptoms of its own.

    Such victims have to inject themselves with PTH everyday, and take excessive amounts of calcium and vitamin D for the rest of their lives, and still suffer some of the symptoms.

    Humpty-Dumpty told Alice, "When I use a word, I want to mean exactly what I mean...no more and no less.." Problems do arise when people (doctors included) mean different things when they use the same word.

    Osteoporosis comes from the Greek workd osteo for bone and porus for porous. Osteoporosis refers to a reduction in bone density and hence its structural stability, but the composition is normal. There is little or no biochemical disturbance of calcium and other minerals in the blood (although we do advise taking in more calcium to try to slow down the bone loss). Under the microscope, you see normal bone trabeculae but they are thinner than normal. And yes, SB, you get osteoporosis when the net balance between bone formation and bone degradation is tilted towards bone loss. That happens in menopause, in women lacking estrogen due to surgical removal of the ovaries, in people taking steroids long term and also in those who have not been using their bones eg. bed bound patients.

    Osteomalacia comes from the Greek word osteo for bone and malacia for softness. It was originally applied to the bones in patients with rickets (Vitamin D deficiency) where the bone is poorly mineralised due to the lack of calcium. Under the microscope, the bone lacks the calcium that is normally deposited on top of the bone matrix. The composition of the bone is abnormal, 'becomes softer' and often children with this condition grow up bow-legged because the bones are not able to sustain the weight of the body.

    Endocrine (hormonal) abnormalities affect proper bone formation. Parathyroid hormone (and its counterpart, calcitonin) are important for regulating the body calcium level. The major store of calcium in the body is in the bones. So if you need more calcium in the blood, parathyroid hormone will mobilize the calcium from the bone (at the expense of destroying some bone).

    In primary hyperparathyroidism, a tumour, usually a benign tumour of the parathyroid gland (adenoma) or excessive growth of the parathyroid gland (hyperplasia) and rarely a malignant tumour (a parathyroid carcinoma) produces excessive parathyroid hormone. This excess hormone leads to excessive mobilization of calcium from the bone by activating specialized cells called osteoclasts which 'eat up' the bone and release calcium to the blood. Under the microscope, the bone is initially mineralized as for normal bone but the bony trabeculae have big holes or empty pouches in them where the osteoclasts have been eating away at them. That's why the bone disease in this condition is also called 'osteitis fibrosa cystica' (Greek kysto- a pouch). Although the mechanism is quite different from rickets, you can expect the bone to lose its structural integrity and so some people used the term osteomalacia (bone softening) to refer to it as well. But you will appreciate the disease is quite different from rickets or osteoporosis.

    Sometime there can be excessive parathyroid hormone produced even when there is no tumour or excessive growth of the gland. In persons with kidney failure, the calcium level drops because the kidney cannot produce active Vitamin D. The low calcium level triggers excessive parathyroid hormone production which is what is known as secondary hyperparathyroidism. The effect of the excess parathyroid hormone on the bones is similar. However, although the bone disease is the same, in this context, the term renal osteodystrophy is often used (Greek. dys- for defective, trophe from the Greek word trephein, to nourish).

    Yet a third scenario exists. If the renal failure is been around for a long time and there has been continued stimulation of parathyroid hormone production, the overly stimulated parathyroid gland can grow very big and sometimes even sprout a benign tumour. As a result, there will be hyperparathyroidism even if you treat the renal failure and correct the vitamin D deficiency. That condition is known as tertiary hyperparathyroidism.
