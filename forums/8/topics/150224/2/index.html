    Research and experience indicate that in the vast majority of cases, unfair dismissal could have been prevented. An investigation of dismissal case studies indicate that most difficulties are associated with poor personnel management policies and practices. Inadequate workplace communication also contributes to employment difficulties.

    If poor performance is an issue, develop an appropriate job description. That is vital. In many cases employees are unclear as to the scope of responsibilities. Quantify performance requirements whenever possible i.e. a data entry clerk could have requirements such as keystrokes per minute and entry accuracy rates. Update job description where work requirements are altered.

    Consider a trial period for new employees or employees assigned to new roles and responsibilities. Monitor performance and provide written feedback during this period. Employees should communicate to management any difficulties they are facing during this time. Managers should make and be seen to be making every reasonable effort to assist employees with any difficulties they experience in their new roles. This should include training if appropriate.

    In many cases, employees terminated on the basis of alleged poor performance are unaware that their employers are unhappy with aspects of their work. Above all, employers have a moral obligation to act in good faith i.e. not restort to unfair dismissal on grounds of pregnancy in avoidance of giving employees statutory maternity pay.
