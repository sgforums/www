    Hi all,

    After reading many replies, and it is time to get back to being serious again (after a good April fool day of course). This thread has rested on a few issues, particularly the most important is about what is it mean to be Chinese?

    Let me start off with a little about my family background. I came from a traditional and disintegrated powerful family (who lost the entire fortune after the war), and my grandfather left 3 wives who have to fend and support the group of kids. Of course, my parents have brought me up quite a strict chinese customs. First of all, they made me learned Chinese calligraphy, so I ended up holding the ballpoint pen differently from my friends. But the learning of Calligraphy allowed me to learn old Chinese (Fan Ti) words, and, of course, I can read Hong Kong comics with no problems. I spoke Cantonese at home because my parents could not speak Mandarin well. Then we have the family dinner with my aunt, and we would stick to soup first and then the food and rice. Strict rules like cannot put elbows or drop the chopsticks are imposed onto me. I was quite a rebel to my strict extended family culture. But I was given the education outside my own for the four tenets : qin (music), qi (chess), shu (literacy) and hua (painting). With the exception of the first, I know the rest. But is that what Chinese culture is all about? If you asked me, these are just strict things imposed on me to know.

    What it did, was to help me to learn about Chinese history and philosophy. Unlike Terence, who claimed that Confucianism is responsible for certain bad dogmatic practices, I do not agree because I took a long time to read the 4 books, which are the major tenets of Confucianism. The pure Confucianism seek to cultivate the individual, and asks him to question even if authority is there. I could go on talking about Confucianism, but there are other important stuff in Chinese Philosophy, of 3 which I am particularly spent a lot of time reading it in actual Chinese : Sun Tzu's Art of War (which mirrors Clausewitz's "On War" in the Western world), Lee Zong Wu's "Thick Black Book" (which mirrors Machieveilli's "The Prince" in politics but written in 1911) and Lao Zi's "Tao De Ching". Of course there are many other books like the 36 strategems, Wong Shi Gong's 3 government strategies. They have an impact to how Chinese culture have been found.

    Then we come to Chinese history, the dynasties which has passed. The transition from Monarchy to Communism to the present hybrid form of Communism+Capitalism, the Chinese civilization has undergone a lot of change. Chinese literature is also interesting since we have the 4 famous books,"Journey to the West", "Dreams of the Red Mansions", "The Water Margin" and "The Romance of 3 kingdoms".

    I once argued with a friend in my secondary school (since my sec school is reputated to have students who are lousy in Chinese and good in English), that it is rubbish that you want to learn about the Western culture without knowing your own culture. A person who does not know his culture, to me, deem unworthy to engage in the learning of other philosophies. I learned about Aristotle, Plato and Kant at that time, and as the years go, I read more about the Western world, and I realize that it is more than just being open and individualistic. The history is interesting as well. For one thing, I do not believe in communism and have always believed in Gorbachev's "Perestrotika" and "Glasnost" (he is my idol in politics) and championed the idea of democracy.

    I could also tell you what is the major differences between Chinese and Western mathematical thought? The Western thoughts are influenced primarily by the Greeks, and hence by virtue of their political setting, their basis is on axiomatic reduction, whereas the Chinese based their form of thinking by checking the consistency of algorithms and devise methods to do things without using axiomatic reduction. There are very interesting parallels to draw. It was known that the Chinese civilization has the longest standing scientific institute called the Astronomical Bureau (from 1 BC to 199AD and it is about 200 hundred years) in the ancient era, compare to the Great Library of Alexandria which was burned down in less than 50 years...

    There are so much one can talk about the Chinese culture, and my opinion is that the Chinese in Singapore do not know much about the Chinese culture.

    Btw, to those gals, who are talking about cheongsams, apparently while attending important functions and balls in my university, I encounter Western blonds wearing cheongsams these days and it is of fashion as well...

    Well, that is all I have to say so far...Awaiting opinions and arguments. Have a good time...

    Dear Yubbadoobs,

    On your first point, the Chinese have undergone 5000 years of civilization, and the basis of the Chinese government were predominantly monarchy, i.e. you have an Emperor who looks after the Chinese people. The Emperor is the mandate from Heaven and of course, not every emperors are virtuous and hence we have wars.

    The Chinese civilization have not developed the philosophy of democracy. This is surprising on one front, but can be understood by historical reasons. In Greek philosophy, the people in Athens gathered at the forum and argued their points. The Greek Philosophers were mainly teachers and the more they teach, they get more famous and more money. Hence, because there are so many of these teachers, they needed a basis to see whose ideas are better. This lead to the development of two things, first, a Parliament where people are allowed to speak on their side of the House (a basic tenet of the ideal of democracy) and second, the systematic rules of thought which lead to the axiomatic reduction, i.e. the more basic assumptions you have to deal with an argument, the more you are likely to be right.

    Then we come back to Chinese civilization, because of the respect of Emperors, we are not allowed to speak for our minds, fearing that we might lose favour with the royal court. It stagnates the system. Hence, when it comes to the development of mathematics, the Chinese do not question the rightness of the ideas they use, but they check for consistency in the algorithms they create so that they can use it to predict things.

    I don't admire Deng Xiaopeng, even though he started the economic reform in China. The main reason was the massacre of students in the Tiananmen incident. It was too harsh. I am more inclined to the democracy illustrated in US, with the Congress, Senate and President.

    Well, Gorbachev could be the last communist, but he is a reformer by heart. I could spend a long time talking about why he is not a true communist based on his policies and ideals.
