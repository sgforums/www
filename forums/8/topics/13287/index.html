The Man in the Looking Glass

    In recent decades, there has been a worldwide phenomenon called
    'globalization'. Like the el-nino weather pattern that swept across the
    Earth in the late nineties, its spread is inevitable and irreversible.
    Globalization is intimately linked with the rapid advances in communication
    and transportation, and indeed, can be said to have had its direct roots
    from them.

    When two brothers, bicycle makers the both of them, drew up a blueprint for
    an amazing contraption, they had little or no idea what a storm they were
    about to create. On paper, it looked like a simple, innocuous device- an
    engine that was mounted on a pair of overblown wings. They were not sure if
    it would work, but their vision, courage and perseverance opened a new
    chapter in mankind's history of accidental inventions. The world was
    introduced to the first airplane, and how the masses roared in appreciation.

    Today of course, we have planes in abundance. Made of tough titanium and
    powered by great engines, these contemporary monsters are the distant
    offspring of the clumsy machine the Wright brothers had first made from
    recycled bicycle materials.

    But just as today's airplanes are able to take us from one part of the world
    to another in a matter of moments, satellites have enabled us to communicate
    with virtually anyone and everyone around the globe. All a person needs is a
    device called a telephone. Distance is no longer a barrier when a small
    robot many thousand kilometers above us is able to transmit our voices, and
    even our images from one corner of the Earth to another; in real-time, no
    less.

    This, more than anything else has opened up the Pandora's box that is
    globalization. Communications is the key to real power, and this is
    evidenced by the fact that some of the wealthiest men on earth are CEOs of
    news and communications firms.

    Of course, this trend has brought about a revolution of immense change.
    Superficially, communication has broadened the reach of democracy, allowing
    greater transparency and more people to participate in the political arena.
    Deep within the heart of this equation, however, it is still the media and
    public relations vehicles of multi-billion dollar corporations who wield the
    real power. Slick advertising and malicious smear-campaigns to discredit
    products and individuals pay very big tributes to this fact.

    For many communities, the rapid advances in communications have simply
    overtaken them. China is so beleaguered by accusations of human-rights
    abuses that they have taken steps to limit their citizens' access to the
    Internet, the main forum where all these grievances are aired out in the
    first place.

    For Europe and America, globalization has enabled them to impart their
    democratic ideals upon third-world countries and developing nations.
    Colonization no longer takes the form of naval fleets and booming cannons.
    The simple email has replaced it. It is a sad fact though, that
    globalization has had very little effect on humans being more open-minded.
    Communities have formed very dangerous opinions about themselves - a
    superiority complex that only manages to alienate countries that are poorer
    and less developed.

    Today, it is somewhat fashionable to label Islam as an archaic faith that is
    totally incompatible with the phenomenon of modernization. The newspapers
    and magazines abound with articles that point accusingly at countries like
    Afghanistan, Indonesia, Iran and Iraq as vivid examples. In fact, the vast
    majority of nations that are plunked into the third-world category have
    Muslims as their leaders. It would seem to many that Islam is the deciding
    factor, or indeed the barrier between enlightenment and obsolescence. But
    Muslim apologists would argue that the appalling plight of the Muslim
    nations is caused by the pervasive influence of western idealism and yes,
    Islam's number one public enemy; the state of Israel.

    In a very real way, the fashionable impression of Islam in the eyes of
    non-Muslims, and the apologists' arguments are both sides of what is really
    the same coin. Both views, however, are not entirely correct, and are the
    musings of people who are too lazy to dig deeper into the matter.

    Islam is not a retrograde religion that suppresses women's rights,
    discourages secular education and permits nepotism. In fact, Islam is the
    direct opposite of everything that has been said about it. All the negative
    attributes that have crept into contemporary ideology are the result of
    human failings and the atrocious mistakes that some have committed in
    interpreting the Quran. Instead of letting Islam guide their paths, some
    people have chosen to subvert the religion to their own aspirations and use
    it, more often than not, as a tool to launch their political careers.

    The Muslim apologists are wrong too in thinking that the backwardness of the
    umma (the Muslim community) is caused only by western secularism and obscure
    Zionist plots. What an oversimplification this is! Those factors may play a
    part, but the largest share of blame must come from within the umma itself.

    Why is this so? More importantly, what are the features that contribute to
    the relative "weakness" of the umma today? There are two main components.

    1. An introverted umma
    2. Muslim disunity

    I have already touched on the first component; introverted umma, in my
    previous essay entitled "Do Muslims Love Themselves too much?", so I shall now focus on Muslim disunity.

    It is a sad thing to note that the umma today is splintered into numerous
    divisions- lines that demarcate race, nationality, family, sects and a whole
    plethora of other distinctions. In relation to this trend, the Prophet once
    said:

    "The Jews were divided into seventy-one or seventy-two sects, and the
    Christians were divided into seventy-one or seventy-two sects, and my Ummah
    will be divided into seventy-three sects."

    One must not take the numbers literally, of course. It does not mean that
    the Jews are really divided into seventy-odd sects and the Muslims will be
    divided into seventy-three sects exactly. What the Prophet was trying to
    convey was the realization that the Muslim community will be even more
    divided than both the Hebrew and Christian communities. It was not a cry of
    victory, but one of lament. Disunity, after all, nurtures weakness and a low
    self-esteem. In a brutal world, it is the Achilles heel that enemies will
    undoubtedly exploit. This is probably why the western nations find it so
    easy to play one Arab country against another. The chinks are already there
    to find, and it is human nature to drive a wedge between these chinks to
    keep them open and festering.

    Disunity has and always will be a social malaise that will only get worse if
    the community that is afflicted does not take concrete steps to eradicate
    the problem. Unfortunately, even in countries that purport to be Islamic,
    Muslims represent an economic and political minority. They seldom agree on
    anything. There is no cohesive and coherent solidarity to speak of, and as a
    result, the so-called phenomenon of modernization cannot take place.

    Why do I say this?

    It is a fact that for its people to engage in inventive and progressive
    pursuits, a community must first be comfortable with itself and confident of
    its strength. Disunity is an illness that imposes a gnawing insecurity on
    the hearts and minds. Muslims who live in an environment where religious
    groups aggressively vie for popular political support are constantly
    assailed by varied and often confusing messages. The problem is exacerbated
    tenfold if the masses are not educated properly. It is therefore, not an
    accident that deviant teachings of Islam consistently crop up in the rural
    parts of countries.

    In the Quran, God clearly condemns disunity.

    "And lo! This, your religion is one religion and I am your Lord, so keep your
    duty unto Me. But they (mankind) have broken their religion among them into
    sects, each group rejoicing in its tenets" (Quran 23:52)

    "As for those who divide their religion and break up into sects, thou hast
    no part in them in the least: their affair is with God: He will in the end
    tell them the truth of all that they did." (Quran 6:159.12)

    "But people have cut off their affair (of unity), between them, into sects:
    each party rejoices in that which is with itself." (Quran 23:53.13)

    One of the symptoms of disunity is a chronic resentment for authority. The
    Prophet warned about this and even went so far as to denounce acts of
    rebellion; knowing that it would lead to much chaos and unnecessary
    bloodshed:

    "It has been narrated on the authority of Ibn 'Abbas that the messenger of
    Allah (may peace be upon him) said: One who found in his Amir something
    which he disliked should hold his patience, for one who separated from the
    main body of the Muslims even to the extent of a handspan and then he died
    would die the death of one belonging to the days of Jahiliyya (the dark age
    before Islam)." (Bukhari & Muslim, Book 20, Number 4559)

    The fault, however, does not lie on the general populace, but rather, on
    those clergy who insist in teaching an irresponsible form of Islam- one that
    is paradoxically at odds with reality and in harmony with some form of
    political aspiration instead. In short, Muslims have relegated Islam to a
    mere political tool, with which to garner votes and grassroots support. This
    is the sad plight of Islam today.

    How did this come about? In my previous essay, I mentioned that the umma has been plagued not so much by backwardness, but by
    inwardness. More and more, the umma has been closing in on itself, so much
    so that the trend has become virtually indistinguishable from Islam itself.
    What was once a global Islamic family is now broken up into factions that
    are based on race, nationality and creed. This is most evident when a
    government implements schemes that benefit only one race, instead of the
    whole umma. The Prophet himself condemns such practices.

    "It has been narrated on the authority of Ibn 'Abdullah al-Bajali that the
    Messenger of Allah (may peace be upon him) said: One who is killed under the
    banner of a man who is blind (to his just cause), who raises the slogan of
    family or supports his own tribe, dies the death of one belonging to the
    days of Jahiliyya (the dark age before Islam)." (Bukhari & Muslim, Book 20,
    Number 4561)

    Race is an illusionary thing. It is flighty and not based on any universal
    truth except those that are created by men who need superficial expression
    to stoke their insecurity.

    But this essay is not a censure of any particular race. However, I strongly
    feel that the direction in which the Muslims have been taking is
    self-defeating and extremely fatal. One should always be a Muslim first,
    before being a Chinese, Indian or Malay individual.

    In the Quran's tafsir (concordance), it is recommended that a Muslim
    community should be led by a Rightful Imam. But it is a sad and almost
    amusing fact that any Imam who aspires to lead the umma must first face the
    questions: "Are you a Malay?" and "Do you belong to the Sunni or Shiite
    Sect?" and "Do you follow the Shafiee or Hanafi School of Thought?"

    Indeed, in such an environment, an environment that the Prophet had already
    predicted would be the eventual plight of the umma, how can we ever elect a
    Rightful Imam? No Rightful Imam would ever come about, and this statement is
    strengthened by the fact that Muslims today seem to be always disagreeing
    with their own governments.

    But let us not be fatalistic. The belief in fate does not mean that Muslims
    should resign themselves to the worst lots in life.

    There is a key somewhere, and Muslims must search for that key in order to
    unlock the vast potential of a community that has suffered from the acts of
    people who have sabotaged the simple ideology of Islam. There can be no
    progress until the Muslims take it upon themselves to reach out towards each
    other and towards those people who form such an integral part of the world
    outside the umma. This is dawaah (missionary activity) in its simplest, most
    uncomplicated form.

    With globalization spreading to every corner of the world, it is important
    for people to have a more practical perspective on world issues. Indeed, one
    of the requisites for globalization should be the parallel evolution of
    mankind's thoughts and culture. There can never be any real change if we
    refuse to treat other cultures, other creeds, other viewpoints on their own
    terms. Ideas and opinions cannot be suppressed just because they are not
    pleasant to listen to. The classical umma was a hotbed of opposing opinions
    and frantic arguments even upon the nature of God. That is the clearest
    evidence that an Islamic community can succeed and sustain itself. But it is
    a community that must be dynamic to change and possess a positive attitude
    towards diversity.

    In this, education is the all-encompassing factor. Each government must take
    the initiative to develop its own people. Each family must impart upon their
    offspring the universal value of human life. Instead of focusing on only one
    particular community in a multi-racial country like Singapore, religious
    authorities must adopt an open-minded approach by reaching out to the poor
    and oppressed people of all creeds and cultures.

    Nobody wants violence to happen in Singapore, our beloved country. I love
    this place, not only because it is a beautiful country, but it is also one
    of the only nations in the world that manages to have a solid foundation of
    multi-culturism and multi-ethnicity. When we give up a seat in the MRT, we
    don't look to see if the person who needs it is a Malay, Indian or Chinese.
    Our compassion and understanding are our greatest assets. And they will be
    our most powerful weapons in the fight that is about to come, if it comes.
    If the whole community, Chinese, Malay, Indian, Muslim, Christian, Hindu,
    Buddhist treats each other with mutual respect and trust, how strong a
    foothold can any terrorist group gain in this region?

    In Islam, there is a beautiful concept that is called fitrah.

    "Then set your face upright for religion in the right state-- the nature
    made by Allah in which He has made men; there is no altering of Allah's
    creation; that is the right religion, but most people do not know." (Quran
    30:30)

    Fitrah refers to the nature of man that God has ordained for all mankind.
    All men were created pure monotheists, but his upbringing and the
    surroundings push him towards other creeds, other forms of religious
    expression.

    Fitrah manifests itself when a baby reaches out for his mother's teat for
    milk; that is a sign of the fitrah working where intelligence has yet to be
    nurtured. When a human cries out for a god in times of dire peril; that is
    the person's fitrah reaching out towards the Creator. When the study of
    genetics established the fact that all animal, plant and human life share
    the same genetic language of A (adenine), T (thymine), C (cytosine) and G
    (guanine), we know that the Creator is One who had established One blueprint
    for life. Fitrah is all of these and much, much more.

    In a real sense, all of mankind are really brothers and sisters. We
    descended from a common lineage, and it is only the petty amongst us who
    find segregation such an important preoccupation.
