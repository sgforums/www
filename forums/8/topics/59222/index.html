    Effectively, short term memory is the memory used to store information relevant to a task that you are actively performing. When the information is rehearsed and coded for future retrieval, it is passed on to long term memory. As a rough analogy, STM is like RAM in a computer, while LTM is like the hard disk.

    Contrary to popular belief, amnesia is often a case of STM failure rather than LTM failure. More often than not, people with Alzheimer's Disease can remember things from forty years ago, but cannot recall what happened five minutes prior - loss of LTM does not precede loss of STM. In other cases of memory being affected, such as Korsakov-Wernicke's syndrome, the effect on STM is fairly marked in the person's behaviour, such as in their inability to stick to a topic of conversation. Also note that skills such as language ability, which reside in LTM, remain intact.

    Language acquisition is not my area, although I am aware of some research that indicates that adults in fact pick up second languages faster than children. There is also anecdotal evidence that deaf adults who receive cochlear implants learn to understand spoken language.

    All things being equal, there is no conclusive evidence that learning capacity diminishes with age. Note that I said all things being equal - this excludes the effects of injury, neurotoxicity and age-related deterioration.

    As much as memory, existing knowledge of language and language structures, as well as exposure to the community that speaks the language, will have an effect on how well you learn the new language.

    Given that you have adequate understanding of language structures, contact with peope who speak the intended language and that you haven't knocked you head about or fried your brain with drugs or alcohol, I would say that you should have no problem learning a new language.

    13 Oct 03, 13:56
    That conclusion is somewhat oversimplified, FireIce. Keep in mind that the research is extremely varied, and that reviews and meta-analyses of previous data tend to focus on a few methodological issues in past research, mostly reaching the conclusion that the evidence is too mixed to conclude that children learn languages more quickly than adults. Singleton (1986), however, concludes that:

    "The one interpretation of the evidence which does not appear to run into contradictory data is that in naturalistic situations those whose exposure to a second language begins in childhood in general eventually surpass those whose exposure begins in adulthood, even though the latter usually show some initial advantage over the former."

    So children in fact learn slower than adults, although they will eventually develop a higher overall level of ability.

    In any case, it appears that you are rferring to children acquiring their first language, rather than the second language. The second language introduces the presence of a possible separate lingustic system. This raises a number of questions:

    1) Are there separate linguistic systems, or doe the person apply a single system to the two languages?
    2) If there are two language systems, do they interfere with or complement each other?
    3) If they interfere with each other, what is the effect of an existing language system on the person's efforts to learn a new language?

    The jury is still out on all of these questions.

    Originally posted by T|red_Guy:
    WOW!!!! Thx alot....can one have poor/normal STM but with great LTM?
    Theoretically speaking, information is held in STM while it is being coded and stored in LTM, so anything affecting STM affects LTM. However, STM is a very basic capacity - effectively, the average person's STM is capable of storing between 5 and 9 characters. What tends to have a more profound effect is your attentional processes (picking up the information in the fiorst place) and how you process the information for long term storage and recall.
