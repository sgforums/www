    Minister of State (Community Development and Sports) Yaacob Ibrahim said that some changes had already been made.

    The latter had accused the Government of being insensitive to Muslims here, by not allowing Muslim girls to wear the hijab, or Islamic headscarf - also known as tudung - in schools.

    Dr Yaacob said that while he and his colleagues can identify with this concern, they did not agree with Mr Zulfikar's approach.

    'The negotiations for space between a community and the Government have to take place as part of a gradual process...Religious freedom has to be guided by a greater need for integration, and racial and religious understanding and tolerance,' he said.

    Citing an example from Senior Minister Lee Kuan Yew's memoirs, he said Mr Lee had received a demand by the Chinese business community just after Independence, to elevate the status of Chinese among the four official languages here.

    But, Mr Lee turned it down because it could have alienated the Malays and Indians here.

    'With time and the emergence of English as the language of business and communication, Mr Lee then created spaces for the Chinese community to preserve their language and culture. Is that being insensitive to Muslim feelings?' asked Dr Yaacob.

    Putting the issue in perspective, Minister-in-charge of Muslim Affairs Abdullah Tarmugi said that no one was stopping Muslim girls from wearing the hijab outside school hours.

    'So, really, we're talking about that period of school, secular school at that.

    'If you look at it from that perspective, you can see how we've tried to balance these needs and the greater good of society,' he said.

    The Government's stand on the issue was spelt out last week when Home Affairs Minister Wong Kan Seng said that it was not just Muslims who had special requirements.

    'The other religions may also have their requirements. So religion is a very personal thing. It's up to them to practise their faith at their own time, but when they are in school, the school rules, the school uniform, they have to be followed...

    'In the context of our multi-racial society, we have to learn to live together and accommodate one another.'

    Asked about Mr Zulfikar's comments that the group would continue to press for a judicial review of the Government's decision, MP Ahmad Khalis Abdul Ghani said that pursuing a legal battle was not the way forward.
