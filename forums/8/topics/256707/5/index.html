    I don't think you understand how admission to law schools are based. Indeed in America, for undergraduate studies, more emphasis is placed on extra/co-curricular activities which the candidate partakes in.

    For admission to law schools however, it is a much more strictly regulated process. Fairly, candidates would have to submit inter alia, complete transcripts of results, past work experience, testimonials, multiple essays etc however, nothing weighs quite as heavily as results and LSAT scores.

    If the law school which you are applying for has a 165 LSAT cut off with a 3.75 GPA minimum, achievements might compensate for a 164 LSAT score with a 3.72 GPA.

    However, as the law is a postgraduate course in America, most of the candidates would be coming from not only brilliant academic backgrounds, but have extensive practical experience. Some people who choose to do law at a later stage in their life would be coming with life/work experience that would easily go on for pages and pages, but that's nothing unexpected.

    LSATs on the other hand are the definitive guide to how one will perform in law school; the teaching method is very different - the socrates method of learning by asking is the predominant medium of instruction and there's no better way to filter out those who will "get it" from those who won't, than the LSATs.

    I don't decide what's tier one; the US News & World Reports publishes the definitive guide to law schools - and of course, your alma matter is part of the tier one law schools.

    In relation to UK law schools, grades are foremost for Oxbridge, as well as the top few universities, including (non-exhaustive) the LSE, Durham (currently ranked top law school in the UK), and UoL (note UoL's prestige is sliding fast, so arguably, this is no longer a top university). However, all the other second rate institutions such as Uni of Newcastle, Uni of Manchester etc., you'd get by with average A level grades, and it would not take alot to graduate with 2nd upper class honours.

    However, whilst the Singapore bar is very very lenient and tender towards UK universities and law schools (the list is incredulous), they are much meaner to US and Australian legal graduates. Last I checked, only the top 4 law schools in the States were approved, and you would have to rank in the top 40% of your graduating cohort; for Australia, more law schools are recognised (but again, these are only the best each state has to offer - e.g. New South Wales - UNSW and UniSyd), but you would have to rank in the top 30% - and this is much harder to achieve.

    In fact, the Singapore bar has recently made a move to recognise Aus graduates who rank in the top 70% but again, this is no easy feat to achieve when everyone around you came in with a HSC score of 99.0/100. Further, they only recognise these Aus grads if they have 3 years of practical experience in a legal field, but seeing that they cannot be associates, this often means that these grads have to be clerks, drawing minimal pay.

    What happens then? Alot of people as far I know leave Singapore; no point being second rate in this country - they easily find employment with large-megafirms in Hong Kong, Australia or the UK; PR comes just as easy. I say this with no offence to our government which I greatly respect for excellent governance.

    Unless the Singapore Bar reconsiders its harsh stance towards our locals from US/AU law schools, we will end up with not only a shortage of lawyers, but find that our better talents are leaving for greener pastures.
