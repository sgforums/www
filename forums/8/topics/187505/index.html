    To clear up some misconception here....

    first number is the Magnification.

    Second number is diameter of objective lens. (in simpler terms. the lens that controls the light into the bino body, the lens does not control per se, but it is the diameter that does)

    Angle of view has nothing to do with these 2 number (but co-related to the magnification). In general, the higher the magnification, the narrower the angle of view.

    But a X brand 10x30's angle may be different to Y brand 10x30.

    This is because they have different lens construction and engineering.

    If you want to see the angle of view, you will need to see the technical literature.

    A bigger objective lens simply let more light into the bino body, the image you see is brighter, colours are richer too.

    Don't get too high magnification for a small bino. It means a little shake in your hands the image movement you see is exaggerated.... makes you dizzy fast.

    Originally posted by Icemoon:
    hehe .. so what numbers mean something for digicam?
    Megapixels as advertised in digicam is the biggest image the camera can create.

    so a typical 6mp camera usually produces 3000 x 2000 pixels. (usually is aroudn this region). 3000 x 2000 = 6,000,000. thus 6mp.

    A bigger mp does not mean a sharper image or better colours. It simply means you have a bigger digital image.

    You may buy a 6 mp camera, but the pixel is 500 x 12000. All the pictures you take will be elongated but the camera maker is not lying.

    Lets say you set a 6mp camera to take 1600x1200 picture. All you get is a 2mp picture regardless the max mp you bought the camera for.

    The multiplication zoom of the lens is basically obtained by dividing the biggest focal by the smallest focal length achieevable in the camera.

    Focal length is the distance of point of focus away from the film plane (in this case the CCD/CMOS panel)

    e.g. A compact digicam focal length is 18-70mm, this will mean 4 times. Another camera maker will sell a 12-48mm lensed camera... it is also 4 times.

    but the image you obtain will be different.

    It will be hard to compare digicam to digicam beacuse there is no standardised CCD/CMOS plane yet. There are few common sized plane but there is no agreed standardised one.

    Therefore, you might want to refer to the "35 mm film equivalent" ratings instead.

    Because in film, the film plane is standardised to 35x24mm.
