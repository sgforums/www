    Some church members are not happy
    BY SETO NU-WEN
    Jun 8, 2002
    EVERY Sunday, for 18 years, he has been attending services at the New Creation Church. Mr Lee, who didn't want to give his full name, said he contributes about $50 to $100 at the Sunday offerings.
    On top of that, every month, he makes sure that 10 per cent of his salary, about $1,000, is given to the church as tithe.
    About a month ago, during one Sunday service, he said he was taken aback.
    He received news that the church, through its business arm Rock Productions, had just bought East Coast Recreational Centre for over $10m.
    It has since been renamed Marine Cove.
    Mr Lee, a 48-year-old businessman, said: "I was upset because the members of the congregation were never consulted about this decision.
    "We were told about it only about a month ago but the decision to buy it was made at the end of last year.
    "Every month, I willingly give 10 per cent of my salary to the church. But right now, I'm concerned about the accountability of that money." Services are held three times a week at the Rock Auditorium, which is leased by Rock Productions from Suntec City.
    Another church-goer, who wanted to be known only as Mr Chung, told The New Paper: "As far as I understand, the church management has been scouting for a place to build a proper church.
    "But in this case, the purchase of East Coast Recreational Centre was for investment purposes. That brings into question the management's priorities.
    "Is the need to spend $10m on investment more important than building a proper church for our services?"
    Mr Chung, a 28-year-old lawyer, said: "The least that the management could do was to seek some views from the members of the congregation.
    "Instead we found out much later. Some church attendees I've spoken to found out only when they read it in the newspapers."
    Like Mr Lee, Mr Chung said he gives 10 per cent of his salary as tithe every month.
    He declined to disclose the amount, but he said that, combined with his wife's income as a sales executive, the monthly contribution was "considerable".
    Ms C H Tan decided to leave the church a few months ago.
    The real estate agent, who is in her 30s, said: "I began wondering: Where does the money go to?.
    "Now I find out that the money has gone into a business arm and investment. Where is the accountability to the members of the congregation?
    "That's why I decided to leave. I had lost my faith in the church and its management."
    However, Mr Lee is not thinking of leaving.
    He said: "I have nothing against the church or the religion. I am a staunch Christian.
    "I have no problems with giving a portion of my salary to the church.
    "I just expect the money to be used for church purposes - like its daily administrative processes, supporting its full-time staff and donating to the needy or members of the congregation who need financial help.
    "But when a church decides to form a business arm and go into a commercial direction, then I think something is not right."
    ROCK PRODUCTIONS IS A SEPARATE ENTITY
    MAJOR decisions are taken at the church's annual general meetings, its secretary, Mr Matthew Kang, said.
    The meetings are attended by the church's registered members.
    He said: "Registered members of the church are those who attend our care groups or serve in our ministries. Such membership is open to all who attend our services."
    Mr Lee and Mr Chung are not registered members, and neither was Ms Tan.
    In an earlier interview with The New Paper, Rock Productions manager Jack Ho said the original plan was to build a church on the site of the East Coast Recreational Centre.
    But after several rounds of applications, this was not approved. He added that they are still looking for a proper site.
    Responding to the issue of tithes, Mr Kang said that "tithes are very personal".
    He said: "We don't impose an amount, nor do we force or pressurise members of our congregation to give a certain amount. We don't discriminate against those who don't pay tithes.
    "We also don't keep a record of who pays and who doesn't. If members want us to keep a record so they are eligible to register as a member, they can fill up a form."
    Mr Kang is also a non-paid director at Rock Productions and a shareholder trustee of the church.
    He added: "We would like to make it clear that even though Rock Productions is the business arm, it is run as a separate entity.
    "Any transfer of funds between the church and Rock Productions is provided for in the church's constitution.
    "This constitution has been in effect since the church was formed and may have been amended over time but each amendment was approved by the Registry of Societies (ROS).
    "Once a year, our accounts are audited and submitted to the ROS."
    But what happens if Rock Productions runs into bad investments? Will the church be liable?
    Said Mr Kang: "We have not encountered this before.
    "So far, we haven't received any complaints about our purchase of East Coast Recreational Centre."
    The New Paper sought the opinion of an experienced corporate lawyer.
    He said: "If Rock Productions runs into trouble, only its shareholders are liable, not the church.
    "This is because the company is registered as a separate entity from the church."
