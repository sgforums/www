    Driving tests in auto cars from next January?
    Learners may opt for it but those who pass will not be allowed to drive a manual vehicle

    By Christopher Tan

    AS EARLY as January next year, people taking up driving will have an option never open to them before: doing the test in an automatic car. The only catch is those who pass with an automatic car will not be allowed to drive a manual vehicle.

    Major driving schools have devised new cirricula to meet the change.
    While the Traffic Police has refused to reveal details, The Straits Times understands that the relevant parties are working towards a January deadline.

    The major driving schools have already prepared for the change. Two of the leading schools, Comfort Driving Centre and Singapore Safety Driving Centre (SSDC), have already budgeted for a fleet of automatic vehicles, trained their instructors, and come up with new curricula.

    But some are cautious, saying such news might cause would-be students to postpone signing up for courses until next year.

    Comfort Driving Centre said it was waiting for an official announcement on the start date, but added that it had already made plans for 20 automatic cars.

    'Certificates of entitlement have already been secured... all our instructors have already been trained, and we are operationally ready,' said Ms Tammy Tan, spokesman for ComfortDelGro Corp, the transport giant that owns Comfort Driving Centre.

    Mr Stephen Kong, SSDC's senior marketing executive, said his company has also prepared to buy 12 automatic cars. It currently has 117 cars, including two automatic ones.

    'We're bidding for COEs for the 12 1.6-litre Honda Civics,' he said.

    'We will progressively increase the fleet with demand.'

    And there is demand. According to Mr Kong, the school has been getting daily enquiries from the public about automatic tests. 'On our website alone, we get more than 20 queries per week,' he said.

    Going by the example in Japan, Mr Kong said demand will be strongest from women learners. About 48 per cent of SSDC's students are female.

    But Comfort, which has conducted polls on the popularity of automatic testing, found only 23 per cent of its students would opt for it. Its surveys found most people were less keen when they were told that they would not be able to drive a manual car if they obtained a licence through an automatic test.

    There is a bright side: a shorter training course. SSDC indicated that the automatic syllabus is slightly shorter than the manual one, but will focus on unique skills such as starting and moving off. The syllabus will need the approval of the Traffic Police.

    When asked, the Traffic Police would only say that offering tests using automatic cars was 'a logical next step'.

    'Such forms of testing is available in other countries. The public too has sought to have such testing choices made available here in Singapore,' said spokesman Phillip Mah.

    He said the Traffic Police was in the final stages of a review and details of the change will be announced 'in a few months'.
