    Japanese in shock over schoolgirl stabbing
    Koizumi asks parents to reflect on how to raise kids

    TOKYO - Prime Minister Junichiro Koizumi yesterday called on parents to reflect on how they raise their children as Japan reeled in shock after an 11-year-old schoolgirl slashed her classmate to death, apparently over an exchange of e-mail.

    Satomi Mitarai, 12, died from massive blood loss after being stabbed by a classmate with a papercraft knife on Tuesday during her lunch break at the Okubo Elementary School in the south-western Japanese city of Sasebo.

    Police said yesterday they were still investigating the motive of the killing that has stunned Japan, a country which had long prided itself on being relatively crime-free.

    'I wonder how murder could ever take place between two elementary schoolgirls,' Mr Koizumi told a parliament committee when asked to comment on the incident.

    'The incident was harrowing...and beyond our imagination. It is the responsibility of adults to think seriously how we should raise children,' he said, adding that the key to a good upbringing was affection from parents.

    Mr Koizumi has three sons. He divorced his wife in 1983 when she was pregnant with his third child. He has never met his youngest son.

    'An educator told me when I was young that the principle of educating children is to hold them tight, put them down gently and then make them walk. I think this says it all,' he said.

    The Japanese leader also criticised parents who leave babies alone in their bedrooms before they turn one or two years old.

    Satomi's attacker was quoted as telling police that she had called the victim to a study room, where she slit her neck and arms with a papercraft cutter, and left her to bleed to death.

    The attacker, whose name was withheld as she is a minor, then returned to the classroom with her clothes bloodstained.

    Paramedics said the victim's neck was cut so extensively that they could not take her pulse.

    Local media reports told the story of a friendship between two classmates - who regularly left notes on each other's home pages and used text messaging - suddenly going awry.

    Japan Broadcasting Corp said the girl had 'trouble over what Satomi posted on the website the girl was operating'.

    The daily Asahi Shimbun's website quoted the suspect as telling investigators: 'I didn't think what she wrote on the home page was funny, so I called her out of the classroom. I meant to kill her.'

    Other students recalled the two girls fighting during a recent school sporting event, Nippon Television Network reported.

    'I am sorry. I am sorry,' the girl was quoted by the daily Yomiuri Shimbun as tearfully telling police during questioning.

    Japanese media said the girl would appear before a family court, which could send her to a special reformatory for children.

    The victim's widowed father, who lives alone with her and her older brother, said he was in shock.

    'That my daughter could no longer be with me is unbelievable. But the unbelievable has happened,' Mr Kyoji Mitarai, the local bureau chief of the Mainichi Shimbun newspaper, told reporters.

    'She was like air to me.'

    JUNE 24, 2004
    ANOTHER SERIOUS JUVENILE CRIME
    Girl, 13, pushes boy off building in Tokyo

    TOKYO - Japanese police yesterday took a 13-year-old girl into custody for allegedly pushing a boy off a high building, rocking a nation still reeling from a series of brutal crimes by children.

    The Chinese boy, five-year-old Sun Jizu, suffered only minor cuts as he plunged into a tree on the ground from an 11-m-high staircase landing of an apartment building in central Tokyo, police said.

    Police took the girl into custody as they alleged that she 'told the boy to sit on a guardrail and pushed him off' on Tuesday, a spokesman for the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department said.

    Local media reports said the two children had known each other for some time.

    The girl, whose name has been withheld because she is a minor, reportedly told police that she had pushed the boy to 'shut his mouth' before he could tell her mother that she was frequenting a game arcade.

    'Mum would be furious if she came to know about it,' Jiji Press news agency quoted the girl as saying.

    The girl told police she regretted what she had done and wished to apologise to the boy, Jiji Press said.

    The case is the latest of the recent series of serious juvenile crimes.

    The nation was stunned when an 11-year-old girl slashed a classmate to death with a paper cutter three weeks ago during her lunch break at an elementary school in the south-western Japanese city of Sasebo.

    The number of juvenile criminal suspects in Japan rose by 1.9 per cent last year from the previous year to 144,404, according to a government report released on Tuesday.

    More than half the offences involved theft and only 1.5 per cent were serious crimes such as murder and rape.

    The report suggested, however, that the authorities may need to review the steps used to investigate offences where the perpetrators are believed to be under 14, the age limit for criminal responsibility in Japan.

    'The rights of police to investigate such offences were unclear, and they have no authority for coercive investigations,' the Kyodo news agency quoted the report as saying. -- AFP
