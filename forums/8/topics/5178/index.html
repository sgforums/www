    hmmm....this topic is actually very wide. it covers ethics, political theory, and possibly some aspects of the theory of knowledge. i really shouldn't write too much and bore everyone to death. but maybe i can try and outline some points. i'll try not to turn this into a thesis (else rinoa will scold me again).

    1. Assumption: ppl are basically evaluative and judgemental. hence there is no possibility of neutral terms, as in bochup's example above. Even the word "donate" is not neutral. it usu has positive connotations and will be evaluated as such. To donate may be seen as the "right" thing to do, because a premium is placed on altruism of whatever sort. but what if i say, well whether or not you get an abortion is up to you. i am "neutral" regarding that. it's your choice and i won't say whether what you choose is "right" or "wrong". again, it would seem that even if you were to say this, a premium would have been placed on "your freedom to choose". this implies that if i forbid you to choose, you would say i have no "right" to do that, or what i am doing is "wrong".
    hence, it would seem that we are basically evaluative creatures and this is something we can't change.

    2. Granted that we are evaluative creatures, where do our evaluations come from? Let's list some possible sources. They may come from religion, tradition, reason or sentiment.

    3. Evaluations from religion: religious commandments are usu taken as the guidebook to social action and to notions of right and wrong. what's good abt this? ppl know what to do and there is less room for moral dilemma. What's bad? Galileo prevented by the Catholic Church to publish a book supporting Copernican theory that the earth actually revolves ard the sun and not the other way round. The church was determining what was right and what was wrong in the realm of astronomy and science. should the church only restrict itself to the realm of morals and morality and not science? other commentators say that religion is a tool of domination and control by the bourgeois agst the "unthinking masses". religion is seen as the opium of the ppl. but is religion all (and only) abt power and subjugation?

    4. Evaluations from tradition: typically of a non mono-theistic and ritualistic community. what's good? see point 3 above. what's bad? is progress really possible? is it even a goal? should it be a goal? if progress is both desirable and possible, what should be the medium of change? that is, how do we decide what to change or what values or practices to abandon? do we resort to communal consensus? do we let the light of reason guide us? is reason universally binding? that is, can reason guide everyone and lead us to the "truth" about what ought to be the case?

    5. Evaluations based on reason: immediate problem: what is reason and rationality? is reason really "objective"? there is a painting by Goya showing a man sleeping on a table and at the background are images of monsters and goblins. the caption at the bottom says (in spanish): the sleep of reason brings forth monsters. meaning of course that if we don't use our reasoning powers and faculty, all kinds of monstrosities may occur. we should let the light of reason rule agst mere feelings and passions and allow the best argument to guide us thru. but in spanish, the word "sleep" can also be translated as "dream". this changes the meaning of the phrase completely: the dream of reason brings forth monsters. meaning, it would seem, that the use of reason itself brings forth all kinds of dastardly deeds. whatever could this mean? does it mean that with reason we can justify any and all kinds of actions? so using reason, can we still find out what is right and what wrong? can arguments convince absolutely? can we say, let's be reasonable abt this and discuss it - may the best argument win. would notions of power and control come in here? or should we rather rely on reason to provide us not exactly with what to do but only with a formula of what to do - perhaps similar to the Golden Rule (do only that which you would have others do unto you). Would this lead to agreement in what is rite or wrong?

    6. Evaluations based on sentiment: are things like "lying" or "murder" or "adultery" objectively wrong? are they wrong in themselves? or do we only say they are wrong (not because reason tells us so but) because they offend our sentiments and feelings? whether something is rite or wrong would then depend on whether they give us feelings of pleasure or displeasure. there is no objective rite or wrong, only rite or wrong depending on how we feel abt it. is this really the case? but wouldn't this be overly-subjective? is reason really a slave to the passions? how do we build consensus on mere subjective feelings? nazi germany managed to stir up the passions of the nation. so feelings can unite. but divorced from reason and good sense what kind of monstrosities could occur?

    7. We've considered some sources of notions of rite and wrong. but let's say u want to build a state. from scratch. what kind of a society would you want? a united one based on unified values? how would you determine these values? from the sources listed above? by means of a social contract? by means of democratic consensus? but based on what ideals? freedom and liberty maybe? but how far would u want individualism to progress? should the individual be free to act on his own values? should there be a limit on his freedom of action? should we say, fine go do and think whatever you want as long as it doesn't hurt anyone else? but this kind of independence and autonomy has repercussions on other parts of the state. it may mean the economy is run on principles of non-interference by the state. is any kind of paternalism then justified? does it mean the govt has no right to ban guns or encourage you to eat healthy food? if u were running a state, what notion of right and wrong would you say best serves the ppl and the country?

    8. so when u ask "are wrongs really wrong and rights really right"? ask yourself first, what kind of a society do u want? one sure of it's identity and values or one that is questioning and liberal but also uncertain and fragmented? there are good and bad points to both options and to any other alternatives for that matter. so perhaps there are no rights and wrongs in and of themselves. but in order to build a society, there has to be some values. what should these values be? well maybe it all depends on your vision of an ideal society. and maybe even in that we will quarrel abt what is right and what is wrong!

    okay....better stop now. there is lots more to say (tip of the iceberg actually), but i should leave it to someone who can say it better. as i said, it's a very large topic. oops! written too much again. sorry guys! now rinoa will scold again.....
