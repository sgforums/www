    He has secrets to tell

    New technique helps pin down time of death of even badly decomposed bodies

    WHO says dead men tell no tales?

    Researchers have found a new technique using radioactive dating to help the police pin down the approximate time of death of even badly decomposed bodies.

    According to senior lecturer Stuart Black, the British-pioneered technique had already been used in tracing not only the date of death but also the origin of a headless and limbless torso dubbed Adam, found floating down Thames in Septmenber 2001.

    'This is the most advanced test of its kind in the world,' he said.

    The Federal Bureau of Investigation in the US has begun its own trials of the technique which relies on the natural occurrence of certain radioactive isotopes in the environment.

    ISOTOPE

    Research found that trace elements of lead isotope 210 is found in the human food chain in fairly even quantities and that people therefore have more or less constant quantities of it in their bodies.

    When a person stops eating, the isotope - which has a half-life of just 22 years - starts to degrade and diminish.

    The half-life measure is the period over which an isotope loses half its radioactivity.

    Monitoring the degree of degradation allows forensic pathologists to determine to within a year the time of death.

    Tracing the degradation of another isotope, polonium 210, which has a half-life of only 138 days and is also spread throughout the environment, can fine-tune the date of death to within a fortnight.

    'There was no reliable way of dating skeletal remains prior to this,' Mr Black said.

    He said the technique suited the police who tended to use 75 years as cut-off point in murder cases, after which it was unlikely the killer would still be alive.

    In the case of Adam, the technique not only gave a date of death but also a region of origin.

    Adam was found to have come from the southern Nigerian city of Benin. - Wire services.
