How you Feel/think abt "Rejection"....

  • Bluediver
    30 Aug 00, 02:52
    How many people have experienced this common rejection
    line,"Why can't we just remain friends"? It's so often repeated
    that it is starting to sound like an insult (which it is actually).
    You were close friends, you shared both your lives, you decide
    to take on the next step but the moment you got too close,
    the other party backs away. And then suddenly, your friendship
    takes a nose dive. You've lost a friend, more importantly, you've
    lost a part of yourself. Because you knew, yes, especially when
    you knew, the other party secretly enjoyed the attention, the hint
    of sexual tension, the kind that is seductive and attractive.

    Why is it so? This is a question I often asked myself and I think the
    reason behind the friendship because the romantic spark was always
    part of the friendship. It's like a 2 way process. As soon as the interested
    party tries to raise the hint of romance, it's very hard to remain friends
    (but not impossible). The person who was infatuated most likely had
    an expectation that things might develop further and even if the other
    party wasn't interested, he or she loved all the attention, the energy,
    the thrill but the moment an interest is raised, suddenly, the magic is
    gone, there are no more expectations, no interest even in retaining the
    friendship.

    Why? Both the parties are still the same as what they were before the
    romance issue was raised. So then, the pursuer suddenly realises that
    he or she is completely mystified. After all, the pursuer is still the same
    person and there has been no change.The pursuer then questions himself
    or herself what has gone wrong, and then here's where their self-blame and
    self-guilt starts and everything from then on, starts going downhill.

    It is ironic and strange that the person who raised the issue of romance is at
    fault and is always the one being blamed (usually for being too hopeful of
    themselves). Simply put, the one who dumps the other usually has the upper
    hand because he or she has the "power control" - the determining factor whether
    a relationship succeeds or fails. The dumper then starts to get annoyed at the
    pursuer because he or she is angry at having been forced to come to a
    decision and then preferred to stay away.

    In total fairness, the one being dumped didn't make things easier. If the
    dumper offers to resume the friendship, the pursuer might think they are
    getting another chance. So most of the time, many people don't reinitiate the
    friendship once that matter has been raised. Some dumpers even resort to
    being cruel to get the attention off their backs. They feel that it is easy so that
    the other party can get more angry and hurt so that thoughts of the dumper can
    be gotten out of their minds quicker. Unfortunately, such situations tend to
    take on twisted meanings because love spurned can quickly generate into hate
    or something much worse.

    Now, it is still possible for the 2 of them to remain genuine friends if it doesn't
    work out. It takes a level of maturity between both of them to sort out because
    friendship and romance are 2 different issues. So the next time your close friend
    suddenly reveals that they have an attraction for you, honestly, look at the person
    for who he or she is, decide properly, think carefully on what you want to say
    before you say it because what you say will depend on how your close friend
    treats you in the future.

    If you knew you secretly liked the attention, I would advise to giving that person
    a chance otherwise, you know you will not be totally honest with yourself.However, if you
    choose not to reciprocate, explain the situation and make things clear. Feelings
    are involved here, if you are cruel to them, then one day, you just might
    find yourself in a similar situation. In the end, just be careful. To the pursuer,
    you have to understand that there are people you can be friends with but never
    as a lover.

    Friendship and love are 2 different feelings. If you feel like giving the question a
    chance, be prepared to handle rejection. If you think you cannot handle it, I
    would advise that you don't do it at all and let the friendship remains at best a
    friendship. If not, perhaps it would be wise to withdraw from the friendship
    altogether if you're not mentally prepared for it.

    Because the more you prolong the idea, the worse it is going to get and then
    suddenly, you wake up one day to find that the idea has so preoccupied your mind
    that to handle rejection or to ask drives you crazy. It's not going to do you any
    good.

    Getting rejected always hurts, regardless if it's the man or the woman being refused. To say
    that a man handles rejection better than a woman is purely nonsense, just as to
    say a woman can easily find another man if she was rejected is false. Face it, all of
    us cannot handle rejection well, I don't think men and women are really much
    different in this respect.

    So how do ya guys think??...

    Rejection is part and parcel of everyone's search for the ideal mate.

    Why do close friends almost never make it ? Well, in the first place, being close friends means that you already know some dark secrets about the other person. Some of WHICH that person doesn't want her potential suitor to find out.

    I can't say much about a girl's point of view, but from a guy's ? I honestly don't think that I would want a close friend as a girlfriend either, truth be told.

    In the first place, she might not be my type of gal. Secondly, I might not be receptive or reciprocative to her advances in the first place. Third, I might even be disgusted by it.

    Things would probably remain awkward between the 2 friends. Since the person dumped would still have lingering hopes that the dumpee would change his/her mind. The dumpee, knowing that, would probably remain friends superficially, but in reality, try to avoid contact with that person for as much as decently possible.

    Some would be able to overcome the fact, and laugh it off. But nonetheless, the memory of that event would remain, and things just don't remain as they were.
