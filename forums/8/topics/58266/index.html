GEEK Chic

  • Hydro
    29 Sep 03, 17:24
    GEEK is the new chic.

    Before, gadgets such as PDAs and digital cameras were considered toys for the boys. Today, gadgets are flaunted and worn by fashionistas like designer shoes.

    Mobiles phones are worn around the neck, digital music players are strapped to the arm, and wireless earpieces for phones are stuck full-time in the ear, giving new meaning to the phrase 'always on'.

    Gadget makers have seen this coming for quite a while. Almost every major electronics company has a product on the shelves aimed specifically at the trendy.

    Technology giants such as Philips and Siemens have gone one step further with entire gadget lines designed specifically for wearing rather than just carrying around.

    Philips' line of Key Ring audio players and digital cameras are designed to be the size of key chains and worn around the neck.

    Siemens takes that concept further with its Xelibri line of wearable mobile phones. Designed to be worn around the neck or clipped to lapel or handbag, the Xelibri line will offer new models for each season much like big fashion houses such as Prada.

    'Today, most people buy the mobile phone that looks the best, and many have a habit of showing it off,' said Mr George Appling, president of Xelibri division.

    'This shows that mobile phones are potential fashion accessories like watches, handbags and shoes. We envisage the scenario where people will own many fashion accessory phones and wear the one that matches their mood, the occasion or their attire.'

    Mr Gautam Kiyawat, Philips' regional marketing and business director of audio and video products, agreed that gadgets are becoming important fashion statements for the tech savvy.

    'These devices are no more just functional products,' he said. 'They are becoming more and more a means of personal expression. Design and materials are becoming more important.'

    Many other big players in the gadget game are not far behind with their own take on wearable technology.

    For example, Motorola showcased its Offspring line of wearable gadgets earlier this year.

    Still in its prototype phase, the line features items such as a watch/PDA, sunglasses with built-in heads-up display and a thumb-sized digital camera that can be clipped to one's collar. All are wirelessly linked to each other.

    The prestigious Massachusetts Institute of Technology even has a research department dedicated solely to research on wearable technology, with its successful projects (like a full-function PC that looks like a school backpack) being sold by its spin-off company, Charmed Technology.

    And fashion tech junkies such as sales manager Regina Lee, 29, can't wait for more of these wearable gizmos to hit the shelves.

    'Gadgets like phones and cameras used to be so big and blockish looking,' said Miss Lee, who wears her Nokia phone and Canon Ixus digital camera around her neck when she goes to parties. 'Now they are petite and have curves and colours. And you know how girls like to mix and match accessories.'
