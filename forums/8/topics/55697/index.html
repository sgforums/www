    AUG 27, 2003
    Mirage of Lee Hsien Loong's image problem

    By Chua Lee Hoong

    THE first time I came across Mr Lee Hsien Loong in person was in the late 1980s, when I was a young civil servant.

    He was the Minister for Trade and Industry, and the Second Minister for Defence.

    He was among a group of ministers, including the Prime Minister, that my department gave regular briefings to. When I wasn't briefing them, I would be there to take notes.

    I have two abiding impressions from those sessions.

    One was that Mr Lee had the uncanny knack of seeming deep in slumber, then popping up the moment the briefing ended to ask the most penetrating questions.

    His long, reposing frame - six foot if an inch - slung loosely in the chair fooled us all.

    The other observation? He sure loved our curry puffs.

    Fifteen years on, I don't know if Mr Lee still loves curry puffs. But he still has the same difficulty of keeping his frame contained within most conventional chairs.

    When Channel NewsAsia featured him in a forum with junior college students last year - the same forum where he wore that famous red shirt - the way his frame was too big and long for the tub chair reminded me all over again of how he used to fidget in the chairs I used to know.

    Mr Lee takes after his father in his fidgeting. He takes after his father too in voice and the cadences of speech.

    Needless to say, he too is brilliant, has a razor-sharp intellect and doesn't suffer fools gladly.

    Where he differs sharply from his father, however, is that he likes children.

    He genuinely likes them.

    No amount of hothousing in political prep school can enable you to fake that.

    You can pat thousands of them on the head and it will still be clear to the discerning observer that you're doing so only because you think you have to.

    Not so the younger Lee.

    My colleague, a veteran journalist who first covered him when he entered politics in 1984, says: 'He is awfully good with children. They respond very well to him.'

    I can say the same from what I have seen.

    My colleague also remembers the first Lee Hsien Loong walk-about in Teck Ghee: 'I was very impressed by how he handled his grassroots chaps who were trying to rush him along by saying, 'We have to cover XX number of blocks'.

    'And he said, 'No, we are not covering blocks. We are meeting people'.

    'At the market - and I have never forgotten this - when he was going around shaking hands, a fishmonger demurred, saying that his hands were dirty. Lee Hsien Loong stuck out his hand and said, 'No Singaporean's hands are dirty', and shook his hand firmly.'

    What then of the 'no-nonsense, uncompromising and tough' public persona that Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong spoke about recently?

    My colleague makes a shrewd observation: 'It is a male versus male thing. If you ask men, they will say, yeah, he is unapproachable. They keep a respectful distance from him.

    'Maybe he exudes alpha male pheromones. Who knows?'

    Who knows. He can be positively charming, even when he is just telling you why the Central Provident Fund contribution rate has to be cut.

    Another colleague notes that Mr Lee doesn't suffer any image problem where heartlanders are concerned.

    They know that he is the son of Mr Lee Kuan Yew, and as intelligent, and that is enough.

    Authoritarian and uncompromising?

    In Chinese culture, these are traits of good, decisive leaders, to be welcomed as long as they bring the bacon home.

    One challenge among the many that Mr Lee will face is that he inherits a Confucian society caught in the slipstream of rapid Westernisation.

    The style expected of him will differ among different segments of society.

    In recent decades, the Singapore administration is not known to believe in the art of the word as balm for the soul.

    It is a no-nonsense government.

    Words are used to convey facts, and facts are then used to move people.

    It is just not done, to use words directly to move people.

    In Mr Lee's case, for instance, he is happiest when he is creating policies, not creating sentences.

    Having said that, however, I tend to believe that he possesses a sensibility of feeling and spirit that equips him with powers of empathy rare in a politician.

    He's hardly your sensitive new age guy or your metrosexual, but he is an intense and thoughtful man who takes seriously the responsibilities that come with assuming the mantle of premiership.

    He sees, for instance, that for Singapore to adapt successfully in the face of global trends, its people need to become less risk-averse and its system less rigid and controlled.

    So while he will make tough and no-nonsense policy decisions, he will recognise that there is a less tangible aspect of leadership - the soft power, if you like, as opposed to the hard power.

    Harnessing this is as important as making changes to rules and infrastructure, and I have no doubt he will do that.

    If he combines the best leadership skills of his father and PM Goh, and throws in a few of his own besides, we are in for a treat indeed.
