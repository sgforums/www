    24 Apr 00, 04:54
    the topic says "is polytechnic education inferior to junior college education".

    the impt pts in this argument are
    a) inferior in what sense? with regards to which context?

    b) are these 2 systems comparable in the first place?

    ok, if u look at it really from the myopic pt of view, where the most difficult to get in one wins (like pple usually think that the more expensive thing is better...crap, but still..), JC wins hands down. enuff said.

    if u're looking at it as a stepping stone to higher qualifications, JC still wins hands down. good results or not, a poly grad cannot get into NUS or NTU without his diploma being relevant to his intended course of studies. think nautical studies. which course in NUS or NTU can u get into, even if u're a perfect scorer?

    JC is tailored for U, poly is tailored for the industry. so with regards to U, it's a no-contest.

    that brings us to the question of whether these 2 systems are comparable in the first place. remembering that of course they are tailored for different things to being with.

    now, let's look at it from the society pt of view. if u're a poly grad, u'll work earlier. will u earn more in the end? depends if the JC grad goes to U. if he does, changes are that he'll out-earn u in yr lifetime, assuming that u get the same job and rise at the same pace. simply coz he's on a higher pay scale. given 30 odd yrs of work, he'll catch up and overtake the poly grad. i'm NOT going to dispute this pt with anyone here, i have stats to prove this from research sometime back but normal argument abt the reliability of stats and the way it's presented is bad enuff. argument here would be worse.

    so, given these is JC education superior? hardly.....

    essentially, if u're looking at the BIG picture, the person himself/herself matters more than anything else. all the EQ stuff and all. attitude. GP teaches u some analytical skills and how not to see things microscopically, but is it effective? i look at the numbers of GP failures from the recently released A level results. i was part of a team which went down to every single JC (except Pioneer) on results day and looking the GP results, i'm beginning to wonder what JC students have learnt from their GP lessons. is it the system's fault or is it the student's fault. i have a inclination to believe it's the student.

    results always go up, more or less. GP is the one which never goes up. marginally maybe.

    so if u're taking GP as an indication of "thinking" skills JC put in u, it failed. period. it might have benefitted some, but not enuff.

    so, are poly grads more creative and more useful in society? it would take more than a few Sim Wong Hoo examples to convince me.

    if u read his "Chaotic Thoughts of a New Millenium", u'll realise that poly had preciously little to do with his success.

    Ability is NOT measured purely by grades. Pure academic results simply means that you did well in that exam. The question I studied for came out. So, I scored. Does that mean I'm good? Even if it's because I'm smart, is that all there is?

    Vision. Drive. Attitude. Tolerance. All these important characteristics of a successful person will not necessarily be present in a person with academic capabilities.

    Still, the piece of paper is the first impt step to society. dun give me the dot.com crap. the time has passed. pple make it big because they were ahead of their times. think Microsoft, Apple, Yahoo!, Creative. there are fewer and fewer "new" dot.com ideas left. some will still make it, but most will probably fail.

    most of us will probably be employees in our lifetimes and the paper is the start.

    however, there's more to life than money and a career. it's u as a person. the system can help u, but in the end, the only thing which can really help u is yrself. even a good system can produce bad eggs.

    conclusion.
    the most impt thing is personal development. it's nothing to do with the system. both are not perfect and are tailored towards different things.

    products from both systems can succeed. in the end, the benchmark for success is yrself. You rate how successful you were.

    To argue which system is better by comparing success stories from both sides is thus a moot point.

    i dunno if you have been keeping in touch with what is happening in Singapore. But i can briefly touch on this "job-hopping" thingy.

    In a knowledge-based economy like Singapore, talent is a liquid asset. Talents flow.

    SM Lee and Dr Tony Tan had recently encouraged people to "job-hop" (they might have used some other words). The reason is simple. For a company and an employee to work well together, each has to satisfy the other's needs.

    The company has its own corporate life cycle, especially if it is a young company. The employees will also have their own life cycle. And their needs change depending which part of the life cycle they are in. Once they (the company and the employee) do not meet each other's needs, they should stop working together. The employee should leave and look for another company which he can fit in.

    This issue is briefly touched on in "Career Guide 2000".

    As a result, by having an employee in the company for a long time does not mean that the company is benefitting. In fact, these "old" employees may become a barrier to the company's growth. Their abilities and ways of working may not already be what the company requires. Hence these "old" workers should leave the company if they cannot be "re-trained".

    "Loyal" employees are only favoured by the old Chinese boss nowadays. They have already lost their value in the new economy.

    She is a diploma holder. She worked as an accountant in a Japanese firm for 27 years. It was her first job.

    She was extremely upset about it cos she is very competent in her job (even her boss dotes on her) and she is so "loyal" to the company. One fine day, she finally stepped into the GM's office and asked why isn't she promoted to a managerial post.

    The GM said this :

    " XXX, I am very sorry, i understand that you are a very good employee and have contributed tremendously to the company's growth. However, you can never be promoted to a managerial post because nobody will like to be managed by just a diploma holder. We need someone with a degree to do the job. I can only increase your wages, in fact you are already drawing a large amount of money considering you are only a diploma holder. "

    My auntie was heart broken. She resigned and found a new job. Her current pay is only about 70% of what she drew in that old company.
