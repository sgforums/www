    I'll speak from my experience, as a teacher and a previous student.

    As an ESL teacher (although it is applicable to any subject), my philosophy is to help those who want to help themselves. If you don't want to improve, you're welcome to leave my class. You don't need to waste your money and my effort. If you have the motivation to begin with, I can surely further motivate you and bring forth the passion to learn...however, I cannot create your passion if you have none to begin with.

    It is important to capture the attention of the students within the first 5 mins of meeting them. But maintaining that relationship is most difficult and requires effort from both ends. I can only do so much to ensure that I answer your questions. However, it is up to you to ask the right questions. Also, we have to ensure there is a clear understanding of our respective goals.

    There was response to write a note to the teacher to explain how you feel. I would suggest taking it one step further and actually speaking to the teacher one-on-one. Before you do this, you have to determine whether you have put forth the effort. If you have always had this bad attitude and negative perspective of the teacher, I would suggest you change your perspective of the teacher and the subject. After a few days, if you still find yourself in an unenjoyable situation, simply ask for a few minutes of the teacher's time. Sympathize and empathize with the teacher by trying to understand why they act that. Then you'll be able to project your own perspectives to the teacher.

    To you I ask: what steps have you taken to show the teacher that you want to be in that class? What steps have your classmates taken? Are you all thinking alike or do you truly want to strive above your classmates?
