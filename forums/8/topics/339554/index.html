    Got this online:

    1. Locate the nest. Usually this is made of paper or mud, and often found under eaves, in attics, or any space protected from the weather.
    2. Get the proper insecticide. Home repair and improvement warehouse stores will have a wide selection of wasp and hornet sprays for a variety of prices. Get the cheapest. They all do the same. 2 or more aerosol cans are recommended for anything larger than a small nest.
    3. Doing this at night is best, after the bees have gone in for the night.
    4. Don protective gear. Long sleeves tucked into gloves, long pants tucked into your socks, and maybe even a wide brimmed hat with a nylon stocking over it, tucked into the neckline of a shirt may be in order.
    5. Wear a respirator, if in enclosed areas or desired. Insecticide kills insects, but it isn't exactly good for people, either. At the very least, provide good ventilation with a fan or retreat quickly after the initial assault.
    6. Mount your assault, saturating the nest with the aerosol cans. Most have a 15 foot or more range, so you can start from far away, but after a few seconds you will want to get in close and really soak the nest. The chemicals kill nearly instantly, so there is not much risk. After applying chemical death, leave the area so that the fumes can clear.
    7. The returning bees will also die, so leave the nest alone for a few days.
    8. You may wish to remove the nest completely. Pull it down (while still wearing safety equipment) and dispose of in a large plastic bag that can be tied off. If the nest was in an enclosed area such as a ventilation duct or air conditioner port, a long, serrated bread knife may be useful in removal.
    9. Inspect your house regularly, afterward. A small nest is a simple affair to remove - squirt the chemicals and yank it down, while a large one will require a repeat of this whole process.
