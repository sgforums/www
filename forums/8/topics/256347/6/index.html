    ok .. i m no lawyer but i'd try to explain things for anyone who doesnt understand. i'm currently studying in australia so i'm not sure if this applies to sg law, but since sg is considered a commonwealth country with english originated laws, i would expect the parliament setup as well as the judiciary setup to be somewhat similar to that of australia's. If i am incorrect, let me know.

    It is true that Parliament makes law. Judges interpret law. Due to the fact that courtroom time is very precious, sometimes judges have to make assumptions about the law cos it is impossible for laws to fit every case perfectly. ( Rightfully, they can consult Parliament if they are not clear on a certain law) Therefore, it can be said that judges 'make' law as well.

    In every case, there's this part called the 'ratio decidendi'. That's the part the judge has to follow. e.g. previous cases of people being convicted of smuggling drugs faced the death penalty, and that particular case was very similar to the one being heard. The judge hence, HAS to follow the previous case ruling. He is not allowed to make his own decisions UNLESS the decision came from a lower court.

    However, because there are SO MANY cases heard, it is physically impossible for the judge to follow every ruling. It is therefore, the lawyer's duty to point out to the judge previous cases which bore significant rulings to support the lawyer's cause. Clearly, the judge needs to do his own research as well. But lawyers are the one who point out to the judge previous ratio decidendi decisions which support their arguments.
