    There are only 3 issues at stake here:
    1. the behaviour of the teacher and PRC student in the video clip
    2. whether the student should have released the video clip on the Net
    3. whether such recording devices should be allowed in the classroom

    None of us know what really transpired; we only see the teacher scolding the student. We do not know what his standard of work was like, his previous conduct or whether he has a track record for misbehaviour. We do not know whether the teacher has a track record for such outbursts. (I was from RJC and have encountered the teacher; I can safely say she isn't the easiest teacher to work with or stay awake in classes for, but is harmless if you stay in her good books.)

    The whole incident has been sensationalised by the press. They've managed to cast it into a student vs. teacher battle which only polarises the opinion of the public. The ST hit paydirt with this story because it has a lot of capacity for drama and for people to show their worst sides. (Teachers siding with teachers, making hypocritical comments; students siding with students, indignantly defending their right to rebel. Wonderful mudslinging stuff.)

    On the issue of privacy, I guess I have a dangerously biased view. I would agree that whatever happens in front of a whole classroom isn't considered 'private' anymore. On the other hand, posting the clip on the Net is the ultimate form of democratic dissemination. ANYONE can see it. As opposed to, for instance, if the student had just shown it to his friends around school. This is what caused the matter to blow up so spectacularly.

    I'm on the side of The Truth coming out and prevailing, but I can't deny a slight feeling of unease at the way the video clip could spread so fast to so many people. (But you could also argue that if anyone is responsible for throwing the matter wide open to public eyes and violating the teacher's privacy, it's the Straits Times. They printed video captures on page 3, for goodness sakes. I thought that was inexcusable. The teacher was clearly recognisable in those graphics.)

    Finally, on the issue of recording devices in classrooms. There is no need for any policy review by MOE on this matter. It is already adequately covered by the existing policy regarding handphones, beepers and other electronic devices. They are not supposed to be used in classes, even if they're allowed in school. This is a pretty clear-cut rule. A Sony Clie is larger than a handphone. If a teacher is able to spot students illicitly using handphones during class and confiscate the devices, the same can be done for PDAs and camera phones (which are still bloody huge and chunky at this stage of development).

    Public hue and cry is healthy to some extent, and certainly great entertainment, but I think this story has outlived its value. There are a lot more newsworthy school-related dramas to report -- what of the ACS boys who filmed themselves beating up another boy? We haven't heard anything more after the initial microscopic news article on it. They didn't even mention the school name. Don't take everything at face value in our Pacific Area Newspaper of the Year.
