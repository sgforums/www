    Gedanken, I did not say it was all about the recording. In my first point, I mentioned the conduct of the teacher and students involved, and was trying to give some background on the teacher's past record, which I am somewhat acquainted with.

    I do see your point that inappropriate conduct by teachers should be brought to public attention and not condoned by the school administration. As a student myself, I naturally wish for accountability from teachers and find incidents such as the videotaped outburst unacceptable.

    However, the point I wished to highlight was the way the newspapers swooped upon the story, and the way the public has responded. Of course I cheer for the student whenever I read a letter defending his actions, but from some responses, you would think that the student had been expelled while the teacher was given some Best Disciplinarian award.

    On the contrary. The student was 'reprimanded'; the teacher 'counselled'. If you think about it, students are reprimanded daily. It's certainly less severe a 'punishment' than detention, suspension or expulsion. However, to a teacher, even being called to task for such an incident is a major blot upon her record. The fact that Hodge even publicly admitted fault upon the teacher's part stunned me. This has been unheard of previously.

    I'm not saying that the teacher should have been let off without a word, nor that the student should have been punished severely. I had no great love for the teacher in question during my years at RJ and frequently found her overbearing, long-winded and resistant to change. However, I also knew her to be honest, hardworking and willing to admit error and apologise for it. I think that, without full knowledge of all the facts of the case, the public shouldn't be making such strident judgements. Stop cruficying the teacher (and the students).
