    to sugarfix:

    I think there is two separate issues here. One of the behaviour of the woman who managed to get this story onto the papers. And another of the behaviour of the shop who put up a discriminating sign at their shopfront.

    The unreasonable behaviour of the woman that you witnessed did not make what the shop did more acceptable. She may have exaggerated the story and publicised it - but if the result is to make people, who were before unaware of all the other illustrations of discrimination that you stated in your post, suddenly become aware of it - then I think the publicity is a good thing.

    Many people do not know anyone who are wheelchair bound/or disabled/or require special needs - and thus, tend to be oblivous to the inconveniences that disrupt the lives of their families and them. If the publicity given to this topic (that MAY give 'free' publicity to the offending shop), can generate more thought, more concern, more awareness - then it is a start.

    Activism to correct discrimination doesn't need to have a 'proper' start just like the onset of what you called 'righteous' indignation doesn't have to be hypocrisy. Singapore doesn't have a strong history of activism for human rights, and there is no sophistication about the reaction when the community is suddenly 'shocked' into actually seeing something they never realised had always been there.

    Boycotting the one shop over a sensationalised story may seem appallingly childish - but if it is the start of public consciousness - then I think less condescension over it may just make the world a better place.

    Actually, I did not know about this incident reaching the papers until my friend who had witnessed the incident with me told me about it.
