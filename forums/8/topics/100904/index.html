    35 staffers laid off, but take it in stride

    SDC retrenchment exercise goes off smoothly

    Wednesday Â• November 3, 2004

    Lee U-Wen
    u-wen@newstoday.com.sg

    BOXES of facial tissues were kept handy and a trained counsellor was on standby. However, the tears and emotional outbursts that were expected never came, much to the relief of Singapore Discovery Centre general manager Jordan Woo.

    Yesterday morning, he broke the news to 35 of his 80-strong staff that they had been retrenched. He deemed the move "inevitable" due to the centre's year-long closure for renovations, which began on Monday.

    The centre is a not-for-profit organisation since its inception in November 1996.

    Col (NS) Woo said: "We won't be collecting any revenue for quite some time and, since January, we had given strong indications during meetings with the staff that we would have to downsize certain jobs. I feel for them, because this affects a person's livelihood. But I'm pleasantly surprised that they took it in their stride in a mature and professional manner."

    The move came just after the Ministry of Manpower announced that retrenchments in Singapore had eased in the third quarter of this year, marking a fifth consecutive quarterly decline amid an improving job market.

    Most of those affected were front-line workers, such as maintenance engineers and ticketing staff from the operations and facilities-management departments.

    Rumours of a major retrenchment exercise had been floating around since the start of the year, as the centre counted down the days to its $25-million overhaul. The exercise, said Col (NS) Woo, will save the centre around $1 million a year in salaries.

    As part of its $500,000 severance package, those who have served three years or more were offered 0.75 month's pay for each year of service, in addition to a pro-rated annual wage supplement.

    The 13 staff who had less than three years of service did not receive a payout but were offered the pro-rated AWS. All 35 were also entitled to exchange outstanding leave for cash.

    In addition, the centre had arranged for the retrenched workers to attend a one-day course on resume-writing and interview skills. Col (NS) Woo also said the centre was working with the Association of Singapore Attractions to identify suitable jobs for those retrenched.

    Facilities specialist Budiman bin Said, 30, one of those retrenched, said he knew it was "only a matter of when" before the announcement came. He said he had already lined up another job at a printing firm.

    The centre's marketing communications director, Mr Charlie Tay, said there was a possibility that those retrenched would be welcomed back to the new centre when it reopens in December next year.

    "It would depend on our needs and requirements. The new exhibits are going to be high-tech and interactive. So, we need people who are IT-savvy," he said.

    After they gathered their belongings and said their goodbyes, staff walked out of the Upper Jurong Road attraction for the final time into a waiting taxi.

    The centre had arranged for each of them to be given a complimentary ride to a destination of their choice.

    Mdm Lilian Tan, 42, an operations host with the centre for eight years, was sad to leave her friends, but put on a brave front. She will start hunting for another job after taking a short break.

    "I don't want to leave, but I have no choice. Colleagues and management have treated me very well here. And if the centre wants me next year, I'll be happy to come back."
