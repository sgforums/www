    Don't barge into my dorm room!

    By Wu Peining

    HE WAS snoozing peacefully in his hostel room bed when an earsplitting noise woke him.

    A hall officer was in the room with a handyman, who was drilling a hole in the wall to put up a No Smoking sign.

    'I was absolutely disgusted by the way they just unlocked my door, disregarding my privacy,' said Mr Aaron Yong, a third-year mass communications student at Nanyang Technological University (NTU).
    Hall staff often enter rooms without prior warning, say NTU dorm students angered by the invasion of their privacy. Students say they deserve to be treated like adults and with more respect. -- WANG HUIFEN

    A similar incident happened again recently.

    He was working on his laptop when a hall staff member walked into his room to paste a sticker reminding residents to switch off lights and fans when not in use.

    'She didn't even knock,' said the 24-year-old.

    Some dorm students at NTU are upset that hall officers keep barging into their rooms.

    Some 15 students said they and their friends have had this happen to them.

    On the other hand, dorm students at the National University of Singapore (NUS), the other local university with on-campus accommodation, did not seem to have this problem.

    Out of 15 NUS students interviewed, all said they had no reason to complain about the hall officers and staff.

    NUS said that its dorm staff would usually knock on the door and wait for it to be opened by the resident.

    Dorm residents are also told in advance if maintenance work has to be done in the rooms.

    And if the resident is not in, hall staff would leave a message to explain why they had to go into the room.

    Apparently, the situation in NTU is quite different.

    Mr Steve Choo, 25, a recent NTU graduate, said he was sleeping when a hall officer came in to check on the condition of the furniture.

    'He didn't knock or apologise - he just walked out again,' he said. 'They should leave us a note to inform us of such checks. When I stayed in a Scottish hostel during an exchange programme, they would inform us and even tell us the timing of the checks. That is only polite.'

    Another NTU student, who only wanted to be known as Mary, said she and her friends had experienced hall staff who entered their rooms without warning too.

    'What if you're in the midst of changing clothes?'

    NTU's rules and regulations for hall residents say that hall office staff have the right to inspect the rooms at all times.

    A spokesman said that with a 9,000 population on campus, there was a need to have checks 'for the security and well-being of our students'.

    The university said it makes random checks only when alerted that students are ill, when it receives complaints that students are living in the halls illegally or if there are other safety and security issues such as students cooking or smoking inside the rooms.

    The spokesman said: 'Our staff officers are required to follow standard procedures when they conduct random checks.

    'For example, a female staff must be present when checks on female rooms are conducted. All our officers are also required to knock and wait for a response before they open the doors with their keys.'

    But Mr Yong said: 'Sometimes, we can be sleeping so soundly that we won't hear their knocks. The officers have schedules to meet, but from the student's point of view, all he knows is that his privacy has been intruded.'

    Mary's friends have even brought the matter up to the hall administration, but said their complaints were brushed off.

    The students interviewed said they were not against hall staff checking on students, but asked that they be treated with more respect.

    Mary said: 'We are all adults, so I think they should treat us as adults.'

    Mr Choo echoed her sentiments.

    'Don't treat us like secondary school students, but more like normal residents living in an HDB estate.'
