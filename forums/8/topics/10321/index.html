    Scientists find brain has special love spots

    Researchers say areas of the brain which trigger emotions linked to romantic love are different from those linked to negative emotions like fear

    LONDON -- Brain scientists think they know what love is, or at least which parts of the brain produce the emotion.

    Researchers Andreas Bartels and Seymour Zeki of University College, London, examined 17 students who said they were ""truly and madly in love''.

    While undergoing brain scans, the subjects were shown pictures of friends and the objects of their affection.

    Four small areas of their brains lit up when they looked at the pictures of their loved ones, the researchers reported last week at a meeting of the Society for Neuroscience in New Orleans.

    All the areas lie within zones associated with pleasure.

    ""Earlier studies of emotions, which were mostly on negative ones, such as fear, anger and sadness, showed activity in other parts of the brain that did not overlap with the ones we found activated for romantic love,'' said Mr Bartels.

    ""This suggests that there is a functional specialisation in the brain for different emotional states.''

    The researchers also found clues to why love can make it hard for people to think straight.

    The students' brain scans showed there were also parts of the brain that became less active during feelings of love.

    Those areas all involved the performance of higher mental functions, such as memory and attention.

    With these areas of the brain tranquilised, the hallmark of the lovestruck state emerged: a dreamlike mood that rendered such simple actions as getting off at the right bus stop or putting the instant coffee in the cupboard, rather than the fridge, more complex. --Washington Post
