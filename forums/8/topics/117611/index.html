    Design
    The Viewsonic monitor is black with chrome highlights. The bezel is 17mm wide all around the monitor, which is great for users looking to deploy these units side-by-side with dual display adaptors.

    Features
    ViewSonic has been able to keep such a small bezel because the five control buttons are all embedded into the base of the unit which is integral to the monitor and cannot be removed. It can however be flipped up behind the monitor to allow wall mounting. The rear of the unit has the four standard wall mounting threads covered by removable rubber plugs. There is only one port on this monitor, it is a standard IEC power socket. The video signal comes in from a two-metre video cable and 15-pin plug. The unit's base is surprisingly sturdy, it would be very hard to tip this monitor over.

    The stand offers a good deal of fore and aft movement, but there is no swivel capability nor is the base removable. There is also a Kensington security lock port at the back of the unit.

    Performance
    When the ViewSonic VE510 was first connected to the host PC it went through an automatic setup which resulted in a very impressive configuration needing little tweaking to improve it.

    The video playback was very impressive with excellent depth of field and contrast ratio. The blacks were very black while not affecting the overall image. There was virtually little if any tearing however we did notice some pixellation in the fast scenes.

    Overall the ViewSonic is quite an attractive monitor and performs quite well, but again it has no DVI input, no swivel base, and no speakers.

    The integrated cable could also be a headache for some, however the slim bezel makes this monitor ideal for users of systems with multiple displays. The base is also not removable however it does fold up to allow for wall mounting.

    a few specifications to take note of:-

    1) dot/ pixel pitch - the spacing between pixels (the basic building block that form images), the lower the pixel pitch, the better.
    2) vertical and horizontal viewing angle - commonly specs are 170 degrees both horizontal and vertial. the easier way to explain this is when you look at the images in front of the LCD, and walk slightly to the left or right and view the images from the screen again. if you can see them even at an extreme angle to the left or right, the LCD has passed this spec.
    3) dead pixels - always get the sales assistant to test and operate the unit which you are going to bring home after buying. look out for that distinct (usually , but not limited to - red in color) dead pixel. reject and ask for a new set for testing if you see even one dead pixel - get what is worth for your money.
    4) response time (in milliseconds, mSec) - the lower the better. BenQ has some LCD models that runs at 8 mSec (i reckon it was 19"?) Though even LCDs with slower seek time( up to 25 mSec) has been tested for no lag in performance playing DVD movies, it should still be a good consideration to get LCDs with faster response time for gaming.
    5) Contrast ratio (x:1). in theory, the higher the x value, the better and higher image quality your display can give. sometime back, the 17" legionaire from samsung launched something like 700:1 contrast ratio but that figure has far been matched by other manufacturers.
    6) burning hours. although not a big debate since most LCDs comes with say 50,000 running hours (MTBF). so in theory again, if you switch on the LCD 8 hours a day, 365 days a year, it can serve you a good 16 - 17 years to come.
    7) signal input. some LCDs aome with solely VGA (the 3 rows 15 pins I/O) connection but DVI(I or D) as an addition/ alternate connection for image signal input is getting common now.
    power consumption and sound output. though not that inportant since LCDs are borned energy savers in a long run (and cost too!) and are rated at consumption rates of 45watts to 60 watts. Sound outputs of LCDs are commonly not that shaking althoug most will prefer external speakers/ subwoofer connections.
