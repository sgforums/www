    One giant leap for conspiracy theorists?
    By Edwin Yeo

    THE other night, I watched this documentary which asked the second most important question in our modern history.

    Which is, did we really land on the moon?

    The documentary presented evidence which suggested that we, err, might not have landed on the moon.

    It's the latest in conspiracy theories, hot on the heels of allegations made in The Da Vinci Code and the most important one which The New Paper exposed - did all the Singapore Idol contestants really meet the judges?

    Those two conspiracies I'll talk about another time, because right now, I'm really bugged about the documentary.

    I mean, I was there when Neil Armstrong took a big step for mankind in 1969, and that has always been an image etched in my mind.

    When I said I was there, I meant I was, like the rest of the human race, watching it on TV.

    When I said that I was watching it on TV, I actually meant that I was sucking on my milk bottle while my dad went: 'He's on the moon! He's on the moon!'

    I just went: 'Burp.'

    In any case, it taught my parents that, if we put our minds to it, we can make babies burp while watching TV.

    The documentary asked: How was it possible that the American flag planted on the moon could be flying in the wind, seeing that THERE IS NO WIND, OR ANY OTHER TYPE OF AIR CURRENT, ON THE MOON.

    What's even more shocking is that no-one thought of it in the last 35 years.

    Or maybe they were like me, wondering why Neil Armstrong would want to plant a flag on the moon when he could be doing other fun stuff like jumping 50m into the air and shouting: 'Able to leap tall buildings in a single bound!'

    The one-hour show also said that all the photos taken on the moon had shadows which indicated multiple light sources, whereas on the moon, there should be only one light source - the sun.

    With these and other evidence, the documentary suggested that the Apollo missions never happened and everything we have seen or heard about moon landings was just one great big hoax by Nasa.

    I've always been a bit suspicious about Nasa.

    You take a look at their control centres for space missions.

    At any given time, there must be at least two million people seated in the control centre during launch.

    I mean, in an air traffic control room, there are, what, five people keeping an eye on arriving and departing planes. The other eye is either reading the Da Vinci Code or watching Singapore Idol.

    Why does Houston need two million people for a space launch? Most of them don't do anything more than just clap after lift-off, or in Apollo 13's case, when Tom Hanks gets out of the space pod after landing in the sea.

    One must admit that the documentary makes a compelling case, and it just might be that the moon remains an unconquered territory.

    Khoo Swee Chiow, remain calm.
