    To provide a balanced view of your obviously skewed opinion, there are many graduates who work tirelessly to earn their keep, and continue to stay relevant in their respective fields.

    The current market has changed. Hard work no longer guarantees job security. While the younger generation of grads may consider themselves more 'mobile' compared to middle-aged and earlier forties staff, there is still a need to continuously 'prove' yourself.

    This is especially so in the private sector, in industries such as aerospace, manufacturing waffer fabs and pharmaceutical. It's not enough to deliver on your product, one has to deliver faster, better and cheaper products annually to demanding customers who expect price cuts and discounts.

    Graduate positions in such industries have a high attrition rate because the pressure-cooking environment is simply not worth the pay companies are offering. Managers can make or break their future. Knowing exactly what your boss needs can play a key role in career advancement. A wrong second-guess can snuff out your future plans. Boot-licking is discouraged and only value-added services are demanded.

    Yes, a degree opens many more doors than others, but ultimately, it is simply a passport to your new career, just like a diploma and an NITEC cert. The pendulum can easily swing the other way and those that underperform will surely face the axe.

    To single out graduates for this kind of behaviour is as naive as the current cold war between polytechnic and JC students over which institution of learning is 'better'.
