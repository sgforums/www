    FYI
    MOBILE phone giant Nokia Oyj plans to launch 20 new handphone models worldwide by H1 this year. The Finnish firm also wants to boost its marketshare from 37 per cent to 40 per cent globally 'in the long term' mainly by pushing multimedia messaging through 3G networks.

    Nokia derived 26 per cent of its 31.2 billion euro revenues from the Asia-Pacific region (including Japan) last year. It is targeting worldwide sales growth of about 15 per cent this year.

    The company currently has a 35 per cent marketshare in mobile networks. 'We expect the first commercial launch of the WCDMA (wideband code-division multiple access) network to happen in Europe followed by Asia, hopefully in H2, but it is operator-dependent,' said Kari Ahola, Nokia's Britain-based senior vice-president of customer operations.

    'It is timely since the new handsets using this technology will be available in H2 this year,' he said.

    Third generation - or 3G - has yet to take off in most parts of the world. 'We understand there are a lot of questions surrounding 3G,' he said. 'So early last year, we commissioned a global study on the opportunities in new multimedia services. It was conducted by research firm HPI (High Performance Internet) Re search Group.'

    The study polled users in the 16-45 age group in 11 key markets including Singapore, and found that 91 per cent of the respondents were excited about technology developments in mobile communications.

    Singapore, Brazil, and South Korea ranked the highest with 95 per cent excitement levels. About 60 per cent of the respondents said a 3G handset was worth paying more for.

    The most popular application on mobile phones was still SMS (short message service); as high as 81 per cent said they want SMS to be available in future handsets as well.

    The new 3G multimedia messaging format - which allows text, audio and video content to be transmitted - was what interested 75 per cent of all users polled.

    Singapore was seen as a 'fertile market', with 41 per cent of the population already using messaging on mobile phones; and 83 per cent indicated they were likely to use multimedia messaging when it was available.
