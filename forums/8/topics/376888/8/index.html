    Whoa...Deadstroke, this is a good question.

    According to my friend, there are different grades of Tamahagane (about 3-4). To my knowledge, Tamahagane is a category of steels made from especially pure iron ore in powder form found in certain parts of Japan. This natural powder ore is low in sulphur and phosphorus content and other impurities that naturally make iron very brittle.

    To make the steel, the ore is further purified and treated using a furnance, traditionally, a Tatara furnace is used. The steel making process will take about 4-5 days straight and the workers usually go without or little sleep. Of course, how long the process takes exactly will depends on the orders of the master who can judge when the steel is ready by the roar of the furnance fire, color of the flames and by knocking on the furnace wall (will produce a certain sound to indicate the steel is ready).

    And this is the interesting part, the lumps of steel obtained will be called Tamahagane. However, high-grade Tamahagane used in the production of nihonto (Japanese-made swords) will be silverish. Grey lumps are rejected as lesser-grades. The Chinese have also learnt how to make Tamahagane from the Japanese.

    Now as for the manufacturing part and heat-treatment of Tamahagane, this part is really up to the blacksmiths. They can vary it as much as they like, they can choose to fold it or not to, it all depends on their fancy. However, of course, certain actions might weaken even the highest grade Tamahagane steels.

    Hope you understand what I'm trying to say. In short, Tamahagane is just a type of steel, with a few grades. How people choose to use it differs from person to person.

    Damascus steel will have rather prominent 'fold-like' patterns from the blending of steels during the forging process. By the way, thats a very beautiful knife in the link you provided. XD

    And yes, you're right to say that the raw Tamahagane lumps that come out of the furnace have to be re-heated and hammered into shape then heat treated before it can become something useful.

    Folding isn't necessary. But it's a real shame if you do not fold it, cause repeated folding and forging will cause the carbon composition in the entire steel block to become uniform and also remove some carbon-content and other impurities so the end-result steel wont be too brittle.
