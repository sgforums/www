    Haha...anyway that's why in the end, all sound systems(ie speakers) still need this thing call DAC(digital-to-analog converters) to smoothen the 'bar graphs' into 'line graph', since speakers can only read and play line graphs of music(ie music is recorded with point A, point B and point C; when the speakers 'move' frm point A to C, they of course have to between the points in the course of the 'journey', so hence the need to convert digital to analog.

    Essentially, there's no such thing as 'digital' music. Digital technology is useful because it is more efficient in recording, easier to transfer from source to source with no loss of quality, and helps to prolong the shelf life of the recording.

    This is unlike cassette tapes, where the method is simply to record it into analog format directly, and hence the magnetism in the tapes will change over time, resulting in loss of quality over time. Any duplication also means that they have to 'record' the recording again, resulting in loss of quality(Digital duplication, on the other hand, is just as simple as duplicating the writings of 1, 2, 3 on a piece of a paper on the other with the same 1, 2 ,3.
