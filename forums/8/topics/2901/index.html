    I shall split this discussion into 2 parts.
    First, the educational system in Singapore.
    Second, the title chase.

    The educational system in Singapore focuses heavily on paper chase. From the beginning of the educational ladder, the kids have been stuffed with infomation to memorise; any attempt to dare think differently is met with scornful rebuke from the teachers. How many times have we heard "If you want to do well, you better listen to me"? Did the eduactional system ever give the students the space to be creative? How many times students got slammed for having unconventional ideas? And how many times did the teachers give them the chance to express their opinion freely? And I'm not even mentioning the word "encourage".
    Reminds me of a word, "concept". Many can memorise definitions, but how many understood ?
    Instead of spending more time explaining the concept to students, many teachers encouraged the students to "practise more" on tutorials that are largely based on ten-year series.
    Does that line "once you get the hang of it" sounds familiar?
    Predictable. That's our system.
    In this sort of environment, how many dare to be different? Different in their approach in solving the problem. How many dared to risk their future, their career when the term "paper chase" keeps lingering in their minds? Imagine yourself crashing at one of the major exams? The system is not so forgiving. There are many cases of Singaporeans topping overseas universities but yet not even eligible for our local ones. Why?
    If your teacher tells you that your method of solving the problem is wrong, "because your method is different from mine", how are we going to develop new and better techniques?
    People fear to take risk as the psychological blow was dealt early in their life. It makes people unwilling to take risks, and you can see this situation in everyday life.
    The future generation of teachers grew up in such an environment, and their teaching style would most probably remain, predictable.
    Education seems to be a homogenous product, lacking in variety.
    This environment points towards the failure of cultivating any entrepreneurs.

    On my second point, I'm referring to people who prefers to do in depth research in their own field. Their ultimate goal is gain fame and recognition from their fellow peers in their own field. These people devote their lives to their own field, and it's their own interest and choice to take this career path. They have little interest in entrepreneurship, hence you seldom find them in CEO positions.

    A point to add, what fadingstar said is true. Scholars that are bounded would find their inital enthusiasm for entrepreneurship waning by the time they completed their bond. How many dare to risk their stable career jobs when they are in their thirties and have a family to look after? How conducive is the environment that we're living in for the entrepreneurship spirit to grow?

    I don't know, but I find that the idea of "teaching students to be creative" hard to buy. Comments?

    ocean - in reply to your question abt whether it's possible to "teach students to be creative", i would say yes and no. but mostly "no" because the kind of imaginative thinking i'm thinking of cannot be taught in classrooms. there is a mini-industry out there right now devoted to teaching ppl in both the public and private sectors how to "think creatively". i've attended two such seminars. one was by Edward de Bono himself. these guys will, of course, tell you that you can cultivate or learn certain techniques so you can "think out of the box". and to a certain extent, they are useful tools. leaving aside the question of how often you're actually ever going to use these techniques (whether in meetings, discussions or in your own personal time and endeavours), i think one thing good abt these courses is that they reveal (and often drive home the point) how one-track minded we've all been and are capable of being. we then have a better appreciation of the genius of true ingenuity, imaginative flair and unwavering determination. but these approaches to "creativity" are quick-fix solutions. for over-the-hill managers trying to appear contemporary. and, more charitably, to get ppl out of the rut of thinking too narrowly, since they were brought up that way in the first place.

    but what i mean by educating our students to be more imaginative requires much more time and patience. and can only be achieved if you start from young and then provide the requisite environment for their learning and exploration. infact it should be "educating our young to be more imaginative" and not just our students cos i think the family plays a role too. ideally, i think this should be the situation that singaporeans growing up should find themselves in. they should, firstly, be exposed to as wide a variety of ideas and influences as possible. singaporeans often live too sheltered lives and have a lack of real-life experiences from which to learn. this increased exposure is not only to teach the child vital skills of social interaction or to kindle the interests of the child, but more importantly, to increase the likelihood that he will get in touch with and find something he appreciates, enjoys and hopefully develops a passion for. too often, ppl grow up without a single passion or dream or yearning that they can truly call their own. ask ppl what they want to be when they grow up, and the majority will tell you, if they are candid, that they don't really know. that they will go out there and "see how". all this is well and good. and in fact prob necessary. but i think the sooner you discover what you really like and what you're good at, the more likely you're going to pursue that dream, and maybe to be more entrepreneurial. this is not to say that you can't be entrepreneurial at a more mature age. but in many cases i believe, if you ain't got it in you to begin with, you ain't gonna have it later on either, esp not in your later years when security is often more impt than experimentation and adventure. but if you've always had that dream....then it's a different thing. but it has to be a dream coupled with the ability to see it through. so the first thing is to expand the scope of possible influences and experiences so that ppl will have a grter chance of finding themselves.

    secondly, whichever field a person ultimately chooses, there should be adequate pathways to realise that interest and that passion. guidance and mentorship are crucial here. but what is prob most impt is to develop a sense of inquisitiveness and wonder and constant questioning and experimentation within the field of interest. at the institutional or national level, the best u can do is prob to supply the systems and infrastructure to nurture that talent or interest. whatever it is, this stage of development should aim at independence of thought and a love for learning (abt everything, not just your own textbooks). there is no place for apathy and inaction if you want a nation of innovators and entrepreneurs.

    and finally (prob the toughest thing to do) what is needed is to change the mind-sets of the ppl. i think that if u want to encourage imaginative and entrepreneurial thinking, not only do ppl have to be more open to the ideas and life-styles of other ppl, but also to the fields in which these ideas occur. that is, value should be placed on ideas that go beyond the economic or scientific or the technological. to me, it is silly to say i want to encourage u to be innovative but only if it has some economic value to the nation. the notion of "value" has to be cast in a wider sense. perhaps s'pore has too small a population base for this. and so that is why our initiatives have to take on the form of "singapore inc.". but the essential message should be: "worth" is not reducible to economic value only. this kind of thinking will free ppl from the evaluations they feel threatened by if they were to do something different from everyone else. and creativity and innovation often come from the courage to be different. a truly creative person can be so in any discipline or field of interest.

    but maybe i'm talking here not just of entrepreneurial potential but of being the best that we, as singaporeans, can be. i think if u encourage ppl to pursue their dreams and their passions, then everything else will follow - including encouraging the entrepreneurial. my only lament is that the way we are proceeding now, we may end up with a notion of "worth" and "value" that is too market-based and market-driven. even if we succeed in making a name for ourselves in this arena, i fear that it will be to the detriment of other (non-economic) aspects of life and to our definition of what would be considered worthwhile pursuits. and these other aspects can't be resolved by simply bringing in more arty-farty plays and musicals or, worse still, buying entire sports teams and other personalities from overseas and making them PRs just so we can be "number one" by some specified date in the next millennium. you have to start off, i believe, by encouraging a culture of respect towards the interests and talents of all our citizens, whichever field they may be inclined towards and find themselves excelling in.

    hmmm....hope i haven't mixed up too many concepts and ideas. do let me know if i have.

    I've to agree with you that we should start nurturing the kids from young. The encouragement to be bold and creative is important, slamming them for unconventional ideas will not only kill their interest, but also the child in them. After all, kids are supposed to be inquistive.
    On your point of "to develop a sense of inquisitiveness and wonder and constant questioning and experimentation within the field of interest", I think I should correct it a little.
    I think it's important to still keep a child's mind when we're researching into our field of interest, 'cos once we get into our "adult" stage, our interest would have waned; the inquisitiveness and will to question would have been lost, and this will most probably result in stagnation of our progress. Hence, to "develop" should be corrected to "keep". As long as we remain as children in our fields, we'll continue to probe and wonder.

    On your point on "mind-sets of people", I would say that people are realistic. If there's no job opportunity for their interest, they would have to look after their rice bowls first. After all, survival comes before interest. If you've nothing to eat, your body has no energy to function, how are you going to pursue your interest? Hence, job opportunities have to be created for the different fields for people to pursue their interest, else we're back to square one again.

    We've digressed a little from the topic, but we're getting a little closer to the root of the problem. Entrepreneurship is a specialised field itself, and it's certainly not something that can be taught on textbooks. To put boundaries on it would simply kill the spirit.
