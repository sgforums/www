    SMS hoax: Police issue warning
    A MESSAGE asking people to avoid Holland Village because a bomb was found there spread via text messages on mobile phones.

    'Police take a very stern view against anyone who capitalises on the recent concerns over the security situation to attempt to cause public alarm or fear by making bomb hoaxes. We will spare no effort to track down persons responsible for creating hoaxes and will press for deterrent sentences when they are charged in court.'
    - Police statement

    But yesterday, the police said it was a hoax, and warned that those found guilty of transmitting false messages under the Telecommunication Act can be fined up to $50,000 or jailed up to seven years, or both.

    In a statement, the police said no bomb was discovered. There had also not been any other security incidents reported at Holland Village, which is popular with expatriates and Singaporeans who flock to the shops and restaurants there.

    As part of recent moves to enhance security, some parking lots there were removed, and parts of two roads are closed to motorists from 6.30 pm to 4 am every night.

    'Police take a very stern view against anyone who capitalises on the recent concerns over the security situation to attempt to cause public alarm or fear by making bomb hoaxes,' the statement said.

    'We will spare no effort to track down persons responsible for creating hoaxes and will press for deterrent sentences when they are charged in court.'

    Many SMS (short message service) messages about the bomb scare circulated yesterday, and some people interviewed said they were shocked and upset to get them.

    Mr Isaac Tan, 25, a civil servant, received seven messages between noon and 3 pm.

    He said: 'I was intending to go there tonight but changed my mind as I thought it was better to be safe than sorry. It's really terrible as it's so easy to create mass hysteria this way.'

    Ms Adrienne Tan, 40, an editor, received one message from an unrecognised number. She said: 'It's quite scary to get something like that on SMS. People really shouldn't spread rumours like this.'

    For writer Wong Phin Wei, 24, who lives near there, the bomb hoax came a little too close to home.

    He said: 'I was very upset as I hang out there almost every day.'

    But the police reminded people that while they should be vigilant, there was no need to overreact and they should go about their daily lives as usual.

    'The public should avoid unfounded speculation and not spread rumours,' they said.
