    I confess that I have not read all the longish replies to this thread so, I might be retreading old ground.

    Should we have capital punishment?
    Firstly, we have to establish why we need to punish:
    i) Incapacitation: to deprive the offender the capacity to harm others.
    ii) Reformation: deterring the offender from future violations, or improving his character so that he no longer gives in to the temptations of committing such violations.
    iii) Deterrence: of someone other than the offender.
    iv) Retribution: Nuff said.

    Hobbes pointed to a state of nature in which life of man is ' solitary, poor, nasty, brutish and short'. As such, the drive of self preservation will motivate the community nto use4 all means to protect themselves if one of them becomes a threat to the community , which would entail the banishment or death of the offending individual.
    By repudiating the discipline of the group by words or actions, he has placed himself outside the 'pale of the law' and have thus lost the immunities that membership in a group confeered upon him, including the immunity from physical harm by other members of the group.
    People accept the legal sanctions against wrong-doers becos they accpet the discipline and authority of the Govt. bec of the protection they believe it is able to afford themaginst the others who wopuld depive them of their property, liberty and lives.

    Many of the people have advocated the retributive 'lex talionis' as their standard view of murderers and rapists. By this I mean that Punishment should be PROPORTIONAL and not IDENTICAL. 'Rapists should be raped'. *hiak* where'd you get guys like these?

    I have to agree that I tend to favour a more hardline approach of sanctioning with extreme prejudice. Why?
    Immanuel Kant maintained that from a moral point of view, punishment is essentailly retributive. He made an example that if the world were to come to an end, and it was therefore evident that no one would benefit from the punishment of prisoners, who have been sentenced to death, those prisoners would nevertheless still be executed in the interest of righting the balance of justice.

    Kant believed in the punishemnt of the guilty is good in itself, regardless of the benefits that may flow from it. In his view, it was the nature of crime committed that determined the type and severity of the punishment that was to be imposed.

    No man should ever be used as a means to another person's end but should always be treated as an end in himself. Therefore, punishing one man in norder to deter another potential offender for committing a similar crime in the future would be to use the person who is being punished.

    Punishement for the sake of reform would have to rest upon the assumption that the punishing authority knows more about what is in the best moral and spiritual interests of the person being punished than he does himself. This is an imposition by the authority of an image of the offender and denying the offender the right to his own moral choices.

    I have spoken to friends about the human rights issues of Prisoners. The thing is, if the Human Rights of life, liberty and pursuit of Happiness is encoded into the American Constituion, then how can Texas, amogst other states still permit Capital punishment?
    The thing is, the utilitarian argument of public interest comes up. So they're in fact still within boundaries.

    Still the issues of whether Prisoners have rights are still a very sticky situation. If they do, what rights and how much?
