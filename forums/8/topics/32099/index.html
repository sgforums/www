    Infertility hits one in five couples in Singapore

    By Liang Hwee Ting

    ONE in five married couples in Singapore finds it difficult to conceive, said a Singapore expert on fertility, revealing the extent of the problem here for the first time.

    Topics on infertility

    The 20 per cent figure given by Dr Christopher Chen shows that Singapore is inching into the higher brackets of infertility rates recorded in many developed countries, where the range is between 15 per cent and 25 per cent.

    This rising figure was estimated from the records of various Singapore doctors.

    It correlates with the latest government figures, which show that the fertility rate - the average number of children born to a woman - was at an all-time low of 1.42 in 2001, way below the 2.1 needed for a population to replace itself.

    But young couples are not just wringing their hands in exasperation, indicates Dr Chen, who heads Gleneagles Hospital's IVF Centre.

    Determined to avoid childless marriages, many are doing what was inconceivable even three years ago, and seeking tests to assess fertility before getting hitched.

    Dr Chen's centre sees about three such couples a month. 'A couple of years ago, we did not see couples requesting pre-marital fertility assessment, but this has started happening over the last two years, especially the last year,' he said.

    This new attitude is seen at Singapore General Hospital (SGH) too, said Dr Yu Su Ling, who heads the hospital's obstetrics and gynaecology department.

    Such tests can help rescue a problematic situation early, especially among the blissfully ignorant.

    Some couples, said Dr Chen, spend the first two years of marriage using contraception.

    When they stop, they spend another two years trying, in vain, to have a baby.

    'So a good four to five years are wasted before they seek help,' he said.

    One person who wants to avoid such heartache is accountant Marian Chan, 30, who went to see a uro-gynaecologist with her fiance recently.

    'We've got married friends who cannot conceive for years and it's putting a strain on their relationships.

    'If we know early, at least we can seek treatment.'

    Research in recent years has shown that infertility is as much a man's problem as it is a woman's.

    More men are producing sperm of abnormal shape and size, which is adding to the problem of low sperm count.

    In women, abnormal egg production and fibroid growth, among other factors, are inhibiting pregnancy.

    The problem is compounded by the higher stress of urban living and the tendency to be married later.

    In Singapore, most couples start trying for babies only in their 30s, which is six years after the most fertile period in a woman's childbearing years.

    KK Women's and Children's Hospital, one of three public hospitals here with IVF centres, sees 30 to 35 new infertility cases each week.

    At National University Hospital and SGH, the corresponding figures are 30 and 20 a week.

    Births fell from 47,000 in 1997, to 41,775 in 2001. For the first six months of last year, there were only 19,712 births.

    Falling fertility rates will result in a population problem for Singapore, said population expert Saw Swee Hock.
