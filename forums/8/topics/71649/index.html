    By Arti Mulchand

    ALL 560 staff members of the Singapore Zoological Gardens, Night Safari and Jurong Bird Park have been offered a 'voluntary retrenchment scheme'.

    Parent company Wildlife Reserves announced this to staff two weeks ago. The move is to 'eliminate areas of duplication that have arisen over the years' in both operations and management, the group's deputy chief executive officer Ho Yew Kee said in a statement to The Straits Times.

    The three parks owned by Temasek Holdings were integrated in August 2000.

    A company spokesman said that some staff members have already been laid off. Security, for example, was outsourced since Monday, he said.

    Under the scheme, those who have worked for more than three years have been offered a month's salary for every year served, if they volunteer to retire, with the payout capped at 25 months.

    Those who have been with the company for less than three years get half a month's pay for each year served.

    Although it is an opt-in policy, the company can refuse a staff member whom they don't want to go. No cut-off date has been set for opting in.

    The company will not comment on the number they want to reduce staff strength by, or how many staffers have already taken up the scheme.

    For full report, see Thursday's edition of The Straits Times.

    Zoo staff offered early retirement
    Voluntary scheme, which covers Night Safari and BirdPark employees as well, aims to eliminate 'areas of duplication'

    By Arti Mulchand

    ALL staff of the Singapore Zoological Gardens, Night Safari and Jurong BirdPark have been offered a 'voluntary retrenchment scheme'.

    Parent company Wildlife Reserves announced this to its 560 staff two weeks ago. More than half work for the zoo.

    The move is to 'eliminate areas of duplication that have arisen over the years' in operations and management, deputy chief executive officer Ho Yew Kee said in a statement.

    He added: 'Regrettably, while many of these personnel could be redeployed, redundancies were inevitable.'

    Some have already been laid off. Security, for example, was outsourced on Monday, said a spokesman.

    Those who have worked for more than three years have been offered a month's salary for every year served, capped at 25 months; those with less than three years' service, half a month's pay for each year.

    It is an opt-in policy, though the company can refuse an employee whom it wants to retain. No cut-off date has been set for opting in.

    The company will not say how many staff it wants to cut, or how many have opted in.

    One staff member, who said several co-workers were considering it, said: 'If they've done many years, it's worth it...With all the management changes, the spirit is gone.'

    Wildlife Reserves has seen many changes at the helm since the three parks owned by Temasek Holdings were integrated in August 2000.

    Its first CEO, zoo veteran Bernard Harrison, left after serving less than two years. About 15 others, many in senior positions, followed suit.

    Justifying the integration after the management exodus, its chairman, former health permanent secretary Kwa Soon Bee, said the parks 'need to be of a certain size to compete with new attractions coming up in the region'.

    Mr Harrison's successor, Mr Asad Shiraz, served 13 months and left in December last year. A month later, Mr Robert Kwan, who brought McDonald's into Singapore, took over from Dr Kwa as executive chairman.

    Then, Wildlife Reserves had said he would take 'a very active role in the management ... of the organisation'.

    Since integration, the group has reaped savings through sharing of resources, buying of supplies and services, and joint marketing. The graphics, IT and education departments have merged. News reports say the company has an annual turnover of $60 million.

    Despite the payout, some staff say they will not leave until they are shown the door.

    Said an animal keeper: 'I wouldn't leave for money. I want to stay for the animals. They are the most important thing.'
