    Heng@,

    If by your definition, we sell our humanity when we do not set vary the degree of punishment for crimes of different severity, then we are not selling our humanity by giving drug traffickers a death sentence. As we already know, our judicial system awards light or harsh punishments to criminals depending on what they have done. A minor gets a lighter sentence than an adult. Robbers get jail; Molesters get the rotan; Drug traffickers and murderers get death. The system is also built upon the concept that a harsher sentence deters the potential criminal, hence the capital punishment for drug traffickers.

    We may compare awarding the death penalty to drug traffickers and doing the same to murderers. A person who snuffs out the life of an innocent knowing full well that it is wrong but does so for money to repay a debt is considered a murderer. The same goes to one who commits the same crime for hate. They deserve the death penalty. A person who imports substances very likely to end or ruin totally the life of several others, and who knows the consequences of his actions is no different from the two mentioned above. Both committed a wrongful act of a severity that warrants capital punishment because they were not ignorant of the results of what they have done.

    Being detached is a necessary quality for a decision maker when it comes to decisions which carry with it a major impact on the people affected by it. In this case, the impact falls on the criminal about the receive the death sentence no doubt. But there is also the impact the decision has on society, or more precisely, current and potential drug abusers. A detached person would be able to judge and balance the options he has with regard to the case at hand and decide that ultimately, society is of the highest priority. Should all our decision makers lose this quality, they would lose their objectivity and illogical decisions would eb the result. What would become of our society?

    Logic cannot be force fed into educated intellectuals with a good sense of judgement, by the way.

    There are also problems arising from the case by case basis of judging drug traffickers:

    1)It will be very difficult to judge the extent of how "good" or "bad" a criminal is in court. There will always be gray areas in every case. In fact, it would be safe to say that in court, there is no perfect proof of exactly how "bad" or how repentant the trafficker is.

    2) Drug kingpins may use the wealth accumulated from his trade to hire expensive but very competent legal teams who are capable of enabling him to exploit loopholes in the law or gray areas in the case to escape the gallows. Runners, being the poorer ones, will not be able to afford the expenses of a good lawyer and might not be able to successfully argue his case. Double standards will arise and the might become unfair. Who knows, the kingpin may even force a runner to take the blame(and thus also the death sentence) for him.

    3) Kingpins who are not awarded the death penalty may continue to run his network from his cell. He may even call upon his pawns to exact revenge on the people who brought him to justice.

    People are forewarned of the penalties of commiting crime and are taught the consequences of their actions. Drug trafficking is a crime of a severity that well exceeds the need for capital punishment. Thus, the mandatory death sentence is justified.
