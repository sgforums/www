    i think the reason we dread exams so much is cos we've been brought up to answer exam questions in a certain way, i.e., cramming as much info as we can within the time permitted. and teachers have been trained to expect these kinds of answers too. but the way to pick a good essay from a bad one is not necessarily based on how much information and material one has crammed into one's answers. often what is impt is whether the student has actually answered a question. this may sound obvious but there are actually several ways of answering (or not answering) a question.

    those who don't know what on earth they are doing, write everything down (C grade staring you in the face here), and expect the marker to sift out the relevant from irrelevant material. many s'poreans do this. then there are those who answer the question briefly, perhaps too briefly, and do not appreciate the complexities of the problem (these ppl can actually get Bs, so if you see ppl walking out of the exam hall early and still getting Bs, they are prob busy answering the question instead of writing a whole lot of irrelevant stuff). and then there are those that answer to the point, and from the way they write, you can tell that they not only have a command of the basic concepts/theories and how to apply them but know the limitations of their positions and can suggest how to move beyond them. all this of cuz presupposes you really know what you're doing. a lot of reading and understanding of the subject is required. but that doesn't mean a lot of writing has to be done in the exam hall. you can do credibly well just answering to the point and doing it in such a way that shows you're pretty knowledgeable abt the subject. writing style matters here. some ppl are really good at suggesting (in their essays) that they know the issues involved when really they don't. if you can convince the examiner you know more than you actually do (99.99% of students are in the exam hall trying to do precisely this. actually to the extent that they actually succeed, exams also train us to be good at marketing - i.e., playing up the positive and downplaying the negative in a way that makes us look good - a skill that is useful not only in the workplace but in life more generally), then you're well on your way to a good grade.

    having said all the crap that i have, besides testing whether the examinee knows what on earth he/she has been taught the past semester, exams also test other qualities like the ability to write in a concise and lucid manner, the ability to understand and answer a question in a relevant manner, maybe even the ability to handle stress, amongst other things. and besides putting less emphasis on the final exams (like reducing the percentage that these exams actually contribute towards the overall grade), more emphasis needs, i believe, to be placed on changing our mindsets towards what is expected of us in exams. if the exams are meant to test our ingenuity or creativity, then a different kind of stress will be placed on us. but one, perhaps, that is more "fun" and enjoyable (yes, i actually know ppl who enjoy exams. they enjoy it cos that's where they can show off that they're better than the rest).

    if not for the problem of the lack of man-power and stuff like that, i actually favour some form of oral examination. phd dissertations require a "public" defense of your thesis from members of a dissertation committee and from other interested parties (usu ppl who are abt to go thru a defense themselves or other ppl from the dept who have nothing else better to do). in a face-to-face scenario, it's harder to bluff your way thru. plus oral exams test how well you speak and deliver your points (this is crucial to how well you perform at work) and how well you can think on your feet (which is good for any number of things, including explaining to your gf why you forgot her birthday). in other words, oral exams test whether you really know your stuff and whether you can communicate what you really know. now that'll be stressful wouldn't it?
