    Internet worm hits 188,000 systems worldwide
    By Ho Ka Wei

    AN INTERNET worm called 'Blaster' or 'LoveSan' has spread across the world over the past two days, infecting and crashing thousands of computers, including some in Singapore.

    The worm, first noticed in the United States on Monday, specifically targets computers using Microsoft operating systems such as Windows XP and Windows 2000.

    It has infected some 188,000 systems worldwide, said the Internet security company Symantec.

    Its regional marketing manager Teresa Wong told The Straits Times that the Asia Pacific accounts for some 15 per cent of incidents.

    Microsoft says although the worm crashes some systems and infects others, it otherwise does no damage.

    But it's not over: Blaster installs instructions in infected computers for an attack at midnight on Aug 16 on Microsoft's Windows Update website - the same site that offers a patch that users should download as protection.

    Internet service providers here - SingNet, Pacific Internet and StarHub - said they received hundreds of calls from affected customers.

    Two universities here also reported problems. Nanyang Technological University's computer centre received about 200 calls on Tuesday - twice the usual number - with 30 more students complaining about infected laptops, said a spokesman.

    Users said their PCs rebooted and could not be used for normal purposes.

    Three servers were also hit. NTU's spokesman said the system has been fixed.

    At the National University of Singapore, about 400 computers on campus were snared by the Blaster, but its servers were not affected.

    Other institutions here, such as banks, had it better, with both DBS and OCBC reporting an all-clear.

    Symantec's Ms Wong said most reports about the worm came from consumers, rather than corporations.

    Though serious, the Blaster worm is not as virulent as previous worldwide viruses, and does not destroy files.
