    Reference is made to your thread on your court appearance on a litigation suit.

    Perhaps, I could shed some light.

    In the event of being sued for non payment of debt, unless it is in a small claims tribunal, the plaintiff's solicitor would have sent you a letter of demand to settle the claims.

    Afterwhich, if there were no response from you, a writ of summons will be issued against you.

    At this stage, you may either defend by an affidavit(detailing your defence) or if you did not file one, the plaintiff can take out a summary judgement against you. In Singapore, it is called : Order 14. That implied that since you have no defence, you have no case and judgement is taken against you unless you appeal.

    In the event that you had filed a defence by affidavit which must be witnessed by a Commisioner of Oath, your case would hve been studied by a judge or usually a Registrar or a Magistrate to see if the defence is valid in law. There will be what the legal fraternity calls an "exchange of documents." Meaning both you and the plaintiff will recieve each other's claims and defence and the judge would have studied all this.

    By virtue of you appearing in court, it meant that the judge has already studied the case and would have come to the conclusion of the verdict by virtue of reading your filed defence papers. That meant had aready given you a hearing.

    Should you have anymore points to add, that should have been taken during the exchange of documents where it was all transparent.

    By virtue of going to court after submitting all your defense and said that the judge did not hear the whole story will imply that you did not tell the whole story in the affidavit. And therefore, it is also implied that you have not been truthful in the affidavit. Worse, by your posting here, you are also implying that the judge did not give you a fair hearing. On both counts, you are dangerously close to commiting a crime called perjury and contempt of court both of which can be custodial offence.

    That being the case, when the judge refuse to listen to your verbal representation, she is probably helping you to escape inconsistencies in your verbal representation and the summitted affidavit.

    Of course, if this matter is adjudicated in a small claims tribunal, then the above mentioned process and procedure is not applicable.
