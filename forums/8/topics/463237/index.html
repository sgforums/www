    MERIDA (MEXICO) -"Doomsday hour" arrived yesterday around much of the globe, but the world kept on turning.

       According to legend, the ancient Mayan calendar ended its 5,125-year cycle at midnight on Thursday, ushering in the end of the world.

       It did not happen, of course.

       No one was sure at what time the Mayas' 13th Baktun would officially end yesterday. Some think it already ended at midnight Thursday. Others looked to yesterday's dawn in the Maya heartland.

       The Maya, who invented an amazingly accurate calendar almost 2,000 years ago, measured time in 394-year periods known as baktuns. Some anthropologists believe the 13th baktun ended yesterday, on Dec 21. Still, archaeologists have uncovered Mayan glyphs that refer to dates far in the future, long beyond Dec 21.

       In Merida, Mexico, the celebration of the cosmic dawn opened inauspiciously with a ceremony to honour the conclusion of the latest Mayan calendar cycle. Despite all the rituals and banter, few here actually believed that the world would end yesterday - the summit was scheduled to run through tomorrow. Instead, participants said they were here to celebrate the birth of a new age.

       Dr Bill Leith, the US Geological Survey's Senior Science Adviser for earthquake and geologic hazards, said that by late Thursday, absolutely nothing out of the ordinary had been detected in seismic activities, solar flares, volcanos or the Earth's geomagnetic field.

       "It's a fairly unremarkable day on planet Earth today and in the last few days," Dr Leith said. "There are no major eruptions going on."

       There had been about 120 small earthquakes and a moderate temblor in Japan, which Dr Leith said was "very much a normal day".

       Others said that doomsday has yet to come. "Wait until the dawn on the 22nd; that is when we Maya will speak," Nobel Peace Prize laureate Rigoberta Menchu said earlier in Guatemala, another Maya area.

       As yesterday's dawn began sweeping around the globe, there was no sign of an apocalypse.

       Indeed, the social network Imgur posted photos of clocks turning midnight in the Asia-Pacific region with messages such as: "The world has not ended. Sincerely, New Zealand." AP
