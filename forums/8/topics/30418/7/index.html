    Y'know, maties, come to think of it, with all the comments that most guys (and a couple of ladies I reckon) have given, I think it wasn't the learning how to kill an enemy in 20 different ways that really daunted any of us. Of course, let's not forget the countless hours under the sun nor the weight of the person we had to carry on our shoulders and running God knows how many miles.

    What I learnt from serving NS was being able to appreciate the simple things in life. Someone mentioned about NS having low pay. But I'd have to say I appreciated the fact that I was being given something rather than nothing. The poor living conditions in the field made me appreciate my bed at home or in the barracks. The poor food I was given both in the cookhouse and the field made me appreciate the fine food I get as a civilian. What I remember most was how that little piece of chocolate was such a treat.

    Today, after having gone through all that, I know that if things don't work out, I can easily switch to the times when simple food was fine dining. When a canvas sheet is adequate as a roof over my head. Mate, the list goes on and on.

    NS is just like university today if you asked me. For most of us, it's not what you learnt in uni that matters these days. It's the tools that you manage to gain that matters. Of course, what you study does matter what job you will get, but what about general degrees such as history and politics? People who did those courses have gained researching tools, methods of analysis, argument and writing skills. Aren't those skills found in Engineering and Medicine as well? It's the same with NS. It prepares you to be a tough civilian able to take on the world.
