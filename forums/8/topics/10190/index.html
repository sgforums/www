Speakers' Corner: Has it run out of steam?

  • no heart
    12 Nov 00, 23:07
    THE initial excitement is gone, the crowds have dwindled. These days, Speakers' Corner in Chinatown, seems desolate as it plays host to only a few die-hards.

    Last week, there were no more than three names up on the nearby police post listed to speak on any one day. The crowd was sparse, the ice-cream seller -- who used to be a regular -- was not there either.

    Gone too are the students who livened up the Corner in its first few days in September.

    But this has not flagged the spirits of the speakers who are still there.

    Former Colombo Plan scholar Tan Kim Chuang made his 20th appearance on Friday and told The Sunday Times that he would continue making his regular Friday and Sunday appearances.

    ""I don't think we can expect too much here. In the spirit of S21, we have to be active citizens and speak up on the issues," said the senior vice-president of a stockbroking firm.

    ""I am here to speak my mind and I have a rapport with the people. If there is no one here, I will talk to the grass."

    As Mr Tan began speaking on the jailing of landlords, the Central Provident Fund and S21 among other things, a crowd of around 10 people began to surround him.

    It was a far cry from the numbers that milled around him in the early days.

    When the corner opened on Sept 1, there were more than 20 speakers registered.

    On Friday, three -- including Mr Tan -- were registered initially and a fourth registered to speak at around 6 pm.

    So what has happened to the Corner?

    Administration assistant Mavis Choo, 26, passes Hong Lim Park after work and said she has seen a visible drop in the numbers of both speakers and their audience.

    She said: ""Maybe people are bored listening to the same thing every day. Even the ice-cream man that used to be here in the evenings doesn't come by so often."

    ""I think when people see cameras and reporters around, it attracts a crowd. But since there is not much media attention these days, maybe there is less interest."

    Miss Choo said when the media seems interested in listening to a speaker, passers-by will stop and listen as they feel that the person has something interesting to say.

    Mr Ong, who declined to give his full name, who claims to be a regular, said: "Whatever the response, I hope this place does not close. Singaporeans should come and at least listen because this definitely better than shopping or going out."
