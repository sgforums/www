    OCT 10, 2003

    Education system a paradox

    WHEN I listed down some observations about the education system, a puzzling picture emerged:

    The standard of English is falling.

    The standard of Chinese is also falling, perhaps even more drastically.

    The end product of the education system seems to be individuals who are apathetic and incapable of thinking independently.

    A handful of outstanding schools that are very well endowed seems to be producing the bulk of the brightest students.

    The O- and A-level results seem to be improving from year to year.

    More students make it to university.

    From what I know, local university students shy away from science and engineering studies. Most of them seem to dislike mathematics by the time they get to university.

    What are we to make of these observations, assuming they are valid? To me, they seem to suggest that the education system is putting students - and their families - through a lot of hardship and stress, only to become end products that we do not desire.

    Rather than a piecemeal approach addressing just one or two issues, perhaps it is time to examine the whole system and ask strategic questions, like what is the vision of the education system and how we can get there.

    WONG WENG FAI
