    Where do 'sick' hard disks go?
    TECH MAD By Ian Tan
    tanyhi@sph.com.sg

    EVER wondered what a hospital for computer data would look like?

    Just drop by the CBL Data Recovery Technologies office in Changi during its visiting hours.

    During my visit last week, the lab was filled with dozens of computer hard disks laid out on tables, thick cables running all over them like life-support systems.

    In one corner was a hard disk, stripped bare and laid under the powerful lenses of a microscope which would discover damaging scratches invisible to the naked eye.

    In another corner, red warning lamps flashed as a computer probed the deepest depths of a hard disk that had given up the ghost.

    Computers are now so deeply integrated into all parts of society, any data that suddenly goes missing due to hard drive accidents can wreak serious havoc.

    Hence, it is no surprise that many of the customers who arrive at CBL's doorstep are usually in severe panic.

    Said Mr Samuel David, who runs the CBL office here: 'For one group of IT engineers, they said that if they did not get back the data from their office server, it could mean their jobs.

    'Another couple actually flew over from Indonesia so that they could recover their wedding photos and videos from their hard drive.'

    According to Mr David, 36, his job not only demands technical expertise, but he also has to play counsellor to frantic clients.

    'I have to wade through the anxiety and find out exactly what happened to the computer first.'

    Data recovery firms like CBL are now becoming more important, not just to businesses, but to consumers as digital devices like digicams, personal digital assistants and laptops proliferate in the home.

    Yes, you may one day find your photographs on the digicam missing if the memory card becomes corrupted.

    Apart from CBL, Adroit Systems and Ibas Singapore offer similar data rescue services.

    These firms usually charge rates from $200 to several thousands of dollars, depending on the complexity and urgency of the job.

    They also boast facilities like clean rooms where dust particles are filtered out so hard drives can be opened up without contamination.

    Mr David got started in the business after he experienced a major hard disk disaster in 2002 and sought the services of the CBL office in San Jose, US.

    Being trained in the design of hard disks himself, his interest in data recovery was piqued and he ended up setting up CBL's 11th global branch here.

    Said Mr David: 'I started work from my HDB home in Tampines and started receiving calls from day one. After a few months, we had so many calls that I needed more office space.'

    Now, he works from a JTC office in Changi Business Park and sees about 60 cases a month, with clients from big businesses or just 'your average mum and pop'.

    The science of data recovery is a unique one.

    Interestingly, the damaged storage media (hard drives, digicam storage cards, magnetic tape storage and so on) is usually never operated upon.

    'We make a clone of the drive and work on the duplicate drive,' said Mr David.

    'This ensures that we never open up the client's drive and hence create problems with manufacturer's warranty.'

    Using CBL's proprietary software, Mr David can extract data from hard drives and CDs where commercially available recovery software have long given up.

    'Depending on the conditions, we can still recover data if the hard disk has been formatted (wiped clean) five times.

    'However, if there has been a deep physical scratch on the hard disk, the data is lost for good.'

    And, in true spirit of globalisation, other CBL branch offices get into the act in difficult cases.

    Said Mr David: 'If I am not in the office, another CBL employee in China can work on the damaged drive remotely over a broadband connection.'

    'This way, we are constantly sharing knowledge and expertise.'

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------
    Wipe out your hard disk

    If data can be so easily recovered by experts, how can you ensure that your private data can be permanently deleted?

    According to Mr Anthony Lee, chief executive officer of data recovery firm Ibas Singapore, there are several ways to go about it.

    Use special software to clean out hard drives permanently

    'We use a software called ExpertEraser which meets standards set by bodies like the US Department of Defence,' said Mr Lee.

    'To use it, the hard drive must be in 100 per cent working condition with no bad sectors. It costs about $28 to delete each hard drive and the process will take a couple of hours.

    Degauss it

    Degaussing the hard disk costs about $120 per hard disk.

    'By placing the hard drive on a device that generates a strong magnetic field, all the data in the magnetic media will be destroyed in a few seconds. This method works for any magnetic media like cassette or video tapes too.

    'But if you degauss a hard disk, you can never use it again, unlike using the ExpertEraser.'

    Destroy it

    Of course, the cheapest way would be to smash your hard disk to smithereens.
