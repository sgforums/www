    LOS ANGELES -- Scour.com, the once high-flying competitor of the popular Napster music website, came to a legal end on Wednesday when a Los Angeles bankruptcy judge approved a auction sale of the site's assets.

    Scour was one of a handful of site that created a sensation last year when it offered offered users the ability to easily swap digital music and video files easily over the Internet. Along with Napster, Scour was seen as a threat to the record industry.

    A consortium of record companies are suing Scour and Napster in federal court for copyright infringement. Legal fees and a shortfall in advertiser revenues led to Scour's fall.

    Scour's assets, which included file swapping technology and a database of 4.5 million registered users, were sold for US$9 million in cash and stock to CenterSpan Communications Corp, an Oregon-based Internet software company.

    "We are happy and satisfied our creditors will be paid and that the process has allowed Scour to maximize the value of its assets," Scour president Dan Rodrigues said in a statement.

    Scour said that it expects to be dropped from the pending copyright infringement litigation.
