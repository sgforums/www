    Why raise more funds when target exceeded?

    I WAS pleased that the Ren Ci Charity Show raised $6.2 million when it was televised live on Jan 8, exceeding the stated goal of $5 million. It is good to have a reserve which the charity could tap should there be an economic downturn.

    However, in the week following the live telecast, there were still commercials urging people to donate. The final sum raised of $7.26 million is 45 per cent more than the stated goal ('Charity show raises $7.26m'; ST, Jan 1. Was it necessary to continue to solicit donations when the target had been reached?

    In the wake of the National Kidney Foundation (NKF) saga, is it wise to allow charities to continue to solicit for funds after their targets had been exceeded?

    A cap on the percentage by which a charity can exceed its stated fund-raising target is necessary to prevent any charity from accumulating excessive reserves, and also to give smaller charities a better chance of raising funds.

    One of the lessons from the NKF saga is the need for transparency, but there must also be regulations on how much a charity can raise over its stated target. It would not serve the public interest if bigger and more organised charities are allowed to accumulate big reserves while smaller ones struggle.

    Liang Thong Huat
