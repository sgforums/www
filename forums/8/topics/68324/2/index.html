    Hmm. I went from undergrad all the way through postgrad in Australia. Some of my observations:

    As has been said before, as far as the student is concerned, the university is as good as the faculty in which the student is studying. For example, I did my first four years at Swinburne, which is a relatively new university - I actually went there because of its Media department but decided that I preferred Psychology by the end of my first year. I decided to go over to Melbourne University for my doctorate, mainly because of its international reputation in Psych research. While I was there, I tutored Honours students who were completing their theses, and I was struck by their lack of knowledge in research methods, something that was drummed into us at Swinburne. For many of these students, I had to take them all the way back to first-year basics and go from there - my classmates at that level, on the other hand, pretty much knew everything they were supposed to by the time they started planning their theses. The problem with Melbourne, I found, was that they built their reputation on taking the best research students, so the material they had to start with gave them an advantage in the first place. The support they provided for postgrad researchers was first-rate, but I wouldn't recommend their undergraduate program.

    To some extent, I would agree with Rob's point, although I personally would not be as vehement about it - sorry, Rob! There are a lot of really shonky universities out there, and not just in Oz.

    What I would recommend is that you ask around in the field that you intend to study - if the university has a good reputation within the field, you're off to a good start. Also, like I said, some universities are good for undergrad and others are good for postgrad - make sure you make the distinction between the two when you're checking them out.
