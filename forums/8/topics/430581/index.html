    LONDON: A blood test that can show how fast someone is ageing will go on sale in Britain later this year, British newspapers reported yesterday.

    The test, which also offers the alluring possibility of estimating how long the person has left to live, will cost £435 (S$880).

    The new technology measures vital structures on the tips of a person's chromosomes, called telomeres, which scientists believe are one of the most important and accurate indicators of the speed at which a person is ageing, The Independent reported.

    Some studies have shown that individuals with shorter-than- normal telomeres are likely to die younger than those with longer telomeres.

    The scientist who developed the test, Dr Maria Blasco of the Spanish National Cancer Research Centre in Madrid, said: 'We know that people who are born with shorter telomeres than normal also have a shorter lifespan. But we don't know whether longer telomeres are going to give you a longer lifespan.'

    Dr Blasco said the test is simple, fast and very precise.

    'We can detect very small differences in telomere length and it is a very simple and fast technique where many samples can be analysed at the same time. Most importantly, we are able to determine the presence of dangerous telomeres - those that are very short,' she said.

    The over-the-counter test can show whether a person's 'biological age', as measured by the length of his telomeres, is older or younger than his actual chronological age, The Independent reported. The results also provide vital insights into a range of age-related disorders, from cardiovascular disease to Alzheimer's and cancer.

    Medical researchers believe that telomere testing will become widespread within the next five or 10 years, but there are already some who question its value and whether there should be stronger ethical controls over its wider use.

    Critics believe that the new technology opens a Pandora's box, whereby insurance companies may demand tests before offering someone a policy, the Daily Mail said. Some are also worried that such testing may be hijacked by organisations or individuals trying to peddle fake elixirs of life, the report added.

    But the test cannot yet predict the exact number of months and years a person has to live.

    Telomere research is considered to be one of the most exciting areas in biomedical science. The three scientists who shared the Nobel Prize in medicine last year are pioneers in the field, The Independent said.

    Dr Blasco's company, Life Length, is not the only company selling telomere tests but is the only one gearing up for over- the-counter sales, according to Professor Jerry Shay of the University of Texas Southwestern Medical Centre.

    Asked why the public would be interested in taking a telomere test, Prof Shay said: 'I think people are just basically curious about their own mortality.'
