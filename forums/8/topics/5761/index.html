    Just a starter for discussion. Basic argument for anyone doing philosophy of religion 101. It can be proven either way of course but i would like to ONLY hear your philosophical comments.

    Premise 1. If there is a God, He must be morally good, omnipotent and omniscient.

    Premise 2. There is evil and suffering in this world.

    Premise 3. If He is omniscient, He KNOWS there is suffering and evil.

    Premise 4. If He is omnipotent, He HAS THE ABILITY end evil and suffering.

    Premise 5. If He is morally good, He WOULD wish to end evil and suffering.

    Premise 6. If He could stop it and would stop it BUT doesn't know about it - He is not omniscient.

    Premise 7. If He knows about it and wishes to stop it but can't - He is not omnipotent.

    Premise 8. If He knows about it and could stop it but chooses not to - He is not morally good.

    Conclusion: Therefore no such God exists.

    edit. correction as someone pointed out: Premise 6 should read 'omniscient' and NOT 'ominpotent' as I earlier wrote. cheers

    I should like to object to a number of difficulties with what is being claimed in this thread.

    1.) It is not true that IF there is a God THEN he MUST be omnipotent, omniscient and omnibenevolent. It is just that in most traditional theistic conceptions, He does indeed have these properties.

    2.) There is no LOGICAL contradiction in the conjoint claims that God is omnipotent, omniscient and omnibenevolent.

    3.) The Free Will Defence (FWD) is logically compatible with the three central theistic tenets and does explain moral evil. It presupposes that a world whereby humans have free will is better than one where they do not.

    4.) God's omniscience would suggest that he knows at the time of creation (and before) those who will and will not be saved. There is no test. (This claim is consistent with Reformed/Calvinist theology). Consider the formulation of God's omnniscience in modal logic, that is: necessarily(if A, then God knows A). It should be reasonably clear what follows from this.

    5.) Augustine suggested that evil was the privation of good. (POG) This presupposes a standard of goodness and this is determined by God. Augustines resolution of the problem of evil employing both FWD and POG is widely considered an adequate response by theists and atheists alike in response.

    6.) If you consider this an adequate disproof (which it plainly is not) of the existence of God, I would refer you to Plantinga's modal formulation of the Ontological Argument. It is an extremely strong and hitherto unrefuted proof FOR the existence of God. I imagine it is available in any decent library.

    According to Plantinga's OA, it establishes a world where there is a being with Â‘maximal greatnessÂ’. Unfortunately (as Plantinga himself admits) this argument does not establish that there is a God in the actual world. It establishes that there is something with maximal greatness. But being maximally great does not mean having the attributes traditionally ascribed to God.

    Plantinga therefore introduces the notion of Â‘maximal excellenceÂ’, which he thinks of as a possible property connected with maximal greatness.

    Quote
    Â“The property has maximal greatness entails the property has maximal excellence in every possible world. Maximal excellence entails omnipotence, omniscience and moral perfection.Â’
    Unquote

    Now, maximal greatness is possibly exemplified. There is a possible world where there is a being who is maximally great. Hence, in any possible world this being has maximal excellence. And it follows from this that our world there is a being who has maximal excellence, which is to say that there is actually a God whose existence follows from his essence and who can thus be thought to exist in reality by reasoning that counts as a form of the OA.

    This of course leads us away from the Problem of Evil and the moral and logical complications and contradicts I wanted to elicit from my initial post. The OA brings about another whole set of critique which non-philosophy trained people here might find hard to contribute to.
