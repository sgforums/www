    It was raining As at the junior colleges when the A-level results were announced on Wednesday.

    A bumper crop of 1,364 students -- 12 per cent of the A-level cohort -- scored As for their four A-level subjects and passed their General Paper, up from 1,228 from the year before.

    At top colleges such as Hwa Chong and Raffles, four to five out of 10 students scored four As. In fact, those who scored three As, and there were plenty of them as well, were seen crying when talking to their parents over the mobile phone.

    Neighbourhood JCs such as Pioneer and Yishun, produced their share of students who scored four As too.

    Ministry of Education figures show that A-level students just keep doing better in the examination.

    For the last five years, about 10 per cent of A-level students scored four As. This year, the figure moved up to 12 per cent.

    It is the same story for those scoring three As. Last year, 2,432 or 20 per cent of the A-level cohort scored three As. This year, it went up to 2,442, about 22 per cent.

    At Hwa Chong and Raffles for example, three out of every five students scored at least three As. The deluge of students with multiple As begged the question: Are the A-levels getting easier or are students here getting smarter?

    Schools pooh-poohed the notion that the A-levels were becoming too easy for students. They pointed out that more complex questions have started appearing in the A-level papers in the run up to the new examinations which students will sit for at the end of this year.

    The ministry is changing the A-level papers to reflect the new emphasis on thinking skills, creativity and independent learning.
