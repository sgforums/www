    It is said that shortly after Sir Stamford Raffles founded Singapore in 1819, he discovered the presence of two warring factions in the island. One was held by the Sultan while the others looked up to the Tengku Temenggong. As a result, there were frequent outbreaks of violence between the two groups - with the aggressors being the followers of the Temenggong.

    As a result, Raffles considered it too dangerous for the Settlement to have the Temenggong living in it. Consequently, Raffles asked the Temenggong to move to a further place with his followeres and promised to provide the expenses for shifting, including some compensation.

    In 1819, the Johor-Riau Empire was broken into the mainland Johor, controlled by the Temenggong, and the Sultanate of Riau-Lingga, controlled by the Bugis. This is when the history of modern Johor began. In 1855, under the terms of a treaty between the British in Singapore and Sultan Ali of Johor, the control over the State was formally ceded to Dato' Temenggong Daing Ibrahim, with the exception of the Kesang area (Muar), which was finally handed over in 1877. Temenggong Ibrahim opened up Bandar Tanjung Puteri (later to become Johor's present-day capital) in south Johor as a major town.

    Temenggong Ibrahim was succeeded by his son, Dato' Temenggong Abu Bakar, who later took the title Seri Maharaja Johor. In 1866, he was formally crowned the Sultan of Johor. Sultan Abu Bakar of Johor (1864 - 1895) was the one who gave Johor its own constitution and developed its efficient administration system and constructed the Istana Besar, the official residence of the Sultan. Due to these achievements, Sultan Abu Bakar is known by the title "Father of Modern Johor".
