    PENNSYLVANIA: An American whose daughter downloaded apps for games on his iPhone and charged them to his credit card can proceed with a class-action suit against Apple for compensation of up to US$5 million (S$6.3 million).

     

    This could open the door for legal actions worldwide.

     

    This month, a California judge said the case could go to trial. Class action means other parents in the same position stand to get compensation if Pennsylvania attorney Garen Meguerian wins the case.

     

    He sued Apple in October last year after his nine-year-old daughter downloaded US$200 worth of what Apple calls in-app purchases for 'free' games, including Zombie Cafe and Treasure Story.

     

    Users can play for free, but must buy virtual goods to progress in the game.

     

    Mr Meguerian accused Apple of unfairly targeting children with such 'bait apps', according to The Register, a British technology news site.

     

    There are numerous free gaming apps, but many are designed to promote in-app purchases of virtual items - such as ammunition, groceries and cash - which are intended to help the user play the game with greater success.

     

    The court case quotes a description of the game Smurfs' Village: 'Players are lured in by enticing pictures of huge bucketfuls of Smurfberries, and just a couple of taps is all it takes to drain money out of an iPhone account holder's credit card.'

     

    Just as the iPhone is a global phenomenon, so too is parental unhappiness with the unexpected charges showing up on their credit cards.

     

    The Smurfs' Village app - Smurfberries retail at US$4.99 for 50 or US$59 for 1,000 - has come under criticism by the United States Federal Trade Commission as well as Australian regulators.

     

    For its part, Apple said parents who do not want their children to make in-app purchases should not give their children their iTunes passwords.

     

    As is the case with many electronic purchases, Apple's iTunes payment system allows users to enter their credit card details once and then simply key in a password to authorise future payments.

     

    In calling for the case to be dismissed, Apple also pointed out that in-app purchasing can now be disabled.

     

    District Judge Edward Davila of the San Jose District Federal Court said that, nevertheless, many parents were misled by Apple's presentation of these 'free-mium' games as free. Apple, he said, did not adequately inform consumers about the potential costs.

     

    In Britain, one parent received a bill for £1,500 (S$3,000), PC Pro magazine reported.

     

    Ms Niamh Bolton said she felt 'physically sick' after learning that her 10-year-old daughter had incurred the charges playing Tap Pet Hotel. The game is free, but generates revenue by selling add-ons that help the user progress.

     

    The bill was 'more than our monthly mortgage repayment', she told the BBC. 'We didn't have that sort of spare cash in the bank account.'

     

    After some difficulty, she said, she was able to contact Apple customer services and get the money refunded.
