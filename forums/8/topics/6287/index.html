    Acne

    What is Acne?

    Acne is the appearance of small to medium sized red spots primarily on the
    face. It usually starts with the onset of puberty, around age 10-12 in
    girls and age 12-14 in boys. Sometimes kids are spared in their teenage
    years, only to have acne flare when they reach their twenties. This is
    particularly common in women.

    The red spots seen with common acne are called "inflammatory papules".
    Often times there is a second type of acne lesion present called the
    "comedo". There are two types of comedones: "open comedones" are
    whiteheads and "closed comedones" are blackheads. The third type of acne
    lesion is the acne cyst, which is a common variant of acne. Cystic acne is
    characterized by multiple hard, red, tender lumps under the skin. It often
    affects the face, but also can be seen on the back and chest, under the
    arms, and in the groin. Comedonal and inflammatory acne can also affect
    the back and chest, and rarely the scalp.

    Acne is a disease of hair follicles. That may sound bizarre, but it is
    very well documented. Most of the time, acne is a disease of vellus hair
    follicles. These are the tiny white hairs everyone has on their face.
    Hair follicles contain tiny structures called sebaceous glands, which
    produce sebum. Sebum is what accounts for the oily nature of the skin of
    many acne patients. Sebaceous glands start to kick into high gear with the
    onset of puberty, and sometimes over burden the hair follicle. Think of
    the hair follicle as a river, and the sebaceous gland sebum as the water in
    that river. When things are moving well, no problems. But when the river
    starts to move faster and the amount of water (sebum) increases rapidly, it
    can overflow its banks and create a problem. Now factor in an additional
    problem: a dam! The opening of the hair follicle is normally wide enough
    to allow the sebum to flow through it without difficulty, even when sebum
    secretion increases. However, in many acne patients the hair follicle
    opening begins to get smaller, acting like a dam in the river. Again, this
    can cause the sebum river to overflow, producing acne.

    The final part of acne evolution is infection. All of the overflowing
    sebum sits under the skin in a tiny balloon-like ball. As it sits, it
    begins to be attacked by Propionobacterium acnes, or P. acnes for short.
    Once the sebum becomes infected, that is when you get the big red acne
    lesion. If the balloon of sebum ruptures, a cyst forms and the infection
    and inflammation spread.

    What causes acne?

    Basically, very few things cause acne. Studies show that nearly 100% of
    teenagers will get some amount of acne. Some get more, some get less, but
    all get some. Poor hygiene, bad washing habits, too much chocolate, lots
    of greasy foods: these are the popular myths about causes of acne, but
    rarely do these cause acne. That is not to say you should stop washing
    your skin, but you don't need to go nuts. In fact, too much washing and
    scrubbing are some of the few things that can actually cause acne. Many
    parents tell their kids that acne is caused by dirty skin. Not usually
    true. The infection component of acne is very deep within the skin, and
    washing the surface will not do much down deep. Just keep your skin clean,
    but don't try to scrub out the infection. You may only make things worse.

    Another cause of acne is excess friction, such as from a football helmet
    chin strap, or holding the telephone against your chin all day. But most
    of the people who get acne get it because that is their hereditary
    predisposition. The more acne your parents had, the more you are likely to
    have.

    Stress is one of the wildcards in causing acne. Most research suggests
    that stress does not directly cause acne, but many of my patients feel
    strongly that stress and their acne are related. What likely happens is
    that patients already have a bit of acne, and the stress induces then to
    pick at their acne lesions, which makes them redder and therefore look
    worse. Hence the standard advice to acne patients: DON'T PICK! For
    patients who are concerned that acne and their stress are related, consider
    visiting a website that deals with this and other issues, http://grossbart.com. While it would be nice if we could make some link
    between acne and stress, there isn't any scientific evidence to do so as of
    yet. That doesn't mean the two won't be linked in the future. Bottom line
    is that we all have stress, and it is tough to predict just what that
    stress does to us. It affects people in different ways, and the temptation
    is there to link stress to our acne. Clearly, stress reduction is good for
    us in general, and if it helps your acne, all the better!

    Some patients, usually women, develop acne because of hormonal causes. A
    consult with your doctor can help to address this issue for you. A new and
    popular treatment for acne is the oral contraceptive aimed at addressing
    this hormonal link with acne. Although a birth control pill (Ortho
    Tricyclen) is approved for use as an acne treatment in women, in my
    experience it is not a magic solution. It can be a nice addition to a well
    planned acne treatment regimen, but alone it is not likely to clear your
    acne. Clearly, you should consult with your family doctor or gynecologist
    before going on the birth control pill, even if it is for your acne.

    Simply washing gently with soap and water is a sufficient daily skin care
    program for most people. If you have very oily skin you may want to try a
    soap made especially for oily skin. But be careful, there are a lot of
    gimmicks on the market! If your skin is not too oily, you may want to use
    a light moisturizer after washing your skin.

    Oily skin is something I get many questions about. It can be almost
    impossible to treat, which is frustrating. Glycolic acid products,
    salicylic acid lotions, special soaps, and astringents can all be tried
    with varying degrees of success. Topical tretinoin (Retin A) and its
    prescription cousins (Differin, Avita) can have some effect, but often not
    enough to make patients happy. Glycolic acid or beta hydroxy acid chemical
    peels can work some, but again are temporary. Blackheads often accompany
    oily skin, and are best treated with topical tretinoin. A new treatment
    for sun-damaged skin that is also effective on blackheads and oily skin is
    called the PowerPeel or DermaPeel. These are performed in doctors offices.

    Can acne be treated?

    The most improtant consideration in acne treatment is patience, and lots of
    it! It will take at least 2-3 months for the acne treatment you are using
    to even begin to work, no matter what it is. I constantly hear from people
    that they used a medicine for a few weeks and it didn't work, and they get
    frustrated. This is a critical phase of acne treatment, because pushing
    ahead often will produce the results the patient is looking for. One of
    our jobs as dermatologists is to educate people so they understand what the
    goals are and why it is important to keep up with the treatment plan even
    if it doesn't appear to be working. It is hard for patients to accept the
    fact that if a medicine isn't working after 3 weeks, that it somehow
    magically will after 8 weeks, but it does happen. The reason is that the
    medicine is actually working at the 3 week mark, but your body just hasn't
    caught up with it and cleared all of the debris left behind by the acne
    lesion itself. After 8-12 weeks, your body and the medicine effectively
    get "in synch" and work together to clear your skin. If by 2-3 months you
    don't see any improvement, then it is very reasonable to ask you doctor to
    change things around.

    Fortunately, we now have many terrific treatments for acne. There are some
    general guidelines that most dermatologist follow in prescribing acne
    treatment. The over-the-counter (OTC) treatments such as benzoyl peroxide
    can be fine for some patients, but that is the exception rather than the
    rule. With acne, it is best to get it under control early rather than let
    it really get going. Rampant or severe acne can lead to scarring in some
    patients (to be discussed later), and it is impossible to predict who will
    get scars. The best treatment for acne scars is to prevent them, and the
    best way to do that is to treat and prevent acne.

    One regimen for treating mild acne is to use a glycolic acid lotion twice a
    day, and benzoyl peroxide 5 or 10% lotion twice a day with the glycolic
    acid. The 5% is just as good as the 10%, in fact a bit better because it
    is less irritating, so try the 5% first. One product that is potentially
    good in this regimen is Neutrogena Healthy Skin with glycolic acid or
    Retinol. Retinol is a precursor of tretinoin, the active ingredient in
    Retin A. It won't work as well as Retin A, but it is a good place to start
    if you can't get to a doctor. You can also add a product that has
    salicylic acid in it as part of this regimen. Naturally, if you don't see
    results after 2 months or so, you should strongly consider a visit to a
    dermatologist. Although many people have home remedies for acne, and would
    prefer not to see a dermatologist, the reality of it is that acne can be
    very stubborn, and the majority of patients would do better with a
    dermatolgist's help than they can on their own. Skin doctors have years of
    training, and our job is to help you clear your acne (as well as to treat
    other skin diseases).

    I get numerous questions about home treatments such as herbal remedies,
    using rubbing alcohol, holistic approaches, taking vitamins, Proactiv
    Solution, and the Igia Clear system. I have never found any of these to
    work for my patients. That is not to say that they can't work, but there
    is no good scientific evidence to say that they will, and in my experience,
    they don't offer much benefit in treating acne. I don't recommend them to
    my patients.

    Some men also experience aggravation of their acne when shaving. I have
    found that a few helpful tips can make this much less of a problem. I
    suggest using a shaving gel instead of a cream, and to use a double edge
    blade razor instead of electric. The one I prefer is the Gillette Sensor
    razor. Also, try to shave in the shower to take advantage of the skin
    softening effects fo the steam. Shave in only one direction on each part
    of the face, the so-called "with the grain" approach. Don't try to shave
    too close because that can cause problems. Also, change the blade after
    every two uses. Can be expensive, but it keeps the blades clean and sharp.

    Small comedonal acne (blackheads and whiteheads) is sometimes effectively
    treated with OTC products such as benzoyl peroxide too. But usually
    comedones are best treated with topical vitamin A based prescription
    preparations such as Retin A, Avita, or Differin. When the comedones
    become infected and inflammatory acne develops, then antibiotics should be
    added by prescription. Topical antibiotics are effective for many
    patients, and these usually include erythromycin or clindamycin. When the
    inflammatory acne is severe or affects the back, scalp or chest, oral
    antibiotics become the treatment of choice for most patients. Common oral
    antibiotics used by physicians include tetracycline, erythromycin,
    minocycline and doxicycline. Many times physicians will use a combination
    of topical and oral prescription medications to treat acne. Please consult
    with your physician if you are using prescription medication for acne, to
    understand the potential interactions of drugs you are taking and their
    possible side effects.

    There are some common mistakes made when treating acne. Topical
    preparations should not be placed solely on the active lesions. Acne
    treatment is preventive, so putting the medicine only on the active lesions
    does nothing to prevent future acne breakouts, and may not help much at
    all. Place the topical medication wherever you tend to get acne, even if
    you don't have it there now. Also, FOLLOW THE DIRECTIONS ON THE
    PRESCRIPTION. You need to use the medicine as your doctor intended you to,
    so read the directions. It usually takes several months for acne to begin
    to improve with treatment. My rule of thumb is 50% improvement every 2
    months, and most patients will see significant changes after 6 months of
    treatment. If you don't see much change after 3-4 months, talk with your
    doctor. Sometimes, acne can even flare within the first few weeks of
    treatment. Again, talk to your doctor if you have concerns.

    For patients with significant or resistant/persistent acne, the oral
    medication Accutane (isotretinoin) can be of significant value. It is a
    highly effective treatment regimen that is taken for 4-6 months under the
    careful supervision of a physician. In some patients, it can even prevent
    the recurrence of acne once the treatment has been discontinued, and in
    some a repeat treatment is required. Accutane has the potential to cause
    some significant side effects, so not only must a physician monitor its
    use, but patients need to have a well-established relationship with their
    doctor, and have a thorough discussion with their doctor before beginning
    Accutane therapy. There is more detailed information about Accutane below.

    What about acne scars?

    First it is important to determine and define what a scar is. A true scar
    is a permanent defect in the skin caused by an injury to the area, such as
    an acne lesion. The key word is "permanent". Most of the acne lesions
    that people call scars are really not scars at all, but something referred
    to as "post inflammatory erythema". In plain English, the red spots left
    after an acne lesion are just that...red spots caused by acne inflammation.
    THESE WILL NEARLY ALWAYS GO AWAY. It may take 6-12 months, but red spots
    after acne will fade. If you want to speed it along, you can try an OTC
    glycolic acid containing product. You may also want to talk with your
    doctor about having glycolic acid chemical peels. This is usually a series
    of 4-6 peels which can significantly help to reduce the appearance of red
    acne lesions. Some people refer to these red lesions as acne "staining",
    but many of my patients feel the glycolic acid peels get their skin looking
    better faster than it would without the peels.

    Dark skinned individuals have a tendency to get brown spots instead of red.
    These can be a bit more stubborn, but they too almost always resolve.
    Glycolic acid works here too, but you can also try adding a bleaching agent
    such as Ambi, Esoterica, or Porcelana. But be careful not to overdo it and
    lighten your skin too much! If you think your red or brown marks are not
    fading, look at your skin very carefully. Usually, it is not that the
    original ones you had didn't fade. They did, but you proabably got new
    spots in the same area that makes it look like you still have the original
    lesions.

    True acne scars are a different matter. These are the subtle and sometimes
    not so subtle depressions in the skin that follow acne lesions in some
    patients. These true scars are also sometimes red, so if you have
    questions consult your physician. If you see a depression in your skin in
    an area where you had some acne, it may be a scar. The big problem for
    physicians is we can't predict which patients will develop scars. Some
    people seem to have mild acne but develop severe scars. Others get severe
    acne and end up with minimal scarring. Again, preventing acne is the best
    way of preventing acne scars. If you have acne scars, then there are
    several treatments available for you. Unfortunately, acne scarring can be
    very difficult to treat effectively and can be costly and time consuming.
    It helps to find a cosmetic dermatologic or plastic surgeon who you can
    develop a working relationship with to have your scars treated because many
    patients need a multi-step approach to therapy. Available treatment
    options include collagen injections, fat transplantation, autologous dermal
    grafting, Fibrel, other filler substances to lift scars, medium to deep
    chemical peels, dermabrasion, and laser resurfacing with the carbon dioxide
    or erbium lasers. Consult your physician for more information on these
    procedures. Be careful about who you choose to actually treat your skin.
    Ask to see before and after photos of their patients, and ask to speak with
    patients who they have treated to get a feel for the doctors skill level.

    There are essentially 3 types of acne scars: icepick, boxcar, and rolling.
    Icepick scars are deep tiny one millimeter holes that must be treated with
    surgical removal. Nothing else works. Boxcar scars look a bit like
    chickenpox scars. These respond well to many treatment modalities,
    including excision (if they are deep), subcision, collagen injections, and
    dermal grafting. They also respond well to laser resurfacing if they are
    not too deep (more info below). Rolling scars give the undulating
    so-called "pock-marked" look to acne scarred skin. These can be tough to
    treat, and require a surgeon who is willing to treat these very carefully.
    I have found subcision, a new procedure for elevating scars, to be
    excellent for rolling scars, and routinely use this as part of a
    comprehensive acne scar treatment plan. Chemical peels, although much
    talked about for true scar treatment, don't work well at all for this.
    They are best used to treat the red and brown marks discussed earlier.

    Lasers are a new addition to the surgeon's war on acne scars. Carbon
    dioxide and erbium lasers are available opitons, and are used to literally
    remove layers of skin until the surgeon gets your skin to the point that it
    is smooth. This is a very involved, expensive, precise, and complex
    surgical procedure for acne scar management. It is a superb tool in the
    right surgeon's hands, but you must be careful to choose your doctor
    carefully. As you can see, there are many treatment options for acne
    scars, and you should ask your doctor which of these s/he performs. If the
    doctor only performs laser surgery and does not have a colleague s/he works
    with for the other surgical procedures (ie. subcision, excision), then be
    careful. Even if the doctor is a superb laser surgeon, results may be
    limited because it is often a comination of the procedures discussed above
    that gives truly superb results. On average, one can expect 40-50%
    improvement in acne scars from laser treatment alone, and the percentage
    improvement can rise significantly when laser therapy is combined with
    other procedures.

    Summary

    As you can see, acne is a multifactorial disease with multiple treatment
    options. Nothing can replace a consultation with your physician, and you
    should be examined if you have any concerns regarding acne or other health
    problems.

    Accutane Information

    Accutane (isotretinoin) is a good drug. It is usually reserved for 2
    types of patients. The first is the patient with cystic or nodular acne.
    The second type of patient is the one with acne tht has been resistent to
    treatment with the other conventional regimens.

    The nice part about Accutane is that some of the patients who are treated
    with it will be cured of their acne forever. Some studies suggest the
    number may be as high as 85%. For most people, their acne will be much
    more manageable after they finish Accutane. The typical treatment course
    is 20 weeks, but I will often push that to 24 weeks if the patient is
    tolerating it well. The ideal dose is 1 mg per kg of body weight. That
    means a 140 pound person (about 60 kg) should have an ideal dose of 60 mg
    per day. I'll usually start them at 20 mg per day to let them get used to
    the medicine, and then increase to 60 mg per day over the first 4 weeks of
    therapy. It takes 2-3 months to begin to see results.

    The main side effect is dry skin, as well as dry eyes, hair, and brittle
    nails. A small number of people complain if hair loss, but this has always
    been reversible in my experience. These symptoms are usually dose related.
    Patients who wear contact lenses can have a particularly tough time because
    of the dry eyes. They use a lot of artificial lubricants, or simply switch
    to wearing glasses. I recommend Aquaphor ointment for the dry lips. A
    good cream moisturizer will help the dry skin. Other side effects to watch
    for are headaches, fatigue, changes in behavior, joint pains (particularly
    if you have broken a bone in the past), and stomach pains. Accutane can
    affect the liver, so it is a good idea to stay away from alcohol while on
    treatment.

    There are two main areas of concern when taking Accutane:

    1. Accutane can cause your Cholesterol and triglycerides to become
    elevated. Your doctor will need to monitor your blood tests on a monthly
    basis to make sure this is not a problem. If you have a particularly
    strong family history of heart or cholesterol problems you may not be a
    candidate for Accutane.

    2. Pregnancy in women is the biggest Accutane issue. If a woman gets
    pregnant on Accutane, she has a very high likelyhood of having a baby with
    birth defects. Only one way to prevent this if you take Accutane...DON'T
    GET PREGNANT!!!! A recent study showed that at least 3% of women get
    pregnant while on Accutane, despite signing forms saying they won't get
    pregnant and getting mountains of information about it. This is the one
    area where the patient needs to take responsibility for her own actions. I
    can't stress enough how important it is to be careful and not get pregnant.
    I'd like to clarify what happens after stopping Accutane. Once a woman has
    comleted 1 (and I tell my patients 2) full menstrual cycles after stopping
    Accutane, she is no longer at risk. What that means is that 2-3 months
    after stopping Accutane, woman have no increased risk of having a baby with
    birth defects other than the risks they would have had in the absence of
    Accutane. Men are a different story. Because of the way Accutane works,
    men are not at risk of transmitting birth defects to a fetus if they get a
    waman pregnant. Having said that, I caution my male patients about getting
    a woman pregnant. This is simply because if the woman were to have a baby
    with a problem, there would always be that little tug of doubt about
    whether the Accutane contributed. The answer is no, but when dealing with
    pregnancies and babies, it is in my opinion best to avoid all prospects of
    doubt.

    If a patient who has taken Accutane begins to get acne back after finishing
    Accutane treatment, I will often give a second or even third and fourth
    course of the drug. I try to separate the treatments by 6 months to give
    the body a chance to recover.

    I hope this information about acne and Accutane has helped.
