    my very own opinion...

    jeff007, if you are bonded to this organisation for 4/6 years because of the scholarship you took up when you were 18 year old - the work sucks, your superior piles up endless amount of work on you, you colleagues have extra and higher expectations of you because you are after all, the company's scholar, attractive offers (better pay and job satisfaction) are knocking on your door - won't you be tempted to take up these offers?

    as repeated and emphasised so many times from scholars, a scholarship is not awarded based on affections, emotions, empathy, sympathy. a scholarship is a commercial contract. and it is really just a pure commercial contract. both parties benefit. because of a scholarship, a student will have to work very hard for that 4-5 years of tertiary studies. if the student doesn't perform well, the organisation will simply terminate the scholarship and ask the student to pay damages. in the same way, if the organisation does not meet up to the student's expectations, why is it wrong for the student to terminate the contract?

    if anyone wants to argue along the line that
    a scholar has moral obligations. i think anyone on the opposition can also argue along the line that the organisation has equal moral obligations. scholar terminates bond - WRONG. organisation terminates scholar's contract - WRONG too! so let's have a debate on how morally wrong it is to terminate a hardworking scholar's contract.

    regarding the scholar who said the scholarship will look good on his resume. i suppose that part of the speech was taken out of a long interview dialouge. and the papers chose to sensationalise that provocative interview portion.

    of course, there will be this group of 'bad' scholars who are already thinking of breaking bonds without serving a single minute. and to equate this balance, there will also be this group of organisations who will treat their scholars like shit. i'll say these two parties best deserve each other.


    just my opinions.
