    Ouch, that's no choc chip!
    By Report Ian Tan

    AS Mr Eugene Ng was happily tucking into his freshly-baked bun, he thought he had bitten into a chocolate chip when his teeth hit something hard.

    But it seemed too hard to be chocolate.
    And it was tasteless.
    'I pulled the piece of bread out and I was stunned,' said Mr Ng, 27, a
    sales consultant at Informatics Singapore.
    A 1.5-cm-long gleaming silver screw was tightly embedded in the bread.
    'I threw the piece onto the table and told my colleagues, 'I got
    screwed!'.' recounted Mr Ng, smiling.
    The bun was one of two Mexico buns he had bought from the BreadTalk outlet
    in Citylink Mall that day, on Jul 23.
    He had already finished the first bun, which had a green bean paste
    filling and chocolate chip topping, when he offered the second one to two of his colleagues.

    He's glad they didn't accept the offer, as he'd have felt responsible if either of them had been hurt.
    He himself was not injured, but he pointed out: 'What if it had been a kid who had bought that piece of bread?'
    And how did the screw get into the bun?
    That is the very question that got BreadTalk officials scratching their heads.

    When Mr Stewart Tay, 40, the Citylink branch manager, and Mr Michael Tan, 30, BreadTalk's area manager, met Mr Ng the very next day, they told him it was a mystery to them.

    'This screw does not belong to any of our machines,' said Mr Tay, who manages the seven-person production crew at the BreadTalk outlet.

    He explained to Mr Ng that since the bread dough is kneaded by hand, the screw would have been detected immediately by the bakers.
    Mr Ng said the bun, which costs $1.30, did not seem to have been tampered with.

    When the New Paper asked if there was a possibility of sabotage, Mr Tay said it was unlikely as his employees are close-knit.

    They are shocked,' said Mr Tay, who has apologised to Mr Ng.

    BreadTalk has also given Mr Ng a hamper of wine and chocolate.

    A spokesman for BreadTalk said: 'This is an isolated case and it is safe to eat BreadTalk products. After a thorough investigation, we are still unable to conclude where the nut came from as none of our kitchen utensils have such an item.'

    After the incident, the production of Mexico buns at all BreadTalk outlets was stopped.
    As for Mr Ng, he bears no grudge against the bakery.
    He said: 'Nobody wants this to happen, and the first bun I had was really quite nice.'
