Zheng Qing Ending SOon?

  • RuthBaby

    BY 9 TONIGHT, marathon soap opera A Kindred Spirit would have been running on Channel U for 566 hours. That is 10 hours shy of 24 days. And its days are numbered.

    GREAT REALITY TV: The long-running series hooked viewers here with its compelling portrayal of family life, along with humourous antics and dialogue.
    The popular prime-time Hongkong family drama, which began airing on the Mandarin SPH MediaWorks channel on July 4 last year, will finish its run by the middle of next month.

    Drawing consistent double-digit viewership ratings, sometimes as high as 17 per cent, it is without doubt Channel U's top programme.

    Mr Chang Long Jong, chief executive officer of rival MediaCorp Studios, describes it as Channel U's lifeline.

    But he also wonders if running it day in and day out on prime-time might hamper Channel U's move to forge a young and vibrant identity, as some viewers might see it as a one-programme channel.

    MediaWorks chief operating officer Man Shu Sum is not worried about this.

    'It's like Manchester United. People associate it readily with David Beckham. But without the team effort of other players and a good manager, how can it become the world's top football club?' he reasons.

    Set in pre-handover Hongkong, A Kindred Spirit centres on the so-called Char Siew family - named after its business of selling barbecued pork - and the relationships among its members, in-laws, relatives and friends.

    Played by veterans Kwan Hoi San and Lai Shuen, the patriarch and matriarch of the clan have two daughters - down-to-earth Ren Shan (Louise Lee), who is married to Char Siew Bing (Lau Dan), and the widowed and greedy Ren Hao (Nancy Sit).

    Supporting them in this Television Broadcasts Limited (TVB) production is a circus of at least 15 others. They include May Kwong, Wallis Phang, Mary Kwok, Kennix Kwok, Marco Lo, Cally Kwong and Lui Fong.

    At 1,128 episodes of 30 minutes each, A Kindred Spirit, or Zhen Qing in Mandarin, holds the record as Hongkong's longest serial.

    It aired there from May 1995 to November 1999, with production stopping about two months before the end of its 4 1/2-year run.

    It was so successful, a stage musical starring most of the original cast was made.

    The series has also aired in Taiwan, Japan, Vietnam, Malaysia and Australia.

    Though first shown here in Cantonese on TVBJ (cable channel 4 in November 1997, it did not catch on with Singaporeans until the dubbed-into-Mandarin version was broadcast on free-to-air Channel U.

    The channel has cut and spliced two episodes, sometimes more, into hour-long packages. On Monday nights, viewers get two hours worth of the soap opera, or about four episodes.

    The telecast date of the series finale remains a closely-guarded secret because Channel U wants to keep Channel 8 on its toes, sources say.

    'The point is to withhold it for as long as possible so that Channel 8 will not be able to stage a 'spoiler' with a special programme during the finale,' says a media watcher who declined to be named.

    And it's not just bored Chinese housewives or retirees who tune in. The show draws students from primary school to polytechnics, and professionals in their mid-20s to their late-30s, says Channel U.

    There are also non-Chinese viewers, with AC Nielsen figures showing 54,000 of them aged 15 and up tuning in.

    Among them are graphic designer Norlyn Yaicob, 21, her housewife mother, who is in her 50s, and her 27-year-old sister.

    'We got hooked by its compelling portrayal of family life, which covers every age group you can think of,' she says.

    'You don't need to be Chinese to dig its humorous antics and dialogue as long as there are subtitles.'

    Housewife Chong Bee Leng, 61, finds it 'great reality TV, because it portrays what everyday families face in trying to get along with each other'.

    More importantly, you can jump in at any point and not feel too lost because the plot is easy to piece together, says administrative co-ordinator Zubaidah Saat, 25.

    She is also drawn by its mix of conservative Asian values as well as its broad stereotypes, such as how characters from mainland China are depicted as greedy.

    Freelance translator J.S. Ng, 27, points out how women in the show are given subservient, self-sacrificial roles.

    'The series encourages us to be homemakers like Louise Lee's Ren Shan, the perfect daughter, wife and mother,' she says.

    'The successful and more educated ones are usually prevented by the plot from developing their careers. They end up choosing marriage and a family.'

    Those who don't are portrayed as temperamental or desperate.

    Though Ms Ng does not agree with these values, she found herself 'sucked' into the show.

    'Now I feel like I've stayed with the family for a long time, and it's funny how you thought it would never end, and you don't want it to, but it has to, at some point.'

    A Kindred Spirit airs on Channel U, Mondays to Fridays, at 7 pm. Reruns air on Saturdays and Sundays from 7 to 9 pm.

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Ratings up with Nancy Sit

    A KINDRED SPIRIT may be the trump card of the 18-month-old Channel U, but it was not an instant hit with viewers.

    Mr Man Shu Sum, chief operating officer of SPH MediaWorks, says it got off slowly. 'When we started airing it at prime-time in July last year, it only got a 3.9 per cent rating,' he recalls.

    It took the TVB-produced show three months before it hit double-digit viewership, but this was something he had anticipated.

    'The same thing happened in Hongkong back in 1995. The first 200 episodes were not very popular and TVB even wanted to kill it off.

    'Then they introduced Nancy Sit into the show and that's when ratings picked up.'

    The series' proven track record in Hongkong and other markets prompted Channel U to acquire the TVB production two years ago.

    It aired initially on Mondays to Fridays from 7 to 8 pm.

    After viewers warmed up to it, Channel U increased the frequency to six days on Dec 16, and seven days a week from March 17.

    Thanks to A Kindred Spirit, Channel U's prime-time share has overtaken Channel 8's since May this year, according to figures provided by AC Nielsen.

    This, despite the latter channel putting on blockbusters like an adaptation of the Qiong Yao classic Romance In The Rain.

    The closest the two stations came to a draw at prime-time was when Channel 8 pitted Kopi-O II against A Kindred Spirit in July.

    But findings by Taylor Nelson Sofres, the research agency used by MediaCorp, show that on average, Channel 8 is still ahead in the prime-time race, with 55 per cent versus Channel U's 45 per cent.

    Mr Man declines to say how much MediaWorks paid for the series as well as for the repeat run of A Kindred Spirit which started last Saturday.

    But he adds that it was Channel 8's loss for not picking it up when it was available on the market.

    This was way before Channel U announced in January last year its exclusive five-year-deal with TVB. Under this, it can have the first pick of at least 400 hours of programming from the Hongkong broadcaster.

    While Channel 8 was aware of the popularity of A Kindred Spirit, sources say that its sheer length - more than 600 hours - exceeded the station's annual quota for acquired programming from TVB.

    Still, while A Kindred Spirit enjoys high ratings, there has been no significant jump in Channel U's advertising revenue. 'No advertiser is foolish enough to put all their budget in one time-belt,' says Mr Man.

    --------------------------------------------------------------------------------

    Who will win this time?

    NEITHER of them will admit it, but both Channel U and Channel 8 are gearing up for the next prime-time showdown when A Kindred Spirit ends next month.

    Can Channel U maintain what it claims is its prime-time lead over Channel 8 when its most popular programme completes its course?

    Channel U is tight-lipped about when it will air the series finale. A recent move seems to suggest that it is trying to prolong the last lap of the soap opera.

    On the weekend of Nov 2 and 3, it replaced without any prior announcement A Kindred Spirit's 7 to 9 pm screening with movies.

    This reduces the number of episodes shown from 10 per week to six, which also stretches out the soap opera a little longer than originally planned.

    Irate viewers called in to complain. A result of this: Channel U started a weekend re-run of the serial from last Saturday.

    Meanwhile, both stations are keeping mum on what they will roll out next month.

    Sources point to a few prime-time contenders that Channel U might use.

    But none look as mammoth or as compelling.

    There is Healing Hands II which stars Bowie Lam, Kit Chan, Ada Choi and Flora Chan. This is a sequel to the successful medical drama shown on Channel 8 last year.

    There are also a few period dramas, namely The Legendary Four Aces starring Nick Cheung and Bobby Au Yeung; Duke Of Mount Deer 2000 produced by Wong Jing; All's Well Ends Well starring MediaWorks' Celest Chong; and Virtues Of Harmony starring Nancy Sit, Michael Tse and Lau Dan.

    Only Virtues, a hit TVB drama into its 200th episode and which is still in production, comes close to A Kindred Spirit.

    Already showing on VV Drama (StarHub Channel 55), it features many members of Kindred's cast such as Sit, Tse and Lau. It also explores, though in a more comic manner, the same familial subjects.

    Channel 8, on the other hand, is said to be launching the 60-part Springs Of Life on its 7 pm slot sometime next month.

    Set in the 1960s and helmed by Chew Chor Meng, this is a follow-up to 2000's Walk Of Life, which satirises the rivalry in Singapore's Chinese restaurant trade.

    The MediaCorp channel is also working on a second wave of home-made family-type dramas. These include Vive La Famille II starring Chen Shuchen and Huang Biren and the 100-episode Holland Village which is still in pre-production.

    Shown earlier this year, the first wave - Vive La Famille, Beautiful Connection and No Problem - was widely regarded as riding on the wave of A Kindred Spirit as they tackled similar themes.

    But dismissing this, Mr Chang Long Jong, chief executive officer of MediaCorp Studios, tells Life!: 'In the late 1990s, we also had a string of family-oriented dramas like Driven By A Car and Family Affairs which did well.

    'Our focus is to get our programming right and to keep to our prime-time plan of 70 per cent local and 30 per cent acquired programming for our 7 pm and 9 pm drama timebelts.

    'While A was one of the better programmes we could have looked at, its end will mean more opportunities for us to gain an upper hand.'
