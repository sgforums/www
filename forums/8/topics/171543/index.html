    The Curse of Count Dracula

    The prospect of a tourist bonanza from a Dracula theme park in Transylvania excites some Romanians, but opponents see only red

    A couple of earnest environmentalists warned author Rudy Chelminski that venerable oak trees on the plain where he was walking would soon be felled. A large billboard explained why. "Aici se va construi DRACULA PARK," announced the text: Dracula Park was to be built here.

    Over the past year and a half, a furious controversy has swarmed around this proposal. It was Romania's minister of tourism who came up with the idea of a Dracula theme park. Few of his countrymen would argue with the minister's economic rationale, but proposing the city of Sighisoara as the site was another matter. Known as the "Pearl of Transylvania," Sighisoara was founded near the end of the 13th century; the old-town citadel remains perfectly preserved. It sits on a hill behind a 30-foot wall punctuated by nine defensive towers.

    In the autumn of 2001, the tourism minister displayed elaborate plans for Dracula Park in a glossy brochure. It depicts a medieval castle complete with torture chamber, alchemy laboratory, vampire den and an initiation hall where "young vampires can be dubbed knights." The International Institute of Vampirology was to be located near Dracula Lake, a wide pond with a restaurant in the middle, and the Old Tower would house a workshop for teeth-sharpening. Restaurant fare was to include blood pudding, brains, and "fright-jellied" meat, a scraps and gelatin concoction.

    Last summer England's Prince Charles, an architecture buff and preservationist, added his own voice to the rising chorus of dissent when he declared that "the proposed Dracula Park is wholly out of sympathy with the area and will ultimately destroy its character." Then, in February, the minister announced that the town of Snagov, just north of the Bucharest airport, would be the park site. Groundbreaking is planned for May.

    The developers had lost; Transylvania had won. As for Dracula himself, it wouldn't have surprised anyone if the mocking sound of his demonic laughter could be heard echoing through the alleyways of the medieval citadel that has escaped his curse.
