    EVER wonder what lawyers mean when they say they are making an 'ex parte' application or tell clients that they have to make an 'ex gratia'?

    If you are stumped by these Latin phrases you can look below or turn to a guide for commonly used Latin phrases.

    The free guide is produced by the Singapore Academy of Law.

    Work on this two-page guide began in February last year when the academy's Law Reform Committee considered the Chief Justice's request that 'changes be made to the terminology and language of the courts in Singapore to make the legal process more understandable to the public'.

    With help from the judges, the Attorney-General's Chambers, the NUS Faculty of Law and the Law Society of Singapore, a sub-committee started working on it in May last year.

    And after several rounds of internal review and inputs from the various segments of the legal fraternity here, the guide was finalised in October.

    It was approved last December and can now be downloaded for free from the academy's website at www.sal.org.sg

    When contacted, Ms Serene Wee, director of the Singapore Academy of Law, said: 'The guide seeks to encourage lawyers to use plain English terms instead of Latin phrases.

    'It is also intended to enable non-lawyers to comprehend the meaning of Latin terms, if and when they come across them.'

    But she noted that while the committee agreed that the usage of Latin terms should be reduced, it does not necessarily mean that legal terms that have become common use, such as the word 'plaintiff', should be dropped.

    Welcoming the move, lawyer Mr Chia Boon Teck said: 'It's always good to be user-friendly, although the tradition of sprinkling Latin phrases in the legal profession does give one's presentation an air of sophistication.'

    In addition to this guide, said Ms Wee, the academy is also conducting workshops on 'Drafting in plain English' this month , to help litigators and draftsmen write in plain language for 'even the most complex legislation'.
