    Originally posted by TruFaith`:
    I believe in God but it crops up in my mind once in a while, who created God? Well, enough abt all those 'God is a powerful ever existing entity and no one has to create God' answers. How could something just exist all of a sudden rite? There must be a source. I know God's mighty and all that but yeah, how did God came about? Just want to know ur opinions.
    If whatever caused the universe need not be supposed to have begun to exist (unlike the universe itself, which, because it began to exist, has to be supposed to have needed a cause), then we do not need to suppose that whatever caused the universe would itself have needed a cause.

    The cause of the universe need not be supposed therefore to have itself required a cause (or a creator); especially as we have no reason not to think that the beginning of the universe coincided with the beginning of time itself.

    By Occam's razor (which forbids us to multiply hypotheses beyond necessity) we would be driven to seek a cause only for the universe's existing (or its having begun to exist), since anything that has begun to exist, would plausibly have needed to have been caused; but we would not likewise be bound to ask ourselves how the cause of the universe must itself have originated (assuming that it did in fact originate in some occurrence), when we have a reason for supposing that the cause in question must have been timeless.

    Thus your statement that 'there must be a source' can only be held applicable to phenomena that we have reason to believe, had (in whatever sense) begun to exist; an entity that we don't have a reason to suppose must have begun to exist, would not be something that we would be bound to think of as something that would have needed to have a source for its existence.

    In other words, we don't have a reason to suppose that God ever needed to 'come about'; and thus we have no reason to think that such a question would have to admit of a meaningful answer.

    I hope this helps.

    Originally posted by starblue:

    Even if you have to believe that God, if He exists at all, must have existed always, this would still not mean that you would have a reason for supposing that God exists; it would only mean rather that on the supposition that God exists, you would have to suppose that God was always in existence.

    Secondly, it might indeed be true that if you didn't believe that 'anything could always be', then you wouldn't be able to 'believe in God'; but the point to be made here is that this line of reasoning doesn't constitute a reason for supposing that there is in fact a God who exists.

    If I believed in unicorns then I might be bound to believe that there are animals that have a horn on their foreheads; but is that a reason for me to believe in unicorns?

    By the same token, if I didn't believe that there could be animals which grow a horn on each of their foreheads, then I wouldn't be able to believe in unicorns; but here again this would not mean that I would have a reason for supposing that such animals do in fact exist.

    As a matter of fact, I do think that we have reasons for affirming that God exists; but none of them turn upon whatever it is that we might be said to be logically bound to believe about God, once we have established that He exists.

    Ben (a Catholic Christian)

    If God's existence is a matter of my believing that He exists (and not instead a matter of whether or not He exists in the real world and of what reasons we may have for thinking that He exists in the real world), then are you saying:

    1) It does not matter if God exists in the real world, so long as I personally believe that He exists.

    or

    2) God would exist in the real world if I believed that He existed in teh real world (in opposition to or quite apart from whatever reasons I may have for thinking that He exists in the real world).

    Christianity denies both these positions because it affirms:

    1) It matters (or should matter to me) that whatever I believe exists, does indeed exist in the real world and is not a product of my imagination; and this matters (and should matter) supremely where the object of my belief is God.

    2) My believing that something is the case does not actually serve to make it be the case; and my believing that God exists does not serve to bring it about that He should actually exist; which would accordingly be something that I would have to acknowledge regardless of whether or not I may feel that I am in a position to prove that God exists.

    When atheists question the existence of God, it would not be helping matters to point out to them that 'God exists because I believe that He exists'; particularly when their disbelief in God's existence is grounded in their perpection that it lacks adequate rational warrant (or is indeed contradicted by the available evidence).
