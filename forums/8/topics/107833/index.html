  • Diesel.Okini
    18 Dec 04, 06:26
    I've just won an entrepreneural idea's competition at my university (Imperial College, UK). The most important thing is planning/strategy to make it feasible/effective, & to getting funding/investment. That's what allows a business to take off.

    What makes a business successful includes how you time your entry into a market, the macroeconomic trends is where opportunities are affected. Is it an emerging market/industry? What are the social/political/legal factors?

    Then there's the quality of the idea, whether the technology/service is truly brilliant, whether it can be protected (patented, copyright), and whether it can fight off competition. That's the microeconomic strength.

    And I always hear, is "a 2nd class idea in the hands of a 1st class person, is better than a 1st class idea in the hands of a 2nd class person". The team is essential. Who forms the company, why are they the right people, the right mixture, you need to convince people that you're talent. Very often you need an advisory board, of experienced professionals who can give invaluable guidance.

    Finally, it's about how you plan/balance the risk-benefits, & pitch/sell the idea.

    How you use talent/ideas, & connect it with investment/spending is what managing and growing the business entails. Where will you be in 5 years time? Most companies really pay off when they go public (IPO), or get acquired/merged with a larger company. Who forms the management? Will you put yourself as a CEO or invite a Harvard MBA? Will the core team stay with the company in the years ahead? Will there be a need to do R&D or cut back the scale?

    If you want to start, it's all about networking. Putting the right combination of minds together can make eureka! If you walk to Topshop/Topman, you may see a label there called "Raygun". Simply by walking in London's Convent Garden I actually met the boss! He's taken my contact details and seems interested in seeking proposals for his growing business. That's what I networked simply by talking to someone in a retail shop. I might end up one day in London's creative fashion niche, though it seems unlikely at present.
