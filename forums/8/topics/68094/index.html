    Watch it! Your Wi-Fi network may be hijacked

    Laziness and ignorance could mean others are helping themselves to your bandwidth - without you being any wiser

    By Ho Ka Wei

    WHILE surfing wirelessly in his living room, Mr Peter Loo stumbled upon another network - his neighbour's.

    Surprised by his ease of entry into another person's not-so-private network, Mr Loo, 48, promptly took measures to secure his own, including cutting his transmitter's range by putting it under a table.

    He also told his neighbour about the unintended breach.

    His neighbour was lucky - a more determined hacker could easily pick out user identities, passwords, files and more if he had 'hijacked' the wireless signals.

    As more homes are hooked up for wireless broadband connections - so several laptops or desktops can share one subscription - some new Wireless Internet (Wi-Fi) users like Mr Loo are concerned that many may not know the dangers in having an unsecured network.

    While there are no numbers to suggest the extent or seriousness of the problem of people hijacking another's bandwidth - simply because the rightful user may not even know it's happening to report it - it does happen.

    Gartner's research director for mobile and wireless, Mr Robin Simpson, told The Straits Times that people have been doing it, 'not with evil intent, but mainly just to get free bandwidth'.

    He added: 'The home user is a big risk as he is a relatively untrained user. Security is easy to set up, but the problem is laziness or lack of know-how.'

    He said although products for wireless access usually come with security features, most require a user to turn them on. However, many do not - with the result that the signals are available to everyone within range.

    Experts say, however, that protecting your network, either from freeloaders or hackers, is easy enough to do.

    A key step to take is to ensure that the identity of your router or other devices is changed from the default - which is usually the device's brand, which also means anyone can gain entry without much trying - to a protected one.

    Other steps include ensuring that firewalls exist and encrypting your data - the latter can be easily achieved by checking that your device offers encryption standards, such as the current Wi-Fi Protected Access.

    This will ensure some measure of security.

    The problem of Wi-Fi freeloaders started with the burgeoning of Wi-Fi hotspots over the last two years - both commercially and domestically.

    In the United States and Britain, debates have arisen over the legality of activities like 'war-driving', where people roam about with their Wi-Fi-enabled devices searching for open wireless access.

    Another popular activity abroad is 'war chalking', where war-drivers mark out areas where free access is available with chalk.

    Such activities are illegal, but detecting if someone else is hopping on board your connection is difficult.

    The signs will not be apparent. Sometimes, the warning will come too late - in the form of an unusually high bill at the end of the month.

    However, if you find that your Internet access speed is unusually slow, that may be a sign that someone is hopping onto your connection.

    For those contemplating such war-driving, technology lawyer Bryan Tan has a warning: 'In Singapore, war-driving may constitute offences under the Computer Misuse Act and the Telecommunications Act, and war-chalking, offences of vandalism and nuisance.

    'One can be fined, imprisoned and even caned for this.'

    How To Protect Yourself
    WIRELESS security has been enhanced - mainly in terms of encryption technology - over the last two years. The latest security move is the Wi-Fi Protected Access (WPA) standard that wireless equipment should now bear.

    Another security standard is expected later this year.

    Internet Service Providers are also aware of the need for more security. Said SingTel spokesman Andrienne Tho: 'SingTel is also monitoring developments in Wi-Fi encryption protocols to see how these can be used in our wireless infrastructure.'
