    the paradox prob goes something like this.

    we have the initial statement:

    A Spanish Barber shaves all the men in his town who do not shave themselves.

    actually to be more precise we should say:

    A Spanish Barber shaves all and only those men in town who do not shave themselves.

    In order to put the paradox in focus we have to ask: does the barber shave himself?

    case 1: the spanish barber actually shaves himself.

    case 2: the spanish barber does not shave himself.

    if case 1 holds, then for the initial statement to be true, the spanish barber cannot shave himself.

    if case 2 holds, then for the initial statement to be true, the spanish barber would have to shave himself.

    Hence in both cases 1 and 2, we have a man who both shaves and does not shave himself, and therein lies the paradox.

    This paradox can be resolved in two ways:

    firstly, we could say that the initial statement is not well formulated and is prob meaningless even tho it seems to make perfect sense - no such barber could exist who both shaves and does not shave himself.

    secondly, we could say we were making an unnecessary assumption that the barber is a guy. only if the barber was a "she" can the initial statement be true without generating a paradox (tho admittedly some women are in a pretty bad need of a shave themselves).
