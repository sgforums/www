    Struggle to make ends meet can affect IQ: Study

     

    WASHINGTON - Being short on cash may make you a bit slower in the brain, a new study suggests.

       Researchers in the study, published in the US journal Science, concluded that poverty consumed so much mental energy that people struggling to make ends meet often have little brain power left for anything else, leaving them more susceptible to bad decisions.

       People worrying about having enough money to pay their bills tend to lose temporarily the equivalent of 13 IQ points, scientists found when they gave intelligence tests to shoppers at a New Jersey mall and farmers in India.

       The idea is that financial stress monopolises thinking, making other calculations slower and more difficult, a bit like the effects of going without sleep for a night.

       "Our paper isn't about poverty. It's about people struggling to make ends meet," said Professor Sendhil Mullainathan, a Harvard economist and study co-author. "When we think about people who are financially stressed, we think they are short on money, but the truth is they are also short on cognitive capacity."

       If you are always thinking about overdue bills, a mortgage or rent, or college loans, it takes away from your focus on other things. So being late on loans could end up costing you both interest points and IQ points, Professor Mullainathan said.

      In the New Jersey part of the study, the researchers tested about 400 shoppers, presenting scenarios involving a large and a small car repair bill. Those with annual family incomes of about US$20,000 (S$25,500) scored about the same as those with US$70,000 incomes on IQ tests when the bill was small. But when poorer people had to think about a huge repair bill, their IQ scores were 40 per cent lower.

       Then the researchers looked at real life in the fields of India, where farmers get paid only once a year. Before the harvest, they take loans and pawn goods. After they sell their harvest, they are flush with cash.

       The researchers tested the same 464 farmers before and after the harvest and their IQ scores improved by 25 per cent when their wallets fattened.

       "For a long time, we've been blaming the poor for their own failings," said study co-author Jiaying Zhao, a professor of psychology at the University of British Columbia. "We're arguing something very different."

     

    ASSOCIATED PRESS

     

    World, The Straits Times, Saturday, August 31, 2013, pg A36
