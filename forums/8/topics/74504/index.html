    APRIL 1, 2004
    Kudos for shake-up in the civil service

    MPs, businessmen and senior civil servants applaud self-examination by public sector workers and their remedies

    By Eugene Low and M. Nirmala

    IT WAS senior civil servants who took a look at themselves in the mirror and didn't like what they saw.


    Staring back at them were three flaws they believed needed fixing.

    There was a need to encourage risk-taking and embrace diversity, deepen their understanding of the market, and improve inter-ministry coordination.

    The diagnosis of what was ailing the public sector, and the prescriptions, were made at a recent meeting of permanent secretaries, public service chiefs and statutory board chief executive officers. They came together to discuss how the sector should respond to changing circumstances.

    Giving public voice to their ideas, Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean on Tuesday spoke about the need for the public service to be more proactive and dynamic.

    Urging it to keep trying new ideas until these bear fruit, he told Administrative Service officers at their annual dinner that it was necessary to challenge the status quo continuously.

    And yesterday, businessmen, MPs and some senior civil servants themselves applauded the new directions that the public sector was setting for itself.

    There were those among the interviewees who said it was high time that public servants left their fortresses, understand the ground better and designed policies that work as well as they look.

    'There are risk-averse civil servants who do not know how the market works and are quick to say 'no' to new ideas,' observed Hong Leong Group executive chairman Kwek Leng Beng.

    It was not unexpected then that a call put forward for consideration was that for a one-stop agency for businesses.

    Mr Ron Sim, founder and chief executive of Osim, said that instead of having to deal with various ministries, there should be a centralised body that companies can contact. This will make dealings with the Government more straightforward.

    Businessman and Ang Mo Kio GRC MP Inderjit Singh, a long-time champion of a more enterprise-friendly public sector, welcomed the move towards a less risk-averse culture in the civil service. But he said unless the reward and punishment system changes, there was 'little chance of this working out' because risk-taking also meant the possibility of failure.

    Agreeing, a retired civil servant, who declined to be named, said: 'It's not that civil servants lack initiative, but that people are made to conform to the system. The end result is unimaginative civil servants.'

    One problem is that change sometimes gets blocked at the top. A serving administrative officer, who asked not to be named, said yesterday that there were ministers who tended to be conservative.

    'At times, civil servants are just second-guessing them. There have been instances where civil servants are ready to change existing policies but the ministers are not,' he said.

    Hence it was significant that Rear-Admiral (NS) Teo himself acknowledged the inadequacy of the top-down approach. The fact that a ranking minister issued the call for change is 'a strong signal that the Government has recognised the problem', said Mr Leong Horn Kee, MP for Bishan-Toa Payoh GRC and chairman of the Government Parliamentary Committee on Finance, Trade and Industry.

    Businessmen also cheered the move to enhance inter-ministry coordination through the setting up of a Strategic Issues Group.

    'Now, you get three or four agencies saying different things about the same issue, and it is the private sector that gets caught in the middle,' said a senior executive at a multinational corporation.

    Out with the straitjacket way of tackling issues

    WHEN a crisis breaks, ministries often work well together as a team. But during day-to-day operations, they tend to be over-compartmentalised and ignore multiple dimensions of issues.

    One solution: get them to work more closely through a Strategic Issues Group (SIG). The group will encourage multi-agency cooperation by setting up specific teams to look at issues.

    Announcing the move during the elite Administrative Service dinner on Tuesday, Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean said there were issues which may not come under the purview of a particular ministry, but whose solutions require a coordinated approach across many ministries.

    'The purpose of the SIG is to free ourselves from the straitjacket of existing policies and departmental interests to develop fresh ideas,' he said.
