The 'sabo' workers

    Bored or unhappy, the saboteurs lie for MCs and do personal chores on office time

    By Li Xueying

    LAST year, Ms K. Sim called in sick with a touch of flu, a head-splitting migraine and stomach cramps that left her bent double.

    She took four or five days of medical leave.

    But in fact, she had cooked up these ailments to avoid going to work.

    'I lay in bed, depressed about having to get up, take the MRT and go to the office. I just couldn't face going to work,' she said.

    Ms Sim, 27, said she hates her job as an administration executive in a trading company, because she finds it boring. It doesn't help that her relationship with her boss is strained.

    A Sunday Times survey on Singaporeans' work attitudes found plenty of people like her. One in 10 admitted asking for a medical certificate (MC) to avoid going to work.

    Nearly four in 10, or 38 per cent, said they didn't care about their work; and 3 per cent said outright that they hate what they do.

    Of those polled, 36 per cent said they had been dissatisfied with work at one time or another; and as many said they would not recommend their jobs to others.

    And how do workers deal with their unhappiness?

    They sashay to work late, refuse to work overtime, disparage their companies, and carry out personal chores during office time.

    These are the 'sabo workers' - unhappy employees who get back at their employers.

    Baker's Inn owner Daniel Tay, who has about 120 employees, said he's had enough of workers who abuse his trust and goodwill.

    He used to let workers call in sick, without an MC. But some began doing it too often.

    A chef at one outlet borrowed $4,000 from the company, and then promptly took two weeks' medical leave, claiming he had a heart attack. He was found out, sacked, and made to return the money.

    Two months ago, when employees called in sick, Mr Tay started asking for the numbers on their MCs and checking with the clinics that gave out the MCs.

    Since then, the number calling in sick has halved to about five a month.

    Though he's not caught employees spitting on a customer's food, or stealing, he's considering installing security cameras in the kitchens.

    Not all unhappy workers become malingerers or subversive malcontents. Some show up for work, but just don't put in 100 per cent on the job.

    Mr Tan Yi Siong, 24, an event planning assistant, admits he's one of them. He said: 'I find my work meaningless. But I can't leave because there are no jobs for me out there.

    'I don't deliberately sabo my company, like taking MC when I don't need it, or stealing stationery. But I'm certainly not giving it my all.'

    A Gallup survey of workers from 11 countries reported last September that dissatisfied Singapore workers cost the economy $4.9 billion a year in lost productivity, and poor employee management is the root cause.

    Human resource experts say The Sunday Times' estimate of 'sabo workers' isn't alarmingly high, but bosses would do well to examine their human resource practices.

    Mr Koh Juan Kiat, executive director of the Singapore National Employers Federation, said: 'Such actions are usually symptomatic of outdated human resource practices out of sync with employees' aspirations.'

    For instance, if a company is so inflexible that it won't allow emergency leave, workers may resort to lying to get MCs. Companies should review their practices every two years, said Mr Koh.

    Those who took part in The Sunday Times poll said their job satisfaction would improve if they were better paid and given more challenging work.

    Accounts executive Lyn Ong, 26, who admits she sends personal e-mail messages during office hours, said her attitude towards her 'boring' job 'borders on hatred'.

    She hopes to move on to financial analysis next year.

    'I do this because it's the only thing my degree groomed me for. I have to drag myself out of bed every day to face rows and rows of numbers. I don't like to sit at my desk, bash away at the computer, and nitpick at details.

    'I hate the mundaneness.'
