    So just why did the beefed-up code for institutions of a public character (IPC) Â— which includes charities Â— recently drop a key recommendation to disclose the salaries of the top three executives in each outfit?

    This clause may eventually be reviewed in light of the NKF saga, but when this newspaper tried to collate information on just how much the chiefs of charity organisations are paid, it was clear that many were uncomfortable about making their incomes public.

    Ironically, the reason for their embarrassment was not that their salaries were too high but that, in many cases, they were too low. Asians who really are paid peanuts do not want to scream that fact to the world.

    An industry veteran said that making salaries of charity chiefs public could be a double-edged sword.

    "Once there are salary disclosures, the public may realise that the CEOs are not well-paid after all. But the flip side is that there will be always people who think NGO (non-governmental organisation) chiefs should not be paid at all!" he said. "So disclosures may work for some, but not for others. It's a question of timing. Are we ready for it?"

    Any way you look at it, Venerable Shi Ming Yi is an exception.

    The chief executive officer and chairman of Ren Ci Hospital and Medicare Centre receives no perks or bonuses, earns $15,000 a month _ lower than the Durai yardstick but higher than most others in the field _ and most remarkably is willing to make it public.

    The voluntary welfare organisation (VWO), which conducted star-studded TV show, somewhat like NKF, also has no problems disclosing that its annual income last year was $30 million and that its reserves can last three years.

    "From Ren Ci's perspective, the more we explain and clarify, the more informed our donors and the public are of what we do," said a Ren Ci spokesperson. "Only then can we depend on their support and goodwill."

    Others seem to echo the sentiment, but when the questions get personal, not everyone wants to broadcast the answers.

    "We also have our sensitivities and there must be some safeguard of privacy (for those is the VWO sector). We do have many safeguard in the system," said Mr Ron Chandran-Dudley, president of the Disabled People's Association. He does not receive any remuneration but was quick to add that his personal financial situation permits him to do voluntary work. Not everyone may be in such a happy situation.

    When asked about his remuneration, one senior VWO functionary was initially testy. "I am reluctant to do that," he said. "My staff doesn't know my pay."

    Mr Gerard Ee, the president of the National Council of Social Service (NCSS), which counts 230 members under its umbrella, gave some idea of how charity bosses were paid. "Take a CEO in a VWO, we look at his qualification, grade, and experience, and see how much he would be earning if he was in the government sector. Then we peg it a fair bit lower than that scale," he told Today, before he was appointed the new chairman of NKF.

    "But I cannot be so definitive and say that a person in this job must be paid this much. If I am running a five-staff charity with 200 beneficiaries, the complexity and energy required is different from a charity that helps 1,000 people," he added.

    When asked if Mr Durai's 12-month performance bonuses are in line with industry practice, he said such one-off perks are not common in Singapore.

    "I've not seen it elsewhere in the VWOs. If you choose to have it, you must be in a position to explain to your stakeholders. If they don't welcome it, then you must be prepared to drop it," he said.

    The Kidney Dialysis Foundation (KDF), which has come under the spotlight after NKF's well-publicised court case, underlined the point about just how unusual such bonuses are.

    With an annual income of $6.4 million in 2003-04, this organisation pays its general manager Madeline Goh a monthly salary of $4,200. Like the rest of her staff, Ms Goh received a bonus equivalent to two weeks' salary last year.

    Still, some may argue that Singapore's charities are too amateurish to be taken as a benchmark for NKF, which is in a league of its own in terms of professional fund-raising.

    Singapore Ministers Pay, Legitimised Corruption
    Singapore Review, 2 May 2003 (updated May 2005)
    By Mellanie Hewlitt

    The below table puts things back in proper perspective: (these are basic figures as of July 2000 and did not include last year's pay hikes or other benefits. Otherwise the updated numbers may well be much larger)

    1. Singapore President's Basic Salary US$1,483,000 (SGD$2,373,100)*a year
    Singapore Prime Minister's Basic Salary US$1,100,000 (SGD1,958,000) a year
    Minister's Basic: US$655,530 to US$819,124 (SGD1,166,844 to SGD1,458,040) a year

    2. United States of America President: US$200,000 Vice President: US$181,400 Cabinet Secretaries: US$157,000

    3. United Kingdom Prime Minister: US$170,556 Ministers: US$146,299 Senior Civil Servants: US$262,438

    4. Australia Prime Minister: US$137,060 Deputy Prime Minister: US$111,439 Treasurer: US$102,682

    5. Hong Kong Chief Executive : US$416,615 Top Civil Servant: US$278,538 Financial Sec: US$315,077

    Source: Asian Wall Street Journal July 10 2000 *Singapore President's salary which was updated in 2005 from the Singapore Straits Times

    In relative terms, less then 20% of Singaporeans here have take home salaries
    exceeding SGD100,000/- A YEAR.

    In stark contrast, BASIC SALARY FOR A MINISTER STARTS AT SGD1,166,844 A YEAR,OR JUST UNDER SGD100,000 A MONTH.

    What these ministers earns in just ONE MONTH exceeds the ANNUAL TAKE HOME
    salary of 80% of Singapore's income earning population. Lets not even begin to compare annual packages which will exceed SGD1 million easily.

    With the above numbers and figures now in perspective, it is easier to give substance to the words "leading by example". Several facts are noteworthy here;

    a) That the ministerial salaries are grossly out of proportion, even when compared with their counterparts in much larger countries (US and UK) who have far heavier responsibilities.

    b) That these salary reductions were long overdue. In the past, such handsome remuneration were "justified" on the back of resounding performance. However, Singapore's economy has been in the doldrums of a recession for several years now (with beginnings reaching as far back as the 1997 Asian economic crisis). This economic barometer is a rough measure of performance and implies that ministerial salaries were due for review at least 3-4 years ago.

    c) That adjustments should be made to bring them back within the industry benchmarks. Taking the salary of US vice president as a rule of thumb, the percentage for reductions should start at 50% of current pay. Even if a Singapore minister takes a 50% pay-cut, he would still be earning much more then the US vice president.

    d) The percentage reductions should greater then 50% if the intent is to bring the salaries within the perspective of Singapore's domestic scene.

    With such inflated figures, it is understandable why the local government controlled media (Singapore Press Holdings) have taken pains to exclude mention of actual numbers for the world to see. The numbers would be too glaring and no amount of window dressing or creative writing could have reconciled these numbers with a sane figure and restored credibility.
