    AUG 12, 2002 MON
    TRADE and Industry Minister George Yeo yesterday brushed aside comment by a Malaysian minister that Singaporeans would be drinking water recycled from bathrooms, saying that this is common response to the problem of over-population and urbanisation.

    Referring to Singapore's super-clean Newater, Brigadier-General (NS) Yeo said: 'There is a water shortage worldwide because of population growth and urbanisation. And this technology will not be used only in Singapore and a few other places, but...in many places.

    He was replying to questions from reporters who asked him to comment on what Johor Mentri Besar Abdul Ghani Othman had said during an interview on a Malaysian talk show last Friday.

    On the talk show, he had said: 'We from Malaysia should know that when we go to Singapore one day and drink coffee, the water comes from behind their kitchens or washrooms.'

    BG Yeo pointed out that far from being a new phenomenon, water is recycled constantly and naturally by the earth's ecosystem. Water recycling is also used by astronauts when enduring long stays in space.

    Speaking after a 2-km walk during an active-lifestyle day organised by Kembangan-Punggol grassroots organisations, he added: 'Over this National Day period, we've all got drunk on Newater. I think the response has been wonderful.

    'There's a sense that this technological breakthrough enables us to be self-sufficient, if we have to be - not that we want to be, but if we have to.'

    He felt that Newater 'would relax' the relationship between Singapore and Malaysia.

    'As long as the Malaysians think that we can't do without their water, they'll always feel that we're being undercharged,' he said. 'So once we have this supply of Newater, the relationship can be based more on the costs that are involved.

    'We're just doing something that we have to do in our own national interest. It's our responsibility to ourselves and to future generations,' he said.

    Separately, BG Yeo greeted Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong's upbeat comments on the economy during his National Day message with full support.

    But with many external forces still likely to have an impact on Singapore's economic performance, he singled out Singapore's government and the relationship with its people as a focal point that should remain stable in uncertain times.

    Quoting a Chinese saying, he said: 'To manage 10,000 changes, there must be a point which doesn't change.'

    AUG 7, 2002 WED
    Mahathir ready to end water pact
    Singapore need not wait till 2011 to let pact lapse, he says, adding that KL prefers to sell water to Malaysian states

    By Brendan Pereira
    MALAYSIA CORRESPONDENT

    KUALA LUMPUR - Singapore need not wait until 2011 to terminate one of its two water agreements with Malaysia, Prime Minister Mahathir Mohamad said here yesterday.

    'They want to remove, it's all right. It can stop even now, no need to wait until 2011,' he said when asked to comment on reports that the Republic indicated it would let one of the agreements lapse.

    Advertisement

    Malaysia did not want to sell water to Singapore, he said, adding that it was a losing proposition for his country.

    Domestic Trade and Consumer Affairs Minister Muhyiddin Yassin, speaking to Malaysian reporters on Monday, said Singapore Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong told him the Republic planned to allow the agreement ending in 2011 to lapse, as it would have the means to produce sufficient water by then.

    The minister, who was on a two-day working visit to Singapore, said the Republic had decided to reduce dependency on imported water to about 20 per cent of its total consumption over a 20- to 30-year period.

    'They have said they will not ask for a renewal or extension because they are looking at new and alternative sources of water such as Newater and desalinated water,' Tan Sri Muhyiddin said.

    He quoted Mr Goh as saying that Singapore would be prepared to continue buying water in the long term to maintain good relations with its neighbour but the price must be right.

    What constitutes the right price has been debated hotly on both sides of the Causeway.

    Singapore Foreign Affairs Minister S. Jayakumar told Parliament last month that the price of any treated water bought from Malaysia after the current agreements expire must be pegged to a percentage of the Newater's cost.

    Under two agreements signed in the early 1960s, Singapore pays Malaysia three sen (1.4 Singapore cents) per 1,000 gallons of raw water.

    Malaysia has proposed raising the price to 60 sen per thousand gallons between 2002 and 2007. From 2007 to 2011, Malaysia is asking for RM3 per thousand gallons.

    Singapore has offered to pay 45 sen per 1,000 gallons when the first agreement expires in 2011 and 60 sen after the second one runs out in 2061, with adjustments every five years.

    Dr Mahathir said yesterday that it did not make sense 'that the price has remained unchanged for 75 years'.

    'We do not want to sell water to them. We are losing money selling to them. We do not depend on them as sources of income to finance the government. Even if we raise the price, by comparison to our national income, it is a very minute fraction,' he said.

    He added that his government preferred to sell its water to other states in the country, saying that Malacca, for instance, was prepared to pay between 30 sen and 40 sen per 1,000 gallons.

    In July, officials from both sides met to discuss a new water agreement and other unresolved issues. The talks ended in a stalemate and both sides are to meet again in Singapore next month.

    Singapore has said it wants to reduce dependency on water from Malaysia. It plans to do so by increasing its catchment areas, desalination and through Newater, which is produced by passing water flowing from the kitchen and bathroom through a series of membranes with microscopic pores.
