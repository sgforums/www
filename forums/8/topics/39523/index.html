    Masks can't stop viruses, admits maker
    by Paris Lord
    An American manufacturer of respiratory masks being bought by terrified residents last night admitted they were ineffective against viruses.

    Almost one million masks made by 3M have been sold in Hong Kong since the viral pneumonia outbreak was first reported in the SAR in mid-February, the company's local spokesman Billy Ho said.

    The most popular mask models are the 8210 and 1860, with the latter being used in hospitals.

    Both conformed to the American National Institute for Occupational Health and Safety's N95 standard, filtering out 95 per cent of non-oil aerosols, Ho said.

    However, the masks only offered protection against infection agents of a certain size.

    ``If it is smaller than that specified, like a virus, we cannot stop the virus. Most of the bacteria we can keep out, but if it is a virus the particle size is smaller, so this mask cannot stop the virus,'' Ho said.

    This verdict is reinforced on the company's website.

    A question and answer page dated last Friday, reads: ``Only [a] certified respirator [mask] and correct usage of [the] respirator can protect [you] against or minimise bacterial infections, but virus cannot be prevented.''

    Despite this, 3M was running its United States factories on a 24-hour cycle to meet demand.

    Masks are reportedly selling at some stores for HK$100, even though Ho said 3M's N95-certified masks were sold for HK$15 each.

    Hong Kong Medical Association president and legislator Lo Wing-lok agreed that viruses could not be screened out by wearing masks. ``But they can stop droplets - the vehicle that carries the virus. As long as the masks can block the vehicle, it can block the passenger - virus - on the vehicle,'' he said.

    However, medical experts in the United States have not ruled out the possibility that the virus is airborne, rather than spread by droplets.

    This belief has been strengthened by the dramatic rise in infections.
