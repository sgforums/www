    Originally posted by ditzy:
    I'm use WI-Fi back in singapore too, but it ain't linksys. Positioning the router isn't a major issue, be it walls or doors. From my own experience, signal strength doesn't really play a big part in getting optimium transfer speeds. Its more inherent on how much data can process in/out of ur router at any one time.

    Anyway, my router is placed on the 2nd floor of the house and my room is on the 3rd, and so far have never come across signals being blocked. Plus, I intercept my neighbour's network at times as well.
    some points to note here:

    1. positioning --> is an issue if the router also acts as an access point for your wireless network. assuming your linksys is a router (R) cum access point (AP). why? b'cos in wireless networks, signal strength is important...which come to the next point.

    2. signal strength --> this will determine how well you are able to receive and transmit your data across the network in your home/house. strong signal = good transmission and receiving...esp. over internet or any data activity between your R and other machines in your home. so, signal strength is a BIG part in having optimum transfer speeds.

    3. data processing --> let's face it, if your router can only accept 10mbps (as compared to 100mbps) then would your data transfer rate or processing be inherent or simplt put it affected? my point here is that your R is only a pass through device to the service provider. see your R as a gateway to the Internet. some R devices come with FW (firewall) as well. in your case, your R is important as it receives signal from your machines/laptop that you have allowed access to this R or AP via your wireless network.

    4. walls and obstacles --> while it is noteworthy that wireless networks have the capability to pass through walls, windows and other objects in the house or office, i encountered that if you are using your laptop behind a wall that is angled or 2 walls behind the R or AP then signal strength will be greatly reduced. this is only logical as signal strength weakens with every passing of walls or objects.

    5. wireless standards --> there're currently diff standards in the mkt. a b g standards....couldn't remember offhand if these are the only ones....standards also got to do with security and compatibility. it is good to know more since u r setting up one at home.

    5. security --> while it is right that ditzy got to "intercept" or intrude which is more appropriate word into his neighbour's network, think of your security for your wireless network. anyone having a wireless network card may gain access to your home wireless network if there is no security measures in place. check whether linksys R has a FW that comes with it. if not, invest in one and also virus scanners a must for your indiv. machines and laptops. check also whether your R can support access control whereby you as the administrator gives access to machines indicated in the network. this will effectively cut off any other machines that tries to come in and use your network resources.

    lastly....no offense ditzy....some of your pointers are not quite accurate.
