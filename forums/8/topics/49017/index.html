    The embattled airline is planning its first retrenchments in 20 years, as it prepares to report quarterly loss for the first time

    By Rebecca Lee and Sue-Ann Chia

    SINGAPORE Airlines is planning a major retrenchment exercise that could lead to the loss of as many as 2,500 jobs.

    The estimate is based on a range of percentage cuts to the group's staff strength of 29,000 being considered.

    Sources told The Straits Times the group is looking at reducing ground staff by between 5 per cent and 10 per cent.

    And it wants to trim its operating crew of 6,600 cabin staff and 1,800 pilots by 2 per cent to 5 per cent.

    Such a range of cuts being considered could mean as few as 1,200 or as many as 2,500 employees being laid off.

    The actual numbers have not been finalised but management may decide as early as this week, said the sources.

    The group's five unions apparently know about the rough size of the cuts but not the exact figures.

    The retrenchments will be the first in 20 years for the group, which includes listed units SIA Engineering and Singapore Airport Terminal Services (Sats).

    SIA spokesman Rick Clements would not be drawn into discussing actual numbers yesterday. He would only say: 'We have made it clear to our staff that some degree of retrenchment cannot be avoided.'

    The move, alongside wage cuts and putting employees on unpaid leave, is part of the airline's cost-cutting and restructuring plan to regain its competitiveness.

    Affected by cost-cutting in the global aviation industry, the carrier was also hit hard by the Sars outbreak. It is set to report its first quarterly loss, after bleeding $204 million in April alone at the height of the outbreak. Last month, passenger numbers plunged 60 per cent, to 506,000, from May last year.

    The Straits Times understands that security concerns mean SIA is likely to give those affected immediate marching orders.

    Meanwhile, a special early retirement package has been offered to selected employees with at least 25 years of service, said Mr Clements.

    Letters were sent to about 100 to 200 employees on Friday, said sources. They have until today to decide whether to accept the voluntary scheme, offering a month's pay for each year of service, up to a cap of 25 months.

    Several union leaders contacted yesterday said they had not been told either of the scheme or the actual retrenchment numbers.

    The 1,600-strong Air Line Pilots' Association Singapore (Alpa-S) also said it was unaware of pilots being offered the special retirement scheme or of the layoffs.

    SIA's dispute with Alpa-S over proposed wage cuts of 15 per cent to 22.5 per cent is before the Industrial Arbitration Court but a hearing date has not been set.

    Meanwhile, SIA is still negotiating with its four other unions on wage cuts of up to 15 per cent for the rest of its non-management staff. But sources said the unions would prefer to wait for the outcome of the Alpa-S arbitration before settling on the terms.

    Yesterday, analysts estimated that $200 million will be saved annually with layoffs of up to 10 per cent.

    And most SIA employees interviewed appeared calm about the layoffs. 'I won't be surprised. The company has been losing money and although they said retrenchment is a last option, they did not rule it out,' said a stewardess.

    An SIA executive said: 'Everyone just wants to know what's going to happen.'
