    There are many and varied.

    In the Old Testsament, where Solomon builds a temple:

    "It was a handbreath in thickness, and its rim was like the rim of a cup, like a lily blossom. It held TWO thousand baths." (NIV 1 Kings 7:26)

    vs.

    "It was a handbreath in thickness, and its rim was like the rim of a cup, like a lily blossom. It held THREE thousand baths." (NIV 2 Chronicles 4:5)

    Also, in the scene where David scores a major victory in Israel:

    "Again the anger of the LORD burned against Israel, and he incited David against them, saying, "go and take a census of Israel and Judah." (NIV, 2 Samuel 24:1)

    vs

    "SATAN rose up against Israel and incited David to take a census of Israel." (NIV, 1 Chronicles 21:1)

    Although it is not specifically mentioned, David counted the fighting men of Israel and Judah to execute them. The numbers between the two narratives do not tally also in 2 Samuel 24:9 and 1 Chronicles 21:5.

    The New Testament is similarly not exempt from contradictions. During Jesus' crucifixion, the Roman guards wet a sponge for Jesus to sate his thirst. In Matthew 27:34, it is said:

    "There they offered Jesus wine to drink, mixed with GALL, but after tasting it, he refused to drink it."

    In Mark 15:23, it is said:

    "Then they offered him wine mixed with MYRRH, but he did not take it."

    Gall is bile, an animal essence, while myrrh is a bitter plant sap.

    Your premise that contradictions only occurs amongst figures and numbers is once again mistaken. The Old Testament specially, is peppered with factual errors regarding, for example, the reigns, and also ages, of various kings. Although the errors are numerical in nature, it is inescapable that they are also event-related.

    One telling example of an event-related discrepancy is found in the New Testament. The area which has generated the most discussion concerns the angels who were present at the tomb of Jesus. The gospels according to Mark and Matthew relate that there was only one angel in the tomb, while Luke and John insist that there were two angels.

    The apologist's explanation for this seeming contradiction is interesting, but I shall leave it out because I'd like to know if your explanation for this tallies with the one I have.
