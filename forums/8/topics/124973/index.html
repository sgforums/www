    April 2, 2005
    Maybank robber gets 4 1/2 years, 9 strokes
    He collapses on hearing sentence and is taken to hospital; judge says stiff penalty needed to protect the public

    By Ben Nadarajan
    DESPITE a heart-rending mitigation plea by a man who tried to rob a Maybank branch last November, a judge dished out a severe punishment to him yesterday.

    District Judge Tan Puay Boon sentenced Brian Khoo, 43, to 4 1/2 years' jail and nine strokes of the cane, after saying that protecting the public was the court's overriding concern.

    Khoo collapsed after hearing the sentence.

    Blind in the right eye and physically weak as a result of being shot twice by a Cisco guard during the attempted heist, Khoo did not hear his sentence when the judge pronounced it.

    He had to turn to the court interpreter to find out what it was.

    But the interpreter could only say 'four years ...' before Khoo gasped in shock.

    'Four years?' he blurted out, before collapsing onto a fellow inmate in the dock.

    The packed public gallery reacted with shock at the collapse, while policemen, lawyers and court staff nearby helped Khoo up.

    The judge adjourned the court session while some police officers rubbed medicated oil on Khoo's forehead. But he remained in a daze.

    An ambulance had to be called, and he was stretchered out of the court and taken to hospital.

    The judge, who took a day to deliberate the sentence, said earlier that he had considered the accused's personal circumstances leading to his attempted stick-up at the Maybank branch in Bukit Timah on Nov 9.

    Khoo had been jobless for 10 months and became increasing depressed because he could not provide for his wife and four children, aged between three and 17.

    In an impassioned plea before the court on Thursday, Khoo moved several people to tears when he expressed remorse for what he did.

    But yesterday, the judge said: 'No banks or financial institutions should be thought of as places where people can resort to obtaining easy money, however difficult their financial positions may be.'

    He added: 'No worker or customer at these places should ever be subject to the terror the staff members and the customer of the bank had felt when the accused committed the robbery.'

    Though sentenced to nine strokes, Khoo may escape caning because of his poor health.

    The judge said he would refer all of Khoo's medical reports to the prison authorities.

    If they decide that Khoo is unfit to be caned, they could refer the matter back to the court, and the judge would then decide whether or not to spare the rod.

    Khoo suffered multiple facial fractures and has two bullet fragments lodged in his head as doctors felt it was too dangerous to remove them.

    Escaping caning may not lighten Khoo's sentence though. Several lawyers told The Straits Times that under the Criminal Procedure Code, the judge could choose to up his jail term by as much as 12 months to 'compensate' for doing away with the caning.
