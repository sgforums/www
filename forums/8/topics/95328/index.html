    GOT DEGREE, NO JOB

    # 2.8M GRADS LAST YEAR
    # FIRMS BECOMING CHOOSY BECAUSE OF LARGE NUMBERS
    # GRADS ALSO SETTLING FOR SMALLER STARTING PAY
    # MUCH TOUGHER JOB INTERVIEWS

    CHINA'S university graduates are struggling to get jobs.
    Although China's economy is growing at breakneck speed, urban unemployment is also increasing.

    A growing number of graduates find that a degree no longer guarantees employment.

    Almost a million students from China's graduating class of 2004 have spent the summer at crowded job fairs, according to The Epoch Times.

    Students are competing with older and more experienced job hunters.

    HIGH REQUIREMENTS

    Mr He Zhang, who was looking for new employees for his logistics company in Beijing, said there are so many people looking for work that companies can afford to be very choosy.

    'Some companies have high requirements,' he said.

    'College students should be fine for them but they raised their standards, asking for university graduates or even postgraduates. So, a lot of university students are shut out.'

    Those university graduates who do manage to find work are often disappointed by their pay checks.

    A study by China's Beijing University says entry-level wages have fallen to an average of US$200 ($33 a month, down from US$250 one year ago.

    What is more, many of the jobs available are seen as poor careers, or offering little chance of advancement.

    Some graduates use a Chinese proverb to explain the dismal employment situation: Too many monks, too little porridge.

    Graduate Xiao Li spent six months looking for a teaching job in Beijing until she settled for a lower-paid position in southern China.

    'Now everyone wants a stable, well-paid job that suits them, it's just impossible!'

    The government has promised to ensure that 70 per cent of nearly three million graduating students will find jobs this year.

    But they are competing with about 24 million urban job seekers.

    And the job market is expected to get worse next year.

    A record number of new graduates, approximately 3 1/2 million, will enter the job market next June.

    Beijing's Ministry of Personnel statistics show that 2.8 million students graduated from China's 1,683 universities and colleges this year, according to the South China Morning Post.

    Miss Xiao Li said that job interviews are more difficult now.

    'We have more written tests, and companies often require second or
    third interviews.

    'You've got to go through a complicated testing process before you are given an offer,' said Mr Liu.

    He now works at Junhe Law Office, one of the leading legal firms in China.

    Another graduate, also named Liu, from Tsinghua's School of Policy and Management, faced the same troubles.

    The 24-year-old wanted to work in China International Capital Corporation, but failed the interview.

    'I got nervous at that time. Very nervous. I don't know why,' he said.

    'Maybe I wanted the job too much.'

    To solve the problem, some education researchers say the Ministry of Education should stop expanding college enrolment numbers.

    But Mr Wan Keliang, vice-chairman of the policy research department of the Chinese Ministry of Personnel, does not agree.

    He admitted unemployment was increasing, but said it didn't mean that there was an oversupply of college students.

    'In the US, people who have received college educations account for 35 per cent of the whole population, and 23 per cent in Japan. In China, the rate is only 5 per cent. So in this sense, China still lacks college students,' he said.
