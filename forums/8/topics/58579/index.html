    OCT 3, 2003
    Top grades don't spell job success
    Tharman says next challenge is to help plug gaps identified by employers; panel set up to review school rankings

    By Sandra Davie
    EDUCATION CORRESPONDENT

    THE next big challenge for Singapore's education system is to turn out young people geared to succeed in a changing work environment.

    Employers have told Acting Education Minister Tharman Shanmugaratnam that straight As alone are no guarantee of success in working life. They want employees who have stretched themselves through co-curricular activities (CCA) or community work, speak well, think on their feet and ask good questions.

    There are important gaps to be plugged but there is no need to overhaul the system, Mr Tharman told about 1,200 school principals, teachers and education specialists at the Ngee Ann Polytechnic.

    Singapore students emerge from the rigorous, competitive system with top grades, but still lack critical skills that are valued by employers.

    Mr Tharman, delivering his first major speech on education, polled several employers and industry leaders for a sense of the challenge facing Singapore schools. The picture which emerged was consistent.

    In the workplace, Singaporeans demonstrated some key strengths: integrity and trustworthiness, a dedication to work and meeting targets, and they are analytical, methodical and focused in getting the job done. The weaknesses covered hard and soft skills, which will continue to be prized in the workplace.

    One employer, Glaxo Smith Kline vice-president Tan Kay Yong, said he found Singaporeans able to 'deliver to promise, but unable to dream beyond the established or to advance the frontiers'.

    Another boss, Venture Corporation chief Wong Ngit Liong, said: 'Singapore staff are more conforming than independent. They are generally not curious enough about most things. If there is no money to be made, most staff do not spend much time on an issue.'

    As one industry leader phrased it: 'Many Singaporeans are put in a position of leadership, but remain taskmasters, not leaders.'

    The system needs to produce people who have more than outstanding academic results. Often, it is people with excellent CCA achievements who generally do very well. It is these activities, done outside the classroom, that provide the best chances to develop resilience, team spirit and leadership, said Mr Tharman.

    The way to go is to boost the CCA experience without adding to students and teachers' workloads.

    Shell's chairman here, Mr Lee Tzu Yang, told him that the best performers after five years on the job are those who are very interested in areas outside their immediate responsibilities. 'They have strong reasoning skills, lateral thinking abilities, and the ability to network with and influence others.'

    Mr Tharman said: 'Every student should have a grounding in the humanities. It will help them develop an ability to deal with ambiguity and situations where there is no right answer.'

    So a high-level committee chaired by a senior education official will study how recognition and rewards can reinforce these directions.

    The committee of principals, teachers and ministry officials will review and enhance the ranking of secondary schools and junior colleges so that it better reflects education's mission and 'the importance of holistic education'.

    Ranking is a vital tool for evaluation and informed action, but it 'only a means and not an end to what we do', and shouldn't be confused with the goal of education, said Mr Tharman.

    Significantly, while he sounded optimistic about the task ahead, underlined in the text of his wide-ranging 18-page text was this: 'We should avoid hubris at all cost, in education as with everything else we do in Singapore.'
