    Forget 21, you become an adult at 35

    VICKY COLLINS

    The Herald, 3 September 2001

    WHEN you become a man, according to the
    Bible, you put away childish things. In the
    modern world, however, there is no longer any
    need - not just yet anyway.

    According to research to be published next
    month, young adults in Britain and America
    are clinging to the freedom of their teenage
    years for longer than ever before, and the
    pressure to set up a home and start a family is
    now a thing of the past.

    Stephen Richardson, a British social
    psychologist based in San Diego, California,
    claims that passing the 21-year-old threshold
    no longer implies adulthood, and that the
    crucial age is now as old as 35.

    The determination to extend the fun years as
    long as possible is epitomised by celebrity
    party-goers like Tara Palmer-Tomkinson who,
    at the age of 30, is showing no sign of settling
    down. However, it is not only the famous who
    want the good times to continue to roll.

    In his report - The Young West: How We Are
    All Growing Older More Slowly - Mr
    Richardson found young adults tended to stay
    with their parents instead of buying their own
    home.

    They spent their money on consumer goods
    such as computers, music systems, and
    accessories.

    The study involved nearly 200 people, mostly
    graduates aged between 20 and 40, whose
    lifestyles were tracked over a five-year period.
    It found that marriage and parenthood were
    also being delayed, leading to what Dr
    Richardson, a 39-year-old Cambridge
    graduate, describes as a generation of
    "fledgling" adults, who only reach full maturity
    at the age of 35.

    "Culturally and psychologically, they become
    what previous generations recognised as fully
    formed adults," he said.

    "Few of us, and I include myself among them,
    really get there before that."

    David Marshall, a 29-year-old engineer from
    Lanark, is one of the increasing number of
    young people determined not to get sucked
    into the responsibilities of adulthood too
    quickly.

    He rents a flat in Glasgow, the city where he
    works, which he shares with two like-minded
    friends.

    He gained a degree in mechanical
    engineering from Nottingham University in
    1995 and then spent a year travelling round
    the world, visiting countries such as Thailand,
    Australia, and America. He now works full-time
    as an engineer in Glasgow.

    He said: "When I was travelling, I said I would
    never do a nine-to-five job but I got sucked into
    it to a certain extent. Even so, I just see it as a
    means to an end."

    That "end" is enjoying life, as far as David is
    concerned. Last weekend, he went shopping
    with his friends and blew
    Â£100 on a new
    steering wheel for their PlayStation - with no
    regrets.

    "If we have got money, we go out and buy toys.

    "I do take my job very seriously and I work hard
    while I am at work but my free time is my own."

    So what about the future? David certainly has
    plans but they have little to do with mortgages
    or taking on any more responsibility.

    He said that, in a few years time, he hoped to
    spend a season working abroad in a
    snow-boarding resort, which would enable him
    to combine work with one of his favourite
    sports.

    "I would hate to get to 40 and think 'I wish I had
    done that when I was young'," he said.

    "I know people in their 20s who are married
    with children and they are just limiting their
    options.

    "I have no intention of getting married in the
    next wee while. I tend to play it day by day - I
    don't have any long-term plans."

    The trend towards young people delaying their
    first foray into home ownership is recognised
    by Stewart McIntosh, a freelance property
    journalist in Glasgow.

    He said: "People are staying at home for
    longer, largely because of changes in social
    values and the debts that people accumulate
    at university.

    "There is also more pressure on people to buy
    cars, which they often need for work, and to be
    better dressed if they work in an office. People
    also want to travel and there are more leisure
    activities that they want to spend their money
    on."

    Paul Freathy, professor of retail management
    at the Institute for Retail Studies at Stirling
    University, feels that it is impossible to
    compare the lifestyles of today's young adults
    and that of their parents or grandparents
    without taking into account the huge changes
    in society over that time.

    He said: "Comparisons of young people today
    with those 20 or 30 years ago are spurious to
    be honest. The amount of disposable income
    young people have nowadays would be
    unimaginable then.

    "You now have the dual income, no children
    phenomenon, which means retail trends are all
    about saving time, rather than saving money.

    "People are also better educated, better
    travelled, and have more choices."
