    AUG 29, 2003
    More women risking late pregnancies

    By Lee Hui Chieh

    NEARLY one in five pregnant women here is over 35, and the trend towards late pregnancies means higher risks for both mum and child.

    In 1985, of those aged between 15 and 45 who gave birth, only eight in 100 were above 35.

    But by last year, the over-35s had more than doubled proportionally to about 19 per cent, said Associate Professor George Yeo, chief of obstetrics and gynaecology at KK Women's and Children's Hospital (KKH).

    He said: 'Most mothers here deliver in their 30s, at the time when they're almost about to stop producing eggs. They still reproduce, but their babies are more likely to be born with deformities.'

    Prof Yeo was speaking at a press conference to introduce a baby-planning programme for couples by Dr Yu Wei Siang, 33, a doctor-turned-entrepreneur.

    Studies show that women older than 45 have a higher than 50 per cent chance of miscarriage, compared with just 10 per cent for those under 35, said Dr Sheila Loh, who heads KKH's reproductive medicine unit.

    Dr Yu said people need to learn how to plan and make lifestyle adjustments to ensure their babies are healthy.

    He is planning a seminar as well as a camp, which will provide would-be parents with information on fertility treatments and tips on the right diet and environment for having babies. 'We are what we eat, and so are our sperm and eggs,' he said.

    Renowned experts, including Australian in- vitro fertilisation pioneer Carl Wood, will speak at the seminar and conduct workshops at the camp.

    The seminar will be held tomorrow at the Swissotel The Stamford from 10am to 4pm and costs $300 a person.

    The two-day camp scheduled for the first week of October costs $550 a person.
