    A huge turnout
    By Samuel Lee

    ZOUKOUT
    NOKIA REMIX
    Last Friday and Saturday
    Marina Bayfront

    IN MANY ways, last weekend's ZoukOut was bigger than its two predecessors.

    Spread over two nights instead of one, the dusk-to-dawn outdoor dance music festival at Marina Bayfront - which overlooks Singapore's skyline - drew a crowd of 3,800 last Friday and 15,000 on Saturday.

    The inaugural ZoukOut at Sentosa's Siloso Beach in December 2000 had attracted 12,000 people. The following November, when it was held at the resort island's Tanjong Beach, 13,000 showed up.

    The cost for staging it has also risen, from $800,000 in 2000, $900,000 in 2001 to $1.5 million last weekend.

    The rise may not have been matched by as big an increase in the turnout, but given the short time the event was put together, Zouk club says it is pleased with the results.

    Marketing manager Tracy Phillips pointed out how the original date for the event was the last weekend of May, but had to be postponed when Sars (severe acute respiratory syndrome) broke out.

    By the time Sars died down, the club had only six weeks to sew up the event, including confirming the line-up of top international acts and DJs.

    This year's event was divided into a live concert on Friday and a dance party from four arenas on Saturday.

    Used to the dance party format, the concert segment threw many off-guard. But the crowd warmed up to the night's line-up of kooky Icelandic electronica group Gus Gus, techno-rockers Dirty Vegas and funky rap merchants Stereo MCs, both from Britain. Gus Gus' blend of techno and house, with members caterwauling songs such as Desire and Israel Is Evil, was mind-numbing and arty, and may not have been everyone's cup of tea.

    But frontman President Bongo won the 20- to 30-something crowd with his cheeky cajoling and the sight of him thrusting a bare beer belly from beneath a suit with pineapple prints.

    He also paved the way for Dirty Vegas and Stereo MCs, which brought up the adrenaline level when they came on later. To reinforce the point that Asian DJs are just as skilled as their more famous Western counterparts, Zouk on Saturday set up the Regional arena. Located just after the entry point, the sounds from the DJs greeted revellers as they entered the 40,000sq m party grounds, but those who stayed on were few and far between.

    The clear crowd favourite was the Global Underground (GU) stage. This was already kicking at 10pm, when Australian DJ Phil K helmed the decks. Following his set were DJs James Lavelle and Sasha at 12.30 and 2am respectively.

    At the Velvet Underground stage around midnight, Japan's Toshio Matsuura had just ended his Latin-infused set with the jazzy number Flying Saucer by the Japanese group UFO featuring Dee Dee Bridgewater.

    He hung around on stage while Blaze, a soulful outfit from the United States, delivered quality house which fellow American Kenny 'Dope' Gonzalez matched beat for beat between 2 and 4am.

    If the Velvet stage was for connoisseurs of deep and sexy sounds, the Mambo Jambo arena was more for mainstream party-goers. Blasts from the past but done with a difference, such as Singaporean Brendon P's calypso remix of Abba's Dancing Queen, were the order of the night.

    By 5am yesterday, most of the crowd had gone home, leaving only about 500 to pogo to Briton Ashley Casselle's sunrise set at the GU stage.

    Apart from memories of delirious dancing to progressive house sounds from Sasha and Lavelle, ZoukOut Nokia Remix will also be remembered as a party where sand did not get between your toes.

    The award goes to...
    By MAY YIP

    MOST Happening Auntie Raver: She is 52, but Music Lee does not let age get in her way. Amid crowds in their late teens to late 30s, she stood out with her blue eye-shadow and violet wraparound skirt, gyrating alone all Saturday night long at the podium of the Global Underground arena.

    Most Pasty Bod: Stereo MCs frontman Rob Birch showed off a typically British haven't-seen-the-sun-in-eons lean white torso.

    Most Hilarious Bod: Gus Gus frontman President Bongo (Stef Stefansson), who bared a bulbous belly beneath a campy suit, was a barrel of laughs.

    Most Clueless Dressers: All glammed-up in stiletto heels and skirts, some women clubbers were seen trying to yank their heels out of the ground, and pulling down their too-short skirts when sitting on the grass.

    Most Indulgent Drink: Miniature bottles of Moet & Chandon champagne at $24, fitted with plastic cup attachments.

    Most Unexpected Celebrity Sightings: Remus Choy from 1990s Hong Kong Canto pop group, Grasshopper, and National Development Minister Mah Bow Tan and his wife, Sheryn.
