I lost the battle, but war goes on

  • cooleo
    4 Aug 04, 11:00


    HE was branded an 'absolute hooligan' by the judge who called his behaviour 'asinine'.

    In the hearts of many Singaporeans glued to the now notorious Everitt Road saga, Chan Cheng Khoon is probably one of Singapore's most dreaded neighbours.

    The 69-year-old was given the maximum fine of $4,000 on Thursday after he was convicted of insulting two female neighbours.

    Despite his conviction, he told reporters outside the court that he considered the fine 'sup sup sui' (Cantonese for a small amount), that it was only a fraction of what he paid his lawyer.

    Indeed, a day after being fined, Chan appeared to be in high spirits when he was interviewed at his home.

    He even joked: 'I lost the case. Why do you want to speak to a loser?'

    For 10 long years, the Chans and their neighbours have been embroiled in a bitter feud which involved spotlights, video cameras, harsh words and lawyers' letters.

    Together with Madam Chua Gek Eng, his wife of 44 years, Chan shared how his life has been since he was cast into the limelight of Singapore's longest-running un-neighbourly dispute.

    Nothing much has changed, he claimed.

    He still sleeps soundly at night (with the floodlights shining on his driveway), dines out with his wife and daughter Soo Yin (a teacher), goes shopping and meets his friends.

    He said: 'Life goes on. Battles are won and battles are lost.

    'I may have lost this battle, but the war goes on,' he said.

    He even goes dancing with his wife.

    Waltz, cha-cha, rhumba, you name it, he says he can do it.

    He loves dancing so much that he would gladly oblige with a little wriggle, a sway of the hips, and even a twirl if you ask him to show you a few moves - pretty much like his famous little jig aired on national TV.

    On being labelled a hooligan, he said: 'I categorically state that I'm not a hooligan and have never behaved like one.'

    ONLY FAMILY, FRIENDS MATTER

    He didn't care what other people said.

    'My wife, my daughter and my friends are all that matters. They've been very supportive and that's what counts,' he said.

    His wife declined to be interviewed.

    After a little probing, Chan admitted being the target of many a wagging finger and tongue. But he insisted it didn't rattle him one bit.

    'If I was made to feel uncomfortable by those gestures, I wouldn't even step out of the house,' he said.

    b> 'I'm determined to remain cheerful.'

    In fact, he went so far as to say that he's become a 'celebrity' of sorts.

    He said, with a hearty laugh: 'I think I'm even more recognisable than some of the ministers.

    'I've heard that when my picture is put on the front page of newspapers, it will sell very well!'

    So, what next?

    Chan said he won't be selling his house to start afresh somewhere else, as that would be a sign of 'cowardice'.

    'But I can't say that I wouldn't sell if someone was to give me an offer which is too good to turn down,' he said.

    For now, his motto is to just take one step at a time - while the spotlights continue to shine and the video cameras continue to whirr.

    'I won't allow myself to be bullied,' he said. 'I'm not the sort to turn the other cheek after being slapped on one cheek.

    'I'll slap the person back.'

    Modesty offence tough to prove

    CHAN was given the maximum fine of $4,000 for insulting his two woman neighbours because he was prosecuted under the Miscellaneous Offences (Public Order and Nuisance) Act.

    However, under the Penal Code, there is a tougher penalty - up to one year's jail for anyone found guilty of ''insulting a woman's modesty'. But such an offence is harder to prove.

    Lawyer Amolat Singh explained: 'Under the Miscellaneous Offences Act, all the prosecutor is required to prove is that the obscene gestures were done or that obscene words were uttered.

    'So the moment you do that in public, the offence has already been committed.'

    But the process is more complex for offences under section 509 of the Penal Code.

    Then, you have to show that the person intends to insult a woman's modesty by making obscene signs or saying certain things.

    For instance, it will be difficult to prove that there is intention to insult a woman's modesty by calling her a 'butch'.

    Chan was caught on camera telling one of the women: 'Butch, put the camera between your legs.'

    But lawyer Lim Kia Tong said: 'I don't think calling a woman a butch and asking her to put a camera between her legs is tantamount to insulting the modesty of a woman.

    'One will have to show that there's an intention to outrage the woman's modesty by those words.'
