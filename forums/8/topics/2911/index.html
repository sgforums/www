    gee....a sudden flurry of replies to this one. wonder why. anyways, since it's easy to get lost in the maze of all these replies, i shall limit myself only to the original post and offer ONE (rather simplistic) perspective on the issue at hand. since i am no expert in the details of a particular faith, i shall leave all of that to the posts that appear above me. they seem to have thot abt these things more than i ever will. but i wonder how many ppl are actually reading these replies. ah well.....

    let's start the way all good story books do.....once upon a time. once upon a time, ppl lived in a world different from the one we live in today. the world they lived in was one where science and religion and what have you were not separated. in fact, there prob wasn't the notion of science at all, at least not the way it is conceived today. ppl had a close r'ship with the earth they lived on and wondered about the heavens above. they postulated a spiritual realm and heavenly beings different from you and i. it was a world of harmony with the environment and a world where the human spirit was less divided and more at peace with itself. things were simpler back then.

    now even in those days there were all these natural calamities (droughts, famine, and so on), and at some point ppl began equating these calamities with heavenly beings. "did i somehow anger the gods?", they may have asked themselves. and so rituals of atonement and sacrifice began in the hope of appeasing these insatiable gods (maybe gods of the earth, of the river or whatever, gods responsible for particular parts of nature). things went on like this for long periods of time, where ritual became an impt part of ensuring good harvests, successful births and a peaceful and prosperous existence.

    slowly but surely, the notion of good and evil was born. gosh, why are we having such bad luck wif the crops this year? we must have done something "wrong". quick! what do we have to do (what do we have to do "right") to chase all this misfortune away? more sacrifices? more attention to rituals? what is impt abt this period of time, however, is not the myths or the beliefs or the legends. it is the way ppl SAW the world and reacted to it. it was a spiritual world, a mythical world, a magical world. there was no sobering effect of cold, calculative science.

    i don't know how it happened, but apparently one of the first signs of a mono-theistic religion came from the philosophy of the persian prophet, zoroaster or zarathustra. there was still nature worship at this point in time, but the focus was on the worship of a single God. This god (which they called Ahura Mazda - yes, i know it sounds like a car) the zoroastrians credited wif creating the world and the lesser dieties.

    the basic idea, however, is this: that there were many gods and goddesses but some were preferred to others. eventually the idea of a single, all-powerful God emerged and this mono-theism is the basis of the three major religions of the western world, viz, Judaism, Christianity and Islam.

    but what functions did these religions serve? well just abt everything. it taught ppl how to think abt the world, what was "true" abt the world and how to act in the world. religion was, quite simply, the whole interpretation of the world.

    my thesis (or rather my opinion of how things turned out) is this: that there is a direct correlation between what we "know" and how we "act". remember this now, cos it is the crucial point. human beings are often such that we will only "act" in a certain way if we "know" a certain thing.

    and so you have the core (and the psychology) behind the major religions today. they offer you a story of how the world began, what it is like and how it will proceed (this corresponds to what we yearn to "know"). and then only if you buy (or more neutrally, believe) the cosmogony presented, will you then be prepared to "act" in a certain way.

    the basic themes of religion follow thus: there is the original sin or suffering in the world. basically, all this sin and suffering is your fault. now if u carry on this way, there will be a certain ending for you. so if u don't want to end up that way, you better do this and act like that and think like this. so there we have the two crucial components - there is the story (what u need to "know" and believe) and then there is the "act" (which you better do if u want salvation. whether this means that religion is ultimately based on fear and self-interest and preservation, i leave that to you).

    these two crucial components correspond to two major facets of human life - our search for "knowledge" and our modes of "conduct and behaviour". there is a third component served by religion - the "spiritual". this corresponds to our search for the meaning of life. it differs from the quest for knowledge in this impt respect - while knowledge seeks "clarification", the spiritual caters to our need for "edification". the "spiritual" is related to our complex of emotions and feelings.

    okay, now i drag in science. under its own quest for the "truth", religion (in particular Judeo-Christianity) ironically planted the seeds of its own demise.

    in the quest for the "truth", the methods of science were born. now science, and not religion, serves our need for "knowledge". but then what have we done? "God is dead", Nietzsche said, "and we have killed him".
    how have we killed him? with disbelief. but why do we now not believe? because, i venture to conclude, we have taken away the source of that belief. we have replaced the religious story abt how the world is like and have placed our faith in science to tell us a new story. now the link between "action" and "knowledge" is broken. why should i "act" in a religious manner if the basis for that action (my "belief" in a religious world order) has been shaken and replaced by a scientific, rationalistic faith? from now on even right or wrong actions have been secularised. and, in the spirit of the Enlightenment, now even human actions are seen as being more appropriately ruled by "reason" (as compared to revelation), in the same way the world is revealed to us with the incisiveness of scientific logic and reason. this weakening of the basis of all human action, this attack on the religious bed-time story is responsible for the current sorry state of religion in the modern, scientific world.

    unless u are quite a creative interpreter, the scientific, darwinian picture of the world cannot be reconciled with any traditional religious doctrine. there is still the possiblity of a God even with science around. but that God cannot be the same one as is told by the major religions of the world.

    I am not saying that science is right and religion wrong. I am merely giving my view of why there is an obvious decline of religious sentiment in our time. Why knowledge and action are now secularised and humanised.

    You only need to go thru the basic problems in the philosophy of science to realise the limits of science. Yes, I believe science is a faith like religion, but of quite a different kind. We have perhaps too much faith in what is measurable and quantifiable, and omit the value and importance of what is not discoverable by science. Why do we think that all there is in the world can only be discovered by our five senses and by human reasoning? Even scientific predictions rely on notions of inductive reasoning that cannot guarantee us the truth. The prime example is that, inductively, the sun has always risen every morning. But what makes us think it will rise again tomorrow? There is no absolute certainty that it will. We just have faith that it will because of past exp. Same problem for cases of causality. If this doesn't make sense to you, well it doesn't matter. You need a full course in the philosophy of science to appreciate the problems and the limitations of reason for discovering the "truth".

    Even key notions and assumptions of science vary such that it no longer is the case that we "discover" reality but that we "create" it. For those interested, read Thomas Kuhn. He'll tell you how the history of science progresses according to revolutions in thinking. from the Newtonian science to the theory of relativity, how we see the world has depended on our basic assumptions abt that very world. It seems as tho if we change our assumptions, we can create a whole new reality. That's how we moved from a Newtonian concept of space and time as absolute to Einstein's view that it is flexible, malleable and changeable according to your frame of reference. so do not be deceived abt it. there are limits to human reasoning and science, just as there have been limitations placed on what religion can tell us.

    To recap, there are three main areas that religion used to take care of - the realm of "knowledge and truth", the field of appropriate "human conduct and action" and the domain of "spirituality". The first two areas have been thoroughly humanised. but there is still the third (and we have forgotten abt this third domain). My view is that our relentless pursuit of science and technology on the one hand and the capitalist mode of economic production on the other, have led to a spiritually impoverished state of affairs. Human beings aren't meant to live means-to-ends according to practical affairs only. We aren't meant to pursue riches and success at the expense of our spirituality. The human animal has lost its sense of the meaning of life. It stands in dire need of some form of edification and comfort. With the cold, calculative progression of science has come an emptiness and hollowness in our relation to the world, in our understanding of life. And I believe it is the place of religion (religion as the third domain - the domain of spirituality) to fill this void. As someone rightly pointed out, religion is abt a "relationship" to a self or being higher than us, bigger than us. It does not matter if reason cannot prove the existence of god. In fact the whole relationship of having to believe the "story" first before you act in a certain way should be challenged. Why should knowledge be primary? In matters of religion, the "relationship" is what is impt and primary (and sacred). I believe there is a place for religion even in the world of science. Because science cannot give to you the meaning and the purpose of life.
