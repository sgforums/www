    But there are a few inspirational books that I would like to introduce to you guys/gals if you are interested. One of my favourite authors so far on this topic is still Robert T Kyosaki. His first book strike quite a big controversial in Singapore and is eventually not encourage as a good book to read in Singapore due to the polictically incorrect title call, "If you want to be rich and famous, do not go to school.", that was the first book, then the second is titled "Rich Dad, Poor Dad", it's contents are rather thought provoking to those who are not so financially intelligence trained.     But do allow me to share some of my own thoughts on financial planning with you all... if you all do not find me a bore.

    Part 1
    Why have fianancial Planning?

    How many of you out there are going to bank on your CPF savings after you retire? If most of you are raising your hands, I can't help but think that you should give it second thoughts. Well, look at it this way, how much are you going to have in your CPF account when you retire at the age of 55?

    Some simple calculations, say through out your life you earn a average of $3000/mth. And you start working seriously at the age of say 22 No share or stocks options, no insurance, etc.

    Your personal CPF contribution(22%) = $660 per month

    Your employer CPF Contribution(20%) = $600 per month

    Total CPF contribution = $1260 per month

    The figures for CPF contribution might flacuate but I kept it simple for calculation purposes.

    55 - 22 = 33 yrs

    33 X 12 = 396 months

    Total CPF Contributions when you retire = 396 X $1260 = $498 960.00

    Now that would be quite a sizeable sum of money you think, but now some more calculations. Say at 28 you got married and you brought a 4 rm flat in say Queenstown, the valuation of the flat is say, $300,000 all deducted from your CPF. But that would not be the figure to deduct of your CPF, for you still need to calculate interest.

    A loan of 25 yrs, at the discounted interest rate of 2.5% per Annum would = 62.5% of interest. You would need to pay the 20% depsotie from your CPF first, and that would be, $60,000 of your account first, the loan would then be for $240,000.

    Interest for load = ($240,000/100) X 62.5 = $150,000

    Thus totla required to pay back to CPF = $240,000 + $150,000 = $390,000

    So the total cost of the flat would be = $60,000(20% initial payment) + $390,000(Loan + interest) = $450,000.

    So just after the housing loan how much do you have left?

    $498,960(total in CPF at 55) - $450,000(housing) = $48,960

    I have to remind you that I have yet taken any money out for the medi-save, medi-save++ and such.

    Now say you live until the ripe age of 70. That would be 15 years after retirement.

    15 X 12 = 180 months

    $48,960/180 = $272 per month

    Now tell me ladies and gentleman, I am doing a very raw calculation with a lot of other expenses not included and at high CPF contribution from employers, and yet, you can only have $272 per month to get by if you lived till 70, tell me if it would be enough? Now you know why people still work after retirement? Not so much that they want to pass time, but seriously they might not have enough. And those who go out and blow their CPF on some new car once they retire would be the stupidest of the lot, for most probably there would not have the money for the fuel left.

    Till the next part.
