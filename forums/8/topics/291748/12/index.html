    Originally posted by ami128:
    I read it. But how does one come up with the numbers? Like say 50% chance of 10% return...is this guesswork based on the investors gut feeling?
    ok there are two schools of thought on this.

    Prior to the computer boom, investors used to invest with their gut based on the limited information available.

    Presently, the new wave of young traders are using computer simulations based on the monte carlo technique. Using computers to crunch vast amounts of statistical information, they try to approximate the odds to get a mathematical value.

    Both methods have their flaws, but they're essentially trying to estimate odds. In an imperfect information system, it's never possible to correctly calculate your odds accurately. That is why it's easy to write programs that will always beat human players in suduku, harder in chess, and impossible in poker.

    In the firm I work at, we crunch historical data, run it through our proposed trading strategy, and find the odds of each perceivable outcome. It's a complicated process we leave to the computer programs.

    So like i said, odds is the holy grail of all investments. It's probably never going to be possible for us to nail it down to a specific number. What we get currently is a range of possible values. Andrew might have you thinking that the old school investors never had these resource at their disposal when they traded, but they were still successful. And in all truthfulness, they may not even know they are estimating odds. But whenever they make a decision, they really are doing so based on their memory of past experience.

    So the simpler answer to your question is : for smaller investors, you mainly have to rely on your gut. Big institutions nowadays use computers to crunch data to justify their actions based on odds. You can actually get in trouble in an audit if you cannot prove sufficiently that you were playing the odds, and not just blind gambling.

    ok i'm going to ignore Andrew until he comes up with new/better points from here on out. Replying to the same unoriginal insults for the 12th time is getting old.

    But i'll elaborate a little here. If you want to make money from investments, odds are everything. If you want to avoid going broke, bankroll management is important. Risk vs Reward. The age old question of finding the balance.

    What andrew is trying to do is confuse the innocent readers that bankroll management has anything to do with deciding what to invest in. It doesn't. Bankroll management is the check in place to keep you from danger against the huge variance in the market.

    Kind of like a car. You want the car to bring you from point A to point B in the quickest time. But you still want to have that handbrake there to save you from yourself.

    Bankroll management isn't even essential for making investments. Big firms leverage(going against core principles in bankroll management) their investments all the time. They accept the huge risk in favor of bigger rewards because they can absorb the damage when push comes to shove. It is however recommended that private investors exercise it though.

    There's a concept in professional gambling that can be used to illustrate the situation here. It's called playing with scared money.

    If you're playing with money you NEED(eg school fees, daily essentials, rent), you need to seriously consider and minimise your risk. If you have 200k, and you need to spend 2k a month, investing with 20k is perfectly fine. It's not 'scared money'. A loss will hurt, but it won't cripple you. If you only have 3k in your account and you need 2k this month, it's a terrible idea to invest that 2k, even though the odds are the same as before(and let's assume extremely profitable).

    The dangers of playing with scared money is obvious. As i have pointed out in a previous post, we as humans can try to play the odds all we want, but eventually we are still guided by our emotions. When you enter the market with a strategy, you have to follow it through. You know your odds, and you know you have a positive expectation only if you follow it through. That's easy to do if you're not playing with scared money. What happens when you are?

    Again let's use the 2k scenario. You have 3k, invest 2k and need 2k at the end of the month. You effectively have only 1k of liquidity left, which is not enough. Assuming you enter at the beginning of the month expecting to cash out profitably at the end of the month, various things could happen to make you second guess yourself unneccessarily if you are playing with scared money

    1) the value of your investment dips momentarily. Scared money usually exit here to "play safe"

    2) the value of your investment increases faster than you expect, but does not hit your expected return yet. There's another week to go, and it could still hit the projected value. Scared money will often take the money here and cash out, thinking they have made a handsome profit. In reality, they have lost expected returns. If they do this often, they can expect to actually lose money in the long run

    3) It's nearing the end of the month, and the price hasn't moved. You need the money soon. The odds tell you you're still a favourite to make a profit, but you lack the time. You cash out, and the price rises to your expected value the following week. This is the most dangerous of all. Because it drives people crazy. Crazy people do not follow the odds. They curse the heavens and start buying without consultation of the odds. This is how most people who don't practice bankroll management usually end up broke.

    That's all I have for this topic at this point. I await Andrew to point out i'm backpedaling by preaching another concept other than odds calculations, but i urge everyone to keep in mind that bankroll management has nothing to do with how you invest. It should not affect the strategy of investment. All it affects is how much you invest. Too many people have confused the two when they are playing with scared money.
