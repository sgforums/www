    Just the news clip from Straits Times Interactive:
    Siamese twins' op enters Day 5
    By Salma Khalik
    HEALTH CORRESPONDENT

    SINGAPORE -- Surgeons are entering the fifth day of continuous work to separate Siamese twins Ganga and Jamuna, in an operation that started at 4 pm on Friday.

    They are in the final stages of the marathon
    operation now.

    At early this afternoon, with 80 per cent of the surgery was done, the surgeons were facing a highly tricky part - deciding how to work on a mass of critical arteries and veins that both girls need.

    As these are blood vessels, the team's neurologist cannot test which part of the body they affect, and who needs them more.

    So, the team has been clamping the vessels and releasing the clamps to give other vessels time to take over the carriage of blood to and from the brain. Once they have, cutting the vessels should not do any harm.

    But the longer the girls are on the operating table, the greater the risk of infection. Another concern is the amount of drugs in their bodies as a result of keeping them anaesthetised for so long.

    There is also a limit as to how long the children can remain under sedation. But no one is hazarding a guess as to what this limit is.

    So far, however, the children have been holding their own. Their blood pressures, respiration and heart beats are all stable.

    Dr Keith Goh, one of the two main neurosurgeons leading the surgical team, left the operating theatre at 9 pm last night to give the anxious family a progress report.

    He told them that, as the condition of the two 11-month babies were stable, the surgeons are comfortable about giving the brains of the two girls more time to adjust to the separation.

    But they are monitoring the infants' condition closely.
