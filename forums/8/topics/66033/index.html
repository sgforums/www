    After Shankar case, men play it safe
    They are more wary of women at parties after the ex-TV presenter was found guilty of molestation

    By Li Xueying

    LEERY, not leering.

    In the wake of the high-blitz publicity given to former Channel News Asia TV presenter Vidya Shankar Aiyar's court trial, some men are pussyfooting around women they meet at parties.

    Mr Raj Arumugam, 36, a special events manager, is one of them.

    'It's made me more wary. Even if I meet someone nice... if she is drunk, let her go home in a cab by herself.

    'This case has taught me to mind my own business. It's really scary how things can turn out. Better to be safe than sorry.'

    Last Tuesday, Shankar, 37, was found guilty of molesting a 30-year-old woman in November 2002 after a housewarming party.

    She was drunk when they left the party together. He took her to his apartment in a taxi and the next morning, the woman said she awoke to find the both of them naked in his bed.

    He had helped her out of her clothes and while she was getting dressed in the morning, he molested her, she said.

    On Tuesday, Shankar will find out whether he is to be jailed and caned or fined.

    The men interviewed said the case had definitely made men more careful about how they behave both at and after a party.

    'They're thinking twice before inviting women to supper after parties,' said Mr Hamish Brown, 40, creative director of an events company and a Gold 90.5 FM deejay.

    But he thinks that this, too, will pass.

    'You can't stop hormones,' he added with a wry chuckle. 'At the end of the day, we're social creatures.'

    Apparently, women have also drawn lessons from the case.

    Mr Raj, an inveterate party-goer, said he sees women being more aloof to men who try to chat them up.

    'Women are going to parties with other women, for safety, and sticking to their own friends,' he said.

    Some don't even party anymore, he said, claiming that he knows of five parties cancelled this festive season because too few of the women invited would agree to go.

    On a personal level, he said, one woman he usually banters with freely during parties has turned distant.

    'We first met two years back and always had very good conversations at parties - about relationships and so on. But when I met her at a Christmas Eve house party last year, she didn't want to talk to me. There was suddenly a distance, a barrier.

    'It's not fair. We men are being penalised for one guy's mistake,' he said.

    Indeed, some women view the case as a reminder to watch their body language, so that others don't read it wrongly.

    Miss Li Meixian, 25, a research executive, said: 'It's made me more conscious about the signals I send out. I also won't allow a man to send me home the first time I meet him.'

    But there are those who pooh-pooh the idea that they need a court case to learn how to handle themselves.

    Said Miss Edlyn Giam, 27, a trainee executive: 'I'm always very responsible when I go partying. I make sure I'm with my large group of friends, and when I'm high, they send me home.'

    Added account executive Kelvin Woo, 27: 'I always take care of myself, even while picking up girls. The key is in moving slowly and watching out for tell-tale signs, to see if they're okay about it.

    'After all, why lose my freedom over a chick?'
