    Letter from Matthew Yim Yew Fei
    Letter from Steven Lo Chock Fei

    I wish to raise some legal concerns over Odex's crackdown on anime pirates..
    After Odex's failure to get a court order against Pacific Internet, the firm invited executives from Japanese anime firms like Toei to come to Singapore to defend its actions. In an interview, Odex's founders, Mr Peter Go and Mr Stephen Sing, vowed to continue their crackdown. However, certain legal issues relating to such a crackdown need to be resolved.
    .
    Odex is just a local distributor of anime in Singapore. It is unlike the Business Software Alliance (BSA) or the Composers and Authors Society of Singapore (Compass).
    .
    When the BSA legally settles and receives compensation from somebody who had used use pirated software, it is taken that the culprit has paid his or her dues to the legal owners since the BSA has links to major software firms. Likewise for music infringement, Compass acts on behalf of the many music composers and publishers, saving the wrong-doer from further legal burden unless he commits another infringement.
    .
    When a person who illegally downloads settles with Odex, is he or she still liable for other lawsuits if other anime distributors and production houses also wish to sue? Can a Japanese production company like Toei still sue the person over other downloaded anime which the firm owns but which Odex does not have the licence to distribute?.

    If Odex does not have the legal right on behalf of other firms, families who have settled may face further lawsuits and possible financial ruin because of the foolish act of the children.
    .
    A Government agency like the Intellectual Property Office of Singapore should look into this to reach a deal for everyone.
    .
    Odex said that it would donate any excess compensation to charities. My question to it: Why can't Odex be charitable to those families with mitigating reasons and let them off with a stern warning?.

    In the recent court case where Odex was seeking details of those who downloaded anime illegally, District Judge Earnest Lau said that "for the SingNet case, the court order was made by consent" and that "SingNet did not even appear in court".
    .
