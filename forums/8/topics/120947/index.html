    March 5, 2005
    Lifeline for struggling HDB businesses
    By Lynn Lee

    LAST Sunday, hairdresser Madam Shu Mee Ying, 60, made less than $20 at a salon she has been running for 34 years on Mei Ling Street. At the barber's shop next door, her husband Mr Thia Mok Chui, 63, fared hardly better.

    But both are clinging on. Their two long-time employees would have no work if their shops folded, they said. Besides, the couple cannot imagine doing anything else.

    It's a familiar story among shopkeepers running the 15,200 Housing Board shops here: They want to get on with their trade but the going is tough.

    Despite makeovers and lifelines, small shops here are in big trouble.

    An estimated 4,000, mainly those closeted in the heart of HDB neighbourhoods, are sinking deeper into the red each day, said the general manager of the Retail Promotion Centre, Mr Chng Hoon Hoon.

    The Government will unveil a help package next week during the ongoing Budget debate. This past week, a number of MPs stood up in Parliament to stress the importance of helping this group.

    Among them was Nominated MP Eunice Olsen. She spoke of the fond memories many Singaporeans have of buying snacks such as flavoured ice and 'Mamee' noodles from their neighbourhood shops.

    She added: 'HDB retailers are very much a part of our national fabric and this initiative is an example of what Singaporeans need to see to believe that we are building a society where every citizen belongs.'

    Iconic of heartland living, HDB shops once served the myriad needs of residents well. But now many play second fiddle to bigger and brighter suburban malls.

    Can their woes be fixed? Or will more of them be relegated to childhood memories?
