    Matrix's third ends it all with a dull thud

    By Ong Sor Fern

    THE MATRIX REVOLUTIONS (PG)
    131 minutes
    Opens today
    ***

    THIS movie is less a revolution than a routine. What was once awe-inspiring and fresh has now become familiar.

    The good news is that Revolutions is better sustained in both tone and delivery than the alternately stodgy and frenetic Reloaded.
    WHY THE LONG FACES: It's just routine stuff for Moss, Fishburne and Reeves.

    The bad news is that by the time you get to the end of this movie, it just all seems like a storm in a cyber-teacup.

    Literally too, since the climactic sequence is a no-holds-barred, CGI-heavy fight between Neo (Keanu Reeves) and Agent Smith (Hugo Weaving) on a set designed by someone who has imbibed a bit too much Bladerunner and Edward Bulwer-Lytton. The latter is the infamous man who coined the cornerstone cheesy phrase: 'It was a dark and stormy night.'

    This movie picks up from where the second left off. So there is a whole lot of action as Zion gets invaded by the machines.

    Neo, on the other hand, is in a coma after discovering that his cyber-prowess also works in the real world. He later wakes up in an antiseptic train station - the Wachowski brothers' thinly-disguised version of the familiar 'limbo as waiting room' concept. And thin seems to be the keyword here.

    The first Matrix was an explosive synthesis of pop philosophy, science fiction, anime and comic influences. It appeared to have been created in a white flame of divine pulp inspiration fuelled by too many collegiate cramming sessions, all-night gaming and gongfu marathons.

    It was a canny and cunning piecing together of pieces of borrowed finery. It worked because it was inventive and novel.

    But attempts at broadening this canvas have only emphasised how thin the Wachowskis' material really is. While The Matrix was sparely economical, thereby suggesting more than it really meant, its sequels have spelt out simplistic ideas with dismaying obviousness.

    Reloaded lurched unevenly between Philosophy 101-cum-Exposition 102 and over-the-top CGI action sequences.

    While Revolutions does not have the turgid speeches that sound like undergraduates cranking out half-understood essays on existentialism, it still has people speaking...like...this, as if talking slowly will invest rather mediocre dialogue with portent.

    It just sounds like everyone's had too much Prozac.

    Perhaps realising that the pop philosophy did not go over too well with the average Joes the last time around, the Wachowskis have chosen to concentrate on the love story between Neo and Trinity (Carrie-Anne Moss).

    This is a safe, if schmaltzy, bet although fans may mourn the downplaying of the grandiose Morpheus (Laurence Fishburne). Similarly, Agent Smith, so terrifyingly unstoppable the first time around, has degenerated into a toothily snarling caricature.

    Still, credit is due to the writing/directing duo for wrapping up their loose ends neatly, without any gaping plot holes to irk hardcore fans.

    This is no Neo wave like the pop cultural tsunami of the first Matrix. But it would have been nice to have ended the trilogy with a big bang, instead of a dull thud.
