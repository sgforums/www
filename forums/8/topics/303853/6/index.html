    We once used to think that dinosaurs were giant lizards.

    Now, as we learn more, we realize that dinosaurs are more closely related to birds than to dinosaurs.

    With that new insight, we now look at how we assembled dinosaur bones before. We assembled those bones based on different theories and assumptions. So now, we try to see if we can assemble the bones in a manner consistent with the new theory.

    if we can't, it's a strike against the new theory. If we can, it doesn't mean the new theory is 100% true. But it just means that the bones don't rule out the possibility of the new theory being true. So the bones become inconclusive as evidence because it supports both theories.

    If you have overwhelming support for a theory - tons of evidence from different fields, all pointing to the same conclusion, and you find one piece of evidence that contradicts it, is it more likely your entire theory is wrong? Or that you might be misinterpreting the evidence. So you try to find out if there's a way to interpret the evidence to fit the theory. If you find it's impossible, then it's a threat.

    And the power of peer-review means that you have to explain what assumptions you made, and why it's reasonable to make those assumptions. Peer-review means that if you try to bullshit, your equally competent peers will catch you on it.

    So let's say you find fragments of a skull and you are trying to reassemble it. Every skull fragment fits perfectly with other skull fragments. Not only that, the sheer lines also match perfectly. The bone structure at the end of each fragment also matches ajoining fragments. Clearly, there's only one way to assemble the skull. If you assemble it any other way, people will ask you why.

    However, let's say you find fragments of skull. The fragments have been fossilized and worn away. Nothing really matches except you can tell by the general shape that it's probably from the same skull. So you try to assemble the skull based on your theories of what you are supposed to find. Then, another theory is raised. So then you try to see if you can reassemble it in a way that fits the other theory. The controversy then is which is the right way to assemble the skull, not whether the overall theory is wrong.

    Evidence from geographical distribution. Again, this is how we see evidence from many fields of science converge. For example, we know that once a group of creatures is isolated from another group, it will start to go down a separate evolutionary path. We can look at the continental distribution of animals and find evidence for migration and isolation. We know that continental drift occurs and we can separately date when land masses separated and combined. Oceanic island distribution also supports evolutionary theory.     A false dichotomy is when you think there are only two possible answers, when there are actually more.
