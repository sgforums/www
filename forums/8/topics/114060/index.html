    Dr. Arun Gandhi, grandson of Mahatma Gandhi and founder of the M.K. Gandhi Institute for Non-violence, in his June 9 lecture at the University of Puerto Rico, shared the following story as an example of "non-violence in parenting":

    "I was 16 years old and living with my parents at the institute my grandfather had founded 18 miles outside of Durban, South Africa, in the middle of the sugar plantations. We were deep in the country and had no neighbours, so my two sisters and I would always look forward to going to town to visit friends or go to the movies.

    One day, my father asked me to drive him to town for an all-day conference, and I jumped at the chance. Since I was going to town, my mother gave me a list of groceries she needed and, since I had all day in town, my father ask me to take care of several pending chores, such as getting the car serviced. When I dropped my father off that morning, he said, 'I will meet you here at 5:00 pm, and we will go home together.'

    After hurriedly completing my chores, I went straight to the nearest movie theatre. I got so engrossed in a John Wayne double-feature that I forgot the time. It was 5:30 before I remembered. By the time I ran to the garage and got the car and hurried to where my father was waiting for me, it was almost 6:00.

    He anxiously asked me, 'Why were you late?' I was so ashamed of telling him I was watching a John Wayne western movie that I said, 'The car wasn't ready, so I had to wait,' not realizing that he had already called the garage. When he caught me in the lie, he said: 'There's something wrong in the way I brought you up that didn't give you the confidence to tell me the truth. In order to figure out where I went wrong with you, I'm going to walk home 18 miles and think about it.'

    So, dressed in his suit and dress shoes, he began to walk home in the dark on mostly unpaved, unlit roads. I couldn't leave him, so for five-and-a-half hours I drove behind him, watching my father go through this agony for a stupid lie that I uttered.

    I decided then and there that I was never going to lie again. I often think about that episode and wonder, if he had punished me the way we punish our children, whether I would have learned a lesson at all. I don't think so. I would have suffered the punishment and gone on doing the same thing. But this single non-violent action was so powerful that it is still as if it happened yesterday. That is the power of non-violence. "

    Allow me to give the perspective as a father of two sons.

    I believe in caning. I believe in punishment..... before someone scream child abuse, let me explain.

    In the course of a child's development ie, from infancy, to toddler, to young school-going and teenager, one can correctly assume that in each stage, a child's understanding and perception of issues continue to grow. It is also a built-in mechanism of each child to test and try the boundaries. Like it or not, boundaries exist, be it a strictly regimented regime or a by-and-large freedom of choice.

    Take a toddler for instance, this child has no concept of right or wrong, only what interests him. It can be pushing the buttons of daddy's hifi system or sticking his little fingers into the VCR slot. In itself, such acts are NOT morally wrong, but they can be dangerous and also socially unacceptable (eg, kids pressing all the buttons in an elevator). The child has to learn (perhaps the hard way) that it is an unacceptable behaviour. The child will NOT be able to comprehend the WHY.

    Eventually this reinforces a code of conduct, ie one that states that the child should not put his little fingers into fan grills etc.

    At this point, if needed, a gentle smacking will reinforce the concept that something is unacceptable.

    When the child grows older, he will find himself in situations where a wrong decision is made (intentionally or not). I'd give him a fair warning and explain that it is wrong and ask him not to repeat his mistake. If he repeats the mistake intentionally, a certain amount of punishment is meted on the child. It can be in the form of a stroke of cane on the palm or a withdrawal of certain privileges.

    I will state categorically that I have found that my method works. I have been told by my sons' teachers and my friends that my boys are extremely focussed, well behaviour, and self-motivating.

    Did I cane them when they were younger? Yes. But I also spoke to them WHY a punishment was meted. Do I cane them NOW. NO! It is because their ability to comprehend the reasons for those punishment and they have the wisdom not to repeat their mistakes.

    Let me also state that I do not support any parents who relentless hit out at their children when in anger. I have NEVER meted out any punishment when I am angry. Reason is simple, punishment meted out in emotion is only a channel to vent out the frustration. It is NOT done in love. When I am angry, I walk away till my anger subsides, then I come back to enforce the punishment.

    Anyone out there supporting the non-cane option, it is entirely up to you. I will not disagree with your methods. But caning is NOT violence. When done right, it is one of the ways that a child can learn.

    Granted, if the non-caning works, it is all the more better. But more often than not, we see kids misbehaving in public and parents turn their blind eyes to such behaviours under the excuse of letting the kids be kids. I beg to differ, kids can be responsible and well- behaved kids, yet can still have that innocence in them.

    I know that for a fact as I have two sons who are student leaders who had gone through a bit of "hell" from me. And before anyone questions the level of our relationship: we enjoy a thoroughly loving relationship, we pursue our hobbies together, laugh at comedy together, learn from each other, and respect each other.

    All said and done, there is no PERFECT approach. It all depends on the dynamics of the situation. There is the offence, the perpetrator and lastly the executioner.

    Some of the punishments that I had meted out does not involve the cane at all. I have never caned my sons for having unsatisfactory results in spite of hard work put in. There should be the focus of the objective of the punishment. In the above situation, good effort was put in but due to some other reasons, the desire result was not achieved. I'd focused on how to get the child to perform better.

    If in the case of being punished for hiding the homework for the purpose of playing computer games, then the emphasis for me is to communicate to the child is his accountability to his school and his teacher. I would not even enter into a situation to bargain for game time after homework is done. I may even remove the chance for the child to play computer game (even permanently) whether there is homework or not. The concept of cause and effect has to be taught to a child at a very young age.

    My two sons are alone at home in the afternoon and they know that their top priority is to finish off their homework. No gaming is allowed. TV can only be turned off after all work is done.
