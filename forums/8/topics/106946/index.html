    Pay $638 for a cut?
    That's what the most expensive haircut here costs. But some say that's a small price for vanity

    KRISTINA TOM

    IT'S A hair-raising price - US$800 (S$1,312). That's what stylist Orlando Pita, 42, charges for a haircut in his New York salon.

    'You can spend a lot on clothes but you wear your hair every day,' the hairdresser to Jennifer Connelly, Naomi Campbell and Kirsten Dunst was quoted as saying in defence of his heady price.

    Here in Singapore, the crown of most expensive hairstylist is worn by Kim Robinson, 50, who charges $638.

    The man whose famous clients included the late Anita Mui - he designed the wig for her funeral in January - tells LifeStyle that unlike an Hermes bag or a pair of Jimmy Choos, your hair is a frame for your face.

    'A woman's hair is her most important accessory,' says the Hong Kong-based stylist and owner of eight-year-old Le Salon in Ngee Ann City.

    He flies in for a couple of days each month to wield his scissors.

    But what's in a cut?

    The industry standard dictates a shampoo, cut and finishing that involves blow-drying and/or roll-setting.

    The price depends on the stylist - a new hairdresser will naturally charge a lot less than the salon's award-dripping director. A director's cut in Orchard Road will set you back $100 to $200.

    Of course, the frugal-minded can opt for a $7 cut at the neighbourhood barber. Or go for 10-minute services at $10, offered in booths in many malls.

    David Mercer, 41, director of the European Toni&Guy franchise here, where a cut costs between $60 and $200, notes that 'value for money doesn't come from just the haircut, but also every aspect of the salon'.

    'Too many hairdressers forget that,' he observes, adding that all the details count, from knowing the type of drink a customer likes to planning the ambience of the salon.

    But fancy decor and free drinks can only go so far to make people part with their money. Why do they pay $219 to someone like David Gan?

    The 42-year-old owner of Passion Salon in Palais Renaissance says: 'People have been complaining about my price since I started 22 years ago. I don't care.'

    Gan, whose VIP clients include Chinese actress Zhang Ziyi, adds: 'I'm not selling hamburgers. I'm doing a personal service - every cut I do is done with my hands.'

    Similarly, Robinson says emphatically: 'You don't take the cappuccino home, you don't take the glossy magazines home. What you do take home is the cut.

    'It's a designer haircut designed by Kim Robinson. It's all about branding. You're paying for a name.'

    Customers willing to pay top money say they are not disappointed. Ms Nicola Harding, the Australian owner of Coconut Boutique and a loyal client of stylist Casey Chua, says: 'I just got tired of stylists who hack your hair to bits.'

    She first went to him three years ago because a friend said he did fabulous things with blonde hair. Chua charges $120 at his Casey Inc salon at Palais Renaissance.

    Ms Cindy Chua, owner of lifestyle shop Nail Room, has been a client of Robinson for eight years.

    'It's very much an indulgence but he gives a really good cut,' she says.

    Although her fashionably cropped hair is a shocking change from her previous long tresses, she says she gets more than enough compliments. In fact, strangers stop her on the street to ask where she cuts her hair.

    Don't assume that high-end styling is only for rich and idle tai tai. Most of Robinson's clients are professional women. While they may want to walk away feeling glamorous, sexy or stunning, practical realities also rear their heads.

    'I'm a jeans and T-shirt kind of person. I have a full-time job and don't have a lot of time to worry about my hair,' says Ms Chua. 'You want a hairstyle you can manage on your own.'
