    I am surprised so much has been discussed without giving consideration to how strain actually helps to "cushion" the stress.

    To illustrate this point, imagine a rectangular block of plasticine(for the sake of this example, let's assume its density is uniformly distributed). When stress is applied, what normally results would be a decrease in the height (ie.the dimension between the point where the external force is applied and the side of the plasticine in contact with the ground) of the plasticine, but the length and width (ie.it will become longer by the sides) would actually increase to "cushion" this external force.

    Hence, that probably explains why your matter (plasticine in this case) will not collapse at a heap as Strain = Stress/Young's Modulus. As Darkness has already mentioned, the factors that determine the durability(ie.for how long it will conform to the principles as determined by your equation denoting stress) of an object have many possibilities - from atmospheric pressure to the density and Young's modulus of an object. As you can imagine from the above example given, should the stress be excessively greater than the permissible Young's modulus, what happens would result in the plasticine becoming totally flat(ie.height --> 0) with its length and width lengthened greatly...

    Hope that clarifies the concept.
