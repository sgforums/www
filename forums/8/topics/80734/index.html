    SYDNEY : A 25-year-old Singapore man has been arrested in Sydney following a breakthrough in last year's double murder of two Singaporean students.

    He has been refused bail and will appear in court on Saturday charged with two counts of murder.

    Tay Chow Lyang and Tan Poh Chuan were found bludgeoned to death in their apartment last September.

    The killings baffled police for months.

    But a breakthrough came when a special police taskforce made an arrest at 8am on Friday.

    While the suspect was being questioned, detectives searched a flat in a Sydney beachside suburb.

    A post-mortem found the victims had been beaten to death with a blunt instrument, five hours apart.

    A baseball bat, a brick and a knife were found at the scene.

    Blood was also discovered on one of the doors, which suggested one of the victims had attempted to flee his assailant during the frenzied attack.

    Mystery over the killings increased when a third man, who was a flatmate of the two killed, told police he was asleep in the apartment at the time and heard nothing.

    Police quickly ruled out a bungled kidnapping attempt.

    They also dismissed racism or murder-suicide.

    The two victims had been studying engineering at the University of New South Wales. - CNA

    SYDNEY: Singaporean Ram Tiwary has appeared in court in Australia over the killing of two fellow students in their Sydney apartment last year.

    Fellow Singaporeans Tay Chow Lyang and Tan Poh Chuan were in the final year of their engineering courses at the University of New South Wales when they were bludgeoned to death last September.

    25-year old Tiwary was remanded in custody and charged with their murder on Saturday.

    Police had described the crime as savage and brutal and set up a special task force to track down whoever was responsible.

    The identity of the killer and the motive had remained a mystery as detectives interviewed dozens of friends of the dead men.

    There was no sign of a forced entry, raising speculation that the killer had probably known his victims.

    Then, on Friday detectives swooped on another apartment just down the road from the original murder scene.

    A few hours later they arrested Tiwary who had been sharing a flat with the victims.

    The accused had told police that he had slept through the attacks.

    Tiwary's appearance in the Parramatta Bail court on Saturday lasted barely 30 seconds.

    Dressed in a beige jacket and jeans, he didn't even have time to enter the dock before the magistrate remanded him in custody until Monday to appear at Sydney's Central Court.

    Tiwary had been studying mechanical engineering at the University of New South Wales, but quit his studies this year.

    Police said the families of the murdered Singaporean students were clearly relieved that an arrest had been made.

    Their only reservation, a spokesman added, was that the Australian legal system, which does not have capital punishment, might not be able to impose a severe enough penalty if the accused is found guilty.

    The deaths of the two young men, who were well-respected and regarded as exceptional students, sent shock waves through the university, which has a high proportion of Asian students.

    Early fears that it was a racially-inspired attack were quickly discounted but the perceived lack of progress in identifying the killer only added to the mystery surrounding the crime.

    One of the senior officers leading the task force said forensic evidence and thorough detective work had aided their enquiries.

    If found guilty, Tiwary could face life imprisonment. - CNA
