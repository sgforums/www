    By Arlina Arshad

    IF YOU want to scare a Singaporean to death, take him up to the cockroach-infested rooftop of a tall building and whisper to him: 'I think the place is haunted.'

    Singapore's deepest darkest fears are heights, ghosts and cockroaches, reports cable station AXN Asia, which surveyed more than 6,000 people online.

    If Singapore is known for being kiasu, then in Hong Kong, they're kiasi. The same survey says that death is among the top three fears there. The other two: fear of heights and of the supernatural.

    And in Taiwan, the people fear in-laws as well as spiders and the dark.

    Don't believe it?

    AXN Asia says that's what it was told by 6,114 people polled over three weeks last November. It made its findings public on Fear Factor, a programme it aired last week.

    In seven out of 11 Asian countries polled, people confessed that they are most afraid of heights. They are also scared of spiders, ghosts, cockroaches, death and being alone.

    The findings parallel those of a straw poll of 105 Singaporeans polled last Thursday by The Sunday Times: 53 said they feared ghosts, 47 feared heights and 39 feared cockroaches.

    Clinical psychologist Harold Robers said: 'It's associated learning. People learn to fear the object through experience.'

    Dr Lionel Lim, a consultant psychiatrist at the Mount Elizabeth Medical Centre, said: 'There are many high-rise buildings here. People are exposed to it, are aware of its dangers and develop a phobia.'

    Fear of ghosts, vampires and darkness isn't surprising, said Dr Robers.

    'They're the supernatural, something that people talk about but have not actually seen. And for cockroaches, man and them have never been friends.'

    Ms Grace Goh, 27, a marketing and communications officer, said that being high up in a building sometimes makes her feel she will fall.

    'Whenever I lean on the ledge, I would look down and want to see more. Then, I feel like falling,' she said.

    Mr John Lim, 45, a businessman, admits: 'If I look down from somewhere that hasn't got guard rails, my legs just go soft.'

    Most people develop ways to cope. Ms Kavitha Pookays, 28, an administrative assistant, knocks on cupboard doors before opening them 'to give lizards some advance warning'.

    A lizard fell on her when she was young. She developed a fever and had to be hospitalised for a few days,     she said. 'It takes weeks for me to get over seeing a lizard. I'm afraid that if I open a cupboard a lizard will pop out.'

    Mr Ahmad Salleh, 45, a technician who fears cockroaches, avoids walking near rubbish bins, especially at night.

    'I'm afraid a swarm of them will charge at me and kill me. No way, I would take the risk,' said Mr Ahmad, a man of sturdy build.

    Like him, most people just try to dodge what they fear. But for some, the fear is so intense it takes over their lives.

    Dr Lim said he has treated a woman so frightened of water that she wouldn't wash her hair, and a man so terrified of heights and confined spaces that he wouldn't take the lift unaccompanied.

    'They would panic and become all sweaty. Besides giving them medication, we help them to relax and expose them to real situati-ons to get them to overcome their fear.' -- Additional reporting by Serene Luo & Wu Peining
