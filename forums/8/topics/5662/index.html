    Is it really that bad to work in a MNC? I would like to know how you guys define having a job in a MNC. If you are talking about having a job in the staff function, like being a clerk or a secretary, of course the job will be boring and etc etc.

    However, a job in the line function will certainly not be boring. If you are directly engaged in the designing and manufacturing processes of a company's product, like a product designer, you'll have pretty much the same responsibilities compared with the start-up. A job in the line function is not a liability, it is an asset.

    The company will surely pay you well to keep you so that you will be able to make more money for them. Is that entirely unsatisfactory? And jobs in the line function travel only in one direction: up. Jobs in the staff funcion travel either horizontally or down in the heirachy.

    There has been much encouragement lately about entrepreneurship by the government, extolling the benefits and rewards enjoyed by those who dare to 'start-up'. In a perfect world, everyone should be earning big bucks. However this is a less than perfect world to say the least.

    In my opinion, I would prefably take a job in the line function of a MNC to learn the ropes. Maybe after 5 to 6 years or longer, and not necessarily in the same company, only then will I consider 'starting-up'.

    I'll probably be lambasted for being cowardly, chicken, not willing to take risks and other unsavoury comments. However, just rememember this is merely the path I have chosen to walk. You guys can make your own decisions.

    My answer is- it depends.

    Firstly, I need to be convinced that the idea or service will be in demand. No point setting up a site to sell stuff to teenagers with no money right?

    Secondly, I'll only join the team if they have a clear idea of what they want to do and how they want to do it. Too many start-ups have been aborted because the founders are really techno geeks, not businessmen. You really need to be a bit of both to make the company fly.

    Thirdly, funding and contacts. I've known start-ups who crash because of bad money management. They don't have all the money they need and can't raise the money in time. Then all the delayed payments are due and they have to close shop. What a waste of effort!

    Contacts are also impt. They help bring you to people who can fund you. And let's face it, you need a lot of support from the supply side to make sure you can earn by providing free services to the demand side. (Forget about earning from the demand side. Nobody is willing to pay; they'll just skip your site). And people from the supply side tend not to be young punks like you but established businessmen. If the start-up has no contacts and no plans on how to get contacts, I'll say forget it man.

    Bottomline is, I need to be convinced that the start-up has a good chance of making it before I jump ship. Stock options may sweeten the deal but it'll never take away the inadeqaucies of the biz plan. And it'll never make up for the loss of my most energetic years.
