    The question is not just whether Rudolph is a terrorist, or whether he considers himself a Christian.

    It is whether he planted bombs at the 1996 Atlanta Olympics, two abortion clinics and a gay nightclub to advance a religious ideology - and how numerous, organised and violent others who share that ideology may be.

    Federal investigators believe Rudolph has long had ties with the radical Christian Identity movement, which asserts that North European whites are direct descendants of the lost tribes of Israel, God's chosen people.

    Some investigators think he might have written letters claiming responsibility for the nightclub and abortion clinic bombings on behalf of the Army of God, a violent offshoot of Christian Identity.

    'Based on what we know of Rudolph so far, and admittedly it's fragmentary, there seems to be a fairly high likelihood that he can legitimately be called a Christian terrorist,' said Mr Michael Barkun, a professor of political science at Syracuse University, a consultant to the FBI on Christian extremist groups.

    However, according to Mr Barkun, most Christian Identity followers are non-violent, and the movement's militants generally adhere to the principle of 'leaderless resistance', believing government surveillance is so pervasive that organised groups are bound to be penetrated and it is wiser to act alone.

    Idaho State University sociology professor James Aho said he is reluctant to use the phrase 'Christian terrorist' because it is 'sort of an oxymoron'.

    'I would prefer to say that Rudolph is a religiously inspired terrorist, because most mainstream Christians consider Christian Identity to be a heresy,' Prof Aho said.

    If Christians take umbrage at the juxtaposition of the words 'Christian' and 'terrorist', he added, 'that may give them some idea of how Muslims feel' when they hear the term 'Islamic terrorism'.

    'Religiously inspired terrorism is a worldwide phenomenon. Every major world religion has people who have appropriated the label of their religion in order to legitimise violence,' he said. -- Washington Post
