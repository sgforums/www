    Swimming pool cushioned fall

     

    THE angle at which Ms Zhou Zhi Hui landed in her condominium's swimming pool and the water in it were probably what saved her life, doctors said yesterday.

    When she got out of the pool, large bruises were seen on her arms and legs. In hospital last night, she said her chest hurt and she could not move her upper body. But she could move her head, arms and legs.

    Dr Adrian Wong, senior resident medical officer at Gleneagles Hospital's Accident & Emergency Department, said of the possible extent of her injuries: 'It all depends on the point of entry into the pool, which part of her body came into contact with the water first, and how deep into the pool she went.'

    If she entered the water at an angle and did not hit the bottom of the pool, it might explain her apparently light injuries.

    The pool is between 0.5m and 1.35m deep. It is not known where exactly she landed although her unit was above the shallower end. It is unlikely that she landed head first, as she had no head injuries.

    Dr Chin Khong Ling, a general practitioner with Healthway Medical Group, said the water may have absorbed the impact, although the height from which she fell and her weight would have still made for a forceful landing.

    'Generally with adults, you would expect the injury sustained from such a fall to be greater,' she added.

    Experts said that falling from a height onto water can feel like hitting concrete.

    Doctors added that, anecdotally, about half of those who fall from a height of four storeys or higher die. But most deaths involve the victim landing on a hard surface.

    Last year, a three-year-old Korean boy survived a nine-storey fall after landing in bushes at a Lorong Chuan condominium. He suffered only a fractured right ankle, cuts and abrasions.
