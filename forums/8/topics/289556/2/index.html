    I guess individuals who partake of this practice of giving 10 per cent of their salary to the churches will argue that they are doing so out of gratitude for the bounty God has bestowed on them. So it is natural for us to give part of our gains back to God. I think to the common man, this notion of giving back in gratitude is reasonable. Even non-believers or adherers of god-centered religions can understand and appreciate the notion of gratitude.

    However, the issue of giving n gratitude is an issue in itself, if we examine the practices exclusively. It is only when we factor it in with other considerations such as where does the money go to? Or who are the ones giving the money? Who are the ones benefiting from it? and at whose expense? That is where many ethical and moral concerns are raised.

    This is not a new issue. Martin Luther, the Protestant reformer, took issue with the church's practice of giving out indulgences for money or property. Of course, Luther's concerns were more faith-based rather than the objective ethical concerns that we share at present. However, like Luther's much detested indulgences, people give materially as a material value of something essential to our human existence - salvation. If we digress further it will be linked with the basic human psychological need for security and identity.

    Thus, can giving out of one's pocket for such a basic human need unethical - no. What about those who head these institutions and supply the needs? I will argue - no. Afterall, you can't wake up one day after watching that advertisement which induced u to buy that 'savy' handphone which everyone is talking about and call the handphone manufacturer and ask for ur money back because you felt cheated?
