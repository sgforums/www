    By Ho Ka Wei

    GEEKS are fun, outgoing and sociable - possibly even more than non-geeks.

    The notion may not square with popular perceptions, but studies show that Web surfing techies are hardly the hermits they are thought to be.

    While the original 'geeks', or the earliest users of the Internet, may have been mostly male, highly-educated and less social, today's geeks are more outgoing.

    For instance, a Singapore study showed that Internet users spent more time with family and friends - 9.3 hours of face-to-face meetings a week, while non-Internet users spent an average of about 6.4 hours.

    Also, the Internet was increasingly seen as bridging the gap between the online universe and real life. For example, one could buy a movie ticket online to catch a show in a real cinema later.

    The study also found that people go online mainly to look for information, send e-mail and for entertainment, in that order.

    Some of these findings were published in a report, called the Singapore Internet Project, by the school of communication and information (SCI) at Nanyang Technological University (NTU) in December 2002.

    Its findings have now been incorporated into a global report, the World Internet Project, put together by the Centre for Communication Policy at the University of California, Los Angeles.

    It has pooled together surveys done in 14 countries, including Japan, Britain, Germany, Sweden and Singapore.

    Dr Jeffrey Cole, director for the centre, said in an e-mail reply to The Straits Times yesterday: 'The real change in the Internet is that over the past four years, the mainstream of our societies has come online and users look and act like everyone else.'

    The centre started the study back in 1999 to monitor the impact of the Internet on people and society, and the NTU has been involved with it since then.

    The then-dean of the SCI, Professor Eddie Kuo and two faculty members, Dr Alfred Choi and Dr Lee Wai Peng, authored the Singapore report.

    Dr Lee told The Straits Times on Wednesday that their study found that the Internet, in fact, promotes face-to-face interaction.

    She said: 'Children told us that the Internet is an efficient tool for them to organise face-to-face get-togethers. In addition, the Internet allows people to contact those who share their interests and professions.'

    Dr Cole said: 'Internet users seem to make socialising an important priority in their lives and again, this was not true four years ago.'

    An SCI faculty member, Dr Mark Cenite, said: 'Hard core geek users who substitute virtual for real interaction may exist, but these studies suggest that they are the exception rather than the norm among Net users.'

    He added that studies have shown that people tend to fit media use into existing patterns in their lives, rather than let media transform them into something they are not.

    There is even an annual contest in the United States to find the Sexiest Geek Alive.

    Geeks The Straits Times spoke to had no complaints about being called one and their interests covered not just technology, but areas like health, the arts and pop culture.

    One of them, Miss Annie Sum, 30, a technology consultant, was probably one of the first few to embrace the personal digital assistant in 1996, when she bought the Apple Newton - a device about the size of a Tablet PC (personal computer) with handwriting recognition technology before Palm popularised it.

    She noted that today everybody rushes for the next big thing - alongside geeks - just to have fun with technology.

    Another geek, Dr Seamus Phan, 39, is a technology consultant who speaks about fitness as passionately as he does about fire walls.

    He had the makings of a geek since Primary 1, when he was the scrawny child who had to wear spectacles.

    While in junior college, he worked on chemistry experiments - with university professors.

    Dr Phan, chief of KnowledgeLabs News Centre, a tech consulting and training firm, said: 'The term 'geek' does not bother me, because one has to rise to the challenge of being one.'
