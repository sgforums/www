    Only one in 8 banks contacted is willing to give details of policy on missed instalments and repossession of homes

    By Hugh Chow

    HOUSING Board (HDB) home-loan lenders may be brash and upfront when it comes to wooing new customers but most turn into wilting wallflowers when the talk turns to home repossessions.

    Banks and finance houses fighting over the new HDB home-loan market were reluctant to disclose details of their policies on payment defaults and home repossessions, despite receiving thousands of new loan applica- tions last weekend.

    Only one out of eight lenders which had HDB home-loan packages in the market yesterday, responded with concrete details to requests for information.

    The key issue: How fast will these lenders be in throwing people onto the street if their loan payments are late?

    Dutch bank ABN Amro offered the most comprehensive information by disclosing its likely response to a missed payment over subsequent three-month periods - and the chances of foreclosure after six months.

    However, most lenders avoided discussing the hot potato - just when is the plug pulled on the loan?

    Two lenders, United Overseas Bank and Sing Investments and Finance, were unavailable for comment.

    Cheap HDB loans have caused a stir among Singaporeans since the market was opened up to private lenders last week.

    In just one week, two banks, POSB and OCBC Bank, managed to attract applications worth a combined half a billion dollars by slashing rates on their respective HDB home-loan packages.

    But many borrowers remain wary of making the switch to private lenders due to the perception that banks and finance companies may be less 'compassionate' when it comes to repossessing their homes if they fall behind on their payments.

    The HDB was known for a policy of leniency as part of its social charter.

    The Code of Consumer Banking Practice - the much-heralded banking industry document which promises greater transparency when dealing with the public - came into effect last Thursday.

    Yet despite the new Code, few lenders were prepared yesterday to offer concrete details of their policies covering payment defaults and home repossessions.

    ABN Amro said that as a general rule, borrowers will have up to six months from the first missed instalment to work out a new repayment scheme with the bank.

    In practice, an agreement is often reached within three months. But if one is not reached after six months, the bank will assume that 'borrowers have lost interest in the property and...prefer the bank to exercise its rights under the mortgage to recover the loan outstanding'.

    However, ABN Amro head of consumer banking sales Seah Boon Ching said: 'We...do not conduct ourselves like pawnshops and sell collaterals at the first sight of a default.'

    He added: 'In fact, we avoid repossession and forced selling of a mortgage property as it is a no-win solution for both the owners and the bank.'

    None of the other lenders would say how much time a defaulting borrower could expect before a loan is foreclosed. Most said they would adopt a case-by-case approach while emphasising a desire to treat cases sympathetically through payment restructuring.

    Examples of payment re- structuring cited by POSB and HSBC included lengthening the overall payment period, reducing monthly payments for a limited period or finding a guarantor to assist in payments.
