    Telcos must promote Web filters from Sept

    SINGAPORE - The Media Development Authority (MDA) has made it compulsory for Internet service providers to promote Internet filters to customers amid the growing usage of the Web among children - made even more so with the popularity of mobile devices.

    From September, telecommunications companies must promote such filters - essentially software which parents can install to block undesirable content - over the counter, at points of sale or when customers renew contracts.

    According to the Synovate Young Asians Survey 2010, 95 per cent of those aged between eight to 14 spend up to eight hours online on computers and smartphones.

    StarHub became the first telco to introduce mobile Internet filters yesterday, at the StarHub Security Solutions event.

    Responding to media queries, SingTel said it has plans to offer mobile broadband filters for phones and tablets soon, while M1 would only say that plans are in the pipeline.

    Teacher Jasmine Koh said mobile filters are especially useful since parents have less control over what their children surf on their mobile phones: "These days, even primary school pupils have smartphones and it can be a real problem."

    Parent Jane Yang said filters would be useful as her son, 12, is left alone at home for between two and three hours on weekdays and on some Sundays.

    Nonetheless, while Internet filtering is effective at blocking access to illegal and inappropriate material, it does not provide security against risks such as fraud, illegal contact or cyber-bullying, research by the Australian media authority has found.

    Noted Ms Koh: "Parents still need to take a proactive role in guiding their children online, to make sure they do not overstep their boundaries."
