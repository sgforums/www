    Chinese cheap labour drying up

    CHINA'S seemingly bottomless pool of cheap peasant labour will soon be dry, according to research by China's leading labour market analyst.

    Thirty years of double-digit economic growth has reduced the rural population of surplus labour to as few as 25 million, or just 5 per cent of the national workforce, says Professor Cai Fang, the director of the Institute of Population and Labour Economics at China's most influential think tank, the Chinese Academy of Social Sciences.

    This means China has reached an historic turning point in the bargaining position of labour, where workers are becoming scarce and therefore more valued, he says. There are fewer employers ripping off workers by failing to pay wages - and those wages have suddenly spiked after a decade of stagnation.

    "We show that the idea that there is a 'bottomless' surplus labour pool in agriculture - the widely accepted belief that there are nearly 200 million surplus labourers, accounting for more than 40 per cent of the total rural labour force - is actually out of date," writes Professor Cai and his colleague Wang Meiyan.

    Their paper, Counterfactuals of unlimited surplus labour in rural China, models three scenarios using different assumptions, including how many peasants are needed to farm the land and whether they can be expected to work 250, 300 or 320 days a year.

    The three scenarios suggest China has a labour surplus of 25 million, 63 million or 108 million workers.

    Rising wages and work standards might force up the price of Chinese goods - against the declining trend of the past decade. It would also force its leaders to consider loosening the one-child policy, eroding a discriminatory residency permit system that hinders urban migration, and protecting rights of migrant workers.
