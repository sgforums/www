    As clear and brutal as I possibly can...

    1. Singlish has a life of its own.

    2. As a mode of comfortable informal social intercourse it cannot be eradicated even if there were a campaign. The pressures from structures do not always reach the organic and mobile intimacies of the venacular amongst agencies. Totalitarian and fascist regimes have, throughout history, attempted to eradicate ethnic languages but have failed. Hence to say that there is an anti-Singlish crusade is laughable. (The S'pore Govt can only try but it will fail.)

    3. However, neither the inability to eradicate Singlish nor parading it as a form of ethnicity is good enough reason to activity promote it. This is because the consequences of promotion without prior language proficiencies will be to our ultimate disadvantage.

    4. English must be the staple language because it is the language of commerce. One should strive to be able to articulate and write in English without flaw. There is no point in being multi-lingual in half measures, not fully masterful in any because one will be superficially conversant in different languages but ultimately impotent in them all.

    5. Singlish is a quick and abrupt manner of speech. It reduces and constricts. As such meanings tend to be limited to generics. It is one thing to say that it is a language to convey simple ideas but another to wonder if the ideas are made simple by default through their conveyance in Singlish.

    6. Its adoption as national identity is potentially detrimental because,
    a. there are fewer people in reality who can switch between Singlish and 'proper' English than the number of people who actually think they can.
    b. Singlish as national identity will make it something to be proud of. Singlish speakers without adequate proficiency in international English will not feel compelled to improve. Singlish as NI will become an asylum for poor English speakers for its a sociological fact that people take the path of least resistance. Not only will English proficiency levels decline, it's replacement by Singlish will be validated.

I would have to make 2 disclaimers : the first, I am not adaquately trained in the social sciences and may make a blunder of the use of terms (perhaps someone can correct me if I made any blunders) and second, is that I would adopt a scientific perspective in looking at the issue.

    Let me go to the tenets of the issue at end, by first defining what national identity is all about. In a very loose perspective, I would define national identity of a country as a characteristic/feature/property which the other countries (excluding the country which we encoded the national identity, of course) would characterize and identify. Now the national identity would take a few forms of manifestation, via 1)language, 2)customs/traditions, 3)sociology and philosophy (if any) or 4)political ideology. With these tenets at hand, I would seek to address whether there exists a national identity for Singapore.

    1)On the point of language, some of the forumites has advocated that Singlish is crucial to the maintenance of national identity. It can be viewed in 2 perspectives, first, Singlish used mixed words from different cultures, draw from the multi-ethnic and cultural population of Singapore and second, Singlish distinguishes the Singaporeans from the rest of the world. To reiterate my stand, I would think that Singlish is the agent which people used to advocate the national identity of the country, but not the feature of the country itself. For example, Japanese is inherent in Japan, because it is a feature of the country itself. Of course, one can argue that Japanese is a derivative of the Chinese language. However, it has evolved to become an independent language of its own. Then come back to the Singlish part, one can make similar inferences and say that Singlish is a derivative of English. Unfortunately, it has not evolved to be an independent language but stood as an aberration to the language for it does not adhere to the rules of the original language.

    2)Then on customs/traditions, I think that Singapore possessed that, because we are a country which boasts of having different new years for different races/religions, something which is not commonly seen in many other countries (for example Britain has only the new year, Guy Fawkes day and the bank holidays).

    3)On the sociology of the society, I would say that "kiasuism" can be regarded as a feature which distinguished a Singaporean from someone from other countries. Someone in my University told me that the Singaporeans he encountered are all "walking encyclopedias" because they seem to get all information on the subject for study to get the best grades.

    4)Finally, on political ideology, as someone pointed it out earlier on, that the political ideology is Machievellian pragmatic or benelovent dictatorship.

    From all these points, I would say that Singapore do have a national identity but may not be rooted as deep as the Japanese or Americans. The reason for that, we are a young nation, and it takes at least 200 hundred years to have a country having a fixed national identity.

    I would be glad to elaborate on these points if required.

    so this is where all the fun is! darn....should have been here earlier. so much has been written already. maybe some random thots just to make me feel good abt myself.

    i wonder if anyone has ever bothered trying to pin down what singlish really is. not that i'm asking for a definition (not all things admit of definitions). but what is it really to be speaking in singlish? it seems a mix of the right (or wrong) accent, littered with phrases from other languages and dialects, some coinages of its own (like the "lah"), and bad sentence structure and formation (because its main medium is english (a medium within a medium!) and yet it doesn't stay true to this medium). singlish has no "right" way of being wrong. as long as you speak badly, and there's a whole lot of ways to speak poorly, then you speak singlish. there is of course a certain structure to singlish sentences (it mimics english but is expected to be a poor artist). no one, however, would fault you for being poor in singlish. the criticism is usu the other way around - atrocious singlish means even worse english. and tho a foreigner may fault you for bad english, it seems strange to fault that foreigner for being poor in singlish. but why is that?

    simply because we expect english to be taken seriously (that is, there are standards of good and bad english) but not singlish. nothing really hinges on someone speaking poorly in singlish (except maybe strange looks from those thoroughly proficient in singlish, and singlish alone).

    ask yourself now, if i took away the accent, and spoke with a british one, while all the other features of singlish remained the same, would i still be identified as speaking singlish? or just bad (even atrocious) english? now if i retained the accent, but littered my speech with lots of big words, would i still be speaking singlish?

    there is a point to all this of course. while there are no "real" standards for speaking good singlish, there is a way that singlish is not to be spoken. it is not, i would contend, to speak it with a foreign accent (the way speaking english in a variety of accents is still to speak english). it is not to be able to use much more than certain simple words that wouldn't offend the singlish speakers' ears. built into the very nature of singlish, is the ability to NOT speak well (relative of course to mainstream languages).

    is singlish really the dumping ground of much-used phrases from the various other languages and dialects? if so, i suggest that these "words" were selected for their ability to convey something that binds s'poreans in some (vague but important) way. it gives a sense of belonging and lends a feeling of familiarity. and so does the accent. to speak singlish is hence to speak in a coffee-shop, auntie-next-door, friend-friend, buddy-buddy kinda way. this is what gives us a sense of community.

    but there will be times you don't want to be friend-friend. when you want to speak as decently as you can. is singlish adequate to such times? since english is the main medium within singlish, to say something more than what you can in singlish is to revert to english. simply because english is a more developed language and so allows you to say more. but this doesn't mean that only english gives you the ability to articulate yourself in a complex (or even abstract) way. any developed language (like chinese or whatever) should give you that ability (coupled of course with some grey matter between the ears). so it is less the fault of singlish than it is of the poor mastery of english that leads to poor english (and by extension, thinking) skills. but if singlish wasn't made (or meant) for complex thinking (i take it singlish came abt as a means of rudimentary, everyday communication of the most basic level), then it wouldn't be fair to criticize it on that account either. it would have to be criticized based on another value (or valuation) that lies beyond singlish, say, in the value of exchanges that can only take place with a more complete language. these exchanges can take the form of higher level business deals or academic debate or legal-speak or abstract contemplation or whatever. without some value placed on these exchanges, singlish itself loses none of its value.

    still, the issue is whether singlish as such hampers the learning of good english. but while most ppl seem to think it does, there is a way that it does not, but actually helps promote it.

    recall that there is no right or significantly correct way of speaking singlish (apart from the few features i pointed out). so if someone's singlish is of a worse quality than someone else's, we are likely to say that person's english sux. we are less likely to say that person's singlish sux (or if bad english is good singlish, then to say that that person's singlish is top notch. we won't say sth like this).

    hence, apart from the few featues i pointed out, there is no competition with english. there IS a way of speaking good english. but no (significantly) RIGHT way of speaking singlish. you don't really have to learn to speak singlish, since any bad use of grammar or tense or whatever immediately falls under the domain of singlish. that is, singlish just is the repository of all bad english, however that bad english habit was formed.

    but could it be that singlish itself led to the bad english habit? again, if it did, it only does so in a derivative way, i.e. from being in a singlish-envt for too long without the benefit of proper english. but singlish itself lays no independent claims since it is not, properly speaking, a language that can be wrongly used (apart from some features i already mentioned). what this means is that to speak good english, we have to counter all forms of deviant english, including singlish. but this countering can take the form of contrasting these deviant forms from what is good english. here singlish serves as a rich source of correction when it comes to learning good english, since singlish is the domain of commonly bad uses of english. there is a place for singlish in learning good english after all. the tricky part is teaching someone to distinguish the two. there is no substitute however for wanting to learn good english and then putting it into practice (like here in the forum maybe).

    further, it is only with a heightened consciousness that singlish is bad english that ppl will feel insecure and compelled to move beyond singlish. as it is now, to make ppl insecure abt using singlish (the way the govt has made PCK insecure) is the only way to push them to learn better english.

    and here, as above, singlish will serve as the starting point (and a point of contrast)from which we should depart towards the fairer shores of the english language.
