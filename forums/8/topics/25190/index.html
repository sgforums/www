    Woman caught with 12,000 Yaba pills at airport
    20 AUG 2002

    Islandwide drugs crackdown hauls in 198, including suspicious Thai tourist with the tablets, worth $118,000, in her bag at airport

    SINGAPORE narcotics officers seized almost 12,000 Yaba tablets at Changi Airport on Monday afternoon, the largest single haul this year.

    The 1.3 kg of methamphetamine tablets, with a street value of more than $118,000, was seized from a 45-year-old Thai tourist during a check on passengers from Bangkok as part of a 24-hour, islandwide operation to stamp out drug abuse.

    Almost 200 people were arrested in the crackdown.

    The woman, who was carrying a beige-coloured bag, had aroused suspicion when she hurried out of the Customs checkpoint. The officers followed her for a while before stopping her. They found two packets of the pink Yaba tablets in her bag.

    One was wrapped in a pair of black pyjamas and the other, in black cloth.

    The controlled drug is becoming more popular than Ecstasy and is bigger than heroin in Thailand.

    If found guilty of importing the drug, the woman faces the death penalty.

    The woman was also given a urine test and she tested positive for amphetamine.

    Another 197 suspected drug offenders were arrested during Operation Dragnet, the Central Narcotics Bureau said in a statement yesterday.

    It added that enforcement agencies at all entry and exit points were stepping up security inspections of all people, goods and vehicles entering Singapore.

    The last big Yaba haul was on Jan 21 this year, when officers found 7,477 tablets with a street value of about $89,700.

    Again, it was found on a Thai tourist arriving at Changi Airport.

    The 27-year-old man had hidden the drugs in the soles of his leather shoes.

    He is now serving 15 years in jail and was also given 12 strokes of the cane.

    Heavenly Thing -Singapore Strait Times - 18 Aug 2002

    Ageless pop prince Aaron Kwok proves his appeal extends all the way to the Land of Rising Sun

    By Samuel Lee

    LANGUAGE is no barrier to Aaron Kwok's popularity in Japan. Last week, the Hongkong Heavenly King, who admits to speaking Japanese 'very badly', staged three successful concerts in Osaka, Tokyo and Yokohoma. Each attracted an estimated crowd of 10,000.

    'When I perform, I talk with my body language,' he said in Mandarin laced with a strong Cantonese accent last Friday night.

    Speaking to Singapore reporters in his suite at the Akasaka Crown Prince Hotel in Tokyo, he was fresh and perky, having showered after his gig at the Tokyo International Forum (see concert review).

    During the two-hour show, he sweet-talked the audience in Japanese, which he said he learnt two months ago. His cooing drove the crowd crazy, even though his set comprised Cantonese and Mandarin songs, except for one Japanese number.

    His appeal, say fans like university undergraduate Yumi Nomura, lies in his Peter Pan good looks, impressive high-energy dance sequences, and a showmanship that has been polished to perfection.

    Ms Nomura, 23, was there with her 25-year-old sister, Masae. 'He sings well and is a great dancer. Besides, he's kakkoii ne,' she said with a giggle.

    Cries of kakkoii, Japanese for cute and dashing, vied with chants of 'A-e-ron' in the auditorium.

    CONQUERING JAPAN

    ANOTHER boon to his growing appeal in the Land of the Rising Sun is his roles in films with a Japanese connection.

    These include 1998's Anna Magdalena and last year's Para Para Sakura and China Strike Force.

    'There are more people coming to see me compared to my first performance here six or seven years ago,' he said with a grin as he shared a box of okashi (Japanese snacks) with the journalists in the room.

    'Many have seen my movies and wanted to get a first-hand experience of my reputation as a livewire on stage.'

    Para Para cashed in on the made-in-Japan interactive arcade dance craze of the same name, while Anna Magdalena also featured heart-throb Takeshi Kaneshiro.

    China Strike Force starred the babelicious Norika Fujiwara, with whom Kwok is linked romantically.

    He confirmed reports that she was at last Wednesday's Osaka show with her parents and younger brother.

    After the concert, she was spotted backstage with a bouquet. But he insisted there was nothing romantic between them.

    'We're just very good friends.'

    Kwok, who turns 37 in October, said he is still single, but will give up his bacherlorhood to start a family with someone he really fancies.

    'Don't be fooled by my flashy, playboy image. I'm prepared to settle down if the right one comes along at the right time - which is not now.'

    For now, he wants to milk his popularity for what it's worth.

    He said: 'Who knows where I'll be in three years' time?'

    NO FLASH IN THE PAN

    STARTING out as a dancer in Hongkong's TVB station in 1983, he was earning a meagre monthly wage of HK$120 (S$27), but became hot property in 1990 after appearing in a Taiwanese advertisement for Honda motorbikes.

    He sparked a bidding war among record labels, which had not even heard him sing.

    The hugely popular title track in his debut album, Never Stop Loving You (1990), made him a household name in Hongkong, Taiwan and South-east Asia.

    Four albums and two years later, he earned the title of Heavenly King, and was crowned alongside Jacky Cheung, Andy Lau and Leon Lai.

    Still, critics saw him as a flash in the pan, with little to offer than good looks and a pair of nimble feet.

    Proving them wrong, he has since released more than 38 albums and has acted in at least 25 movies and three television series. Last year, his estimated worth was HK$58 million.

    His concerts here, such as the two at the Singapore Indoor Stadium in April last year, played to full-houses. Of the 8,000 tickets available for his upcoming gig on Sept 6, 90 per cent have already been sold.

    The show will feature new songs from his upcoming greatest hits album out next month, such as The Great Love Party and Saviour.

    'It's a whole new sound, and a sexier Aaron Kwok. I want fans to know I'm not lazy,' he said.

    His next target is Korea, and he is working on songs to cater to female listeners there.

    'Women in different markets have differing tastes. They also taste different,' he said.

    Now, did he say people should not be fooled by his playboy image?

    Razzle dazzle
    CONCERT
    AARON KWOK LIVE
    ON STAGE IN JAPAN CONCERT TOUR 2002
    Tokyo International Forum
    Last Friday

    MID-WAY through his 27-song set, the Heavenly King descended the stage and asked his fans to come forward to shake his hand.

    And they did, swarming to the front of the hall.

    There were no burly security guards, surly police officers or metal barricades separating him from the sea of female worshippers.

    But there was a curious clockwork synchronicity with which the 10,000 Japanese concert-goers responded to his music.

    As soon the opening bars to a song hinted at something upbeat, they would stand en masse, swaying with a regularity that reflects the neat, clinical lines along which Japanese society is run.

    The moment the tempo dropped, they would return to their seats, before repeating the above sequence when the pace picked up again.

    Even the concert had a rhythmic routine to it.

    Each of its six segments would start with a robust lead-in by Kwok's nubile dancers before he emerged, either on the raised dais in the middle of the stage or through the open door beneath it.

    After the fast number, the dancers would exit, leaving him to engage in cheeky Japanese chatter, mostly a declaration of love for Japan, Japanese food like shabu shabu and sashimi, and, needless to say, his Japanese fans.

    A ballad or two would follow before he closed each segment on a high note. His dancers would then reappear.

    His Tokyo show was also marked by four Gs: gloss, groove, gimmicks and goose-pimples.

    Most of his wardrobe screamed camp and clash.

    Some examples: A black-and-white Moschino jacket crammed full of letters of the alphabet worn over a checker-board T-shirt; and a gold mock-turtleneck paired with red jeans.

    But these clashes seemed appropriate when coupled with his dramatic dance moves. Indeed, his fancy footwork rivals that of Michael Jackson's.

    But what was truly amazing was how he lost neither breath nor pitch after a strenuous 10-minute Latin medley.

    On Transcending Time & Space, he sang Japanese lyrics to the tune of his Mandarin hit. Though gimmicky and unprecise in diction, he steamrolled the audience.

    Predictably, they greeted his effort with shrieks, as they did his cringe-inducing attempts at connecting with them in Japanese.

    'There are lots of hot springs in Japan. I'd love to invite you to come soak with me. But then, I'm such a shy guy...'

    Leon lies low - 20 Aug 2002

    He's happy to hold court on any subject, but Hongkong Heavenly King Leon Lai bristles when asked about actresses linked to him like Shu Qi and Zhang Ziyi

    By Clara Chow

    LEON LAI is touchy about his love life.

    In town on Monday to promote his new horror trilogy, Three, the 35-year-old Hongkong singer-actor refused to talk about it. He would not even say whether he has a girlfriend.

    'This is my personal life. I feel I don't have to tell anyone. Thank you,' he said in Mandarin.

    Wearing a T-shirt, Japanese brand Evisu blue jeans, sneakers and Yves Saint Laurent sunglasses, he clammed up when asked about rumoured on-off girlfriend, sexy Taiwanese actress Shu Qi, 25.

    'I feel I don't have any need to tell anyone,' he said, leaning back in his chair to distance himself from the query.

    Japan's Asaka Seto, 25, and China's Zhang Ziyi, 22, - two other actresses once linked to him - are also no-fly zones with him.

    Seto starred with him in last year's Bullets Of Love, and drowned her sorrows in alcohol reportedly after their rumoured romance ended.

    The 1.78-m tall and 68-kg actor would not say if he still keeps in contact with her or Zhang.

    Lai's irritation showed despite the soft, level tone of his voice.

    He also refrained from commenting on Hongkong bad boy singer-actor Nicholas Tse taking a break from making movies, and on Zhang being voted one of the world's 10 ugliest women by Britain's For Him Magazine (FHM).

    But the media-savvy entertainer regained his composure quickly.

    Minutes after the questions were put to him, he posed for a photograph with some reporters after his Straits Times Interactive webchat, which took place on Monday afternoon. He left Singapore for Hongkong yesterday evening.

    Perhaps being testy with journalists comes with the territory of being a Heavenly King.

    Together with Andy Lau, Aaron Kwok and Jacky Cheung, he has worn this tag for at least a decade.

    Lai was careful to portray himself as understanding of the media.

    'I don't have any way of coping with the invasion of my privacy,' he said, a little too quickly perhaps.

    'Reporters have a right to write what they think can sell the paper and earn their wages. But if all newspapers write the same thing, won't readers be numb and stop reading?'

    Yet scandal has dogged his career from its outset.

    Born in Beijing, Lai's family migrated to Hongkong when he was six years old. He was 23 when he first appeared on the Hongkong entertainment scene as a singer and television actor.

    In 1990, he released his debut solo album, Meeting In The Rain. TV serials such as The Challenge Of Life (1990) and The Breaking Point (1991) propelled him to heart-throb status.

    He cemented his brooding lover image with film roles such as Fallen Angels (1995) and Comrades: Almost A Love Story (1996). In City Of Glass (199, he starred with Shu Qi, and the two soon became an item.

    As early as 1993, however, the press had a field day with reports about actress Athena Chu Yan seducing Lai on the set of TVB serial Legend Of The Lone Ranger. She later denied them.

    Recently, the heat has been on him and Shu Qi. After a rumoured split with her three years ago, he downed sleeping pills in a suspected suicide attempt, providing more fodder for the tabloids.

    Not many would have guessed that this clean-cut man would generate such frenzied dirt-digging.

    But his support for the Chinese government has also kicked up controversy.

    Last year, he was said to be a contender to sing the theme song at the Beijing 2008 Olympics, although the Chinese government has yet to confirm this.

    Predictably, he said he rooted for China during the World Cup two months ago.

    And given a chance to turn back the clock, he would change things so that the 'Chinese will have a stronger sense of pride'.

    His new movie, Three, is a culmination of not just Chinese pride, however, but also Asian pride.

    The film, produced by regional film company Applause, is made up of three spooky stories. The first two are directed by Korea's Kim Jee Woon and Thailand's Nonzee Nimibutr (Jan Dara).

    In the third part of Three, Going Home, he is Chinese physician Yu Fai. Yu moves to Hongkong in search of the good life, only to find that his beloved wife (Eugenia Yuan) is dying of liver cancer. He soaks his wife's body in Chinese medicinal herbs daily to keep her soul in her body

    The segment is directed by Peter Chan Ho-Sun, who also directed Lai in City Of Glass.

    In one scene, Lai has to bathe Yuan's naked body. The PG film was censored here to tone down the nudity.

    Already in Hongkong, reporters have made an issue of the connotations of necrophilia in the scene.

    This angered the actor, who believed the media's picking on only one scene detracts from the movie as a whole. Still, he was willing to discuss the scene.

    'Please ask,' he urged this reporter politely.

    He said, with a serious expression: 'It was not difficult at all.' He and Yuan filmed the scene in less than an hour.

    He added: 'It's like when I was young and my Dad bathed me. I also imagined it to be how I'd bathe my parents when they are old.'

    He was equally evasive about the chemistry between him and his fresh-faced co-star.

    'Whether sparks fly between us is up to the audience to decide after watching the movie,' he would only say.

    After all these years, he still has baby-smooth skin. About the only change is that he looks slightly thinner.

    Indeed, retirement is far from his mind. The subject made him think for a long hard moment.

    He looked off in the distance, exhaled loudly and said after a long pause: 'I think it's hard to answer. It's up to whether the market needs you.'

    Hongkong singer-actor Tse announced this month that he was taking a break from showbusiness.

    Two months ago, Hongkong singer-actress Sammi Cheng squashed rumours that she was to retire, too.

    But Lai came up with an analogy for his own career's future: 'A lighter burning at full blast will run out of gas quickly. The same thing goes for my shelf life. I'd rather just be burning slowly.'

    Then he was whisked away for his next publicity engagement.

    The fragrant pop idol charm he exuded seemed to linger in the air after him. And despite being prickly on some topics, he came and left with nary a wrinkle on his face.

    Sex-brokering scandal set to hit S. Korean entertainers - 18 Aug 2002

    Prosecutors say they have evidence to nail culprits, even the bigwigs

    SEOUL - South Korean prosecutors are investigating allegations that some female entertainers are providing paid sexual services to television producers, politicians and corporate officials in a bid to advance their careers.

    The women reportedly split their payments with the brokers who make the introductions.

    Advertisement

    The Seoul District Prosecutor's Office says liaisons such as these have been practised for years, but it now has enough information to nail the culprits.

    The alleged sexual brokering for actresses, pop singers and models is done by talent agents, directors of beauty salons and madams who run bars known as 'room saloons', where men are provided with female companions, according to investigators.

    Recently, a 38-year-old woman was questioned for 13 hours by the prosecutor's office for reportedly securing female entertainers for influential figures, reported the local English paper JoongAng Ilbo.

    She told investigators that an entertainment agency had provided her four women, including two popular actresses, who gave sexual services to a high-ranking political figure and the son of a conglomerate official.

    The prosecution said several high-profile political and business figures had paid three million won to 10 million won (S$4,000 to S$15,000) to spend a night with famous actresses.

    They often gave the women gifts on top of cash.

    'Most of the services are provided by aspiring entertainers who hope to boost their careers by meeting high-ranking figures,' said an official at an entertainment agency.

    The Supreme Public Prosecutor's Office earlier this year questioned three saloon madams during an investigation into the activities of President Kim Dae Jung's second son.

    Prosecutors said the television producers might be charged with receiving sexual services in exchange for putting the entertainers on the air.

    They said that business and political figures might be charged with violating prostitution laws.

    Stricter rules for Chinese medicines
    By Salma Khalik
    HEALTH CORRESPONDENT

    CHINESE medicines will have to meet stricter testing and labelling requirements before they can be sold here.

    The new Health Ministry rules require:

    The products to be labelled clearly - 'Allowed for sale as a Chinese Proprietary Medicine' (CPM) - from January, to distinguish them from Western drugs.

    Every consignment to be accompanied by a test report from an accredited laboratory, from January 2004.

    Higher risk products, like those for slimming and impotence, to be subjected to even more stringent tests.

    Factories making the medicines to have good quality control.

    Health Minister Lim Hng Kiang announced the changes yesterday, which are aimed at preventing another Slim 10-style tragedy, where a woman died and another needed a liver transplant after taking the made-in-China slimming pills.

    But he cautioned that no matter what regulations were in place, the consumer was ultimately responsible for what he bought.

    So while Singapore will have some of the world's most stringent rules controlling the sale of Chinese medicines, Mr Lim stressed: 'The Health Sciences Authority cannot give Singaporeans a blanket guarantee that CPM from now on will be safe.'

    The rules only apply to Chinese medicines made from a combination of compounds. Herbs can still be brought in without fuss.

    And they do not apply to non-Chinese traditional compounds like Indonesian jamu, as there have been no serious problems involving these products, said Dr Clarence Tan, the Health Sciences Authority's (HSA) chief executive officer.

    The new testing requirements were needed because, unlike Western drugs which must list their chemical ingredients, Chinese medicines contain many natural animal and plant materials.

    But the tests will only eliminate known harmful compounds, which is why the manufacturing process must be audited to weed out 'backyard factories'.

    The HSA is talking to regulators in countries like China, Taiwan and Malaysia about using their checks as a guarantee of quality.p> Singapore already has an arrangement with Australia.

    Without such guarantees from the country of import, HSA officials may need to inspect overseas factories.

    Mr Lim said China plans to regulate manufacturing of health products by 2004.

    The new regulations will, however, raise the price of Chinese medicines. For example, laboratory test reports will cost about $500 to $1,000 per consignment, which will be passed on to the consumer.

    There are 9,800 Chinese medicines sold here, compared to about 7,500 Western medicines. But Chinese medicine sales account for only $15 million to $20 million, or about 5 per cent of the $400 million to $500 million worth of medicines sold here annually.

    The ministry's move was welcomed by Eu Yan Sang, the biggest player here.

    Mr Richard Eu, its chief executive officer, said his company 'does everything right' when producing its own Chinese medicines. But when others cut corners, it gives the whole industry a bad name.

    No-headscarf school rule 'not against Islam'

    Advertisement

    How safe is your chicken rice?
    Fast turnover at chicken rice outlets means that harmful bacteria that cause food poisoning have less time to multiply

    By Magdalene Lum

    HUNDREDS of chickens hang in food stalls around Singapore in the heat of day.

    And you can get food poisoning from the main ingredient of Singapore's most popular dish as easily as you can from any food left out too long.

    'This is because bacteria multiplies at room temperature,' warns Dr Chua Sin-Bin, the deputy chief executive officer of the Agri-Food & Veterinary Authority of Singapore (AVA).

    'Bacteria can double within 20 to 30 minutes. In four hours, you can be looking at 12 generations of bacteria.'

    Dr Chua, who is also the AVA's Director of Food & Veterinary Administration and the man behind food safety in Singapore, says chicken rice lovers should patronise reputable outlets that have a 'fast turnover'.

    Most outlets claim that they sell out their chicken in 45 minutes to two hours - though not all do. (See story below)

    The two most common bacteria found in chicken are salmonella and campylobactea.

    Another is staphyloccus aureus which is found on human skin and hair as well as on chicken skin. It can be passed to the chicken when a hawker squeezes his pimples, scratches his head, picks his nose or has an infected cut on his hand while on the job.

    Last year, there were 1,256 cases of food poisoning here, compared with 986 cases in 1998. Of these, 28 were linked to chicken rice.

    No chicken rice-related food poisoning cases have been reported this year but Dr Chua says people should not let their guard down.

    A person usually feels the symptoms of food poisoning two hours after eating contaminated chicken.

    'You'll feel unwell and nauseous, and vomit. You may have headaches, suffer from abdominal cramps, retch and feel chilly without having a fever,' he says.

    It takes a day to recover from such food poisoning and it is rarely fatal.

    Cooking kills all bacteria including the staphyloccus aureus. But poor hygiene and handling can lead to cross-contamination.

    For instance, if a food handler or housewife, who prepares cooked food, also handles raw chicken or other raw meat at the same time, bacteria in the raw food can contaminate the cooked food, he points out.

    'As a principle, when you handle raw chicken or meat, you should not handle cooked food,' Dr Chua says.

    People should either use different cutting boards for raw and cooked meats, or wash the cutting board and knives thoroughly before switching from raw to cooked foods or vice versa.

    He says the United States has about 80 times more cases of food poisoning than Singapore while New Zealand has 10 times more.

    Despite this, he urges people not to become complacent: 'Everyone must do his or her part to make Singapore not only a food paradise, but a very safe one.'

    Otherwise, a random survey of more than 10 chicken rice stalls, restaurants and chains showed that most claim to sell out their chicken in 45 minutes to two hours.

    The Mandarin Singapore's Chatterbox Coffee House serves 250 chickens per day to more than 700 customers.

    ''We cook chicken five to seven times daily, 10 chickens at a time,'' he says, adding that each outlet sells out the 10 chickens within two hours.
