    Teens crushed by study pressures
    Survey covering HK, Shanghai and Taiwan finds that more than one in five teens rates study fears as the No 1 factor in suicidal thoughts

    By Terence Tan

    CALL it the Asian curse.

    A study of suicide trends among the young in Shanghai, Taiwan and Hong Kong shows a common denominator - an inability to cope with the pressures of their studies.

    Nearly 40 per cent of the teenagers polled in Shanghai and Taipei, and more than a fifth of those polled in Hong Kong, cited schoolwork pressures as the main reason leading them to entertain suicidal thoughts.

    The survey, of 500 students aged between 13 and 19 in the three cities, was carried out by the Chinese edition of the Reader's Digest. The findings were published in its latest issue.

    While educational pressures are generally great in Asian cities, peer competition in the three cities is especially fierce, parental expectations are unusually high and school assignments are aplenty.     All these become too much for the teenagers to bear.

    In the case of Taiwan, there is the added pressure on students to show creativity, which has become important since education reforms began a few years ago.

    While Taiwan's education system was based on rote learning in the past, 'these days students are told to engage in creative thinking and show originality in schoolwork', said Mr Chen Po-han, a teaching counsellor.

    'Yet when it comes to examinations, students still can't run away from the need to memorise their work; thus many can't adapt to the changes which require fundamental adjustments,' he told The Straits Times.

    In Shanghai, rapid economic development has caused many parents to place great emphasis on their children's performance in schools, said Associate Professor Tse Wing Ling of the City University of Hong Kong, as better results often mean better job prospects.

    After school work, family problems are the next potential cause for suicide, according to the study. Further down the list are relationship and work problems.

    In Singapore, the local support group Samaritans of Singapore (SOS) said that of the 357 people who killed themselves in 2001, 27 were teenagers. Figures for 2002 are not yet available.

    Speaking to The Straits Times, Ms Susan Lim, the public affairs executive of the SOS said: 'Youths in Singapore are affected by relationship problems, anxiety about exam grades and depression.'

    She has this advice for teenagers: 'Growing up is a vulnerable period where youths search for identity, connection and meaning. It is extremely stressful for youths when they attach success and meaning in life to the type of grades they get in exams.'

    She added: 'It is important that they learn to communicate their anxieties and stress to others and not keep things to themselves. Do seek professional help if there is a prolonged feeling of hopelessness and despair.'
