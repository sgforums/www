    Anyone with a decent bit of conscience would know that what the Japanese government is doing is wrong. However, if the Japanese could exercise their own right to disrespect China by twisting history, couldn't the Chinese exercise their own right to tell Japan that they do not condone those actions too? Fathers and brothers have been killed, mothers and sisters have been raped. Old wounds have been re-opened, where family honour is at stake naturally emotions run high.

    How else would you expect the Chinese to behave? Wait for the government to summon the Japanese ambassador so that both parties can just at least show angry faces at the TV camera? Sit civilly in front of their computers and chat in chatrooms, "Aiya, relax lah... the Japanese schools maybe won't use the textbook anyway"? Put up their hands and implore to the heavens, "Father, forgive them; for they know not what they do"? Or just take ten deep breaths, say "woo-sah..." while exhaling and carry on life as normal?

    Withholding the truth is as good as supporting the lie. As mentioned in an earlier thread, "mute acceptance of the Japanese actions is tantamount to the acknowledgement of their version of history." Somebody has got to tell the Japanese not to test their neighbours' patience, and who better to rely on than the offended neighbours themselves? (Speaking of which, what's to stop the Vietnamese and Americans from protesting against the Chinese history textbooks if they wanted to?)

    The unruly mob behaviour of the protesters have made their cause look bad. Since this is not the first time the Japanese government have taunted the neighbours, many protesters are also aware that protests are not the solution to the problem (Is there even a solution to the problem?) and the Japanese government is not going to change. However, just because it is not a solution doesn't mean people shouldn't do it. Not every single thing that we do in life must solve problems. No solution to the problem? No problem. How about making a stand instead? And if protests are the fastest and most direct way to unite the people together and make a stand, then why not? Protests can be peaceful, too.

    Action speaks louder than words. Some may object the protests and criticise the Chinese of not being undiplomatic, but at least nobody could accuse the Chinese of being hypocrites. Which is more than what many could say for some other country.
