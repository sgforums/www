    Sombre countdown to 2005 on tap

    Parties expected to be muted, focus will be on aiding tsunami victims

    Friday Â• December 31, 2004

    Jasmine Yin
    jasmine.yin@newstoday.com.sg

    TONIGHT'S New Year countdown parties will proceed Â— but with less fanfare, and with many incorporating fundraising activities for the tsunami victims.

    As many Singaporeans called for tonight's parties to be cancelled out of respect for victims in the disaster, the Singapore Tourism Board also urged its partners Â— organisers of various big parties islandwide Â— to "be sensitive in organising their events" in light of the calamity.

    "This has been well-received by our partners, many of whom were already in the process of incorporating humanitarian initiatives into their events, for example donation of proceeds to relief funds, or adopting a more subdued tone for their New Year celebration events," said its assistant director (Arts), Miss Kim-Kyna Tan.

    Explaining that it would be difficult to cancel parties in a short frame of time, party organisers nonetheless pledged to usher in the new year in a meaningful way and with a more sombre mood.

    At the New Year's Eve at Esplanade party, a minute's silence will be observed instead of a countdown, and there will be no fireworks display at midnight. Donation boxes will be placed at the venue, with proceeds going to the Red Cross.

    The decision to cancel the fireworks display, part of an estimated $160,000 programme, was taken to "put into context the tragedy around us", said an Esplanade executive in a statement.

    The fireworks will be "reserved" for a "more appropriate occasion" in future, the statement said.

    When asked about the costs of modifying its programme, CEO Benson Puah said: "Surely everything that we do as a society cannot always be calculated in dollar terms. The right decision in this case is to cancel the fireworks. We need to rally as a people at this time and perhaps harp less on the costs of decisions made."

    Similarly, the Downtown East Countdown Concert organised by the NTUC Club intends to observe a minute's silence at 10pm and has cancelled plans for a fireworks display.

    The public can donate cash, clothing, food and the like at the event. All proceeds will be channelled to the Red Cross, and the Club will match dollar for dollar the cash donations collected. The total amount collected will be topped up to a minimum of $100,000 Â— regardless of the amount collected.

    The Nation's Countdown party at Sentosa, jointly organised by MediaCorp TV and Sentosa, will proceed, and a one-minute silence will be observed at the event to mourn the victims.

    However, as a mark of respect, MediaCorp TV will cancel its "live" telecast of the party on Channel 5.

    Said Ms Selena Ho, vice-president of Networking Programming and Promotions: "As a national broadcaster, we are mindful of the sentiments of Singaporeans on this recent tsunami tragedy."

    On the club scene, celebrations to usher in the New Year will proceed alongside initiatives to get support from its patrons for the victims.

    Said Zouk's marketing manager Tracy Phillips: "We will be going ahead as planned (because) we have a lot of sponsors. It is a tragedy, but life has to go on. We are going to do our best to try and help."

    Mr Michael Ma, 37, owner of the IndoChine bar and restaurant chain also plans to raise funds during IndoChine's celebrations. "We will continue to celebrate the New Year. We are very fortunate and we should celebrate this," he said.

    While other grassroots celebrations will also carry on, one organiser, however, will turn its countdown party into a prayer session that will also be a collection drive for cash donations.

    Northeast CDC, which shelved its countdown plans, will, instead, gather residents from Eunos and other parts of Singapore to show support for the tsunami victims. Religious leaders will be present for the prayer session, and the organiser hopes to raise at least $10,000 at the event, which will be held at Petal Gardens at Bedok Reservoir Road.

    Said Northeast CDC Mayor Zainul Abidin Rasheed: "The timing is wrong for celebrations like that. It's our small way of showing our solidarity with our neighbours and all those who have died and their families who are suffering. And we thought that rather than celebrate the new year, which we can do anytime, we'll convert that countdown gathering into a show of support."

    Show organisers to scale down celebrations of New Year countdown

    SINGAPORE : It is just two days before Singaporeans usher in the New Year.

    Islandwide, many event organisers have scaled back their celebrations at the last minute, as a mark of respect to the thousands of tsunami victims.

    Unlike the 2004 countdown at Sentosa, ushering in 2005 will be a different affair.

    Although the show will still go on, there will not be a 'live' telecast on TV.

    The images of the tsunami-hit areas and the victims are still fresh in many people's minds and it has undoubtedly changed the mood, as people usher in the New Year.

    As they say in show business, the show must go on and it will, but it will take on a whole different meaning.

    Selena Ho, Channel Vice President of Network Programming & Promotions 5, MediaCorp TV, said, "As a national broadcaster, we are mindful of the sentiments of Singaporeans and as a result and a mark of respect, we've decided not to telecast this countdown party in Sentosa.

    "In light of what has happened, Singaporeans, I think, would actually be open and welcome this move and gesture. It is a serious event that has happened and they won't be disappointed but instead, take this time to think about the unfortunate and hopefully be able to provide and help out."

    And the Downtown East party, which is expected to attract some 40,000 people, will be turned into a charity event.

    The NTUC Club will match every dollar donated by the public.

    It hopes to raise at least S$100,000 for the Red Cross.

    Chng Hee Kok, CEO, NTUC Club, said, "To have the party continue without expressing outwardly a sign of support would not be quite right. The easiest thing to do for us, in getting so many thousands of people together, is to get some money from those who participate, so they understand the seriousness of the problem."

    Preparations over at the Arts House have taken a back seat, as the area has been converted into a temporary collection centre, with hundreds of people making generous donations.

    Mary Loh, Audience Development, Arts House, said, "Asia has changed irretrievably, and we felt we could not celebrate the countdown without some sense of compassion. We've done this out of respect and we believe all Singaporeans similarly would remember their countdown in the same way."

    The Arts House, Esplanade and many other venues will not have pyrotechnics or fireworks.

    The Clock of Victoria Memorial Hall will also chime, for the first time in 50 years, to help remember those lost in the tsunami. - CNA
