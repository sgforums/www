    Project SuperStar has been the highest-rated variety show on MediaCorp TV Channel U this year.

    With the make-up of its audience stretching from teenagers to aunties, the singing competition attracts about 11 per cent of viewers over the age of 15.

    But the programme has been rocked by news that Malaysian semi-finalist Chew Sin Huey (picture) has been threatened on the MediaCorp Communities' Project Superstar forum.

    According to sources, a post on the forum threatened the Singaporean Permanent Resident with "extreme measures" when she returned to her hometown.

    Sources claim Chew was threatened with "rape" if she ever returned, but this couldn't be verified as the link was soon removed by the website's moderators.

    Project Superstar producers are taking the post extremely seriously because it included Chew's address in Malaysia.

    "I was shocked after reading it, because the writer posted my address and threatened to implicate my family, too," said the 24-year-old. "I can accept criticism directed at me, but not attacks on my family, and I was worried for their safety."

    Despite reaching the semi-finals, Chew has been one of the most criticised female contestants on the Project Superstar forum, drawing unflattering viewer comments such as "pretentious" and "ordinary looking".

    Being the only non-Singaporean among the semi-finalists, Chew has also had to deal with postings that claimed she "bought her way into the semis". The contest is open to both Singaporeans and Singaporean Permanent Residents.

    Critical comments on talent show postings are nothing new, of course. There have been heated discussions online since Project Superstar debuted on June 8 and Singapore's two previous major talent contests Â— MediaCorp TV Channel 5's Singapore Idol and Channel U's The Next Big Thing Â— also attracted their fair share of catty comments about the contestants.

    But a contestant had never had his or her family's address revealed online before and the website's moderators moved quickly to remove the post.

    When asked about the post, Lam Yen Fong, the assistant editor, NPP, Web Interactive (the forum's moderators) said: "Any post that is crude or vulgar, in bad taste and, most importantly, posts that have intention to threaten and/or cause fear and invading one's privacy will be deemed offensive and be deleted."

    Since the incident, Chew said she has been escorted to and from rehearsals and recordings by MediaCorp staff.

    Mr Paul Chan, channel vice-president of network programming and promotions for Chinese broadcasting at MediaCorp, says steps will be taken to ensure such incidents are not repeated.

    "Moving forward, the IT department will be implementing a membership login system, should the surfer wishes to leave a message, he or she will need to log in for verification," he said. "This will discourage surfers from posting offensive messages."
