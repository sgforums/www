Monism and Dualism, the final verdict. :P

    Finally I am able to find some time to write
    briefly about this. I repeat, briefly,
    eastern philosophy has been dealing with this
    topic for centuries and no conclusive
    conclusions have been reached. It is not a
    topic that can be covered in just one post.

    Let us first understand monism and dualism
    first. Monism is a philosophic equipoise that
    purports that all things, in whatever
    variety, can be reduced to one unified thing
    in space, time or quality. Dualism is the
    philosophic equipoise that purports that all
    things consist of 2 components, mind and the
    object being perceived.

    Eastern philosophy itself is highly
    fragmented and diverse in its views. I will
    try to describe from the point of views of
    3 main eastern philosophies, buddhism,
    hinduism and taoism.

    Before I go into describing, I have to once
    again reiterate, western philosophic
    arguments rest upon clear logical
    demarcation, and its argument style stems
    directly from that motivation. Eastern
    philosophy has a different motivation, and
    therefore you may notice that the way I
    describe Eastern philosophy will not fit
    the normal protocol western academics
    describe western philosophy.

    Buddhist point of view does not fall into
    such clearly divided lines - monism vs
    dualism. Madhyamikan (a buddhist philosophic
    school) demarcation perhaps is a better way
    to describe it to people trained under the
    western philosophic tradition. Madhyamikans
    propose 2 types of existence, inherent
    existence and conventional existence.

    Inherent existence - entity exists on its
    own rights.

    Conventional existence - that entities, on
    the perception level, exist.

    The buddhist position on this, is that
    entities do not have inherent existence -
    that it requires a mind (for example) for
    the entity to exist. That everything exists
    only in the web of interconnectedness of
    relationships (if there is no ear to listen
    to a voice, does the voice exist or not?).

    However, to deny all inherent existence is
    nihilistic, and that is not the position of
    buddhism. To account for human perception of
    entities, madhyamikans propose that entities
    conventionally exist, but do not inherently
    exist.

    In short, buddhist point of view can neither
    be classified under monism nor dualism. I
    shall refrain from talking more, buddhism is
    an art, a way of life, hardly something which
    can be intellectualized.

    Taoist point of view is clearly non
    dualistic (though I will not go far to say
    that it is monistic either), or rather
    I would say that Taoists would not even
    intellectualize upon the matter. It is clearly
    exemplified in chapter 2 of Chuang Tzu,
    where Chuang Tzu remarks after a dream
    about being a butterfly, that he does not
    know whether he is a butterfly dreaming that
    he is a man, or that he is a man dreaming
    that he is a butterfly. An important point
    to note is that he behaves as he is expected
    to be, unheld by all intellectual restriction
    and comtemplation (is he a butterfly or a
    man?)

    A taoist mind seeks to harmonize with his
    world. Living according to the world's rules
    and does not seek to change it, or even to
    intellectualize upon it. Taoism is an
    exemplary example of anti intellectualism.
    What is dualism and monism to a taoist?

    A hindu mind can be naively seen as being
    dualistic (hinduism is highly complex, there
    are opposing views, even within hinduism
    itself). Bhagavad Gita purports a view in
    which spiritual enlightenment is a process
    in which one's soul is being cleanse of all
    evil thoughts.

    However, that view is highly naive and if you
    read more deeply into Hinduism, you will find
    that Brahma somehow acts as a conciliatory
    principle to both dualistic elements, vishnu
    and shiva. How Brahma acts as a conciliatory
    principle to neutralise the dualism, forgive
    me, I am not that learned yet to understand
    it, or have even given it enough thought.

    With this, I think I shall end. I do not know
    much on how to conclude this subject matter,
    since eastern philosophic views are so
    diverse. But one thing for sure, eastern
    philosophic views are not based on strict
    logical demarcations that western tradition
    has subjected itself to.
