    interesting topic. i think abt it once in a while and just the other day my colleagues and i were talking abt this. but nothing much came out of it cos i was spending too much time figuring out what mask this particular guy was trying to wear! anyways, just some random thots and ramblings....

    closely tied to the idea of masks is how we want to project ourselves and let others see us. in other words, one's sense of identity and sense of self-worth and pride have a lot to do wif it. i think we are constantly creating ourselves. but this "image-crisis" is strongest when we are in our late teens and early twenties i believe. after that, it may be less a case of us actually discovering ourselves than that we have started to be less concerned with finding an answer. at this point we find ourselves more content with ourselves, with who we are and what we've become. in a way, we make peace with ourselves and learn to be happy with who we are. but here too lies the possibility that we may wake up one morning and, as in some kind of mid-life crisis, wonder whether this is all life was meant to be. but i digress....

    from the replies so far, i think the issue is a complex one, and one that means different things to different ppl. i'll focus on one aspect of it. i think it is necessary to distinguish between putting on a mask because it is part of your work and putting on a mask as a means of self-discovery. here is what i mean. when we are at work, there is an expectation that we carry ourselves in a particular way, that we act a certain way. if this is what you mean by having to put on a mask then, well, there isn't too much there to be said. there are several parts to this issue. on the one hand, we may have to put on a false front in front of bosses or colleagues or customers. but there is another word for that. i believe it's called professionalism. so even when we are crabby or tired or just plain sick of life, we cannot just be ourselves and "heck care everything". just the way life is.

    what i've found to be the best way to get past this "false front" thingey, is to be frens wif ur colleagues and bosses. this of course depends on the culture of the organisation. but if you can be frens, it takes the load off a lot and ppl see you for who you are. being frens allows u to show your vulnerabilities and your concerns. it shows you are human. but then this other side of us may feel that showing our weaknesses would detract from our performance appraisals or would put us in a bad light. so it's a delicate balance between being able to show your "true" self and being (perhaps overly) concerned abt how it will affect our climb up the corporate ladder. suffice to say that a positive result in the case of the latter doesn't always come from a suppression of the former. IF (and that's a big "if") you are the very ambitious type, then you will not find much happiness if there is too great a divide between what u feel is your "true" self and what u think is permissible to let others see abt you (your front). i don't know why, but it seems to me that the more driven a person is, the more he/she will experience a gulf between their true selves and "false" fronts. the successful ones are those who can bridge the gulf. in my view, if ur not very ambitious, then why on earth put up such a front. just be yourself and you'll be much happier. but if u are terribly ambitious, unfortunately impressions (and sometimes only impressions) count a lot. in such a case, "right" impressions matter - and so "fronts" are created.

    on the other hand, the more interesting qns abt "real" and "false" selves are, i feel, whether it makes any sense to say that role-playing, far from being "other" than your true self, is really an essential part of creating your "true" self. we are all caught up in a nexus of expectations and more expectations. do u feel a sense of responsibility to fulfill these expectations (whether from family, society or whatever)? why do you feel this way? because u really believe in something? because u don't want ppl to say things abt you, talk abt you?

    whatever the particular reasons, fronts are put up because of certain expectations we feel we need to fufill. so maybe we should ask ourselves - which of these fronts are really important to us? cos some fronts are there because of who we really want to be. in such cases, masks are the projection of an ideal, something we want to attain. and sometimes (not always) playing a role affects a blurring of the line between "true" and "false" selves. so false fronts may not always be bad. sometimes the tension between true and false selves is transformational - it helps us become who we want to be. because if everything we do has to be "in character", then there is no possibility of great change. doing something out of character, by maybe playing a role, sometimes leads to self-improvement. having said that, i understand the growing pains we get trying to discover ourselves. instead of seeing masks as a bad thing, maybe we could try focussing on those masks we really can't stand wearing. those are the ones we remove, as far as we can. the others help shape and define the kind of person we'll be.

    hmmm....not very organised....but i hope u catch my drift anyways.
