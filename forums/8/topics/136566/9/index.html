    Hi Fugue,

    You raise an interesting question.

    I feel that the answer to it will have to depend on what exactly you mean by the term 'to moonlight'.

    If by 'to moonlight' you are referring to the act of a person who is already employed in one profession, seeking to be employed in another profession, then I'm not sure whether it would be prudent that a pastor should allow himself to 'moonlight' in this way.

    If the pastor already draws a salary that is paid to him out of his church's coffers, then I don't see why he would have to seek employment in a different profession, unless he feels that the remuneration his current salary affords him is insufficient for the purposes of meeting what he conceives to be his needs.

    In a case where the pastor is truly paid by his church a salary that cannot meet what may truly be ascertained to be (with reference to objective criteria) his authentic needs (as opposed to whatever desires of his own that he would like selfishly to conceive of as corresponding to his authentic needs), then I think that it would be legitimate that he should seek to moonlight, in what would, of course, be a legitimate mode.

    In contrast to what some of the other so-called 'Christian' posters here have declared, I do not think that there is anything that could in principle be wrong with the idea of a pastor moonlighting as a singer.

    If my church truly pays me a salary that truly is insufficient to meet what are truly my authentic needs, then of course I may moonlight as a singer, insofar as it is the case that to moonlight as a singer would involve me in doing no more than the singing of songs, regardless of whether or not the songs which I should sing should win the approval of the few or the many; for I do not think that there could be anything that in itself would be wrong with the singing of songs so as to disqualify it as a mode whereby a pastor might legitimately seek to 'moonlight'.

    In other words, I can conceive of situations wherein a pastor could legitimately function as a popular singer as part of an attempt on his part at 'moonlighting'.

    The problem that people ought to have with a pastor moonlighting as a singer of popular music therefore should not turn upon the fact that the music in question should be popular; rather, what ought to perturb someone about a so-called pastor who 'moonlights' as a popular singer, is the question of whether or not it is the case that the songs which he or she sings or the music which he or she produces is calculated to appeal to the masses in a mode that is not legitimate and which accordingly involves him or her in the flouting of moral boundaries.

    I hope this suffices to answer the question you have raised.

    Ben
