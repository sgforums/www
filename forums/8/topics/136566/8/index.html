    Response:

    If it's a question of accepting what is merely a stamp that my friends have bought for me and intend to be giving to me as a gift, then I would agree that it is true that I would be doing them a disservicing (all other things being equal) if I were to throw back into their faces the stamp that they have bought for me.

    However, rejecting openly the invitation to attend the sevices of a church that someone may issue to me (regardless of whether or not he is a friend of mine), on the grounds that the said church professes beliefs and endorses practices that are in my opinion dangerous, would not be tantamount to doing this particular person a disservice.

    In asking me or in otherwise inviting me to attend the services of his church (regardless of the trouble he may have been put through, in order for him to have been able to do this), this person ought to realise that the right is entirely reserved to me to refuse this invitation; and that because I may refuse it on the grounds that I disagree profoundly with some of the practices and beliefs of his church, it would follow therefore that I would be not only entitled to refuse his invitation to attend his church but even indeed to do so in a way that would have left him in no doubt as to where I stood concerning my esteem of his church.

    To accept a gift like a rubber ball (where the giver of the rubber ball was well-intentioned in giving it and where one knew of his good intentions in this regard) would be an action that in itself would be moral; but to accept an invitation to attend a church would, however, not be necesarrily moral even if one should have been made aware of the fact that the person issuing such an invitation had no ulterior motives for having issued it.

    In fact, the people issuing an invitation to others to attend their church have ABSOLUTELY NO RIGHT WHATSOEVER to take it personally when this invitation of theirs is turned down openly; for in inviting another person to their church, they ought to realise that this person may have conscientious and sincerely felt objections to whatever it is that their church may stand for.

    How do you know that you care for them?

    I can assure them that Christ cares for them because I believe that He gave His Sacred Blood on the Cross for them (and for me), but I would think twice myself about making grandiose claims about the affection that I supposedly bear them.

    Do you think (as you appear above to have ignorantly implied) that a person's care for someone else is necesarily made manifest in and by an invitation that he issues to this particular person to attend his church?

    I don't deny that if A loves B then it is plausible that A, by way of loving B, would invite B to his church; but I do deny nonetheless that one of the ways in which it would be afforded us enough of a warrant as to be certain that A loves B, is by our seeing A invite B to his church.
