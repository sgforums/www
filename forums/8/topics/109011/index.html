    Ireland's gum tax
    Plan to discourage rampant littering may net govt up to $11 million a year
    By Neo Hui Min
    Straits Times Europe Bureau

    LONDON - FED UP of blob-splattered streets, Ireland plans to impose a chewing-gum tax that could also net the government between 4 million and 5 million euros (S$8.8 million and S$11 million) annually - twice the sum raised from littering fines last year.
    STICKY PROBLEM: Streets littered with blobs of gum are a common sight in Ireland. Some 500 tonnes of used gum can be found on the roads. Installing gum bins in public places has not stopped the litter bugs. Hence, the new measure of levying a tax. -- PHOTO ILLUSTRATION: LIM YONG & TERENCE TAN

    It collected 2.74 million euros in such fines last year.

    But more than that, the move marks the beginning of a concerted effort to discourage people from littering the streets with chewing gum.

    Some 500 tonnes of used gum can be found on Irish streets. And the problem has been getting stickier over the years.

    In 2002, used gum made up about 18.49 per cent of all litter, but last year, it grew to 28.26 per cent.

    The government had installed chewing-gum bins in public places to discourage people from littering, but this did not really work.

    So, this has now given way to the more draconian measure of levying a chewing gum tax.

    A consultation paper put out by a litter-monitoring body recommended that the Irish government impose a mandatory levy of 10 per cent on the manufacturer, distributor or importer of chewing gum.

    It said the industry should 'share the responsibility of the pollution caused by its products'.

    It is believed that once the proposed tax is passed by Parliament, the price of each pack of gum could go up by about 5 euro cents.

    The Irish government is hoping the move would enable it to recoup at least some of the amount it spends on cleaning up the streets each year, though it would be far from adequate.

    The group's report said that the total amount of money spent on cleaning would be much more than what the tax could bring in to government coffers.

    Besides, there is also the 'social cost of chewing-gum litter' which the figure does not reflect, the report said.

    It noted that the total expenditure on litter collection was about 65 million euros.

    Of this sum, about 12 million euros would be spent on cleaning up chewing-gum litter.

    The idea of a tax comes as the Irish government moves to get tough with other anti-social behaviour.

    It banned smoking in pubs in March after imposing a levy on plastic bags in March 2002.

    The levy, meant to reduce litter pollution, resulted in a 90 per cent decrease in the use of disposable plastic bags.

    It also generated some 20 million euros in funds which now go towards environmental projects.

    Prior to the introduction of the levy, plastic bags made up 5 per cent of national litter. It now makes up only 0.3 per cent.

    The litter-monitoring group believed that, besides chewing gum, a levy should also be imposed on fast-food packaging and ATM receipts to curb littering.

    It also recommended imposing a sales levy of 2 per cent on the price of fast food consumed off premises, which could raise about 3.7 million euros per year for the government.

    To tackle the problem of ATM receipt litter, however, it said the government should have an agreement with the banking sector on reducing such waste.

    The Minister for the Environment, Heritage and Local Government, Mr Dick Roche, told Parliament recently that his department was evaluating feedback on the report, and that 'a final decision will be made on this matter as soon as possible'.
