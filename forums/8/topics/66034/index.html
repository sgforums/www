    Flying apart - to avoid wipeout
    Family members split up when travelling to avoid losing all kin at one go

    By Tracy Quek

    WHEN Mrs Wong Ee Ling and her husband went on holiday with her family in November, they didn't take the same flight.

    Photo: WANG HUI FEN
    Her brother and his wife, her parents and her younger sister were also booked on separate flights, said Mrs Wong, a 28-year-old executive.

    Her father, a businessman, decided to split them up because he 'didn't want everyone to die together should something go wrong', said Mrs Wong.

    Though no Singaporean died as a result of the Sept 11, 2001, terrorist attacks in the United States, the spectre has made some families here insist on separate flights to the same holiday destination or when flying home.

    They do this deliberately, to minimise the risk of losing all their loved ones in a plane crash or hijack, especially when there are young children who might be orphaned in one fell swoop.

    In an instant, 9/11 turned air travel into a high-risk activity in their minds.

    At least three travel agencies contacted by The Sunday Times had clients who requested separate flights for family holidays.

    Crest Travel's general manager, Ms Bun Guat Eng, said she handled such bookings for about five families last year. Before the 9/11 attacks, there were just one or two requests a year.

    'Most are professionals, or from prominent, well-off families. They usually pick the more established airlines as well. They will also have made some sort of financial arrangements for their children and spouses,' she said.

    The parents take separate flights, dividing the children between them.

    Mr Francis Telgallego, director of operations at Excel Travel Services, said three clients had made such requests last year. There were none two years ago.

    He said: 'For long-haul flights, they may fly the same route but on different days.'

    Mrs Margaret Oh, 58, insisted on her family doing just that when they flew Air Asia to Kota Kinabalu, East Malaysia, two weeks ago. The homemaker and her husband, a retiree, left first. Their younger daughter, Lorrine, 27, an air-traffic controller, followed the next day.

    Said Mrs Oh, whose other daughter is 31: 'The chances of both planes crashing are very remote, so at least some of us will survive.'

    However, going to such lengths seems unnecessary and a hassle to others. Fifteen other travel agents said most families still prefer to fly together.

    Said accountant Ann Tay, 36, mother of two young children: 'By being together if disaster strikes, we'll have the chance to take care of each other and, if so destined, depart as a family.'

    But for some, separate flights have been a way of life for years.

    The last time Miss Chin Jit Peng, 27, took a flight with sister Jit Fong, 25, was a decade ago, when they were teenagers. Their businessman father couldn't risk losing both daughters at once.

    'It was when we were about to leave for university and my parents realised what it felt like not to have us by their sides,' said the elder Miss Chin, an associate with a corporate finance company.

    Like them, Mr J. Wong, a businessman in his 50s, has avoided travelling with his brother for the last 30 years, even by car.

    'We agreed that if something happens to me, he will look after my family and vice versa.

    'So we are never in the same plane or car,' he said.

    He makes sure his three children, all studying in Australia, fly home on separate flights.

    'If I had 15 children, I don't think I would care so much. But with two or three, I won't take such risks,' he said.
