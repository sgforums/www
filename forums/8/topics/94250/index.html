    Demand for writing skills is getting greater but firms say a third of workers fall short of requirements
    NEW YORK - A majority of US employers say about one-third of workers do not meet the writing requirements of their positions, according to a survey by the College Board's National Commission on Writing.

    'Businesses are really crying out - they need to have people who write better,' said Mr Gaston Caperton, president of the College Board, the organisation which administers standardised exams used by universities in making admissions decisions.

    Advertisement

    Company officials say solid writing skills - including brevity, accuracy, punctuation and grammar - are increasingly ranked among the most sought-after skills, particularly when hiring or promoting salaried workers.

    'There's no way to say that writing has gotten worse,' said Ms Susan Traiman, director of the education initiative for the Business Roundtable.

    Rather, 'the demand has gotten greater'.

    The commission surveyed Business Roundtable members in six sectors - mining; cons- truction; manufacturing; transportation and utilities; services; and finance, insurance and real estate.

    Two-thirds of salaried workers in large companies in the United States have jobs that require some writing - and this includes some of the most technical occupations.

    But some industry executives say solid writing skills are necessary even for hourly workers and for varied groups such as electricians, engineers and foremen, who often draft proposals for government agencies and regulatory bodies.

    A majority of survey respondents said about two-thirds of employees meet writing requirements.

    More than half of the companies surveyed reported that they assess writing in hiring and promoting salaried employees.

    The College Board, which administers the SAT, PSAT and Advanced Placement tests, says the responsibility for churning out better writers lies with grade schools and universities.

    But training represents another potential solution.

    'You're never too old to learn,' said Mr Bob Kerrey, co-chair of the writing commission and president of the New School University in New York City.
