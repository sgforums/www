    The 2007Â–08 Writers Guild of America strike is a strike by the Writers Guild of America, East (WGAE) and the Writers Guild of America, West (WGAW) that started on November 5, 2007. The WGAE and WGAW are two labor unions representing film, television and radio writers working in the United States. Over 12,000 writers joined the strike.

    The strike is against the Alliance of Motion Picture and Television Producers (AMPTP), a trade organization representing the interests of 397 American film and television producers.[3], the most influential of which are eight corporations: CBS Inc. (headed by Les Moonves), Metro-Goldwyn-Mayer (headed by Harry E. Meyer).

    The Writers Guild has indicated their industrial action would be a "marathon." AMPTP negotiator Nick Counter has indicated negotiations would not resume as long as strike action continues, stating, "We're not going to negotiate with a gun to our headsÂ—that's just stupid."

    The current strike has lasted 9 weeks and 6 days, as of January 13, 2008. The last such strike in 1988 lasted 21 weeks and 6 days, costing the American entertainment industry an estimated $500 million ($870 million in 2007 dollars).
