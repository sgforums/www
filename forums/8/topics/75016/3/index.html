    Originally posted by subudei:
    A little knowledge can be a dangerous thing. A VERY dangerous thing.

    Registering your marriage and signing a pre-nup in another jurisdiction do absolutely nothing to improve your position. We're dealing with Conflict of Laws / Private International Law principles here - and under this set of rules, while ordinary international contracts are normally accorded validity with little difficulty, pre-nups are an exception. They will be denied validity in Singapore on one, or both, of the following grounds: 1) general non-enforceability; and / or 2) being contrary to public interest / public policy.

    As for enforcing your pre-nup in another country, e.g. the U.S.: this would only work if most of your assets were situated in that other country. Any assets you have in Singapore would be subject to the normal 50-50 split mandated by the Women's Charter. If you obtained an order for the enforcement of a pre-nup in a U.S. court, and attempted to enforce that order vis-a-vis assets in Singapore, the Singapore courts wouldn't give you the time of day. You could try moving your assets out of Singapore and into the U.S., but your estranged wife would easily be able to obtain a court order to freeze your Singapore assets and deny you access to them.

    Signing a pre-nup wouldn't even be useful for the purpose of 'evidence', as suggested by a previous poster. 'Evidence' for what? Whatever 'evidence' you think the pre-nup offers would be completely disregarded by the Singapore courts, since under the law, as represented by the Women's Charter, a 50-50 division of assets is mandated regardless of any other factors - regardless of fault or culpability or any prior agreement between the parties.     Originally posted by :
    Then, what if you held a trust for the children or to your parents would be better. Hence, all your assets will be put down into this trust for your parents, can the ex-wife still have a hold to the trust?
    Hmmm, my trust law isn't that up to scratch, but off the top of my head I'd say you have two main problems here:

    1) the trust is likely to be held to be fraudulent, i.e. set up to evade legal obligations, and declared invalid; and

    2) even if the trust is a valid one: assuming you're declaring yourself the trustee, that would mean that you would be under an obligation to administer / spend the trust assets in the best interests of the beneficiaries of the trust, i.e. your children or parents in this case.

    That would mean that you couldn't actually spend any of that money on yourself - you would have to invest / spend it in such a way as to improve the trust assets, for the benefit of your beneficiaries. You could probably buy property with it (since that would count as an investment), but you couldn't do things like use it to buy cars, clothes or even food for yourself! And whatever investments you made with the money would themselves be held on trust for your beneficiaries.

    You could probably pay yourself a small remuneration from the trust fund to compensate you for your work as trustee, but it would have to be a reasonable amount, and probably not anywhere near sufficient for even your basic living expenses. Of course, the bigger the trust fund, the more remuneration you can probably justify for yourself, but for you to lead a decent lifestyle, it would have to be a bloody HUGE trust fund.

    So really, there are so many problems with the trust law route that it's really quite an ineffective approach.

    Oh yes, an additional problem re: the use of trusts which I forgot to mention previously: the beneficiaries of a trust, as long as they are more than 21 years old and are mentally sound, can collectively order that the trust be dismantled and the trust assets automatically transferred to their full legal ownership. So, for example, if you declared a trust in favour of your children, once they all reach 21 years of age, they can collectively apply to the court for a dissolution of the trust, and have the trust assets transferred to their name.

    In that situation, you would of course completely lose access to the trust assets - your kids, or whoever your beneficiaries are, would get them.
