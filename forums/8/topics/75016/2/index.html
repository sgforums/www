    A little knowledge can be a dangerous thing. A VERY dangerous thing.

    Registering your marriage and signing a pre-nup in another jurisdiction do absolutely nothing to improve your position. We're dealing with Conflict of Laws / Private International Law principles here - and under this set of rules, while ordinary international contracts are normally accorded validity with little difficulty, pre-nups are an exception. They will be denied validity in Singapore on one, or both, of the following grounds: 1) general non-enforceability; and / or 2) being contrary to public interest / public policy.

    As for enforcing your pre-nup in another country, e.g. the U.S.: this would only work if most of your assets were situated in that other country. Any assets you have in Singapore would be subject to the normal 50-50 split mandated by the Women's Charter. If you obtained an order for the enforcement of a pre-nup in a U.S. court, and attempted to enforce that order vis-a-vis assets in Singapore, the Singapore courts wouldn't give you the time of day. You could try moving your assets out of Singapore and into the U.S., but your estranged wife would easily be able to obtain a court order to freeze your Singapore assets and deny you access to them.

    Signing a pre-nup wouldn't even be useful for the purpose of 'evidence', as suggested by a previous poster. 'Evidence' for what? Whatever 'evidence' you think the pre-nup offers would be completely disregarded by the Singapore courts, since under the law, as represented by the Women's Charter, a 50-50 division of assets is mandated regardless of any other factors - regardless of fault or culpability or any prior agreement between the parties. It is a strict division of assets, and even if your estranged wife has been an adulterous whore and guilty as sin, she still gets half of your assets.

    That's right, Singapore law is -that- stupid.
