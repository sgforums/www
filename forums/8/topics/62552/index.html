    Scholarship may mean China stint
    PM says this will boost Singapore's understanding

    By Jason Leow

    BEIJING - Singaporean students eyeing government scholarships to the United States may in the future be asked to also enrol in Chinese universities, as part of a dual scholarship scheme.

    This idea was mooted yesterday by Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong in a media interview mid-way through his six-day working visit to China.

    The scheme, he said, would be a way to nurture a large pool of Singaporeans who understand China and see its potential.

    Students now see the US as the preferred destination.

    But he wants a realistic way to shift their mindset to China, and handing out dual scholarships is one solution.

    'Now a scholarship of choice is a scholarship to the US. I don't think we can change that.

    'So what we have got to do is to send our brightest government officials on two scholarships,' he said, hours before leaving for Kunming, Yunnan province.

    The idea is to award such scholarships to the brightest candidates - the potential permanent secretaries.

    Unlike now, education in either Harvard or Stanford will not mean they 'have it made'.

    They also need to have spent time in China, perhaps after graduating in the US, he said.

    'If they can do that, in the course of their careers as young officials, we would have top civil servants who understand China much better,' he said.

    The scheme would ensure that when China becomes a big and powerful economy in the next two to three decades, there will be many Singaporeans who understand China.

    'If we don't, we will lose out on the advantages that we have,' he warned.

    Asked when the scheme will be launched, he said: 'It is just an idea.'

    Details, such as getting Chinese universities to cooperate, need to be worked out, he added.

    Some courses, for instance, last more than two years. So the Government needs universities' help in planning shorter programmes, perhaps of a year, before the scholarship scheme can take off.

    Yesterday, Mr Goh also responded to comments made on Wednesday by former Chinese ambassador to Singapore Chen Baoliu, that Singaporeans should shed their superiority when working with the Chinese.

    Mr Goh said Madam Chen was a friend of Singapore, having served in the Republic. 'She knows us well and I read her comments as wanting to be helpful to Singapore,' he said.

    Some Singaporeans might have come to China thinking they have something to teach the Chinese. If the arrogance is prevalent, then Singaporeans must change, he said.

    The Government's approach is one of humility, coming to China and acknowledging that the Chinese also have lessons for Singapore, he noted.

    Asked what these lessons were, he cited hunger for learning and ability to adapt.

    'It's not just the infrastructure, the office buildings, the hotels that we see. It's the thinking in the Chinese people that has begun to change, particularly.

    'That is something we can learn,' he said.

    China is growing, no doubt. But it is not growing evenly. The cosmopolitan parts of China are growing too fast for the rural parts of China to catch up. This is esp. unhealthy national growth, considering these built-up cities make up only a small percentage of the country, with the bulk made up by the rural areas.

    Let's take for example a teenager now going through the growing stage of puberty. Can you imagine what will happen if say the arms grow super fast but the rest of the body grow slowly? This is the same with China. It is understandable that they are changing as they need to catch up wif the western powers, but if they continue with this rate of growth, nation-wide segregation will arise.

    Initially when the gap is not as wide, the more backward people will admire the developed people, and work towards being like them. But this will not be, as the government is not letting the country grow evenly. Eventually the gap will widen, and this admiration will turn into resentment. Social classes may develop, and unhappiness will brew.

    China holds one-fifth of the world's population, and is now growing like a ticking time bomb. Watch out.
