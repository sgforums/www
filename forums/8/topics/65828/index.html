    NTUC Income facing legal action over map download

    It is among almost 100 companies sent bills of up to $50,000 for downloading maps from a street directory website

    ABOUT 100 companies, including big names like NTUC Income, are finding out the hard way that downloading information from the Internet can be expensive.

    Around 40 of them are now facing legal action after downloading maps from Streetdirectory.com. The rest have settled out of court.

    Many of the companies, especially the smaller businesses, are crying foul after being slapped with letters from lawyers representing Virtual Map, which runs the website, demanding the payment of five-figure sums as high as $50,000.

    The maps were mostly used to show the location of their offices on their corporate websites.

    They said the letters came without warning and that the maps had been either downloaded without their knowledge or authorisation, put there by an outsourced Web design firm, or removed from the website for more than a year.

    One of those facing action is NTUC Income.

    Its chief executive, Mr Tan Kin Lian, declined to comment on the suit on the advice of his lawyers.

    When contacted, Virtual Map executive director Ghanshyam Uttamram said the company had decided to take action after major clients walked out when they realised others had acquired the maps for free while they had to pay up to $3,800 for one.

    Virtual Map, a local company specialising in navigation and tracking programs, hired a detective to track down the freeloaders.

    Almost 100 firms, local and foreign, are alleged to have committed copyright infringements.

    He told The Straits Times: 'Our stand is that we will not be held to ransom by any infringer, no matter how big they may be.'

    Exceptions could be made for personal users who download the maps for private purposes, like wedding invitations, or even 'mom and pop' shops, like the case of a chicken rice stall in Toa Payoh, Mr Ghanshyam said.

    'We tend to close both eyes when it's for more personal use,' he added.

    So where does he draw the line?

    'Everyone now will say, 'I'm a mom and pop shop'. So we want to treat it on a case-by-case basis,' he said.

    Several lawyers The Straits Times spoke to said that the common perception was that online downloads were free.

    While many people know books and lately music should not be copied freely, the tendency to right-click the mouse and download an online graphic or picture is rarely seen by most as an offence.

    One lawyer said: 'Read the fine print, as ignorance is not an excuse for copyright infringements.'

    Mr S.C. Tan, director of a car financing company facing legal action, said he had just been dealt a lawyer's letter demanding $13,908 for copyright infringement.

    The sum covered the licence fee, investigation and legal costs, according to the letter, he said.

    He said he had made an offer to settle but the company had turned it down.

    Mr Ghanshyam told The Straits Times: 'If any of the companies face difficulty paying, we can offer them options like interest-free instalments or settle it on a private basis without lawyers.'

    DOWNLOADERS TAKE NOTE

    COPYRIGHTED materials sent over the Internet or stored on Web servers are no different from material in other media.

    In cyberspace, the temptation to pilfer - from music to images - is strong, as it is so easily available.

    But it is still considered stealing.

    Several lawyers offered this analogy: In the real world, when someone invites you into his home, it doesn't mean you can help yourself to whatever is in it.

    The Intellectual Property Office of Singapore (Ipos) put it this way: 'The fact that they are made available on the Internet does not constitute a waiver of copyright, nor does it carry any implied licence for anyone to download or reproduce the material without the permission of the copyright owner.'

    Ipos assistant director-general Wong Sheng Kwai said downloading material infringed copyright except where it could be considered 'fair dealing'.
