    TRIAD GANGS ARE BACKED by a compelling mythology. They emerged centuries ago as secret societies sworn to oust the alien Manchus from China and reinstall the Ming dynasty. Operating under a strict code of loyalty, triads were regarded as jaunty bands of patriotic scoundrels who employed guerrilla tactics against evil forces. But such a beneficent image is long since outdated, perpetuated only in the movies, which, in Hong Kong and Taiwan at least, are believed to be dominated by the triads' latter-day descendants. Indeed, there is little that is romantic about the gangs' challenge to authority today. It consists of drugs, prostitution and gambling, as well as pyrotechnics from the occasional public shootout or gangland slaying.

     

    And the influence of triads has spread wide. In Taiwan, they now wield significant political clout. New Zealand's police chief said just recently that his men were in danger of losing control over triad activity in his country. Mounting gangland capers are reported as far away as the United States and Russia. The triads are also diversifying their activities, especially into legitimate businesses such as entertainment, food, interior design and even property development and management. In Hong Kong, the local Association of Banks three years ago warned its members against employing triad-linked organizations to collect debt on their behalf. Even so, reputable companies continue to do so. The gangsters may even be menacing the territory's freedom of expression. When a top local editor had his arm severed recently by suspected triads after his magazine published an underworld exposé, alarmed journalists raised $650,000 to offer as a reward for information leading to the capture of the culprits
