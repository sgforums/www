    IN JULY, Singapore Prime Minister Goh Chok Tong urged his countrymen to snap out of their gloom and focus their attention on signs of "good growth." He may be right.

    The Monetary Authority of Singapore, the country's de facto central bank, projected in its semiannual review of the economy on July 18 that growth this year will come in at the higher end of the government's 2%-4% forecast range. That would mark a significant recovery over last year, when the economy contracted by 2% in its worst performance since independence in 1965.

    Is the economy starting to revive? Preliminary second-quarter numbers certainly exceeded expectations. Advance, or flash estimates, from the Ministry of Trade and Industry show that GDP grew a better-than-expected 3.2% year on year in the April-June quarter following four quarters of contraction.

    Technically the numbers indicate an end to Singapore's worst recession in its history. But it was just a handful of sectors that lifted the economy, economists note; the trade ministry attributed the second-quarter gains to a surge in international demand for non-electronic products, such as petrochemicals and pharmaceuticals. And recovery in the small South East Asian city state still lags those of several of its Asian competitors such as Malaysia, Taiwan and South Korea, some economists note. A broad-based recovery hinges on a global rebound in demand for electronic goods, which account for about two-thirds of the nation's total exports.

    Falling prices and sluggish sales of electronics chips underscore how fragile the recovery is. Singapore's non-oil domestic exports in the month of June fell a nominal 0.6% year on year to S$7.75 billion ($4.4 billion) because of weak sales of electronics goods. Indeed, Goh's appeal to Singaporeans to shake off their gloom about the economy may be harder to achieve in the coming months. The recovery in the U.S. seems to be faltering. Singapore's construction sector remains weak on a sluggish property market. And while preliminary government figures released on July 31 indicated that the unemployment rate in June fell to 4.1%, against 4.5% in March, job losses are still a big worry. The government forecasts that unemployment will grow to around 5.5% this year, up from 4.4% in December 2001. Singapore posted its worst unemployment rate of 6% during the 1986 recession.

    A number of proposed price rises in essential services are adding to the gloom--particularly a recent proposal to hike transportation fares. Many Singaporeans are also concerned about the increase from 3% to 5% of the goods and services tax that will go into effect from January 2003.
