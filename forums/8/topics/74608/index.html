Is that chatline babe being paid to talk to you?

  • F Takumi
    2 Apr 04, 14:22
    Chatline companies may be hiring women to befriend callers and keep them on the line

    By Joann Tan and Deborah Koh
    THAT friendly female speaking to you on a telephone chatline may not be a fellow user looking for a friend, but a woman hired to chat you up.

    Three chatline companies appear to be employing staff to do this, The Straits Times has discovered.

    These 'telephone operators' have been told to pretend to befriend the caller and keep him on the line for as long as they can.

    Depending on the company, a caller can be charged from 50 to 90 cents for each minute he chats. Some users of the lines have monthly bills that run into hundreds of dollars.

    This is how it works: A caller uses the line to get in touch with someone, generally of the opposite sex. He leaves a voice message giving some information about himself, such as his name, age and hobbies.

    A hired telephone operator who uses the line keys in her user identification number, given by the company, to listen to the messages left by male callers.

    After selecting a man to chat with, she engages in a conversation via voice messages. Both are on the line at the same time, but they are not allowed to speak directly to each other.

    The operators earn about eight to 10 cents for each message the man leaves.

    Three companies have been advertising for female telephone operators in the newspapers over the last two weeks.

    When The Straits Times contacted them, it got through to only one, Voice Response Technology, and it denied it was hiring female operators and declined to comment further.

    A director of several chatline companies, who wanted to be known only as Mr Lee, said that some companies hire operators due to the shortage of female callers.

    'Male callers usually outnumber female callers,' he said. 'So to cope, the companies hire women to chat with the men.'

    Several chatline companies said that they are aware of the situation, but declined to provide more details.

    The president of the Audiotex Service Providers Association (Aspa), Mr Samuel Lim, said there is no rule prohibiting chatline companies from hiring female operators.

    The association was set up to regulate the industry and enforce guidelines, censoring lewd and sexually suggestive messages and determining callers are at least 18 years old.

    But only four companies in the industry of about 10 players belong to the association.

    The three regulatory bodies - Infocomm Development Authority (IDA), Media Development Authority (MDA) and SingTel, each of which has a hand in overseeing various aspects of the audiotex service - also said there are no clear regulations on the hiring of female operators.

    IDA only issues licences that regulate the 'technical aspects' of such services, while SingTel is the billing agent for the companies which provide the 1900 telephone services.

    MDA regulates the content of chatlines. It disallows offensive material and people offering sexual services, among other things.

    In an e-mail response, the executive director of the Consumers Association of Singapore (Case), Mr Seah Seng Choon, advised chatline users to 'exercise caution' when making use of such a service.

    Last year, Case received 14 complaints about companies offering 1900 services. Most involved information presented in a misleading manner in their advertisements. The majority were made against chatline services.

    Technician Peter Kong, 21, who spends up to $900 a month on chatlines, was disappointed to learn he may be chatting up a phone operator.

    'I don't want someone who is paid to talk to me,' he said. 'I'm looking for real friends.'
