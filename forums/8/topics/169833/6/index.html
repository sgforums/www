    After being pilloried in the press and slammed by the KPMG report, former NKF chief executive T T Durai gave a point-by-point rebuttal to the various issues raised about him.

    According to his lawyers' calculations, Mr Durai had in fact foregone $1.18 million in salary increments since he became chief executive officer in 1992 Â— "less than he would have if he had accepted the offer even after taking into account retrospective increment and bonuses".

    However, KPMG pointed out that the former board had delegated all its powers to the Exco, which in turn delegated most of them to Mr Durai.     Mr Durai responded that as Exco members and specialised sub-committee members, the board members were "always involved in decision-making, acting together with others who had special skills and an appreciation of the mission and programmes of the NKF".

    This logic was put down by KPMG as "overly-generalised and disingenuous".

    In a defence of former director Matilda Chua's high exit payout package when she resigned in 2000, Mr Durai said she was instrumental to the success of the NKF's charity show that year, and that she had deferred her resignation tendered in January 2000 to run the charity show.

    "The June 2000 show was a success and raised $7 million gross. It was the practice of the NKF in previous years to pay a performance bonus to staff involved in organising and running a successful charity show. In accordance with this practice, the NKF paid Matilda a six-month performance bonus (worth $75,000) in July 2000," he said.

    As for what has become known as the "elastic patient numbers", Mr Durai said his knowledge of these numbers was "entirely dependent on the information supplied by the Department of Patient Advancement".

    But even as internal requests were made for consistent patient figures, KPMG uncovered in an email trail that "(Mr Durai) says to stick with 2,000 patients".
