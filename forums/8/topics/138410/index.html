    School exams 'likely to be dropped'

    Click to enlarge photo
    GCSEs and A-Levels were likely to be replaced with a new diploma amid mounting pressure from schools and colleges to drop them, according to the head of Ofsted.

    David Bell said that "practice might outstrip policy" despite the Government's insistence they are here to stay.

    Education Secretary Ruth Kelly controversially rejected proposals by the former chief inspector of schools, Sir Mike Tomlinson, to replace GCSEs and A-Levels with a new continental-style diploma.

    She announced the "gold standard" qualifications would stay and instead proposed a system of mainly vocational diplomas to run alongside existing exams.

    But Mr Bell said many schools and colleges were now working together to set up courses that better reflected students' interests, in line with Sir Mike's proposals.

    He told The Times: "I just wonder if this is a good example of where practice might outstrip policy. If you go to schools and colleges they are beginning to do more of the things that were envisaged.

    "The Secretary of State has quite categorically said GCSE and A-Level are there, but three years down the line how does the qualifications structure fit with the practice?"

    Mr Bell's comments mirror those of England's exams chief Ken Boston. Earlier this month he said A-Levels would disappear within the next 10 years.

    Dr Boston, chief executive of exams watchdog the Qualifications and Curriculum Authority, predicted a new diploma system would replace A-Levels as the 'natural' qualification for people to take.

    However, despite the predictions, Ms Kelly on Tuesday underlined her insistence that the exams would not be swept away. She reportedly told political journalists at a Westminster lunch: "The education world can sometimes cloud the debate and give the wrong impression. A-Levels and GCSEs are here to stay."
