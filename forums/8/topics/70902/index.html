    Scientists link mouth cancer to oral sex

    LONDON - The risk is small but scientists have uncovered evidence that points to oral sex being a cause of mouth cancer.

    Researchers have for some time suspected that a sexually transmitted infection linked to cervical cancer could also be associated with cancerous tumours in the mouth.

    Now a study by researchers working for the International Agency for Research on Cancer in Lyon, France, appear to have confirmed it.

    'Oral sex can lead to oral tumours,' New Scientist magazine said yesterday, referring to the latest research.

    The scientists studied more than 1,600 mouth-cancer patients from Europe, Canada, Australia, Cuba and the Sudan, and more than 1,700 healthy people.

    They found that patients with oral cancer containing a strain of the human papilloma virus known as HPV16 were three times more likely to report having had oral sex than those without the virus strain.

    The researchers think oral sex performed on men and women could infect people's mouths, the magazine added.

    Dr Raphael Viscidi, a virologist who worked on the research, believes the findings substantiate the link between HPV and oral cancer. 'This is a major study in terms of size,' he said. 'I think this will convince people.'

    High consumptions of alcohol and cigarettes are estimated to cause 75 to 90 per cent of all cases of oral cancer. The combination of tobacco smoke and alcohol are thought to produce high levels of cancer-causing agents. -- Reuters
