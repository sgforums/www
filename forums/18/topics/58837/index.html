    New male treatment to prevent pregnancy

    SYDNEY - Australian scientists have announced a breakthrough in the development of an effective, hormone-based contraceptive for men.

    A team led by Mr David Handelsman of the Anzac Research Institute in Sydney said his team has successfully experimented with the use of two hormones to suppress sperm production.

    Mr Handelsman and Mr Rob McLachlan of Prince Henry's Institute in Melbourne tested the treatment on 55 couples and over the course of a year, no pregnancies occurred.

    The treatment involved inserting testosterone pellets under the skin on the men's abdomens every four months and combining this with quarterly shots of progestin, a hormone used in female contraceptive pills.

    Mr Handelsman said the final product would be a single injection for men on a three- to four-month basis, but he could not predict if or when the contraceptive would be commercialised.

    He added that drug companies had expressed doubts that men would use an injected contraceptive. -- AFP
