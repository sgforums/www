    Don't leave home without identification papers

    DON'T leave home without it - some form of identification, that is. Or you might just be unlucky enough to end up spending the night locked up in the police station.

    This was what happened to my boyfriend on Feb 12. We parted ways shortly before midnight, promising to call each other after we got home. His call never came and when I tried calling his mobile phone, it had been switched off.

    It was only at 8am that I finally got a call from him and learnt that he had been locked up the entire night. His offence? He wasn't carrying any form of identification when approached by three police officers on patrol.

    He was handcuffed, taken to the police station and locked up. His mobile phone was confiscated and requests to call someone to fetch his passport were turned down repeatedly.

    My boyfriend is from China. I asked an officer whether he would have been treated the same way if he had been a Singaporean. I was told that this was the standard procedure.

    TEO ZUI WEN (MS)

    What actually happened ......

    (Straits Times Interactive - 21 Feb 2004)

    Woman's beau was found drunk at interchange
    I REFER to Ms Teo Zui Wen's letter, 'Don't leave home without identification papers' (ST, Feb 1.

    Ms Teo claimed that her boyfriend was stopped by police in the street.

    When he was found without any identification papers, he was taken to the police station and kept there overnight.

    She further alleged that police seized her boyfriend's mobile phone and that he was not allowed to get anyone to take his passport to the station. This is inaccurate.

    On Feb 12 about 12.45am, police were alerted by a member of the public to a drunk man at the Toa Payoh Bus Interchange.

    When our officers arrived, they found Ms Teo's boyfriend lying on the floor. He smelt very strongly of alcohol.

    He was incoherent, unable to stand and care for himself. He was also not carrying any identification papers.

    Our officers then took him back to the Tanglin Police Division HQ to ascertain his identity and allow him to sober up in a safe environment.

    In accordance with our operating procedure, all the personal belongings of the detained man, including his mobile phone, were kept in a locker at the holding bay where he was held.

    In the course of the night, the drunk man vomited and our officers had to help him clean up.

    He became sober only about 7am. He then contacted his friend on his mobile phone. The friend subsequently arrived at the police station with his passport. After verifying his particulars, police released him unconditionally.

    We thank the public-spirited person for alerting police to the incident.

    PHILLIP MAH
    Assistant Director (Media Relations)
    Singapore Police Force

    Miscellaneous Offences (Public Order and Nuisance) Act - Chapter 184

    Section 18:-

    Drunkenness in public places
    18. Any person who is found drunk and incapable of taking care of himself, in any public road or in any public place or place of public amusement or resort, or in the immediate vicinity of any court or of any public office or police station or place of worship, shall be guilty of an offence and shall be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $1,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding one month and, in the case of a second or subsequent conviction, to a fine not exceeding $2,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 3 months.
