    Holy week - extending from Passion Sunday to Easter Sunday is celebrated each year as the greatest week in the Church's of praise. It is much more than a historical re-enactment of past events, more than a passion play or a religious pageant.

    During Holy Week, we celebrate the pascal mystery of Jesus Christ: his dying and rising in obdience to the Father's will. We also celebrate our sharing in this lifegiving mystery through the sacraments of initiation.

    Holy Week encompasses the conclusion of Lenten preparation and introduces the great Easter Triduum. It is a time when we are in tune with centuries of Christians tradition. All the people of God are being called to conversion, to turn back to the Lord Jesus. Holy Week provides a graphic remembering of God's love for us in Jesus, of Jesus' total giving of himself for us in his dying and rising in glory. Holy Week is a combination of history and mystery.

    Holy Thursday
    - Evening Mass of the Lord's Supper

    Tonight's liturgy includes the celebration of the Eucharist with the washing of feet, communion under both forms and a Eucharistic procession. This is followed by the stripping of the altar and a period of personal adoration. At 11.30 pm we begin the Service of Darkness; it is characterized by a series of Scripture readings in stages while lights (candles) are gradually extinguished to symbolize darkness not only of Jesus' death but of hopelessness in the world without God.

    Washing of Feet
    At the supper, Jesus performed the work of a slave in washing the feet of his disciples. He, the master, did this in order to give them a dramatic - even shocking - expression of what he wants of us. Now he is asking us to show our love for one another in practical deeds.

    Renewal of Commitment
    and Bless of Oils

    Holy Thursday is a time when the bishop, assistant priests and laity realise their interdependence in Christ and his call to serve. During the Chrism Mass, which is usually celebrated in the morning, the bishop calls upon his assistant priests to sacrifice their personal ambition for the sake of the people of God. He then asks them to renew their promises to be faithful ministers of the Word and sacraments. The interrogation concludes with the bishop asking the faithful to pray for their priests and for himself. Then the Blessing of oils will take place - the oil of the Catechumens, the oil of the anointing of the sick and the oil of Chrism (which is used for anointing at Confirmation and Ordination of priests). These oils are later distributed to all parishes to be used for the entired year.

    EASTER TRIDUUM
    The Easter Triduum celebrates the paschal mystery of the Lord Jesus, his dying and rising seen as one saving action. The three days from Holy Thursday evening are celebrated as one great feast, the most solemn celebration of the Church's year. The passage of Christ through death to new life is the fulfilment of many death-life passages in the history of the Jewish Scriptures, and is in turn the pattern for our own death-life passage.

    GOOD FRIDAY
    CELEBRATION OF THE LORD'S PASSION

    Part II

    The Lenten penitential thrust continues on Friday and Saturday of Holy Week. On Good Friday, we are invited to continue our Lenten work of baptismal renewal, listening to God's word, praying, and doing penance. In a particular way, we are invited to join in the paschal fast. Some people fast from Thursday evening to Saturday evening. Others fast only on Friday with one full meal either at lunch or in the evening. But everyone is reqired to abstain from meat. One good way is to fast on bread and water only. Whichever way you decide to fast, the right spiriti of fasting is what is important.

    GOOD FRIDAY
    CELEBRATION OF THE LORD'S PASSION

    Part III

    The day's main liturgical celebration has three main parts: Liturgy of the World, the Veneration of the Cross and Holy Communion. The entrance procession takes place in silence and the presider bows and prostrates in silence. This is to emphasize the relfective starkness of this day's liturgy. The Passion according to John will be proclaimed and this will be followed by the solemn intercessions. This form of intercessions was the usual Sunday format in Rome in the third century. It has been retained here as a way of encouraging God's people to pray with Christ for all the needs of the Church and the wolrd. A large cross is then brought to the altar. All are invited to venerate the cross in their own way, simply by bowing, genuflecting or kissing it.
