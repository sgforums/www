    LAWYER ALLEGES:
    SHE GAVE SON STEROIDS

    Defence paints Madam Amutha as a domineering mother
    A PUSHY mother who forced her will upon her children, even giving steroids to her son so he could excel in sports.

    By Crystal Chan

    02 November 2007

    A PUSHY mother who forced her will upon her children, even giving steroids to her son so he could excel in sports.

    This was how the defence portrayed Madam Amutha Valli, 51, as the Novena Church 'exorcism' suit resumed in the High Court yesterday.

    She is suing the church, two of its priests and six church members over what she claims was a forced 'exorcism'.

    Her children, Miss Subashini Jeyabal, 22, and Mr Jairajkumar Jeyabal, 28, depict her as a devoted and supportive mother in their affidavits.

    Madam Amutha, an ex-national walker, also said in her own affidavit that she coached her children for international walking competitions.

    But lawyer Denis Tan, who represents three of the church members, painted her as a domineering and eccentric woman who was estranged from her own family.

    Mr Tan noted that Madam Amutha has seven siblings - three sisters and four brothers - but none was seen to have visited her during her convalescence after the 'exorcism'.

    Mr Tan asked Miss Subashini, testifying for the third day: 'Am I right to say that your mother pushed you and your brother very hard in your career?'

    She agreed, and Mr Tan asked if Mr Jairajkumar was a reluctant sportsman who was forced by Madam Amutha to excel in athletics.

    In March 1998, Mr Jairajkumar announced he would be quitting walking to study, having deferred his A-Levels in 1997 to focus on the SEA Games.

    ABUSIVE

    Miss Subashini said her brother lost interest in the sport after the World Junior Championships that year.

    To emphasise his point that Madam Amutha was a domineering parent, Mr Tan alleged that she pressured her son so much that she gave him steroids.

    Miss Subashini claimed she was unaware of this.

    Mr Anthony Lee, representing the other three church members, also painted Madam Amutha as an abusive woman.

    He asked Miss Subashini why her brother quit during his third year of an engineering course at the National University of Singapore.

    She replied that he was unhappy, but Mr Lee said there was more to it.

    He claimed Mr Jairajkumar's decision was to spite his mother as she disapproved of his then-girlfriend.

    Mr Lee asked: 'Do you know your mother called the girl's parents and abused them over the phone, and your brother was so ashamed of her?'

    Miss Subashini, a student, said she did not know about this incident, and denied Mr Lee's allegation that her mother was a drunkard who took sleeping pills.

    In her usual combative demeanour, she said: 'My mum was only admitted to the Institute of Mental Health for alcoholism for only a day. How does that make her a drunkard?'

    SNAKE-LIKE BEHAVIOUR

    Another point that came up in yesterday's hearing was Madam Amutha's skilfulness in behaving like a snake - something that was frequently mentioned in court earlier.

    Mr Tan claimed that around 1994, Madam Amutha trained some children for an Indian dance performance, and one of the children was a Chinese boy who stayed with her temporarily.

    She had been paid by the boy's parents to care for him while they were overseas.

    He said: 'Am I correct to say that he was dressed up as an Indian boy, with a tilak (Hindu symbol) on his forehead?

    'He was taught to wriggle like a snake and move upwards like a cobra. Am I right?'

    While Miss Subashini said she had not seen such a performance before, Mr Lee said he has witnesses to rebut her denial.

    Mr Lee cross-examined Miss Subashini at the start of yesterday's hearing and said the dance was held at Ang Mo Kio Community Centre.

    Mr Lee said Madam Amutha choreographed the dances and during one rehearsal, she went into a trance and moved like a snake on the floor.

    When Miss Subashini denied ever seeing her mother going into trances, Mr Lee said: 'Your Honour, we wish to apply for rebuttal witnesses. We had contact with potential witnesses who came forward.'

    The cross-examination was one of several dramatic scenes in an eagerly anticipated day, as Madam Amutha was supposed to take the stand yesterday.

    Mr Tan even compared Madam Amutha's allegations with a scene from a movie.

    When Mr Tan's questioning drew objections from Madam Amutha's lawyer, Mr R S Bajwa, Justice Lee Seiu Kin said 'rigorous cross-examination' was necessary as the trial was about finding out the truth.

    Earlier, Mr Tan asked Miss Subashini why she did not call the police when Madam Amutha was supposedly taken into a room in the church's pastoral centre for the 'exorcism'.

    Her reply?

    She did not think anything was amiss as the family was in a place of worship.

    'Now, I think we should have called the police - we would have probably avoided all this,' she said.

    Often during the cross-examination, there was laughter from the public gallery, and Mr Bajwa raised this with Justice Lee, saying the crowd was intimidating Miss Subashini.

    Justice Lee then warned members of the public not to make any noise, adding: 'If necessary, I will clear the gallery.'

    Nov 14, 2007
    Woman visited hospitals, clinic 7 times in 2 days after alleged exorcism
    By Jermyn Chow

    MADAM Amutha Valli went to two hospitals and a private clinic seven times in two days after the alleged forced exorcism on her three years ago, the High Court heard on Wednesday.

    This was revealed by her sworn brother, Mr Resham Singh, during cross-examination by a defence counsel at the resumed hearing of the exorcism suit brought against the Redemptorist Order which runs the Novena Church in Thomson Road, two priests and six churchgoers.

    Madam Valli is seeking compensation for the trauma she suffered from the alleged ritual on Aug 10, 2004.

    Mr Singh, 38, a private equity investor, said since the incident, he had taken the plaintiff to various hospitals and clinics more than 50 times.

    He said the following morning after the alleged forced exorcism, Madam Valli went to the Tan Tock Seng Hospital twice, and also saw her a doctor at Flame Tree Clinic.

    Later that night, she called for an ambulance to send her to the National University Hospital.

    The next day on Aug 12, she went back to Flame Tree Clinic three times between 9 am and 7 pm. Mr Singh agreed with senior counsel Jimmy Yim, who is representing Father Jacob Ong, one of the two priests accused of carrying out the ritual, that Madam Valli was given painkillers and sedatives on these trips to the outpatient clinics.

    Part of a scam
    Counsel then put it to Mr Singh that these visits were an attempt to build up a case against the church and defendents, and were part of a 'scam'.

    When Mr Singh insisted that Madam Valli was unwell, counsel said: 'There's such a thing as lawyer shopping. But she is doctor shopping.'

    Mr Singh shot back: 'There's such a thing as seeking second opinion.'

    Counsel also drew attention to discrepancies in Mr Singh's testimony, and questioned his relationship with Madam Valli and her family.

    He noted that besides staying over at her family's Ang Mo Kio flat two to three times a week, he also had meals with her at coffee shops and regularly accompanied her to temples.

    Mr Singh said he spent time with Madam Valli not as an 'act of kindness but out of a duty to the family'.

    Daughter denies intimacy
    At an earlier hearing before the case was adjourned on Nov 1, Madam Valli's daughter, Miss Subashini Jeyabal, 22, a student, had denied that her mother was intimate with Mr Singh.

    But a private investigator had reported that Madam Valli was emotionally attached to Mr Singh and that the two had even travelled together.

    According to Miss Subashini, Mr Singh is her 'father's good friend's son' and he began staying at the family's Ang Mo Kio flat only early this year.

    No sign of Mdm Valli
    Madam Valli did not show up in court on Wednesday when the hearing resumed after a two-week break. Her lawyer, Mr R.S. Bajwa told the court that she has been warded at Changi General Hospital and was 'certified unfit' to attend court until Nov 20.

    The hearing was adjourned on Nov 1 after Madam Valli repeatedly said she could not recall her family history and past medical records when cross-examined by defence lawyers.

    It is not known when or whether Madam Valli will be back in court for the hearing, which is expected to stretch until next year.

    Hearing continues on Thursday.

    Nov 15, 2007
    Woman's 'brother' in exorcism case: I wasn't negligent in caring for her
    By Jermyn Chow
    A DEFENCE lawyer in the continuing exorcism trial has acused Mr Resham Singh, the sworn 'brother' of plaintiff Madam Amutha Valli, of being negligent in caring for her - because he allegedly stood by while doctors prescribed her a whole bulk of sleeping pills and sedatives, the High Court heard on Thursday.

    Madam Amutha Vali is suing the Redemptorist Order which runs the Novena Church in Thomson Road, two priests and six churchgoers to seek compensation for the trauma she suffered from the alleged exorcism ritual on Aug 10, 2004.

    On Thursday, Mr Resham Singh returned to the witness box and was cross-examined by defence counsel Tito Isaac.

    Mr Isaac told the court that even while Madam Valli was hospitalised for a week in Nov 2004 at Tan Tock Seng Hospital (TTSH) for overdosing on Panadol, medical records showed she still visited private clinics repeatedly - at one period every other day - and got a bulk of sleeping pills and sedatives.

    Mr Isaac then said: 'We see a pattern here... Someone has been either negligent in her care or doesn't want her to get better. But that someone seems to like her to stay hooked on drugs.'

    He went on to argue that Mr Singh, who was supposed to be her constant companion, did not see fit to tell Madam Valli's doctors at the private clinics and at TTSH that she had been prescribed a large amount of drugs.

    Mr Singh replied that 'she is old enough to talk to the doctors and take of herself'. He added that he was there only to accompany her.

    At this point in the cross-examination, Madam Valli's daughter, Ms Subashini Jeyabal, 22, was seen shaking her head.

    Robust exchange
    The morning's preceedings were also dominated by a robust exchange between defence counsel Mr Isaac and Justice Lee Sieu Kin.

    This arose after Madam Vali's lawyer, Mr RS Bajwa, objected to Mr Isaac's line of questioning on Mr Singh's involvement in Madam Valli's visit to TTSH and private clinics, saying 'he was casting fear' over the witness.

    Justice Lee rebuffed some of Mr Bajwa's objections, telling him:' You have been interrupting a fair bit...It's a legitimate cross-examining technique... to verify the veracity of the evidence.'

    He then added that 'if he (Mr Singh) is telling the truth, then he shouldn't worry' and assured Mr Bajwa that he too will be extended the same latitude when he cross-examines the witnesses.

    Defence counsel then proceeded to ask for Mr Singh's and Madam Valli's passports.

    Mr Singh declined, saying he felt uncomfortable giving the passports to Mr Isaac, but would not object to giving them to Madam Valli's lawyer, Mr Bajwa.

    Mr Bajwa then objected to the relevance of handing over the passports, to which Justice Lee Sieu Kin replied 'Why not?'

    Mr Isaac explained that they had wanted to verify the dates of the overseas trips taken by both Mr Singh and Madam Valli.

    Mr Singh, from the outset, has denied being the primary caregiver of Madam Valli, although defence lawyers have established that he had always been her constant companion during her medical appointments and hospitalisations.
