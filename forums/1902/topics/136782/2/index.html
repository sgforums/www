    Volcano Barnacle (Tetraclita sp.)

    Barnacles are crustacea and not molluscs. In their larval stage, they look like tiny shrimps.When a larvae finds a good spot to settle down, it releases cement from the tips of its first pair of antennaes and glues itself head down on the surface. It then secretes a thick shell made up of a series of plates. Inside the shell is a segmented body and six pairs of segmented legs modified into feathery appendages. At high tide, the animal waves its legs outside the shell to filter feed and breathe, as the legs also contain the gills.

    Mussels

    Mussels attach themselves to hard surfaces with byssus threads.
    During low tide, they clamp up tight to prevent water loss.
    When the tide returns, they open up to filter the water for titbits.

    Drills (Family Muricidae)

    These innocent looking snails have sinister habits. They are able to drill a hole through the shells of barnacles and bivalves by secreting an acid. They then use their rough ribbon-like tongues to 'drill' through. These Drills are laying eggs on a rock.
