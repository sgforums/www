    Looking East in Early Evening



    Today our planet Earth sweeps between the sun and the red planet Mars. We go between the sun and Mars about every two years. Today's chart shows where to find Mars at sunset tonight in the eastern sky. For all of us, around the globe, it rises within about half an hour of sunset this evening. Why does Mars rise as the sun sets? Think of Earth and Mars in orbit around the sun. Earth is going between Mars and the sun, so the three bodies in space (the sun, us and Mars) are in a line, with Earth in the middle. We're on Earth so we look out into space in one direction to see the sun, and the other direction to see Mars. Thus Mars rises at sunset, and sets at sunrise. You'll see the red planet all night tonight, shining like a brilliant red beacon.

    Saturn goes "retro" tomorrow

    Tuesday, November 22, 2005

    Looking Southwest One - Two Hours Before Dawn



    Today's chart is set for tomorrow morning at about the same time and place as yesterday's chart. The planet Saturn is now about 4 degrees to the lower right of the moon, and the lovely sky pattern actually looks even more like a "Y" than this morning's appearance did. Saturn, like all the planets in our solar system, can be found along a specific pathway in our skies known as the "ecliptic" (marked in pink on today's chart.) This pathway is also the tract that the sun and moon take as they rise and set in our skies. The normal movement for planets in our sky is eastward along this line. Starting tomorrow, however, Saturn will appear to move westward on this line -- an apparent backward motion. This westward movement of planets in relation to other objects in our skies is known as a "retrograde motion." Of course, Saturn hasn't really changed the direction of the orbit. The cause of this temporary planetary backward apparent motion became apparent when the geocentric model of the universe was scrapped in favor of the heliocentric model.

    There are 88 "official" constellations, whose boundaries were drawn by the International Astronomical Union in the first part of the 20th century.     Dark side of moon

    Saturday, November 26, 2005

    Looking Southeast One Hour Before Sunrise



    Here is tonight's waning crescent moon. It's now underneath the constellation Leo the Lion. Many people write to ask about the fact that, from Earth, we see only one side of the moon. The moon rotates about once a month, in contrast to the once-a-day rotation of our own planet Earth. It's the moon's monthly rotation that keeps us from seeing the back side of the moon. It spins once in the same amount of time it takes to orbit Earth, which is why we don't see the moon's back side. And it's no coincidence -- over the long history of our solar system, Earth's stronger gravity locked onto the moon and forced it into the one-sided relationship we see today. Other moons in the solar system share this property. In fact, nearly all the known moons of planets in our solar system are gravitationally locked with respect to their planets. Nearly all of them keep a single face toward the planet around which they orbit.
