Throughout history, and in the lore of many nations, the Pleiades have had their own special mystery. This beautiful sky object has been compared to a swarm of fireflies or bees, to seven maidens, to a flock of doves, and to a hen surrounded by her chicks.

    Where stars are born

    Sunday, February 26, 2006

    Looking South-Southwest In Early Evening



    You can see a vast cloud made of gas and dust, floating in the space of our own galaxy. This object is seen by the eye as a fuzzy spot(s) below Orion's Belt, visible in the south in the evening at this time of year. This is the Great Nebula in Orion the Hunter, also called simply the Orion Nebula. Since it can be seen with the eye alone, the Orion Nebula has been known since antiquity. But we, with our modern telescopes and cameras, have the ability to look closer. Our ancestors didn't know, for example, that this fuzzy object is actually a birthplace of new stars. The lighted region is illuminated by hot young stars, newly born, visible only with telescopes. A generally accepted value for the distance to the Orion Nebula is between 1600 and 1900 light-years. At that distance from Earth, the Orion Nebula itself -- the lighted portion we can see -- spans about 30 light-years, or more than 20,000 times the diameter of our solar system. Beyond the lighted part of the nebula, there is a vast complex of clouds in space, mapped by astronomers since the 1970s using infrared and radio telescopes.

    Uranus reaches conjunction today

    Wednesday, March 1, 2006

    Visualization Only -- Looking West-Southwest Shortly Before Sunset



    Today the planet Uranus cannot be seen in the night sky. Of course, Uranus is never visible in the same sense that Jupiter and Saturn are, anyway. ItÂ’s never easy to see with the eye alone. It reaches what is called its "conjunction." That is when a planet, in this case a superior planet (a planet that orbits the sun outside of Earth's orbit -- such as Mars, Jupiter, and Pluto), is located on the opposite side of the sun as viewed from Earth. (On today's chart, we marked Uranus in black just to show its position behind the sun.) It is therefore impossible to see such a conjunction because the sun blocks our view of the planet. Even an inferior conjunction, or one where an inferior planet (Mercury or Venus) passes in front of the sun -- is rarely possible to see (the June 2004 transit of Venus being a notable exception.) Consequently, when Earth reaches inferior conjunction with respect to the view on a superior planet, that superior planet is said to be "in opposition" with respect to our planet. Most recent examples include Saturn's opposition on January 27th and Mars' opposition on November 7th of last year.

    Why Sirius appears to move about Orion

    Thursday, March 2, 2006

    Looking South In Early Evening



    Â“A couple nights ago, Sirius was on the upper left of Orion...last night, it was around the belt, and tonight it is below the foot. Please explain its' cycle.Â” Sirius, in fact, never moves in relationship to the stars of Orion. The stars are so far away that we can't detect their movement in space over the scale of a human life, or even many lifetimes. So, to us on Earth, the stars appear "fixed" relative to each other. What does change is our orientation under the stars, as Earth spins. So we can definitely imagine that you saw Sirius "move" in relationship to Orion, in the sense that the whole sky moves as Earth spins beneath it. Earth's spin causes the stars and constellations to appear oriented differently with respect to the horizon from hour to hour within a single night, or as the seasons pass. But Sirius would never move with respect to Orion's hand or foot because the stars are "fixed." One thing to notice about Orion is that there are two bright stars on either side of the Belt -- reddish Betelgeuse and bluish Rigel. Try looking carefully at these stars, as they'll enable you to notice Orion's orientation with respect to the horizon.
