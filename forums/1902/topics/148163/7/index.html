    "Hands" of Big Dipper, Queen

    Monday, January 9, 2006

    Looking North-Northeast in Late Evening



    The Big Dipper and the constellation Cassiopeia the Queen are like the hands of a clocks, wheeling around eternally in the northern sky. The Big Dipper is an "asterism" . . . or noticeable pattern of stars . . .within the constellation Ursa Major the Greater Bear. The Big Dipper can be used to find Polaris, the North Star. Simply look in the sky, and draw an imaginary line between the two outermost stars in the Dipper's Bowl. This line points to Polaris. Keep that line going . . . and you will come to another famous constellation Cassiopeia. The Queen is very recognizable for having the shape of the letter "M" or "W," depending on what time of year and what time of night you see it. At this time of year, the Big Dipper is in the northeast in late evening, and Cassiopeia is in the northwest. These two constellations are "circumpolar" -- as seen from northerly latitudes, they never set. They circle endlessly around the North Star, are always opposite each other. In other words, when the Dipper is at about 4 o'clock -- as it appears on today's chart -- then Cassiopeia must be at about 10 o'clock -- using the North Star as the clock's middle.

    How to tell a planet from a star

    Thursday, January 26, 2006

    Looking East-Southeast in Early Evening



    Today's chart shows a view of the east-southeast in early evening, where the famous constellation Orion the Hunter resides with a smattering of bright stars and the planets Saturn and Mars. We get lots of questions about how to tell the difference between stars and planets. We might take a few tips from our ancient astronomers. When they looked into the skies, they noticed that most bright objects in the sky travel in fixed positions with relation to each other, while a few bright objects didn't seem to follow this pattern. They took the "fixed objects" and grouped them into constellations, whose movements were so regular and reliable that these groupings helped them find their way on the ocean, on land, and even helped them predict annual events like floods and harvests. If you were to look at the sky night after night, you'd notice the same thing. Stars will appear in fixed positions; think about how constellations retain their familiar shapes but still move across our sky. Planets, on the other hand, move in much more complex paths with respect to stars -- sometimes even appearing to move backwards in our sky, in an event known as retrograde. So, it'll take a little practice -- and some patience -- but if you can track the movement of objects in the sky for several nights in a row, you will be able to tell which are stars and planets by the movements that they make relative to each other in the sky. Another way of telling planets apart from stars is that stars appear to twinkle in the sky, and planets generally do not.
