    Some meteor showers have been watched each year at the same time for many centuries. But the Ursid meteor shower, which peaks tonight, has been observed for only a single century. It was first observed around the turn of the 20th century, when a skywatcher noticed that some meteors seen around this time of year werenÂ’t random in their direction of motion across our skyÂ’s dome . . . but instead appeared to radiate from near the star Kochab in the bowl of the Little Dipper asterism. But typically the shower is much sparser than that. It might produce only five to 10 meteors per hour at its peak.     Hunter in close view

    Friday, December 23, 2005

    Looking East-Southeast at Nightfall



    This month, we have focused a lot of our charts on the constellation Orion the Hunter, now in the east-southeast in early evening. And what a rich constellation it is! Besides the obvious placement of the stars on Orion's Belt -- Mintaka, Alnilam, and Alnitak -- the Hunter is comprised of a number of very bright stars. One star whose name we particularly love is Bellatrix. According to the classic book by Richard Hinckley Allen called Star Names: Their Lore and Meaning, the name Bellatrix means Female Warrior or Amazon Star. Rigel, which represents the left foot of the Hunter, is a blue-white supergiant and one of the most luminous stars known. Meanwhile, Betelgeuse represents the Hunter's right shoulder. It's a red supergiant whose outer layers extend very far from the star. In fact, if Betelgeuse replaced the sun in our solar system, its outer layers would extend beyond the orbit of Jupiter! Also look for the star Saiph which, like most of Orion's stars, is located very distantly, at approximately 720 light-years away.

    Moon occults Spica before dawn for some

    Sunday, December 25, 2005

    Looking South-Southeast Before Dawn



    The star near the moon tonight is Spica, the brightest light in the large, rambling constellation Virgo the Maiden. Since August, the moon has occulted (or passed in front of) Spica from someplace on Earth about every 28 days -- and will continue to do so until the year 2007. Tomorrow morning marks the first visible event for parts of the U.S. this year. In the wee hours of the morning, the waning crescent moon can be seen rising in the east-southeastern horizon, with the star Spica located less than a degree to the bottom of it. The moon and Spica will travel in each otherÂ’s company until a couple of hours before dawn, when the moon will actually occult, or pass in front of the star Spica in some locales. Although most of North America is in a prime location to see this, it will occur after sunrise for most of us -- making it a difficult occultation to view. It will happen before sunrise for those in eastern Alaska, western Canada, and parts of southern and western U.S.

    Second new moon this month

    Friday, December 30, 2005

    Looking Southwest Before Sunset



    Don't expect to look westward this evening and see this ghostly moon near the sun. The image shows up fairly well on a computer screen -- but it won't show up at all in the real sky -- and you should never look toward the sun without a protective filter. Today's chart is purely an illustration of the fact that the moon is new today. That means it's passing between the Earth and sun as these bodies move in space. Thus, although the moon is near the sun in the sky today, you won't see the moon anywhere in today's sky. There are two reasons why. First, the moon's lighted face -- its "day side" -- is turned entirely away from us now. Also, the moon is hidden in the sun's glare. As the moon moves eastward in orbit around Earth, it'll edge slowly away from the sun on our sky's dome. Today's new moon marks the second for this month (the first being on December 1st). Thus, this month has completed a full lunar cycle from start to finish.

    Stars in other galaxies

    Saturday, December 31, 2005

    Looking West-Northwest in Late Evening



    "Are there any stars outside our own galaxy that we can see with just the eye?"

    The answer is no -- unless you count seeing the combined light of many billions of stars. From the northern hemisphere, the only galaxy outside our Milky Way that's easily visible to the eye is the Great Galaxy in the constellation Andromeda. In January, this galaxy is in the west-northwest each evening. You need a very dark country sky, on a night like tonight when there's no moon, to be able to look up and notice the galaxy without consulting a chart. It'll appear as a hazy patch in the night sky, as wide in diameter as a full moon. And, indeed, this haze represents the light of the Andromeda galaxy's billions of stars. But we still can't see individual stars within this galaxy -- not with the eye alone. Even with amateur telescopes, the patch of light that we see as the Andromeda galaxy looks, at best, like haze.

    Finding the North Star

    Wednesday, January 4, 2006

    Looking North-Northeast in Late Evening



    There is a neat trick to finding the North Star, also known as Polaris, in the night sky. That is, your latitude gives you its height above the northern horizon. In other words, if your latitude is 30 degrees, then the pole star is 30 degrees above the northern horizon. From a latitude of 40 degrees N., it is 40 degrees up -- and so on. Polaris can be seen at any time of night. But, at this time of year, you might want to wait until around midnight to look for it -- so you can use the Big Dipper as a guide. The two outer stars in the bowl of the Big Dipper point to Polaris.

    Latest sunrises this week

    Thursday, January 5, 2006

    Looking East-Southeast at Sunrise



    Today's chart shows the eastern sky shortly before sunrise . . . the last object to fade from view in the east before dawn is the planet Mercury. The sun rises very late in early January. The exact date of latest sunrise varies with latitude, but the sequence never varies: the earliest sunsets come in early December, the shortest day comes on the winter solstice around December 21, and the latest sunrises come in early January. For 40 degrees N. latitude (a line that runs approximately through the vertical middle of the U.S.) , January 5 is the date of latest sunrise. The reason it happens is complicated. Here's how sky guru Guy Ottewell explains it: "The reason why earliest/latest sunrise/set do not coincide exactly with the solstices is the slight eccentricity of the Earth's orbit. While rotating at essentially constant speed, Earth is traveling faster around its orbit at some times (Jan.) than at others (July), thus changing its angle with respect to the sun at different rates."
