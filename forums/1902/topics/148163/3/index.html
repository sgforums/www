    Looking East One Hour Before Dawn



    Here's a view of the waning crescent moon in early morning -- near the constellation Leo the Lion.

    The brightest star in Leo is Regulus, a blue-white star located some 85 light-years away. Regulus is located at the bottom tip of a noticeable pattern within Leo -- an "asterism" called The Sickle -- which appears on our sky's dome as a backwards question mark. In ancient skylore, this Sickle pattern represents the head and shoulders of Leo the Lion.

    There is another bright star in The Sickle. It's Algieba, located about 110 light-years from Earth, known to stargazers with telescopes as a double star system. Both stars in the Algieba system are yellow giants. The distance between the two stars is about four light-hours, about the same distance as that between our sun and its planet Neptune.

    Neptune orbits our sun in 165 years, however, while the two mighty suns in the Algieba system move through space more slowly. Estimates regarding the length of their mutual orbit vary from around 400 to around 600 years.

    Also in the predawn sky, higher up in the horizon is the farthest of the five easily visible planets to the unaided eye, the planet Saturn. The other four are Mercury, Venus, Mars, and Jupiter.

    I say "easily visible" because people who can see large stars in the sky can easily view them. The planet Uranus is theoretically visible to the naked eye...but it's never easy to see with the eye alone. ItÂ’s certainly possible to see Uranus with the eye, but you need a very dark sky to do it.

    Uranus reached its opposition at the beginning of this month, which makes right now a good time to try to see it. You can find Uranus, which is currently located in the constellation Aquarius, up in the southeast shortly after sunset.

    Looking Northeast After Sunset



    Cassiopeia the Queen can now be found high in the northeastern sky after sunset. This constellation has the distinct shape of a W, or M, depending on your perspective.

    The stars of the queen are quite luminous -- its stars Schedar, Caph, and Gamma Cas all have approximately mid-second magnitudes. The star Ruchbach is close in brightness to them, with a magnitude of about 2.68. Segin trails the other stars on the famous M (or W) constellation shape in brightness with a third magnitude rating.

    However, there is an intriguing star, often overlooked by skywatchers as it is not a part of the traditional letter-shaped part of the Queen. It is a fourth magnitude star, located slightly off the M (or W) between the alpha star Schedar and Gamma Cas. It's name is Eta Cas, and it is particularly of interest to astronomers because of its similarity to our sun. Eta Cas is similar in color, size, and temperature to our own sun. In fact, if we could somehow travel to Eta Cas (which is a relatively scant 19.4 light years away) and catch sight of our own sun, it would look very much like how Eta Cas looks in our skies now.

    Looking Southwest After Sunset



    Today's chart is aimed southwest. The time is in early evening. At this time of night, the brightest objects in this part of the sky are the planet Venus and the star Antares in the constellation Scorpius the Scorpion. Tonight Antares appears very close to a thickening waxing crescent moon. Yesterday morning (or yesterday afternoon for those in the U.S.), Antares appeared so close to the moon, that in some locations across the globe (central Pacific Ocean, West Samoa, and the western tip of northern South America) the moon appeared to occult, or pass in front of the reddish star Antares. This morning occultation is pretty difficult to see, and perhaps should be left to seasoned astronomers. Those of us in all the other areas of the world, take heart -- Antares is occulted by the moon approximately once every 27 days throughout the rest of this year and on into the next. The best chance for those of us in North America to witness the moon occulting Antares will come up around the end of December this year.

    Looking Overhead in the Northwest at Nightfall



    We get many questions from people in northern latitudes about if and when they can view the Southern Cross asterism in their portion of the sky. The truth is that unless you live close to the equator (Hawaii, or the southernmost parts of Texas or Florida for those of us in the U.S.), you will not be able to view this famous asterism, also known as the constellation Crux. We in the northern half of the globe have its less renowned, but elegantly beautiful cousin rising high in our skies tonight. The star Deneb on today's chart identifies the top of the astronomical grouping called the Northern Cross. Unlike its southern counterpart, the Northern Cross asterism is a clipped version of another constellation Cygnus the Swan, which you can find high in the northwest at nightfall.

    Looking East-Northeast in Early Evening



    Today, the moon has moved eastward in its path across the sky, as it always does. It's now less than one degree from the famous Pleiades star cluster. Wait a day or two as the moon moves on from the sky, and you can easily recognize this cluster as a tiny dipper shape. In fact, a lot of skywatchers comment on the fact that the Pleiades cluster looks more like a dipper than the Big Dipper. Most people see just six stars here, and, because this cluster is sometimes called The Seven Sisters, there's a persistent question in skylore about the whereabouts of the so-called Lost Pleiad. People with superior vision have been known to see more stars in the Pleiades with the naked eye -- up to 16 stars have been reported in recent centuries. Modern photographs show thousands of stars in this vicinity, but only about 250 of these are thought to be true, gravitationally bound members of the Pleiades star cluster.

    Looking West-Southwest One Hour Before Dawn



    The constellations are made of stars in various patterns, and so, to identify them, you'll want to look for noticeable patterns on the sky's dome. An easy one to find now is the constellation Orion the Hunter. You'll find it due to its very noticeable pattern of three medium-bright stars in a short straight row. These stars represent Orion's Belt. The Hunter is in the west-southwest before dawn at this time of year. You can use the famous Â“BeltÂ” stars -- Alnitak, Alnilam and Mintaka -- as a pointer to other bright stars. If you draw an imaginary line through Orion's Belt, you'll see that it points in one direction to the reddish star Aldebaran, which lies in a V-shaped pattern of stars representing the Face of the Bull in the constellation Taurus. The same line drawn through Orion's Belt points in the other direction to Sirius, the sky's brightest star, in the constellation Canis Major. And that's how you come to know the constellations -- by using what you've already learned to "build" outward to find new patterns.

    Looking East After Sunset



    Yesterday, Earth caught up to Mars in its shorter, faster orbit around the sun. Because we orbit the sun one step inward, our planet Earth moves faster than Mars in orbit. We move at about 18 miles per second, in contrast to 15 miles per second for Mars. Thus, every two years, Earth catches up to Mars in orbit and passes between the red planet and the sun. Earth will pass between the sun and Mars on November 7th this year. This event is called an "opposition" of Mars. At opposition, Mars rises at sunset and appears in our sky all night, at its closest and brightest for that two-year cycle.     Looking East-Northeast in Early Evening



    About an hour after sunset, if you're looking in a dark-enough sky, you can spot the tiny constellation Aries the Ram in the east-northeast in early evening. Maybe you can spot it in relationship to the W-shaped constellation Cassiopeia the Queen, and the planet Mars, and the tiny misty dipper-shaped Pleiades star cluster. Aries isn't conspicuous. And yet, in newspaper astrology columns, Aries often comes first in lists of constellations of the Zodiac. These are the constellations that provide a backdrop for the sun, moon and planets in the course of every year. It's traditional to place Aries first on this list because it marked the sun's location at the time of the vernal equinox between about 1800 B.C. and 1 A.D. This point in our sky retains this name, even though the actual location of the sun at the time of the vernal equinox has shifted into what astronomers now recognize as the constellation Pisces. It's moving, due to the Earth's motion of "precession." This point in our sky -- where the sun is at the vernal equinox -- is still moving.
