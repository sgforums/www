Most stars that make patterns on our sky's dome aren't really related to each other, but the Pleiades stars are related. They were born from a single cloud of gas and dust in space and still move together through the galaxy.

    Winter vs. summer sky

    Tuesday, December 20, 2005

    Looking East-southeast in Late Evening



    "Why do stars seem so much brighter in winter than in summer?"

    In winter, as in summer, we can see edgewise into the disk of our Milky Way galaxy (marked in translucent blue on today's chart). However, in contrast to late summer (when we face the galaxy's center), on winter evenings we're looking toward the outskirts of the galaxy. The winter stars are so bright because many of them -- for example, Betelgeuse, Rigel, Mintaka, Alnitak, Alnilam -- are really giants in the spiral arm of the Milky Way galaxy in which our sun resides. Another difference between winter and summer skies is that there's a "hazy" quality to the summer sky due to the center of the galaxy being farther away than the nearest outer edge. The "haze" is really the combined light of billions of stars in the direction toward the Milky Way's core. The winter sky, in contrast, looks darker and sharper. Winter stars shine with a diamond clarity because on winter evenings there are fewer stars (and less gas and dust) between us and the vast space beyond.
