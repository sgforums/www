In the past century, skywatchers gazing outward from Earth have seen these two stars draw slowly but inexorably farther apart, as they move in their vast mutual orbit. It's believed their maximum separation will come about another century from now.

    Tuesday, March 14, 2006

    Looking East at Moonrise



    When you see the full moon rise this evening, it'll be in the midst of a brush with Earth's shadow. This is a penumbral eclipse, which happens when Earth's shadow sweeps across the moon's face. In this sort of eclipse, however, it's not the dark inner part of Earth's shadow, called the "umbra," that encounters the moon. Instead, the lighter, outer part of Earth's shadow brushes the moon's face tonight. This lunar eclipse -- visible but in progress at moonrise for most of the U.S. -- will not be seen at all by the westernmost parts of North America, such as British Colombia.
