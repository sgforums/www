    Flashing star in the east

    Wednesday, April 19, 2006

    Looking East in Early Evening



    We recently got this question: "We see a star...in the eastern sky around 8-9 PM which flashes different colors. What is this star or planet?" We think you may be referring to the bright star Arcturus in the constellation Bootes, which can now be found shining bright and strong low in the eastern sky around the time that you mentioned. When people see this orange star (a giant star with an estimated distance of 37 light-years) low in the sky it often appears as a multicolored shining star in that area of the sky. Its colorful appearance when seen low in the sky isn't unusual. Any bright star can appear to flash different colors when near the horizon -- because then you're looking at the star through a greater thickness of Earth's atmosphere than when the same star is overhead. Also, you sometimes glimpse a bright star through polluted air, or even just haze or clouds low in the sky. These factors, too, can distort the light of stars and planets seen near the horizon. Arcturus is special because it's not moving with the general stream of stars, in the flat disk of the Milky Way galaxy. Instead, Arcturus is cutting perpendicularly through the galaxy's disk at a tremendous rate of speed . . . some 150 kilometers per second.

    Find North Star; advice on northern lights

    Monday, April 24, 2006



    Today's chart faces north. From 40 degrees N. latitude, the North Star (known as Polaris) lies 40 degrees above the northern horizon. Likewise, from 30 degrees N. it lies 30 degrees up in the north. From 50 degrees N. it lies 50 degrees up, and so on. From any latitude, at any time of night, the two outer stars in the bowl of the Big Dipper in Ursa Major point to the North Star. Many readers have asked, "When is the next possibility of viewing the northern lights?" The answer is that no one knows precisely when there will be a spectacular display of the aurora, or northern lights. The northern lights happen when there are storms on the sun, which send charged particles our way. The charged particles take a couple of days to arrive. So there is some advance warning, and observatories around the world do monitor the sun for such events. The wrinkle is that our sun has an 11-year cycle of activity, and the peak was around the year 2000. So, although solar storms can and will occur in the coming years, they won't occur as frequently as in the past several years.

    Identify Hercules, "Keystone" pattern

    Thursday, April 27, 2006

    Looking East-Northeast in Mid-Evening



    Hercules the Kneeling Giant can be seen ascending in the east-northeast by around 9 p.m. Notice that Hercules lies on a line between two bright stars -- Arcturus in the constellation Bootes the Herdsman and Vega in the constellation Lyra the Harp. These two markers in the sky can help you identify Hercules, whose most noticeable pattern is a square-ish figure in the center of the constellation. This sky pattern, or "asterism," is known as the Keystone in Hercules-- which is a helpful pattern for more reasons than one. First, it's noticeable on the sky's dome, so can lead your eye to Hercules. Also, it can help you find the most fascinating telescopic object within the boundaries of this constellation. This object is a "globular" star cluster. It's an inhabitant of our Milky Way galaxy, but -- unlike our sun -- it lies outside the galactic plane. Known to stargazers as M13 or the Great Cluster in Hercules, it's barely visible the eye alone in the darkest of skies. Binoculars show it as a nebulous patch. And telescopes show stars both on the periphery of the cluster and toward its center. This beautiful object is one of the galaxy's oldest inhabitants -- a tightly packed spherical collection of some one million stars.

    Spotting the Summer Triangle

    Wednesday, May 17, 2006

    Looking East-Northeast in Late Evening



    In the east-northeast in late evening, you'll find a famous asterism, or noticeable pattern of stars, known as the Summer Triangle. The Triangle consists of three bright stars in three different constellations . . .Vega in the constellation Lyra the Harp, Deneb in the constellation Cygnus the Swan, and Altair in the constellation Aquila the Eagle. It's called the "summer" triangle, because, for us in the Northern Hemisphere, summer is the season in which these stars are most prominent. Every summer, this Triangle of stars soars overhead. Tonight, Vega, the brightest of the three, rises first in the east-northeastern horizon around sunset, and so will be easy to spot first. Second to cross the horizon will be the star Deneb about an hour later. The complete triangle will be up after about 10 PM, when the star Altair will cross the horizon.
