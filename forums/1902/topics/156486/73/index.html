    Getting food to quake survivors



    A full meal

    Little Gulrukh looks happy. She knows her parents have been worried about the depleting larder.

    But now the family can sit down to a meal of chapattis, lentils and maybe some rice pudding.

    What she does not know is how her poor family - with its house destroyed in the South Asian earthquake - suddenly got so much food.

    Text and photos: Aamer Ahmed Khan
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:40


    Multi-national effort

    Gulrukh lives in the village of Kanur in the Neelum valley which was cut off for nearly a month after the quake.

    The multi-national effort that finally got food to her started at 0730 at the Chaklala airbase near Rawalpindi.

    The American vehicles there have started to look like Pakistani trucks that bring in rations from local markets.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:43


    Race against time

    Loading the Chinook helicopters is a messy business - a task entrusted to the Northern Light Infantry. This paramilitary outfit is part of the brigade that fought Indian troops in the Kargil conflict in Kashmir in 1999.

    Now they racing against time to save the lives of those stuck in the remote Himalayan mountains.

    On a typically busy day, these men will load over 120 tonnes of rations for repeated sorties by US and British Chinooks.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:44


    'Sling operation'

    Barely two weeks ago, the food situation in the Neelum valley was so desperate that the Royal Air Force tied up with the World Food Programme to start a six-day "sling operation".

    The idea was to pump in 1,000 tonnes of dry rations into the valley.

    The operation's success went a long way in averting what the UN had described as a possible "second wave" of deaths.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:44


    Food supply chain

    Unloading the choppers at any one of the 14 dumping sites set up by the army in Neelum Valley is quite a sight. Soldiers form a chain with sacks of flour flung from one person to the next.

    The idea is to empty the chopper quickly so that it can carry out the maximum number of sorties. The soldiers usually hate this job and the officer in charge often has to act as the chief whip.

    But sometimes, they find inspiration from unexpected quarters.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:45


    Macho game

    At the military base in the village of Dhanni, personnel from the Australian military hospital have turned the tedious task of unloading the supplies into a game. Men and women rush in whenever a Chinook lands and try to outdo each other in unloading the most. The competition is fierce. The Pakistani soldiers do not want to be left behind.

    Sometimes, the chain gets broken as the more macho among them run the 100 metres from the chopper to the dumping site carrying 40kg at times.

    30 Nov 05, 20:46


    Distribution process

    Meanwhile, people from the village that will get rations that day are summoned to the distribution point.

    Men, women and children queue up - 10 at a time - all carrying little pieces of paper given to them by the committee set up in every village to streamline the distribution process.

    Those who do not have able-bodied men in their family are given extra rations on the assumption that their 'carrier' will charge in kind.

    30 Nov 05, 20:46


    A month's ration

    On this day it is Gulrukh's village's turn to receive food. Everyone is walking away with at least one month's rations.

    Only two weeks ago, food was so scarce here that these people would have counted themselves lucky if they got two days ration at a time.

    They are so relieved at the amount they are getting that even the three-hour trek back to the village - involving some very high slopes - seems bearable.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:47


    Three-hour trek

    The gruelling trek begins - made more difficult by the 30kg weight that everyone is carrying.

    The journey involves many pit stops but the break is never longer that 10 minutes.

    Everyone is in a hurry to get back home and to show their rations.
  • poon cho tang
    30 Nov 05, 20:47


    Clearing roads

    With their food problems sorted for a month, villagers can now start clearing the roads of quake debris.

    The army is concentrating mostly on keeping the main road from the capital of Pakistan-administered Kashmir, Muzaffarabad, open.

    Once the link roads open, local transport and private enterprise will take over.

    In the meantime, Gulrukh can be sure of enjoying her simple meal of rice and lentils.
