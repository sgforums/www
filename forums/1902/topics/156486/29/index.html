    The rainbow house



    Fiery past

    Meet Mohammed Akram, once a fighter with the militant outfit Hizbul Mujahideen. He came to Pakistan-administered Kashmir in 1995 as a fiery 21-year-old to fight Indian rule in Kashmir.

    But life was not what he had expected. "People from across the Line of Control were not really trusted."

    He was tempted to quit many times but there were no jobs. The Hizb would pay them $55 a month.

    Weaned away

    A distant cousin, Kulsoom, weaned Akram from the Hizb in 2001. "It took me a while. There was so much fire and anger in him." They married in 2003 and their son Omar was born the next year. Akram looked for a job but with no luck.

    "It was only after my son was born that I realised that I was good for nothing. I had no skill, no education and no contacts. I just wasn't fit for normal life," he says.

    3 Nov 05, 20:11


    Lucky outing

    When the quake struck two years later, Akram was out with his family, which is probably why they are still alive. Their entire neighbourhood was reduced to rubble.

    This was all the couple could salvage from the ruins of their home. To his shock, he saw his outsider status count against him. "Even my neighbours were telling the relief workers who visited the camp that they were locals and had first right to relief goods. We were second priority."
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:12


    Getting ready

    For three weeks, Akram tried to secure a tent for his family. He was desperate. "I walked into Neelum stadium [a medical relief helicopter base] two days ago and walked away with whatever plastic sheets I could lay my hands on," he says.

    Another trip to his ruined house produced some bamboo sticks, iron rods and pieces of wood. A pair of pliers was taken from a wrecked car. Akram was now ready to build his own shelter.

    3 Nov 05, 20:12


    'Useless clothing'

    Work starts at sunrise. Four hours later, a bare structure is in place. None of the wooden pieces he has collected are long enough. They have to be tied with rope he makes from tearing strips out of the useless "aid clothing" which now litters Muzaffarabad.

    Kulsoom keeps needling him throughout. "He is a bit of a perfectionist. For all we know, he may pull down the entire thing tomorrow and start again."
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:14


    Sore thumb

    The hardest part for Akram is handling the materials. Bamboo is one of the toughest things for DIY. And most of the nails he is using were pulled from pieces of wood and hammered straight with the pliers.

    "Shut up," he growls, when he nicks his thumb for the umpteenth time - as predicted by Kulsoom - and she giggles. They keep teasing each other and laughing as the shelter starts to take shape.
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:15


    Tricky task

    It is time to pull the roof on. "Couldn't you have found something thinner?" Kulsoom laughs. This is indeed tricky. The slightest mishandling could tear the thin plastic sheet and it is the only one large enough. It takes two hours.

    So how was this struggle different from the one he crossed the LoC for? "That was fun, this is a pain," he says.
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:15


    Bit by bit

    With the roof up, it is time to raise the walls. Akram finds two long sheets woven in straw. They have to be stitched together. But even so, they can only cover three sides. For the fourth, a separate sheet is employed.

    "I told Kulsoom when we got married I was very poor and we would have to put together our life bit by bit," he says.

    "I couldn't have imagined that the bits he was talking about would be these," Kulsoom says.
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:15


    Perfect timing

    Akram's timing turns out to be perfect. The shack is up and running 45 minutes before it is time to break fast.

    It is Kulsoom's turn to take over. Within 30 minutes, she has their few belongings neatly tucked away inside what she calls their "rainbow house" because of the colourful materials that have been used.

    3 Nov 05, 20:16


    No going back

    Akram says he thought of going back to the Indian side when they lost everything in the quake. "But how do I do that?"

    What about the opening up of the LoC? "I came here illegally," he says. "I don't think I qualify among those who will be picked to cross the border."

    He says life could be much better on the other side but now he has a family and "won't do anything to jeopardise their safety".
  • poon cho tang
    3 Nov 05, 20:16


    Nice little house

    "At least we have a shelter now," says Kulsoom when asked what she thought of her new home. Looking at Akram teasingly, she adds: "A very nice little house I must say - at least till the rains come."

    She picks up little Omar. "He is just not taking to powdered milk which was the only kind we could get from relief camps.

    "Come one, young man," she says. "Grow up. Don't make life any more difficult than it already is."
