    Routemaster retrospective



    End of the line

    From Friday 9 December, this quintessential London scene will disappear forever as the last of the capital's veteran red Routemaster buses reach the end of the line.

    On that day the last Routemaster route - the 159 running from Streatham to Marble Arch - will convert to modern double-deckers, and barring two tourist friendly 'heritage routes', the 53-year old buses will be gone.

    Routemaster record

    Photographers Ralf Obergfell and Jet have spent the last year capturing the RoutemastersÂ’ swansong for their website, LastStop!

    Combining still life, portrait and documentary images they hope to provide a unique record of the buses and the interaction between drivers, conductors and passengers.

    For Ralf, the project is the culmination of a long-held fascination for the buses and their classic design. For Jet, whose father was a bus driver, their demise represents the passing of a constant object in her life.

    'Design landmark'

    "The Routemaster is an icon, a landmark known all over the world," says Ralf.

    "As kids growing up in Germany, we were fascinated by the music, the clothes and the designs coming out of England. The Routemaster was part of that."

    "The bus is beautiful, it looks great on the eye. It's a shame no-one has designed a new, modern one as Britain is so well known for its industrial design."

    'All aboard'

    Another familiar London sight - people running to catch a departing bus - will be no more with the withdrawal of the open-backed, jump on-and-off Routemaster.

    Many Routemasters are being replaced by articulated 'bendi-buses', which offer improved accessibility to disabled people and parents with pushchairs.

    But the buses' demise has prompted a strong backlash from some, with a number of 'Save the Routemaster' campaigns and petitions launched.

    In safe hands

    With the retirement of the Routemaster another London icon - the bus conductor - will also pass into history.

    Although ticket machines like the one pictured have long been replaced by modern electronic versions, conductors like Kathy are regarded by many as a protective presence against crime and vandalism, as well as a source of information and help.

    "I often built up a rapport with conductors while I was working," says Ralf. "Some would even introduce me to the passengers and explain what I was doing."

    Changing times

    Routemaster conductors will be offered redundancy packages or retraining as drivers when the buses are taken out of service.

    "Most conductors are quite sad about leaving the job," says Ralf. "For some, they have been there 10, 20 years ... it is their life.

    "Others are glad it's coming to an end, as the job has got much harder. People are more abusive and there is less respect."

    Retirement options

    Although Routemasters may no longer run in revenue-earning service, their chugging presence is likely to endure for many years to come.

    When they leave service, the buses are usually sold on to private companies and enthusiasts.

    A retired 'RM' can expect either a restful retirement receiving plenty of TLC, or an active old age as a promotional tool for a company eager to exploit their global image as a symbol of London.

    Symbol of London

    "When we were out on the street, we would sometimes meet bus-spotters collecting numbers. But we were drawn to the bus as a landmark of London, says Ralf.

    "I feel like I learned a lot about London. It won't be the same again."
