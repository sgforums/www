In pictures: A day in the life of Wenzhou

    One of the many Chinese cities helping to transform the national and world economies is Wenzhou. Many outside China may not even have heard of the city, in coastal Zhejiang province, but it is undergoing fast growth and a retail boom.

    In the old city the back streets are lined with hundreds of small garment shops while local businessmen lead the investment in large factories exporting goods to rest of the world.

    Photographer Alex Hofford spent 24 hours capturing the life of its inhabitants.

    About three million of the seven and a half million residents of Wenzhou are migrant workers from other poorer provinces.

    Many of the suits are re-branded with the Verri label and exported to Italy.

    The shoe recycling facility in Nanbaixiang, outside Wenzhou, is one of the largest in China, with shoes arriving by the truckload from all over the country.

    Xie Yu-bing heats rubber shoe soles so that the remaining upper sole can be easily ripped away using a pair of tongs. The rubber is then recycled.

    He says: "I earn 30 RMB a day ($3.74) doing this work. I don't worry too much about the burning rubber fumes, but I am looking for another job".

    Mrs Zhang, 68, sorts waste plastics into different piles at a trash collection and recycling area on the edge of central Wenzhou.

    She says: "I often do not make enough money to eat. I earn no more than 10 RMB ($1.24) per day, sometimes as little as RMB 3 a day."

    Her home does have electricity, but her water comes from a well. "Although we know it's polluted, there is nothing we can do," she says.

    As China's middle class grows in number, new ways for them to spend their disposable income are emerging.

    Consumerism is sweeping across China, but the spiritual void felt by many has led to an explosion of Yoga clubs in the last couple of years.

    This class is run by Indian instructor Ashwary Kadian, who has been in Wenzhou for one year and came to the city to teach. "I make more money here than in India," he says.

    Zhang Juhua, 42, from Hubei province, a worker in the Wenzhou Fortor Lingerie factory sorts white brassieres that have passed quality control into piles ready to be packed into boxes and sent to a major high street brand in Italy. She says life is better in Wenzhou than in her native Hubei province as "Wenzhou has more factories and it is easier to find a job here.
