    New lemur species found in Africa

    Two new species of lemur have been discovered on the island of Madagascar.

    The first new breed is a giant mouse lemur the size of a grey squirrel with a bushy tale, called a Mirza zaza.

    The second is a giant mouse lemur called Microcebus lehilahytsara. It's small enough to fit in your hand, with round ears and a stripe on its nose.


    Microcebus lehilahytsara is little bigger than a mouse. (Photo: Robert Zingg)

    Lemurs are only found in the wild on Madagascar. The island broke off from mainland Africa 165 million years ago and is home to many rare animals.

    Separate species

    Experts used to think only one type of giant mouse lemur existed, living in different areas on the north and west of island.

    But scientific evidence found by German and Malagasy scientists now proves they evolved into separate species two million years ago.

    The findings, by scientists at the German Primate Centre and the University of GÃ¶ttingen, mean there are now 49 known Lemur species in the world.

    - Lemurs are the closest living relation to our ancient primate ancestors who lived about 55 million years ago
    - They are primates, which means they have hands and feet
    - A third of lemurs are extinct
    - They are threatened from hunting and habitat destruction

    Giant panda gives birth to twins

    Xue Xue the giant panda gave birth to twins at an animal rescue centre in northwest China on Saturday.



    The 18-year-old bear gave birth to a girl who weighed in at 151g, and a boy who weighed just 122g.

    Staff have at the centre have named the girl Da Xin, meaning joy, and called the boy Xiao Xing, meaning prosperity.

    Although Xue Xue is looking after the baby girl very well, staff are now caring for the boy as she was struggling to look after both cubs.



    In the wild pandas would usually look after just one cub at a time, and staff are hoping that Xiao Xing will build up strength in the rescue centre's nursery.

    They have asked experts from the Chengdu Giant Panda Breeding and Research Base in a nearby province for help with rearing the baby.

    Still endangered

    The new arrivals bring the total number of cubs born at the centre to six.

    That's good news for the rare bears, as there are thought to be only 1,600 of the animals left in the wild.

    Pandas can only get pregnant on a few days between March and May each year, and give birth between 83 and 181 days afterwards.

    Turtles get help after killings

    Turtles in Mexico are to get special protection after about 80 of the creatures were found dead on a beach.

    It's thought they were killed for their eggs, which are sold for lots of money.



    The dead Olive Ridley turtles were found on Escobilla beach, which is one of the most important nesting grounds for the protected creatures.

    The government has slammed the attacks as "an act of vandalism" and is working with the navy to help the turtles and prevent further killings.

    The government has already sent two navy boats to patrol the beach and look out for anyone who might want to hurt the turtles.

    The Olive Ridley sea turtle is the smallest sea turtle in the world.

    They've been protected under Mexican law since 1990. There's a penalty of up to nine years in jail for anyone caught killing them.

    Goats helping starvation in Niger

    Aid is now arriving in the famine struck African country of Niger, but one agency is sending something a bit unusual - goats!

    A bad drought this year, and a swarm of locusts the year before, destroyed crops, leaving a severe food shortage.



    Unicef has sent out over 12,000 goats, which families use to make milk and cheese to eat and sell.

    They can keep the goats as long as they give the first male and female baby goat born to another family in need.

    Goats have about three kids two times a year, so the herds are steadily increasing.

    That means the project will keep itself going, and help more people get the vital food and medicine they need.

    Farmers admit to gassing badgers

    Some farmers in UK are illegally gassing badger sets because they think the animals spread disease.

    Badgers are a protected species, so it's against the law to kill them or tamper with their homes, called sets.



    But the National Farmers Union (NFU) says badgers are being killed all the time, and wants to make it legal.

    Many farmers think badgers spread a disease called tuberculosis (TB) to their cows, but environmental groups say there is no proof of this.

    Farmers say the spread of TB is out of control, and think it's been caused by making it illegal to kill badgers.

    But environmental groups say the disease is more closely linked to cattle movements than to badgers.

    The Department of Environment Food and Rural Affairs (Defra) said it would look at the issue again in the autumn.

    Net trade threatens rare animals

    The world's most endangered species are being driven to the brink of extinction because people are selling them on the internet - even though it's illegal.

    A three-month inquiry showed that in just one week more than 9,000 animals were advertised for sale on the web.



    At least 70% of these were from species protected by law. They included a gorilla being sold in London.

    The inquiry was restricted to five categories of animals so it's thought the problem is actually much bigger.

    The study was carried out by the International Fund For Animal Welfare.

    It says that every day thousands of wild animals and animal parts - such as ivory tusks - are being traded in cyberspace.

    A spokesman for the organisation said: "This situation must be tackled immediately by governments and website owners before it is too late.

    "Our message to shoppers is simple - buying wildlife online is as damaging as killing it yourself."

    Millions of insects on the move

    Millions of insects are on the move in London - but there's no need to panic - these wandering bugs are part of the Natural History Museum's refurbishment.

    Around 28 million dead spiders and insects are being temporarily moved while their present home is demolished, to make way for their new dwelling.



    The bugs move into a environmentally controlled building in 2008, to keep them safe against the effects of time.

    The new Darwin Centre will also house 120 scientists researching insects.

    Global collection

    The bugs in the collection have been gathered for 300 years, and feature creepy-crawlies from around the world.

    The insects range in size from the barely visible fairy fly, with a wingspan of 0.02cm to the world's largest moth, Thysania agrippina, with a wingspan of 30cm.

    The largest specimen to move will be a 105 x 42cm hornet's nest from China.

    The bugs will be housed in different parts of the museum or in a special warehouse in south London during the building work.

    Pets saved from Gaza settlements

    Animal charities have moved into the Gaza Strip in a bid to rescue pets which were left behind following last week's evacuations of Israeli settlers.



    Dogs, cats, chickens, goats, geese, ducks, turtles and an iguana are thought to be among up to 200 creatures which remain in the former settlements.

    Rescuers have already recovered around 100 animals and those wearing tags will be reunited with their owners.

    More home alone pets get stressed

    More cats and dogs are getting stressed out because their owners are leaving them home alone, according to research.

    Eight in 10 vets said they have seen pets with problems caused by their owners leaving them on their own for long periods of time.



    The survey of 350 vets and 1,700 animal owners found that more than a quarter of pets were affected by the problem.

    Other reasons for pets feeling upset included the arrival of a new baby or the loss of a family member.

    Experts say that ideally dogs should not be left alone for longer than four or five hours, while cats are likely to up and leave if they don't get enough attention.

    Vets said signs of stress in dogs included "destructive behaviour and excessive barking and yowling".

    Injured elephant gets false foot

    An elephant is learning to walk on all four legs again after a landmine accident left her with one leg shorter than the others.

    Motala, a 44-year-old Thai elephant, has been fitted with a false foot designed to strengthen her bad leg.

    The foot, which looks a bit like a punch bag, is made from green canvas filled with sawdust and cushions.

    She will practice walking with it for up to seven hours a day for the next six to eight months.

    By then it is hoped her leg will be strong enough to have a permanent foot fitted.



    Vets are pleased with her progress and relieved she has not rejected the foot.

    Motala was hauling logs when she trod on a mine six years ago.

    Since then she has been recovering at an elephant hospital in Thailand.

    Soraida Salwala from the hospital said: "She has been very cooperative and I think when we put more weight on it, then she could finally stretch out the muscles and the tendons and we hope she will finally put the weight on it."

    Giant poisonous bug found in UK

    A giant poisonous centipede has made a surprise appearance in a house in the UK, thousands of miles from its home.

    The 22.86cm long creature - also called an arthropod - was heard scuttling around by Aaron Balick under some paper behind his television.

    He trapped it and the next day took it to the Natural History Museum, who told him he shouldn't have gone near it!

    Bug expert Stuart Hine said Mr Balick was "incredibly brave" to have trapped the centipede himself.



    It's thought the creepy-crawly came to the UK hidden in a box of fruit or even of electrical goods.

    Mr Hine said it's not unusual for big bugs to be transported alongside goods, but added that something this big and ferocious is normally spotted much earlier.

    There are reports of one such creature growing to twice the size of this one in Venezuela in South America.

    Their sting, which is given through the front claws, can result in a blistering rash, nausea and fever.

    No antidote to the venom exists, but the sting is very rarely life-threatening.

    Family living with colony of bats

    A family in Aberdeenshire is sharing its home with a colony of 2,000 bats.

    The pipistrelles live in the loft of the Bestwick family home in Rothiemay, Scotland. Because they're a protected species, they can't be moved.

    The Bestwicks said that although the smell could get bad, and they had to sweep up bat droppings, they'd learned to live with their furry guests.

    Five-year-old Rebecca said she liked the way the bats squeaked and could fly through small holes.



    The bats were in the house when the family moved in seven years ago.

    Mr Bestwick says he has no problem with bats being protected, but wished the law was a bit more in favour of people in their position.

    Mrs Bestwick said: "One day we discovered there were 23 bats in our water tank, which was why the water wasn't tasting very nice."

    Thousands of cormorants face cull

    Up to 3,000 sea-birds could be culled this winter, to keep them away from fish in lakes and rivers in Britain.

    Cormorants feast on fish, and for the second year running the government has said fishermen can shoot them because they're coming too far inland.



    The RSBP accepts the birds are a big problem, but said the cull threatens overall UK cormorant numbers.

    But National Federation of Anglers said plans include bird-scaring and fish shelters, not just shooting cormorants.

    Recently the sea-birds have moved away from the coast and started eating fish from rivers, lakes and canals.

    This move has made them unpopular with anglers, who see the birds as unwelcome competition.

    And fish farms say the birds are pinching fish they want to catch or have reared to sell.

    Cull puts population 'at risk'

    A spokesperson for the RSPB said there are other ways of treating the cormorant problem.

    "Fish and cormorants have evolved together for millennia - if you put a fish refuge in, the fish can hide and the problem can be reduced."

    He added that if the full 3,000 quota of birds were killed, the cormorant population across England could be put at risk of decline.

    The RSPB also say the government may be breaking European rules on protecting wild birds.

    Last year 1,200 cormorants were killed in the cull.

    Baby chimps laugh and smile young

    Baby chimps learn to laugh, smile and play games at a really young age - just like human infants.

    Scientists found that during their first weeks, little chimps smiled at their human carers to show happiness, and showed angry faces when cross.



    They also said greetings and imitated their carers, just like baby humans.

    Scientists also found very young chimps that had a cloth put over their face got upset, but within a few weeks learned to play the game "peek-a-boo".

    Tickled

    And by a few weeks old they even laughed out loud when they were tickled by their human carers.

    The study was done by Yerkes Primate Research Centre in the US, and scientists say it shows humans and chimps developing in a similar way.

    Scientists said they could tell the difference between the happy smile and an unhappy grimace, as the grimace was re-enforced with a nip if the chimp wasn't in the mood to be tickled.

    Flood city's animals being saved

    As hopes of finding human survivors of Hurricane Katrina fade, some rescuers are focusing their attention on saving the animals which got left behind.

    New Orleans zoo is on higher ground and escaped the worst of the flooding, meaning most of the creatures there are in good shape.



    Two otters died in the storm and an alligator is still missing, but the rest of the animals have survived.

    Meanwhile, volunteers are scouring the streets to rescue much-loved pets.

    Charities are working from a 138-page list of addresses from survivors worried about the animals they were not allowed to take with them when they fled the city.

    Stranded dogs

    Thousands of dogs have been stranded on rooftops and rescuers are painstakingly dragging boats filled with cages through the flooded areas to take the creatures to safety.

    At the zoo, staff remained with the animals throughout the hurricane.



    They had been prepared for such an emergency for a long time and made sure they had enough food to keep the animals going.

    But some of the creatures cannot stay at the zoo.

    The water is now too contaminated for the sea lions, so they are being taken in a refrigerated lorry to another zoo in Texas.

    Great apes to get more protection

    Gorillas, chimpanzees and orang-utans are to get more protection after a group of countries, including the UK, agreed to work to keep them safe.

    The first Declaration on Great Apes was put together in Kinshasa, in the Democratic Republic of Congo on Friday.



    The meeting had looked at things like getting the support of local people and doing more to fight poaching to protect the endangered animals.

    A recent report said unless action was taken, apes would soon be extinct.

    Jim Knight, the UK's representative at the meeting, said it was really important to get people who live near the apes to realise how important they are.

    He said they could earn cash from the creatures through things like tourism.
