    Now I would like to contribute a fair and frank opinion. And I hope Singaporean fans don't get me wrong and don't be offended. Just an opinion only. And I hope anyone can tell me the reason. I would like to know.

    I realize that soccer fans in Singapore tend to support Japan very very much, especially the youngsters ( teens and below 25 group) no matter how. In terms of Asian football, even the media in Singapore focus more on Japan team than any other Asian country like Korea or China. This, I don't see it so obvious in other Asian countries.

    When you randomly ask any young man on the street about stating a powerful team in Asia, right away the answer from the majority is Japan. Very few would answer Korea, Iran or Saudi. Believe it or not, if you travel elsewhere in Asia, the answers from them are more of a mixed of different teams instead of only Japan.

    Can any Singaporean fan tell me the reason? I am curious to find out. I can see many more young people wearing Japan's jersey than Korea's or China's.

    In East Asian soccer, no doubt Japan has improved a lot from the 90s till now but to be honest, it is Korea that has a longer tradition of soccer as a powerhouse since the old days. The 80s and 90s, we would not see a Korea team failing to qualify for Asian Cup, Asian Games and the World Cup (since 1986) and the players in the 80s and 90s played some of Asia's best attacking football too, only failing to win the Asian Cup on many occasions after the quarter finals or semi finals or ending up as losing finalists. Fine Korean players with quality attitude were produced too but nobody remembers. Japanese players in recent time like Inamoto, Nakata Hidetoshi or Ono Shinji, despite having longer names, they can remember.

    Players' names should not be difficult to remember too because Korean names are very similar to Chinese names. We all have the same surnames anyway. People here in Singapore tend to remember Japanese players' names more easily despite the names being longer and so different from Chinese names.

    For ninja's case, he supports Japan because he's been living and working in Japan since 1992 (am I right, Shortninja?). So naturally, he's become like a half Japanese. I can understand that.

    Not only myself but many of my Korean friends living here are also surprised by the great support the young Singaporean guys have for Japan team.

    I do have friends in Singapore supporting Korea very much but they support Korea because I'm their friend or one of their good friends is a Korean.

    I do notice there are Korea or Thailand supporters but much fewer compares to Japan supporters here in Singapore.

    In terms of standard, Thailand might lose to Japan but I don't think Japan is better than Korea. From club levels to international levels, (no offend to Japan or Ninja) Korean clubs and national team have beaten Japanese clubs and national team more than losing them in statistic.

    Ninja, I'm just stating what I have observed here. I'm not unhappy or anything. It's just that my friends and I are very curious about this. If you can explain to me the reasons, I'll be happy to know.

    and for 798's saying that the 3rd/4th placing Korea-Japan game is more interesting. Is it because of Japan?

    Verbeek Likely to Remain Despite Asian Cup Setback

    By Kang Seung-woo
    Staff Reporter

    Pim Verbeek's stint with the South Korean national football team will not come to an end despite a disappointing loss to Iraq in the Asian Cup semifinals.

    The Korea Football Association (KFA) said that it doesn't have any plan to release the Dutch coach.

    ``The Asian Cup is not over yet. As Korea has to battle against Japan Saturday for third place, we need to encourage him, not disturb him,'' KFA secretary general Ka Sam-hyun said after the semifinal match.

    Lee Young-moo, chairman of the technical committee, was also negative on removing Verbeek when Korea sneaked into the quarterfinal with a record of 1-1 with one draw, saying, ``It is an issue to talk about after the Asian Cup.''

    KFA president Chung Mong-joon backs Verbeek.

    ``We cannot win and score in every single match,'' he said in a media interview.

    In the post-game press conference, Verbeek shared the same opinion with the KFA.

    ``I made my decision already but I am not going to say what it is because I want to keep the Korean people excited for the next couple of days,'' he said.

    ``But if the Korean fans think we didn't play a good tournament, they must have a different vision of playing international football.

    Korea, Japan Vie for Third Spot

    By Kim Tong-hyung
    Staff Reporter

    With its dreams for an Asian Cup trophy shattered by Iraq, South Korea will look for the consolation prize of a third-place finish against defending champions Japan in Jakarta this evening. The winner of the match will be awarded with an automatic spot in the next Asian Cup.

    Tonight's match-up is what many Asian football fans had hoped and expected for the final, which would have added another page to one of the greatest regional rivalries in the sports world.

    However, the Taeguk Warriors finally saw their luck run out against a spirited Iraqi team, losing 4-3 on penalties, a deserving conclusion to a streak of five, uninspiring performances that saw the team score only three goals in that span.

    Japan, which looked much more potent than their East Asian rivals, had the misfortune of running into a football juggernaut in Saudi Arabia in the semifinals.

    For South Korean manager Pim Verbeek, who predicted a title ahead of the tournament, it would be important for his players to show they still have a pulse, with his future with the national team hanging by a thread.

    It's questionable whether the team could finally find its form, which would still be a game too late.

    With injuries wiping out nearly half of his starting lineup ahead of the tournament _ including midfielders Park Ji-sung (Manchester United), Lee Young-pyo (Tottenham), Seol Ki-hyeon (Reading) and Kim Nam-il (Suwon Samsung) _ Verbeek opted for a defensive approach, packing the midfield with five players to cover a four-back defensive line while sending out a lone striker as the offensive point-man.

    It would be hard to qualify the 4-5-1, or 4-2-3-1 and whatever the Korean press chose to call it, as a success.

    Although the enlarged midfield allowed the Koreans to hold a larger share of possession, and thus grind the game down to a slower pace as they prefer, it clearly compromised the offense as goal-scoring chances were rare with the target man finding himself isolated for long stretches.

    Verbeek's forwards had to be clinical with the sporadic chances they got from the net, and they failed miserably. Middlesbrough forward Lee Dong-gook, Asian Cup's second all-time scorer with 10 goals, played with an alarming lack of urgency that defied, or perhaps explained, his deer-in-the-headlights expressions.

    Shimizu S-Pulse forward Cho Jae-jin is proving once again that is gifted with the body of a great striker but not the head.

    It bears further watching whether Verbeek would try switching two a 4-4-2 for the Japan match to find an answer to the team's scoring doubt, which would give him the opportunity to pair Lee or Cho with Ulsan Hyundai winger Lee Chun-soo, who stepped up as perhaps Korea's best player during this tournament with his relentless attack on the right flank.

    The two-man front would be a worthy experiment, especially with defensive backs Kim Chi-woo and Oh Beom-seok displaying enough skills and pace to support the attack in transition. The talents of holding midfielders Kim Jung-woo and Son Dae-ho also suit better to the defensive roles they would likely be assigned to under a 4-4-2 system.

    However, Verbeek would be reluctant to play a transition game against Japan, and compromise their share of possession with a smaller midfield, considering the opponent's potent attack led by the brilliance of star-striker Naohiro Takahara and playmaker Shunsuke Nakamura.
