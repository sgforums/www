    Iran Presents Ultimate Test

    By Kim Tong-hyung
    Staff Reporter, Korea Times

    After sneaking into the knockout rounds with a 1-0 victory against Asian Cup co-hosts Indonesia, the South Koreans are now bracing themselves for a quarterfinals match against their old foes Iran.

    Pim Verbeek's boys have yet to find their form in the tournament, never scoring more than once in any of the three group stage matches and showing an alarming tendency to tail off in the second-half, squandering early leads to both Saudi Arabia and Bahrain.

    Meeting Iran on a one-way street doesn't inspire any confidence, a country that has repeatedly proved itself as Korean football's biggest nemesis in international competition for the past two decades.

    The Iranians seem relieved of facing a familiar opponent they had much success against recently, instead of a surging Saudi Arabia that is peaking at the right time.

    ``It is interesting that we are playing against them (South Korea) in the Asian Cup quarterfinals again but hopefully we can win this match again,'' said Iran midfielder Andranik Teymourian, who plays for the Premiership's Bolton Wanderers.

    Despite declaring themselves as the team to beat in Asian football time after time, the South Koreans have won the Asian Cup only two times and nearly a half-century has passed since their last title in 1960. And Iran has much to do with their futility of trophies.

    Iran beat Korea 2-1 in extra time in the 1972 finals to lift their second consecutive Asian Cup trophy. In the 1996 tournament in United Arab Emirates (UAE), Iran blasted Korea 6-2 in the quarterfinals, which is still considered among the country's most embarrassing moments ever in football.

    The loss resulted in the sacking of manager Park Jong-hwan, hailed as the Korean equivalent of Vince Lombardi before the drubbing, leaving him with a tainted reputation as an underachiever who wasted what was then considered as the best national squad ever, with all-time talents such as striker Hwang Sun-hong and midfielder Hong Myung-bo in their primes.

    Korea seemed to solve its Iran jinx in the quarterfinals of the 2000 tournament, beating them 2-1 on a double by Lee Dong-gook, a striker now playing for Middlesbrough and also starting for the current national squad. However, Iran returned the favor in the 2004 tournament, beating Korea 4-3 in a dramatic game.

    Verbeek says that his squad has enough talent to beat Iran this time around, but the Taeguek Warriors' uninspiring play in the past three matches makes his words less convincing.

    The Dutch coach had employed a similar defensive-minded game plan in all of the three group stage matches, packing the midfield with five players to dominate possession and slowdown the pace of the game, while taking what the opponent's defense gives them on the attack.

    However, it would be hard to qualify the strategy as a success, which resulted in a shortage of goal-scoring opportunities with most of the midfielders playing deep to cover for a shaky defensive line.

    Verbeek could only hope that his team breaks out of his funk against Iran, which owns what clearly is the tournament's most potent midfield lineup.

    Bayern Munich midfielder Ali Karimi scored five goals in the 2004 tournament and seems to be back in top form.

    The former Asian Player of the Year will be supported by other Europe-based players such as Teymourian, Eintracht Frankfurt 's Mehdi Mahdavikia and Osasuna's Javad Nekounam, forming a unit capable of terrorizing Korea's enlarged midfield.

    Iran will also trot a proven finisher upfront in Hannover 96 striker Vahid Hashemian.

    Verbeek is likely to be forced to keep his 4-5-1 formation to cope against Iran's superior firepower, meaning that the Koreans will have to be more clinical in front of the net since there could be long minutes between goal-scoring chances.

    He would have to find someone to provide composure in front of the net, as the dismal play of forwards Lee and Cho Jae-jin, both looking uncomfortable in the role as lone striker has grown into the team's biggest concern.

    Lee, No.2 on the Asian Cup's all-time scoring list with 10 goals, looked disturbingly hesitant in his limited appearances in the tournament, although he showed a hint of aggressiveness off the field with his rambling complaints about lack of touches.

    Cho, who won the starter's job over Lee, is out proving what his critics have always said about him __ a player gifted with the physique of a great striker but not the senses.

    Ulsan Hyundai midfielder Lee Chun-soo has clearly been Korea's best player so far with his relentless attack on the right flank, and his set-piece prowess could be crucial for the team, especially with the strikers struggling to break out of their funk.

    Perhaps Verbeek would wish to screw Ulsan Lee's head atop Cho's body to solve Korea's scoring woes. Mad-science could hardly be an option, but the Dutch coach seems to be running out of ideas to coax the team into playing better than the sum of its parts.
