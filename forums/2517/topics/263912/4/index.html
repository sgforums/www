    China hammer co-hosts Malaysia
    Jul 10, 2007
    PA Sport

    KUALA LUMPUR Â– Han Peng and Wang Dong netted two goals apiece as China kicked off their AFC Asian Cup 2007 campaign in style with a 5-1 trouncing of Malaysia at Bukit Jalil National Stadium on Wednesday.

    Shao Jiayi also scored for the 2004 runners-up who over-powered the co-hosts to send out a clear message of intent to Group C rivals Iran and Uzbekistan.

    Substitute Indra Putra Mahayuddin grabbed a consolation goal for the Malaysians, who became the first of the four co-hosts to lose a match in the competition following victories for Vietnam and Indonesia against United Arab Emirates and Bahrain, respectively, and a draw for Thailand against Iraq.

    China threatened as early as the second minute when Mao Jianqing crossed from the left to Wang whose first-time shot was well saved by Malaysia goalkeeper Azizon Kadir.

    Han then missed a glorious chance in the seventh minute when he outjumped two Malaysia defenders to meet WangÂ’s cross from the right only to put his header wide.

    Malaysia enjoyed a brief period of pressure with Akmal Rizal Rakhli narrowly caught offside on a through pass by Fauzie Nan but the Asian Cup 2004 runners-up would take the lead on 15 minutes after a finely worked goal.

    A defence-splitting pass by Shao released Wang who took the ball to the right byline before cleverly lofting it to the far post where an unmarked Han headed easily into the back of the net.

    The home side nearly found their way back into the game as Hairuddin Omar just failed to meet a Hardi Jaffar corner with his head.

    But China turned up the heat as the half progressed and should have gone two goals up on the half-hour mark as Mao turned a Wang cut-back over the crossbar from three yards out.

    It would be only a brief reprieve for Norizan BakarÂ’s side as China notched their second in the 36th minute. Chinese skipper Zheng Zhi was the provider this time with a cross from the left which was narrowly missed by a leaping Han but fell nicely for an unmarked Shao to stab the ball into the unguarded net from six yards out.

    As the half came to a close, Akmal turned Zhang Yaokun on the right flank and centred the ball into the danger area where an alert Li Weifeng managed to scramble the ball away before it could fall into the path of Hairuddin.

    Hairuddin had the first effort of the second half, heading a corner wide two minute after the break but the Chinese soon regained control when ShaoÂ’s left-footed shot from the edge of box was turned out by Azizon for a corner.

    China duly extended their advantage on 51 minutes when WangÂ’s shot from edge of box took a big deflection off Fauzie Nan and rolled agonisingly past Azizon.

    Four minutes later, it was 4-0 as Han easily notched his second of the game, tapping in at the far post after MaoÂ’s cross from the left flew over the head of defender Norhafiz Zamani Misbah.

    Substitute Indra Putra grabbed a consolation goal for the Malaysians in the 73rd minute, firing a low drive past Li Leilei from 25 metres after a fine lay-off by Hairuddin.

    They could have got another shortly afterwards as Hardi fired in a free-kick from the right flank that eluded a crowd of players in the middle and very nearly surprised goalkeeper Li.

    But Wang piled further misery on the co-hosts deep into injury time with a towering header that flew over the head of Azizon to complete an emphatic win for the Chinese.

    Verbeek Mulls Striker Options

    By Kim Tong-hyung
    Staff Reporter

    In a country that lives for its next football tournament, the national team players are treated like the heroes of Greek tragedies, serenaded by heavy praise and curse as they fight against the fate of disappointment.

    And the end of the stories have been similar to the Greeks too, with the Koreans finding new ways to fight bravely and lose in most of the major international tournaments in past few years, an exception being the impressive fourth-place finish during the 2002 World Cup held at home.

    Korean coach Pim Verbeek hopes to put an end to the recent mediocrity by brining home the crown of the Asian Cup, the regional football tournament that recently kicked off in Indonesia, for the first time in 47 years.

    However, critics question whether the Dutchman has the right men to pull off the job as he struggles to find an answer to his team's biggest woe __ the lack of a proven finisher in front of the net.

    No other position in football is subject to more scrutiny than the striker. However, the list of Korean forwards who shined in international tournaments is quite short.

    Cha Bum-kun, the Bundesliga legend now coaching Suwon Samsung, is probably the greatest Korean footballer ever, but he was 33 years old and nearing the end of his playing career when Korea qualified for the 1986 World Cup in Mexico.

    Hwang Sun-hong, a gifted player who developed a habit of picking up season-ending injuries in every other year, is still bashed by sports fans for his horrific performance against Spain in the 1994 World Cup when he sent more than a handful of his shots into the stands.

    The most prolific scorer of his generation, Hwang never got the chance to fully redeem himself as he missed the next World Cup due to a knee injury suffered in a friendly against China, which was the premise of the disastrous performance by the Koreans in France, which saw then-coach Cha axed just after two games.

    Fast forward to 2007, and Verbeek would love nothing more than to have players as talented as Cha or Hwang. However, he will have to settle for either an injury-hobbled, former prodigy now experiencing confidence crisis or another player with all the physical gifts but sometimes can't seem to kick the ball into the ocean.

    Since his international debut at the age of 18 during the 1998 World Cup, Middlesbrough forward Lee Dong-gook has often drawn comparisons to Hwang for his fluid movement and creativity in front of the net, although the knack for injuries is where both players might be most similar.

    Just as Hwang did in 1998, Lee suffered a knee injury ahead of the 2002 World Cup tournament and was forced to watch from the stands as former teammates such as Park Ji-sung and Ahn Jung-hwan become national heroes on what was supposed to be his team.

    Although the 28-year-old striker seemed to have regained full health, as he showed flashes of brilliance for Middlesbrough in limited minutes last season, he still looks reluctant to test himself for longer stretches.

    During the training camp for the Asian Cup, Lee pulled himself from practice two times complaining of discomfort in his surgically-repaired left knee, although doctors weren't able to find any problems. And with Lee admitting to news reporters that he is scared of re-injuring his knee every time he steps on the pitch, confidence could also be a problem.

    ``Lee needs to push himself harder. Our opponents in the Asian Cup will challenge you a lot harder than your teammates in practice,'' said Verbeek in response to Lee's comments, hinting a sense of frustration.

    Lee regaining his vintage form could be the key for Korea's goal to top the tournament, especially with the team's midfield lineup devastated by injuries to starters such as Manchester United's Park, Reading winger Seol Ki-hyeong and team captain Kim Nam-il of Suwon Samsung.

    This has forced Verbeek to adopt a 4-5-1 formation, packing the midfield with five players, which would apparently help the team keep possession. However, the format also relies heavily on the grueling role of the lone striker __ now penciled as Lee's job __ who would have to be aggressive and diligent to avoid isolation. Lee tore apart opponents in his past two Asian Cup tournaments, scoring 10 goals, but rarely played the role of target man.

    Verbeek's other option is Shimizu S-Pulse forward Cho Jae-jin, who might be a better match as lone striker as he played that role for Korea in last year's World Cup.

    In the tournament in Germany, Cho did show an impressive ability in fighting for the ball in the air and keeping it until support arrived, but often found himself isolated with wingers Lee Chun-soo (Ulsan Hyundai) and Seol forced to play deep to cover the defense.

    Standing at 1.85 meters, Cho provides a rare aerial threat for the Koreans in front of the penalty box and his dribbling skills have improved over the years. However, he is often criticizing for playing mechanically and never had a reputation for having a nose for the net.
