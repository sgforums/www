    China Southern Buys 10 A330s to Add Long-Haul Routes (Update2)

    By Jiang Jianguo and Irene Shen

    Oct. 24 (Bloomberg) -- China Southern Airlines Co., the country's biggest carrier by fleet size, agreed to buy 10 Airbus SAS A330-200 aircraft as it adds more overseas routes.

    The planes, to be delivered starting in 2010, are valued at as much as $1.77 billion at list prices, the Guangzhou-based carrier said in a Hong Kong stock exchange statement late yesterday. The airline paid a ``significantly lower'' price.

    Chinese carriers, led by China Southern and Air China Ltd., plan to expand international networks as economic growth fuels demand for overseas trips. China Southern, which flies about a third of the country's airline passengers, is adding at least 10 overseas routes before the 2008 Beijing Olympics.

    ``China Southern is trying to secure its market share,'' said Li Lei, an analyst at China Securities Co. in Beijing. ``It's particularly adding mid- and long-haul routes to increase its international business.''

    As well as the 10 A330s, the airline has also ordered 20 A320s and 80 Boeing Co. 737s in the past year. These 110 planes will raise the carrier's capacity 43 percent, it said.

    China Southern rose 4.5 percent to HK$11.62 in Hong Kong trading today. In Shanghai, it gained 7.1 percent to 20.87 yuan.

    The airline will use cash and bank loans to pay for the 10 A330s. The A330-200 can carry 253 passengers in a three-class configuration and has a range of 12,500 kilometers (6,750 nautical miles), according to the Airbus Web site.

    China Southern flew 42.7 million travelers in the first nine months of the year, about 15 percent more than a year earlier. Cargo volume rose 6.2 percent to 640,000 tons. It filled 74.2 percent of available seats in the period, 1.9 percentage points more than a year earlier. Air China's passenger load factor in the period was 78.3 percent.

    China Southern plans to expand its fleet 22 percent this year from the 309 planes it had at the end of 2006. Chinese carriers may fly 185 million passengers this year, 16 percent more than a year earlier, according to the General Administration of Civil Aviation.
