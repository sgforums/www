    The Expanding Universe


    The Universe is expanding, at an apparently uniform rate - the galaxies are receding from us and each other. Mentally running the expansion backwards, we see that there must have been a point in time when all the matter of the Universe was together in an arbitrarily small volume; the 'event' that created our Universe is known as the Big Bang (a term coined by the English astrophysicist Fred Hoyle in 1950. Hoyle, who championed a rival cosmological theory (steady state), meant the "Big Bang" to be a term of derision, but the name was so catchy that it stuck.). Though the Big Bang suggests a colossal explosion, it wasn't really an "explosion" in the sense that we understand it. Space itself exploded.

    Mysterious 'dark energy' still expanding universe

    By MARK CARREAU
    Copyright 2004 Houston Chronicle

    The universe is expanding so rapidly under the influence of a mysterious force that stars may one day begin to fade from view, astronomers said Tuesday.

    Since the influence of a so-called "dark energy" was first observed a half-dozen years ago, its role in the gravity-defying expansion of an already vast cosmos has vaulted to one of the top mysteries in science.

    Findings from the NASA-sponsored study reported Tuesday in Washington not only confirmed the existence of "dark energy," but widened speculation about its impact on the fate of the universe.

    "The acceleration of the universe is as surprising as if you threw an apple into the air and instead of slowing down and falling back, it was to accelerate upward," said Andy Fabian, one of two British astronomers who presented the findings. "It requires new physics beyond the everyday experience."

    The studies led by Fabian and Steve Allen, both from the Institute of Astronomy in Cambridge, England, suggest that if the universe continues to expand at its current pace, stars could fade from night view in 100 billion years.
