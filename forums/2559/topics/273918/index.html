A380 in town again for extensive tests by SIA, authorities

  • SMRT Buses
    13 Aug 07, 22:11
    SINGAPORE: The exact date of Singapore Airlines' maiden A380 commercial flight to Sydney in October will be announced by next week.

    For now, the jumbo aircraft, which is in town again, is being put through extensive tests by airport authorities and SIA to ensure all its systems are working well before that maiden flight.

    Over 400 people from Temasek Polytechnic and the People's Association were passengers in simulation tests of an A380 flight arrival at Terminals 2 and 3 on Monday.

    These tests allow airport staff to test a variety of areas, from passenger disembarkment, cargo and luggage unloading to security screening.

    Four such trials have been conducted so far since the plane flew in on National Day, 9 August.

    Airport authorities said it takes only about 100 minutes to do a full turnaround for the A380, about 10 minutes more than what it takes for the Boeing 747.

    A full turnaround includes disembarking the passengers, cleaning the plane and getting a new batch of passengers on the plane.

    While the tests have been going well, some fine-tuning processes will be needed for the double-decker aircraft.

    Foo Sek Min, director of Airport Management at Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore, said: "It's mainly about how to communicate to people which deck they are supposed to board because there is an upper deck."

    SIA's engineers and ground staff are also testing the jumbo aircraft during this week-long visit, to help cut down the time between delivery and the first flight.

    Stephen Forshaw, vice-president of Public Affairs at SIA, said: "This is something we would normally do after the delivery of a new type of aircraft, but when we were told about the delays, we sat down with Airbus and worked out ways that we could minimise the impact of that delay together.

    "So, instead of waiting for a month before entering an aircraft into service, we will be bringing that timetable back ten days or so."

    Once the plane is delivered, there will be more familiarisation time, especially for the cabin crew.

    "The crew have to be familiar with the layout of the aircraft. That'll take a few days for them to do that progressively and then we'll be using the aircraft ten days or so after delivery," said Mr Forshaw.

    It is also at the delivery of the first aircraft that SIA will reveal what the A380 interior looks like, as the airline has a few pleasant surprises up its sleeve.

    (SINGAPORE - BT) An Australian has snapped up his personal slice of aviation history by paying more than US$100,000 for a pair of tickets on Singapore Airline's inaugural Airbus 380 flight from Singapore to Sydney on Oct 25.

    Julian Hayward picked up the two First Class Suite tickets for US$100,380. That is exactly US$100,000 more than the US$380 figure used as the baseline to set the bids rolling for First Class tickets.

    'It's been so long since we've had an aeronautical feat of engineering - the 747 or even Concorde,' said Mr Hayward. 'I'm extremely excited to have the opportunity to be the first ever passenger on such a large feat of engineering and (it's) even better that charities are benefiting from the maiden flight.'

    SIA has put up a bulk of the 471 seats on the inaugural flight of the world's largest passenger plane for auction on eBay, with the proceeds going to charity.

    The move has caught the fancy of aviation enthusiasts. Some individual bidders have bid as high as US$20,800 for a single First Class Suite ticket, US$8,303 for a Business Class seat and US$5,000 for a one-way seat in Economy Class. These are huge premiums compared to the normal cost of $1,200 for an Economy Class seat, $5,000 for Business Class and $7,200 for First Class.

    And these are for new generation, specially-designed seats they have never even seen yet!

    Meanwhile, SIA yesterday opened up bidding for the Sydney-Singapore return leg, which kicked off at 10 pm, Sydney time (8 pm Singapore time) last night.

    Bidding will continue until Sept 10.

    Stephen Forshaw, SIA's chief spokesman, said he did not expect the bidding for the Sydney-Singapore seats to match the highs of the outbound leg.

    'There is a novelty value in being on the first ever A380 flight in the sky, which is reflected in the high bid prices,' he said. 'The bid prices for the return leg should settle down a bit, we think.'

    The return leg of the charity flight will depart Sydney Kingsford Smith Airport on Oct 26 at 4 pm (Sydney time) and arrive in Singapore at 10 pm (Singapore time).

    Meanwhile, bidding for the few remaining seats on the Singapore-Sydney leg will end this Friday.

    SIA, the first airline to fly the A380 super-jumbo, is putting up about three-quarters of the 471 seats in each leg of the flight - roughly 350 seats in all - across all three classes for bids. With seats auctioned on both legs of the flight, about 700 seats in all will be released for sale.

    The airline has placed an order for 19 of these planes, and will take delivery of the first aircraft on Oct 15.

    All proceeds of the auction will be donated to charities, with one-third of the funds going to Singapore's Community Chest; one-third split between Sydney Children's Hospital, Randwick and The Children's Hospital at Westmead respectively; and one-third to Medecins Sans Frontieres, also known as Doctors Without Borders.
