    hmm, taking two case studies, namely the mid air between Bashkirian flight 2937 and a DHL 757, and a near miss between two 747s over China, which would you rather trust? TCAS (traffic collision avoidance system) or air traffic control?

    In the second case, 2 747s were flying toward one another on the same airway but with a safe 2000 feet seperation given by ATC. however the TCAS on the lower 747 called a climb, and because of this, almost caused a mid air between the 2 of them, and they barely missed each other.

    Â• ATC may not know when TCAS issues RAs. It is possible for ATC to
    unknowingly issue instructions that are contrary to the TCAS RA indications.
    Safe vertical separation may be lost during TCAS coordination when one
    aircraft maneuvers opposite the vertical direction indicated by TCAS and the
    other aircraft maneuvers as indicated by TCAS. Accordingly, during an RA, do
    not maneuver contrary to the RA based solely upon ATC instructions.

    Â• Maneuvering in a direction opposite to a TCAS RA will negate safe separation
    as a TCAS-equipped intruder follows its RA, which has been coordinated to be
    in the opposite direction.

    If a pilot makes the decision to not follow an RA, the crew should be certain that they have identified the correct aircraft and should consider that the intruder may be TCAS-equipped and may be maneuvering toward the aircraft in response to a coordinated RA.

    TCAS Use on PRM Approaches

    The TCAS mode should remain set to TA/RA while conducting ILS PRM
    approaches. Flight crews should be aware, however, that ATC instructions are the primary means of providing separation. Should a TCAS Resolution Advisory conflict with an ATC instruction - comply with the instruction. If a reaction to a TCAS alert causes the crew to deviate from an assigned clearance, inform ATC immediately.
