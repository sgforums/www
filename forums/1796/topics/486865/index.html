The cats devouring wildlife

  • NeverSayGoodBye

    A cat moves through the tall grass with a small, squirming rodent in its mouth. Eye shine reflects the light as the cat turns to look at the camera. Then it moves off into the undergrowth, unafraid and lord of its domain.

    It could be any patch of open ground in urban Melbourne and the cat, indistinguishable from any well-fed domestic feline, could be ridding the land of one more rodent that will scarcely be missed. This is, after all, what cats do, what we expect them to do.

    But this is not suburban Melbourne. The rodent is not a pest. And there is nothing domestic about this cat. Indeed if a new study is to be believed, feral cats such as this one are eating their way through Australia's native wildlife at a rate that is almost impossible to fathom.

     

    The Action Plan for Australian Mammals, released in June, found that 55 of Australia's land mammal species face serious threat of extinction. Dr John Woinarski of Charles Darwin University and co-author of the plan says "for land mammals in particular, predation by feral cats is the principal factor that is contributing to decline".

    Such is the language of academia, necessarily measured and cautious.

    But there is so much more to this story than dry, scientific assessment. The Australian Wildlife Conservancy, Australia's largest private conservation organisation, puts it more starkly: according to the AWC, there are 15million feral cats in Australia and they kill an estimated 75 million native animals every night across the country.

    It is a figure, almost apocalyptic in scale. And yet, Atticus Fleming, the AWC's Chief Executive, describes this figure as "very conservative". There are other published studies, he warns, that record up to 30 native animals being killed in a night by one cat alone.

    It is a perfect storm. And it is sending many of Australia's native wildlife species spiralling towards extinction.

    Where did all of these cats come from? To answer this question requires a journey back into the early decades of white settlement.

    "Cats arrived here very early on," Fleming says. 'Some probably survived from shipwrecks. Others came with the first settlers.'

    Whether brought as pets, or as a means of controlling rodents on ships bound for Australia, these cats quickly gained a foothold in areas surrounding the first settlements.  "Feral cats probably became widespread in Victoria and NSW by about the 1840s," says Woinarski.

    Throughout their relatively short stay in Australia, feral cats have proved themselves to be a highly adaptable species with few natural enemies in the wild. And since they arrived they have been breeding like rabbits.

    Indeed domestic cats going wild are only a small part of  this long-established problem. "Strays are a problem, particularly around the urban fringe," says Fleming, "and obviously in urban areas with cats that are now going wild. But we have to remember that feral cats have been in the landscape for a couple of hundred years."
