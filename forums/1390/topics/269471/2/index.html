    Anyway I believe EOD should be considered elite judging, by the high amounts of risk in their job scope, especially so when they are dealing with real, live ordance.
    If you wanna classify in terms of special-skill identifications (badges & tabs), then other such experts like snipers, parachutists/riggers, Rangers, etc. must also be recognized alongside the EOD personnel. But they're highly-trained soldiers, rather than highly-trained units per se.
    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Awards_and_decorations_of_the_Singapore_Armed_Forces

    In the Singapore context, those in 36 SCE train only as combat engineers too. EOD = Explosive Ordnance Disposal, geddit? i.e. Bomb disposal unit. They're not like the Viet Cong sappers who infiltrate to plant explosives as sabotage or ambush. This local equivalent would instead be the CDOs, who train their own demolition experts. NDU also has their own EOD sub-unit.

    You know Jason Lim, the weirdo artist (~lol~) who dropped his chandelier at the Venice Biennale? He's a Pre-U buddy of mine & a former bomb-disposal expert at 36 SCE. He'd brought me up to his bunk once to "take cover" when I was in his camp, & I got to learn that his vocation was quite a good-life, even boring one. Sure, the dangers are there (when activated) & the technical demands are quite high. But he also had a lot of downtime -- plenty of standbys & on-calls -- when not attaching out to follow combat units doing blind clearance inside the SAFTI live-firing areas.

    GDS = airmobile (or, more popularly known here, heliborne)
    CDO = airborne

    GDS are supposed to be trained for seaborne assaults too, no idea how much. CDO specialize in amphibious missions & as special forces.

    GDS are known more as elite infantry, a bit glamorous now cuz they've got their own beret (since 1994). Where I came from, those in Armour used to have a big rivalry with those in Infantry (with or without guardsmen); they're the only formation I knew of, other than OCS, which listed boxing as a PT activity. The SAR units considered themselves "heavy" infantry & the SIR units as "light" or "line" infantry, although there's a big of a military tradition in this. Armour is the modern cavalry, in our case mechanized; whereas normal Infantry evolved from the skirmishers & musketeers of old.
