    Haha to quote the seniors, "fitness can be trained".

    I'm not sure what are the exact figures from ptp batch and enhanced batch. One thing's for sure, when I went for my VA, my detail IC told me his intake was about 270 plus I believe. While it is 250 is usually the case, CMPB will choose more for the initial enlistment because the ooc rate is really high.

    Enlistees are chosen based on recommendation given by the Commando who interviewed you. After which CMPB will decide who are the final 250+ to be enlisted into SOTAC.

    What I was told was that it is the people of CMPB who make the final decision. Therefore even if you have performed well during the VA ie Interview, physical + psycho motor excercises and the IQ test, you might not make the cut.

    Nevertheless I believe if you're PES A, with a clean family background, plus you did well for the IQ test taken during the first medical checkup, you're likely to be selected for the final enlistment.

    Hope this helps!

    Hmm cdos.boyz, I think you might have misunderstood me.

    As mentioned in my earlier post, the mindset that I bear is really to do justice and to honour the beret, not because I want recognition or people to know that I'm a commando.

    I believe that if I i'm entering the course just for the sake of getting the beret or to gain the respect of my peers, I probably will not make it through the first 3 months.

    I believe what matters most is to really understand the history and tradition behind the beret, to know that so many people have selflessly given themselves to the beret.

    You know, I'll be lying if I said i'm not afraid of sustaining a long term injury. I have a bad knee and an injured back. I may even be the first recruit to be OOC to be honest.

    But injuries are really inevitable if you think about it, big or small, you can't really run from it.

    That being said, I think it's really more important to go through the course not for the sake of getting the beret, but really to understand and respect what the seniors have been through, to appreciate the rigour, and to hopefully have the aptitude to earn the mark of a commando.

    I believe there's really so much more to it than acquiring the beret for respect. I say that because when I listen to stories shared by ex commandos (local or foreign), they don't really care about how nice the berets look on their heads nor the amount respect they get from people simply because they are commandos. But really, what has stuck with them most, was the blood, sweat and tears that they have shed in their time, and how ordinary men, like you and me, would unconditionally sacrifice themselves in times of war and terror, just so their love ones would be able sleep peacefully in the night.

    I mean, what do you say to that really? Personally I cannot be more thankful enough for such men who risk their lives for the safety of ours.
