    hello snk86,
    agreed with eac, it sound much more like rhinitis.
    I usually prescribe my patients something more definitive than those you mentioned, which are only good for controlling symptoms (if at all) temporarily.

    My best answer to your question is to:
    1. identify possible allergens (allergy-causing sources) in your environment - e.g. dust, pollen from flowers; and avoid them
    2. those medications you mentioned above
    3. more targeted treatment - you'll need to see a doctor for this, but the most effective treatment are steroidal nasal sprays. I prescribe these to my respiratory patients and they find the most relief with this.
    * some people are worried about steroid use, but my answer is that nasal steroids have much less side-effects than oral steroids, and the effect is mainly local - at the nose. Better than going around looking like Rudolf the red-nosed reindeer every single morning!
