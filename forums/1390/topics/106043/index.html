    this is mostly due to convenience - our name tags have the initials of our first names, and our surname, so naturally, A B TAN will be called Tan, which sounds more suitable than calling the person A B rite? but of course, some of us have more unique name tags with only christian names and such.
    Actually, calling by surname is also informal as far as the military is concerned; it's the same culture for foreign armed forces. Remember the "Tour of Duty" TV series? The enlisted characters called each other "Taylor", "Purcell", "Horn" (the RTO), "Johnson", et al.

    As for calling by full name - presumably for only Chinese - IMO it's no different than how the Koreans, Taiwanese or PLA Army troops address each other, even with buddies. It's part of the general Oriental accent: an underlying self-awareness that many hailed from different parts of the country, & thus politesse is accentuated as they began to train together. It's the same with the S'pore context, less so geographically but the initial unfamiliarity is still there (the contemporary "connectivity" within the island only came with the MRT system).

    Wait till you reach reservist. My own experience was that even the men-officers were on a surname basis - at most, they'd address the junior officers by their appointment or, more generically, by rank ("El-Tee"). The senior officers were addressed mostly by appointment, sometimes funnily like the MO being nicknamed "The Doctor" cuz no self-respecting LTA was willing to acknowledge his CPT rank! Only the CO was regularly addressed as "sir", except by his driver who called him by surname!
