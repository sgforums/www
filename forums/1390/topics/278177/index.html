    You're using the wrong label. Reservist cycle means the whole thing: still 13 years, but now split into a "10 plus 3" (ICT & BIT respectively) cycle.

    If I interpret correctly, you're referring more to a ICT window. Well, there's no strict rule that says MUST do one in-camp in a certain workyear, like for IPPT. Just that if you already kena one call-up, then you're safe from another ICT within the same workyear (April to April).

    Sometime during the last decade, they also introduced a new policy: any ops-ready reservist who deferred an ICT must do a make-up (with another unit) within the same workyear, as much as possible. Makes sense in the long run, if you think about it.

    There's generally no insistence that reservists MUST serve a selected number of low & high-keys either. So why not take advantage of the low-key call-ups & "clear" that year? BTW Sometimes a unit, with solid test results, can get rewarded too: either having their next high-key ICT becoming a low-key one or, more usually, getting no reservist call-ups at all during the next low-key year.

    Okay, I went digging ... Found an oldie article that announced the revamped cycle.

    NS has been the bedrock of Singapore's peace and prosperity for the past 34 years of our history as an independent state. For a small state like Singapore, our survival depends on the ability of our NSmen to go into action within a very short time. After all, the bulk of the SAF's strength comes from our NSmen who fight shoulder-to-shoulder with the regulars in the defence of Singapore.

    FromR TONY TAN KENG YAM DPM AND MINISTER FOR DEFENCE MARCH 2001

    Under the Enlistment Act, Chapter 93 of the Singapore Statutes 1992, NSmen will continue to fulfil your military obligations up to the age of 40 for NS Warrant Officers, Specialists and Enlistees (WOSEs), and 50 for Officers.

    National Service Training Cycle

    NSmen will be required to undergo a national service training cycle, which comprises several 'high key' and 'low key' training. 'High key' or intensive training involves operations and in-camp training, which last for seven days or longer. 'Low Key' training refers to training duration of six days or less.

    Completion of the Training Cycle

    Upon completion of your NS training cycle, you will be posted to the MINDEF Reserve (MR) List. While in MR, you may still be called up for national service, as you remain liable for national service up to the age of 50 for Officers and 40 for WOSEs. You are to disregard your previous unit's mobilisation codewords as you are not required to report for mobilisation activation for your previous units. Should you be required to be mobilised while in MR, you will be informed of your codewords accordingly. However, you are still required to abide by the existing exit controls up to your statutory age limit. Please see the section on Overseas Travel for information regarding exit control requirements.

    REVISED NATIONAL SERVICE TRAINING SYSTEM (WITH EFFECT FROM APR 03)

    Revised Training Cycle

    With effect from April 2003, a revised National Service (NS) training system has been introduced for Operationally Ready National Servicemen (NSmen).

    Under the previous NS training system, NSmen had to perform 13 years of NS training, of which, at least nine must be high-key in-camp training (ICT). With the revised NS training system, the majority of NSmen will only need to serve at least seven high-key ICTs. A two-day Basic Individual Training (BIT) programme that focuses on physical fitness, marksmanship and refresher training has also been introduced in the last three years of their 13 years of NS training. This ensures that NSmen maintain their currency in basic soldiering skills. Mobilisation exercises will also be conducted during the BIT phase to maintain operational readiness.

    To minimise inconvenience to NSmen pursuing full-time local university studies, specific periods during the university vacation have been set aside for NS training. No academic programmes are conducted during these periods. This arrangement enables NSmen to better manage their academic studies and NS duties.

    Training Improvements

    Performance-oriented training has been introduced as part of the revised NS training system. Through a modular approach, training is customised to focus on the specific training needs of each NS unit instead of adhering to a standard training syllabus. This achieves more effective and purposeful utilisation of training time.

    The revised NS training system also incorporates an incentive-based component to motivate NSmen to put in their best. Those who achieve good results at the individual or unit level need not undergo additional training. For example, NS units that perform well in major evaluation exercises could have a subsequent high-key training replaced by low-key training.

    Conclusion

    Over the years, NS training has progressively become more effective and efficient with improvements in technology and administrative efficiency. The revised NS training system capitalises on these improvements to effect a reduction in the number of high-key ICTs and the introduction of BIT, without compromising the operational readiness of the Singapore Armed Forces.

    Our citizen soldiers have to shoulder the multiple demands of family, work and duty to country. We recognise that this requires significant commitment and sacrifice. The Ministry of Defence continually reviews the NS training system to optimise training time and ensure a meaningful NS experience. The revised NS training system is the latest of a series of improvements to NS management and administration, following the increase in NS allowance in July 2002 and the revised Individual Physical Proficiency Test (IPPT) system in April 2003.

    Thinking back -- I was under the old system -- we rank & file were never told which year was HK or LK anyway. My first half-dozen ICTs in the first 10 years were all for between 14 to 27 days. Quite grueling.

    The confusion, if any, occurred with my subsequent call-ups (all for only a week or so) with only a "ORD"-style range shoot & the usual IPPTs the "serious" military activities. Most the time were taken up by long-service award ceremonies; COC (from combat-arms to PDF command) & then finally stand-down parades; KAHs' recycling-on news; etc. Plenty of wayang & handshaking, as my unit winded down. Even the token refreshers' training itself was half-hearted, with the Arty formation already moving on to new equipment & its trainers must consult old manuals before "instructing" us!
