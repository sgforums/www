    Witnesses say commando trainees were terrified during dunking exercise
    By Dominique Loh, Channel NewsAsia

    SINGAPORE : Captain Huo Wan Ho, who was nearly drowned by the commando trainers during the ill-fated Combat Survival Course, was screaming in fear and gasping for air as he was being dunked.

    Third Sergeant Ong Chin Wei who was helping with the course said this on day 7 of the trial of four commando trainers.

    The four are accused of nearly drowning the captain and causing the death of another trainee, Second Sergeant Hu Enhuai.

    Sergeant Ong was the enemy in the survival course and Captain Ho Wan Huo was his prisoner of war.

    The trainees were made to line up to wait their turn for the water treatment.

    And Sergeant Ong says the commando instructors picked Captain Ho from the back of the line and brought him to the front.

    The sergeant's task was to escort Captain Ho to the water tank to be interrogated by the commandos.

    Third Sergeant Ong Chin Wei is the second witness to testify what he saw during the water treatment phase of the course.

    He had only seen the water treatment on Captain Ho.

    He said what he saw had frightened him.

    He had also demonstrated in court using a dummy and a water tub how Captain Ho was immersed during the water treatment.

    The sergeant testified that Captain Ho was blindfolded, his hands tied behind his back - and was then dunked 6 times.

    The commando instructors, he said, seemed very enthusiastic.

    In stark contrast Captain Ho, he said was very frightened and was screaming and gasping for air.

    The sergeant, who is a former classmate of Hu En Huai - the trainee who died - however did not see the commandos dunk Hu.

    The court also heard that this is not the first dunking death in the military - there was another case back in 1976. - CNA

    Dunking survivors testify in trial of commandos

    SINGAPORE: Captain Ho Wan Huo has testified in the trial of the four commandos accused of nearly drowning the captain and causing the death of another trainee, Second Sergeant Hu Enhuai, during a combat survival course.
    On day 8 of the trial, the court also heard dramatic accounts from three other trainees about what the commando trainers did to them.

    Captain Ho said he was dunked more than six times in the water tub by the commando trainers.

    He said he was held in a wheel-barrow position, his head and upper body in the water tub while the commandos held his legs up.

    Testifying in court, Captain Ho used a dummy to demonstrate how his entire body slid into the tub up to his ankles as he struggled violently for air.

    The course was to prepare the trainees for the treatment they might receive as prisoners of war.

    The commando trainers had used the dunking to force the trainees to reveal secrets and other personal details like their rank.

    Captain Ho told the court: "I could not take it anymore and decided to reveal my actual rank, that I was a Captain. I heard the instructors cheering and doing 'hi-fives'. I was left in the tub and I crawled out of the tub on my own."

    Captain Ho was later sent to the Singapore General Hospital where he was warded for seven days for near drowning.

    Two other trainees had even more chilling accounts of what happened.

    3rd Sergeant Gary Han said he was dunked three times.

    Twice he was held underwater for at least 20 seconds and ended up breathing in water through his nose.

    "I felt the dip was very scary and death crossed my mind, at one point in time I felt I was going to die," 3rd Sergeant Han said.

    Another trainee, 3rd Sergeant Tan Li Thong who was dunked 4 or 5 times, told the court: "I felt traumatized as I could hardly breathe and I was choking because I had inhaled water through my mouth and nose. After the water treatment, I was panting and weeping under my blindfold."

    The trainees were asked if they knew they had the option to surrender and give up information in order to stop the water treatment.

    They said they knew.

    But they also believed if they broke down under interrogation, they would in effect fail the course and may have to repeat the course starting from day one. - CNA

    Personally, I have mixed feelings about this. As an instructor, I need to simulate as close to reality as possible. As a trainee, I am scared shitless if the same happens to me, even in a training scenario.

    My guess is no one made any effort in doing additional research in measuring the average human reaction to dunking, from the pre-dunking psyche and how it affects behaviors under water, what are the various reactions during the time of submersion and from there list out clearly what are the dos and donts in handling trainees in this exercise.

    The high-fives and pat-on-the-back can be perceived in 2 ways. Firstly, it is want will likely happen if the enemy breaks you, providing that extra psychological stress in the form of mockery and shame on the trainee's psyche. If this is the case, the instructors should not be penalised.

    On the other hand, the exercise might have mutated and evolved into an internal competition among the instructors to see who can break the trainee faster OR whose "way" is the most effective. If it is the latter, so much for professionalism. In this context, they should be punished.

    My suggestion to the SAF is to review the high risk lessons, conduct more primary research on them (real scientific observations and measurements rather than just gathering opinions from instructors and trainees) and develop a better script for the future. I am sure that if I am unsure of the what is described in the lesson plan, going to my superior for clarity will still yield me an ambiguous answer.

    But the fundamental issue is, do we expect the same level of standards between conscripts and regulars? Yes, ideally we always say no compromise. But under this macro rule, some degree of wastage (pardon my language and all due respect to those who are hurt on both sides by this incident) will bound to happen. It is just a matter of probability.

    In any case, if it is related to ambiguity of the doctrine, then SAF is to be blamed. If it has to come to this, lets face up to the blame as an organization instead of putting the instructors in a civil court and hope this will go away forever. It wont as long as the fundamentals remain flawed.

    To be fair, this story is becoming to sound like a murder case in a civilian court, tried by civilians and reported by a civilian media.
