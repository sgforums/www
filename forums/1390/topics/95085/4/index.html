    Dunking 'okay' if done safely, says ex-instructor
    By Selina Lum

    COMBAT survival trainees undergoing mock interrogation should not be dunked in water, according to the training guide, but a former instructor yesterday conceded that dunking was 'okay', as long as it was done safely.



    Warrant Officer Anbarasan Velu (above), who had helped to review the lesson plan for the course in 1998, was testifying in the trial of four commandos accused of rashly causing the death of Second Sergeant Hu Enhuai.

    The 19-year-old full-time national serviceman died after he was repeatedly dunked into a tub of water during combat survival training at Pulau Tekong on Aug 21 last year.

    Another soldier who was dunked, Captain Ho Wan Huo, 26, a regular, was severely injured.

    The four accused commandos are Capt Pandiaraj Mayandi, 33; Lieutenant Ng Chin Fong, 27; Lt Divanandhari Ambat Chandrasekharan, 28; and WO S. Balakrishnan, 45.

    Yesterday, WO Anbarasan, the first witness who has conducted the course, told a district court that instructors should not have physical contact with trainees except when leading them to the stations.

    Trainees should only be sprayed with water or have water poured on them, and at no time should their heads be dunked into water, he said.

    In fact, to make it clear to instructors, when he and WO Alex Quah reviewed the guide, they included a picture to show a trainee lying on the ground being sprayed with water.

    But during cross-examination, defence lawyer Selva K. Naidu pointed out that the lesson plan states the trainees should be 'doused with cold water during questioning'.

    According to the Oxford Concise Dictionary 9th Edition, he said, 'douse' can either mean 'to plunge into water' or 'to throw water over'.

    Which method was the witness referring to, he asked.

    WO Anbarasan replied: 'Either way would be okay, provided safety was not compromised.'

    Mr Naidu, who is acting for Pandiaraj, referred to the combat survival training management programme, which states that the training must be 'tough', 'demanding' and 'replicate actual battle conditions'.

    In the light of this, asked the lawyer, which of the two meanings better fit the requirements laid down in the programme. WO Anbarasan said he did not know.

    Further questioned by Mr Naidu, he said he was not aware of any combat survival trainee - prior to 2nd Sgt Hu's group, the 80th batch - being plunged into water.

    He disagreed with Mr Gurcharanjit S. Hundal when the lawyer said that he was, in fact, one of two warrant officers who had taught Divanandhari how to dunk trainees.

    The warrant officer had told the court that when he conducted the mock interrogations between 1994 and 1999, he used a long hose attached to a water tap to spray trainees who were in a kneeling position.

    Mr Naidu also asked WO Anbarasan if he knew there were no taps at Kampung Permatang, where the fateful training was conducted. He replied: 'There are no taps.'

    The trial continues.

    Accused commando trainers did not follow training plan: chief instructor
    By S Ramesh, Channel NewsAsia

    SINGAPORE : The current chief instructor at the School of Commandos has testified that the four commando trainers accused of causing the death of a trainee did not follow the approved training plan.

    Major Yeo Hock Seng says the commando trainers should never have interrogated the prisoner of war trainees individually.

    Advertisement

    Second Sergeant Hu Enhuai died after he was dunked repeatedly.

    Major Yeo testified that during the water treatment phase of the combat survival course, the trainee prisoners of war are supposed to be divided into two groups, and then sprayed with a hose as a group.

    So when the commando chief instructor was shown pictures of trainees being taken one-by-one to the square tub of water during the ill-fated course, Major Yeo told the court that what the trainers had done did not tally with the approved lesson plan.

    "There is a potential for man-handling. There is a potential for water to be forced ... and there is a potential for individual prisoners of war to be targeted," he said.

    The School of Commando's chief instructor told the court that its not necessary that everything that is being taught in the classroom for the trainees undergoing the prisoner of war lessons need to be executed on the ground.

    He said when it comes to carrying out the training on the ground, the trainees' safety remains paramount and this should not be compromised.

    Major Yeo also told the court that if the training area did not have a tap so the trainees could be doused using a hose, then the lesson should have been moved.

    He said there are two other locations on Pulau Tekong, where he had conducted combat survival training, which had taps.
