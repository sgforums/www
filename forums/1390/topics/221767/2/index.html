    Threadstarter,

    I think you should heed Ged's words quite seriously.

    Since you seem not too averse to military life, according to your insistence that others read in between the lines, here's some advice for Army life.

    Leave the equivocation out of your daily interactions with your commanders and peers when it comes to training.It helps.

    No one wants to have to interpret what you have to say and spend valuable time doing so when it could mean time spent on training and, consequently time saved overall.

    What a good soldier/officer should have is calmness under pressure, a clear and fast mind that does not tolerate extraneous BS from any angle. I may not be an officer, but I've trained officer cadets, and their usual mistake and flaw is the assumption that they are always right, and a huge case of ego that usually will manifest itself negatively when a brouhaha happens.

    Lead by example, admit your mistakes and rectify them promptly.

    That's all.
