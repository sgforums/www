  • Unsung_Hero

    Yes, I am a lao jiao who used to wear Temasek green. In fact I was in the last BMT batch to be issued it in 1982. The BMT intake after me got the original camo which had a rubber lining inside (meant to make it a bit waterproof) -- and was hot and itchy as hell -- not to mention the rubber degraded quickly -- it was like wearing a pair of rubber gloves without the powder inside.

    My batch wore Temasek green and were permitted to exchange only Temasek green for the same (SAF was trying to use up inventory and our CSMs insisted if one had temasek green we all had temasek green) from BMT until we passed out from OCS. Man, were we jealous of the OCS batch behind us in camo -- looked so sloppy marching around in unstarched uniforms -- and lucky pricks didn't have all the work involved in maintaining them -- scholar batch somemore! Still the Encik's in those days tried to make some standards persist but it just didn't look right so they dropped any pretense after a while.

    The Temasek green was no joke. Yes the pants and shirt scould stand up by themselves due to starch -- but just for parade uniforms -- you walked up and down stairs like a robot with no joints to prevent any creases prior to parade inspection -- of course once you drilled on the road/square for even 15 mins the perfect look was gone. Most had to book in early every Sunday afternoon/evening just to spray starch and iron uniforms as well as polish combat and leather drill boots with the metal studs in them -- in those days the rich ones could send extra uniforms (bought at Beach road, I think we were only issued with 6 sets of no 4, 2 had to be in the full pack at all times for standard exercise and turnout) to special laundry for starching -- some of those uncle/aunty shops in-camp would even do it.

    You always maintained at least one drill set heavily starched (most people had two) cos the tekan for having a bad uniform was not worth the price of buying an extra one. Drilling in those uniforms with sweat running into the starch led to skin problems for many. On top of that all the formation insignia etc, were cloth/cardboard and pinned to the uniform with metal studs or pins either on the collar or the sleeves, plus the plastic name tag -- had to remove and clean them separately or keep buying new ones...bascially designed to limit your downtime to meaningless hours keeping your uniform to stand-by and drill standards. The field no 4 was only lightly starched or just well ironed at the start, by the time you put on your sbo or fbo and fell-in or loaded the 3-tonners it didn't matter. I do remember some unfortunate souls who ran out of clean uniforms in camp and were forced to wear parade no 4's into the field -- especially in weeks when weekends were burnt and no time to wash uniforms in-camp. Our recruit pay was $90 a month and our junior cadet pay was $110, maybe $150 as senior cadets.

    I was probably the last SAFTI instructor to wear temasek green until I ROD'd (ORD now). In fact I wore it to my first ICT years later and my RSM almost fainted -- good thing i was the S2 so he could only complain to CO, hehe. I still have two sets of original temasek green in my "collection", along with my tin pot helmet, sbo and all other fbo items minus M16 of course. My webbing is also the original khaki cloth webbing and not the nylon webbing/pouches which were introduced around 1984. But I just had to trade in the old khaki fullpack for the nylon one which was much more waterproof and lighter when wet.

    Looking at the pictures of us in temasek green makes me and my batch mates feel very old now, haha, but we were the batch that bridged the old ways and the new ways in terms of uniforms anyway -- well that, the introduction of the SAW, LAW, SAR80, and the change in SAF philosophy from being "rugged" to "people oriented" -- but those are other stories.

    1967: DETAILING OF A BEGINNING



    The SAF supplied its very own set of clothing to replace the old pattern drill uniforms used by the British on 1 September 1967. Made from Â“Temasek Green cotton drillÂ”, the uniforms of 1967 only had 2 patched pockets for the shirt, and 3 pockets for the trousers, with the uniform always to be worn tucked in.

    There were very minute details to observe. The dress specifications stated, for example, that Â“the first button on the shirt had to be 3.75 to 4 inches from the collar downÂ”. The uniform material itself had to pass certain standards: the trouser thread had to be of a specific tensile strength, and the buttons were not supposed to soften or turn colour when in contact with water or heated to 80 degrees Celsius.

    1979: TEMASEK GREEN II



    The uniform was changed in November 1979. Retaining its name of Â“Temasek GreenÂ”, the uniform was a darker green and now had 4 instead of 2 pockets. In July 1981, the uniform was standardised to be both the No.3 and the No.4 dress. As a No. 3 dress, it was to be worn tucked into the trousers, and as a No. 4 dress, it was to be worn tucked out.

    1983: A DRESS REHEARSAL

    The first camouflage dress was originally introduced in June 1983. However, there were immediate complaints on the poor colourfastness of the new uniforms. The SAF thus had to delay replacing the old Â“Temasek GreenÂ” uniforms with the new ones and instead conducted further studies on different materials to use for the uniform before selecting in 1985 a new material which was lighter in weight and had more air flow permeability.

    In a few years, the SAF ran into another problem with the uniform: due to its low price, availability, durability and the Â“RamboÂ” image it portrayed, the uniform became fashionable and popular wear with outdoor campers, construction workers and youth alike. It was feared that the misuse of the uniform could tarnish the image of the SAF. Thus in 1988 the SAF warned that offenders wearing the uniform could be fined up to $400 or jailed up to three months under the Decorations and Uniforms Act of 1922.
