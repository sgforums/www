    For the record, MINDEF has promoted closer ties between the school units & SAF formations under the NCC 21 Masterplan (from, I believe, 1998 or 1999 onwards). It's already a milestone to see your HQ being located inside Amoy Quee camp, as my recce team saw when we last drove around inside there.

    I don't deny such uniformed school-cadets being better physically & mentally prepared for military service for the various branches. You guys do have a headstart for the Best PT or Best Marksmanship awards, if not Best Soldier, during BMT. That said, it's not an entitlement &, if anything, it's also a tradition for BMT instuctors to accentuate the NCC as an antithesis instead - i.e. that Tekong is the "real deal", & not just an extra-curricular activity. If a recuit has the quality, he'll naturally emerge past the tekan-ing.

    The other negativity the average SAF soldier has for the NCC counterpart is - as evidently suggested by the posts here - the common sharing of the camoflague uniforms. On the one hand, IMO we haven't quite moved on from the generation that still sees the school cadets in Temasek Green. On the other, it's also downright off-putting to see a lot of cadets-in-uniforms lack an military cutting or bearing: from haircuts (touching collar or ear or past eyebrow), to appropriately-folded sleeves (for smart No #4), to the dragging of boots, to even opening up an umbrella when raining (try doing that in front of a grouchy RSM)!

    You can't really blame our NSF's either, cuz the armed-forces culture everywhere is such that you have to toil to initially earn the PTE rank, otherwise recruits wouldn't need the POP parade. Believe me, you'll feel the same affront after you've gone through your own BMT, with proper SAF indoctrination & eradication of "civilian" habits.

    [FYI I don't mind 16-year olds with "senior NCO" rank as much - it certainly beats the NPCC or scout version with one arm massively overloaded with badges & awards!]
  • Moxie
    9 Sep 04, 17:50
    Appendum: The NCC/Temasek Green days also saw an arguably stricter sense of military dress. The shirts had to be starched (sometimes overstarched to the extent that white streaks showed post-ironing) & tucked in, the belt buckles must be well-polished, the trousers suitably tailored if they're too loose, & the boots spit-shined. In fact, so prevalent was the fear of premature apparel wrinkling that the cadets preferred bringing them to school in hangers - & only wear them prior to forming up.

    The desired effect was mostly cosmetic, but the resultant "kilat" impression also signal to the rest of the school - as well as the general public - how dedicated the cadets were on individual sense of discipline & decorum.

    Of course, it's usually mothers who ended up ironing the uniforms ~lol~ but I think the emphasis on primp attire also permitted many bonding sessions between the different secondary batches on late-afternoon, post-ECA Saturdays. It's during those occasions that tips on personal maintainence were passed down - eg. lightly Kiwi-ing the boots after cleaning 'em - & ditto the more bawdy cadences/songs.

    Thereafter everyone headed straight home. By contrast, nowadays you see occasional baggy-attired NCC cadets from neighborhood schools hanging out in town centers during the early evening.
