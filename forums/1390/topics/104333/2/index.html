    27 Nov 04, 16:31
    Returning from Ex Lancer was very unforgettable for me - a huge culture shock (wasn't prepared for it) & significant fitness improvement (didn't realize it).

    Imagine being 5 weeks away from civilization (plus spending R&R doing area-cleaning in another camp), & then suddenly boarding a SQ plane & seeing those Singapore Girls ... Man, talk about being self-conscious with 600+ pair of eyes oogling at them!

    I was awashed with emotions during the landing approach, then - most vivid of all - felt completely alienated after clearing customs. The world outside was functioning as per normal, as if my epiphanic super-siong jungle training was for naught. Worse, my unit didn't organize any transport - turned out they had no idea when I'll be back! Talk about reverse-AWOL!

    Left improvising on my own, I had to roundabout-phone my unit (didn't have its phone no. with me) & apply verbally for emergency leave. The disbelieving COS wanted me to head back to camp to clarify my status (he thought I was posted out for good); the battery was out in the field & he didn't wanna take responsibility. I cut him off "urgently" - first time I unequivocally disobeyed the chain of command - &, clutching my Ali-baba bag, ran searching for the 3-tonner transporting the other attached personnel. They agree to tompang me, the dropped me off along the PIE near my home in Tampines. Slept for 14 hours straight.

    Everyone commented how "harder" I looked when I returned to camp - weight loss, sunken cheeks, dazed look - but things didn't sink in until I ran the SOC in 8:32 a few days later. That's an improvement by 40+ seconds & I wasn't even panting heavily like usual.

    During BMT fieldcamp, rained 7 out of 7 days. on the 5th day night, due to some delay we reached the new camp site only at 11pm, the weather looked clear so our sergeants think we need not pitch basha since it was late. we laid ground sheet and slept.

    at 3am+, it rained heavily with high winds blowing, i was in reclaimed land, we really shivered cold and can't sleep. Slowly, we congregated and lit all the remainining solid fuel we had left.... also cooking milo and other hot drinks. we gathered round the fire and passed the mess mugs of hot drinks around. we suffered together but in the suffering still could find comfort among us.

    In taiwan during their winter time, the moment we arrive it was sunny but when we reached camp, it just became cloudy and rainy and remained so for the next 2 weeks. During Navex exercise, we braved the rain and cold, though tired we cannot stop as it would mean bringing our body temperature down. Our uniforms are wet at night and sleeping meant shivering all night long. Once again, i was with the same friends that suffered thru the BMT fieldcamp. again, we cooked the combat rations and hot drinks with loads of oranges given by locals. haha.

    Once again, we forge very strong friendship despite testing times.

    When we had our non-tact break after 2 weeks, the sun appeared again it was warm dry. We are so happy to have felt warmth after 2 weeks that we ran out of bunk to their parade square to bask in the sun. haha.

    u took an SQ flight to lancer? and you sounded like you went there all by yourself.
    Huh? There's usually 3 ways of aerial commute for servicemen: via the C130, via commercial plane "by nominal roll" (i.e. back gate) or, more rarely, via commercial plane by passport.

    I flew commercial (SIA & China Airlines) to Starlight twice later, both "by nominal roll" with the rest of the unit. The infantry BN did the same for Lancer, except for we attached personnel & some senior commanders who checked in normally - with passports - for the same flight. FYI Full access to duty-free shopping during the return trip too, unlike the men who had to order liquor & cigarettes through their RSM.

    My attached Arty group was a hodgepodge of regulars/NSFs (a FSO & TA from W SA, a TA from X SA, a FO from Y SA, plus a full FSO team from Z SA) & I only knew one of them from my SOAY-course days. So, yeah, I guess there was a lot of autonomy. The only thing my unit did was to get a Land Rover to drive me to, inexplicably, the rendezvous camp that was furthest away from Changi Airport! ~lol~

    In fact, I ended up so "latchkey-ed" that I had to fight entirely on my own for the owed off-in-lieu's. The senior officer of that Arty group - one of 2 regular CPTs - verbally agreed to back up everyone's claim for 9.5 days' off (5 Saturdays & 5 Sundays) but failed to follow up on returning. I pleaded in vain for my BC to contact him, while my BC in turn sought to bargain down to 2 days as a "magnanimous internal solution". Things was left in limbo until the unit ORD'd &, as the old bird, I was able to "manipulate" the newly posted-in officers into honoring the quota ~wink~
