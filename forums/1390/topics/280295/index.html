    Combat life is simpler, particularly if posted to the company line. The unit will be training for REDCON status (twice during NS, & also every reservist ICT in the future), so the field training is usually structured around passing the proficiency tests -- first at the company & then the battalion level. They'll take a few months to achieve, or kena re-test. Thereafter, the unit becomes ops-ready & will rotate with other active units to do weekly standby. There are also chances to go overseas, or train locally alongside other foreign armed forces. Sometimes a unit gets arrowed to do other non-military stuff too, like NDP or blind-clearance in the Live Firing Area.

    Some BMT activities like IPPT, SOC, ATF (range shooting), BCCT (if applicable), guard duty, area cleaning etc. will continue with the unit. But no more stand-by-beds, unless it's a tekan session. IMO You can really become self-independent in unit life, in everything from buying a pail to do your own laundry (instead of bringing home ala BMT); to bartering with your Encik on when best to clear your off-in-lieus; to booking out regularly during nights-off to "wind down" (so much so I didn't mind burning holidays or Saturdays -- but not Sundays -- doing guard duty).

    If you're with a frontline unit, you'd better get along with your buddies too. As I told my old mono-intake batch, most of you guys are going to be together for the next 10-plus years including reservist -- unless you're posted out due to studies (NS-disruption or reposting to IHL unit), medical downgrading (only the most serious cases) or overseas-based work (reservist deferment or holding list). So get used to each other.

    I left out mob manning, which was always nervy even though we only did it on weekends in NS. Standby duty, I should also add, was done at the COY (or equivalent) level. But the unit can usually see the assignment rotate back to it in every 3 weeks or so, hence regular internal preparation (like T-loaning each other stuff to meet the logistics SOP).

    I dunno whether it's changed through the years, but one big difference between unit & BMT life was that recruits in my time weren't allowed to see the training programme (& were too sotong to ask) They just lived day-to-day, as a result.

    But, at the unit, a fortnight's (sometimes up to a month's) timetable was usually displayed on the notice board, prompting many to consult it to see if they could bend the system their way. Usually cannot "siam" important activities like SOC & field EX, but chao kengers could try to see about scheduling medical appointments on less-important mornings, while the aquaphobic would plan to volunteer for guard duty instead of going for swimming, etc. For the others, they would just check when the nights-off were or whether they could apply for a long weekend, etc.

    basically service life is slack, without mentioning the frequency of regimental duties you have to cover. other than that, its still okay. life will get kinda boring. if you are a storeman, life is sianz. if ure a clerk, life is full of paper work. and yes, office politics come into play. if you're attached to a combatant unit and stay in, thats it. your life is also damn boring. ok, at least you get to watch those combatants chiong sua.
