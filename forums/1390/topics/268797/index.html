    Let me give a lowdown since I'm in this position.

    All Ministries and stat boards follow the civil service guidelines anally (in a sense) so dun expect any flexibility.

    As an ex-regular, you will not be given any NS-increments when you are hired by any of these government organisations since you have been deemed to have been "employed" during your NS period. You can consider yourself lucky if the organisation chooses to give you increment for your "work experience" during that period instead.

    If you further your studies and gain a degree after resigning from the military, all your previous "work experience" (including positions other than the military) will not be considered at all, hence you will be employed as one "without NS" and without working experience. In other words, start from scratch.

    You're thinking too linear-ly, too much in terms of vocational trade. Also a bit antiquated since MINDEF already has a number of recent schemes in place to help ex-regulars switch to a new job outside. I first noticed them after the economic recession & SARS epidemic earlier this decade, then discovered the initiative has been around since 1998. To summarize briefly:-

    1998 - MINDEF partnered with RMC (Right Management Consultants) to help its ex-officers. This is now coordinated by the Career Transition Branch, under SAFPSC.
    2004 - RSAF signed MOU with AAIS (Association of Aerospace Industries).
    2005 - RSN signed MOU with ASMI (Association of Singapore Maritime Industries).
    2005 - SAF signed MOU with ASPRI (Association of Process Industry), to help its ex-technical WOSPECs.

    So, from my observations: yes & no. Those who "blindly" sought civilian employment on their own instead (for various reasons) found it tough, esp. for middle-management jobs. Hence, you'll see them becoming insurance or property agents, security executives, enterpreneurs, etc.

    Ex-combat WOSPECs, as a whole, also visibly lack a safety net for now. They can't look forward to being disciplinary masters in primary schools forever ;-) but I can see either them or the Police ex-regulars staking the security industry one day, once better professionalism is in place & a proper Association set up. I can see them fitting into the enviromental health industry too if ever the NEA seeks to promote (& outsource) more civilian businesses to work alongside the agency.

    BTW Ex-regulars are quite highly demanded for senior-management jobs, but more so with employers seeking appropriate organizational & supervisory capabilities -- for example, in logistics, transportation, charities, education (curriculum planning), human resource, enviromental health, etc.

    Back to topic: The Civil Service is not for me, but their wages bad, meh? Their total variable component payout last year was 2.7 months' pay plus a one-off $220 (2.15 months plus $200 in 2005), on top of their individual performance bonus (graded by their superior). With a entry-level Poly grad earning $1,500 monthly on the lowest Grade V scale, I work out him/her to have received a total bonus of at least $4,250 for the whole of 2006.
