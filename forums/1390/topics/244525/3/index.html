    Originally posted by chanjyj:
    I was referring to the 120mm round coming out of tanks (like our upcoming leopards), not the motar.... was wondering why 155mm arty round casues so much more damage than a 120mm tank round.
    When one talks about damage, then the items being damaged have to be mentioned. A tank round is a direct fire ammunition which other than the chemical energy (HE round tank round is converted into heated molten jet) is used against an armored target, the kinetic energy of the round (or molten metal jet) also contributes to the penetration. If an APFSDS round is used (with a tungsten rod penetrator), then the penetration effect is due mainly to kinetic energy. For a tank round, the objective is to penetrate and destroy and is used against an armored target.

    An artillery round, generally, is used to neutralize an area, targets will mainly be unprotected infantry and soft-skinned vehicles. The fragmentation effect of the round or spread of sub-munitions (bomblets) will be used for the area damage.

    Since intended targets and objectives are different indirect fire artillery and direct fire tank rounds are used differently.

    Of course, currently, it is possible for IR sensing bomblets of an artillery cargo round to destroy armored vehicles (from top when armor tends to be thinnest). Direct hit of a 155 mm round on the tank will also cause disastrous effect to the vehicle.

    Well I read this account of this paratrooper in WW2, when he and his friends came under arty fire they started digging foxholes in the frozen ground for cover, then another one of his friends ran over to a crater that had just impacted and started digging there, saying that it's unlikely that another round will impact there. The rest did not follow but by the time the arty had lifted that guy had dug twice as deep as the rest and was better covered.

    Of course it depends on the CEP of the guns... if the CEP is small enough then several rounds are going to impact very close to each other and even possibly overlap. Going to the spot where a shell then impacted then would be suicide as the other rounds are going to arrive very near by. In this case it would be best to get out of the area as quickly as possible instead of taking cover, especially if the shells are landing near by.

    If the CEP is extremely large- meaning the guns are firing at the max of their range and the rounds are impacting all over the shop... then a shell crater is probably quite safe to take cover in then being caught in the open.
