    I was referring to the killing of unarmed civilians by Armed combatants so the relevant statues will be:

    Geneva Conventions Act 1985 Common Article 3
    Geneva Conventions Act 1985 Additional Protocol Art 51

    If you are a commander and your subordinate kills unarmed civilians, you are also liable if you
    1) knew or ought to have known that those forces were committing or about to commit a crime; and
    2) failed to take all necessary and reasonable steps within his or her power to suppress the crime or cause it to be investigated and prosecuted
    (The above's legal source is the "Far East Tribunals. USA v Tomoyuki Yamashita", not statue. But international law's sources include more than just statutes.)

    If you are a soldier and you have been ordered by your superior to kill an unarmed civilian, please remember that you are only required to obey
    lawful orders and I can assure you that in Singapore, killing an unarmed civilian is not a lawful order.

    And the fact that a person who his alleged to have committed a breach of the Law of Armed Conflict has done so pursuant to the order o his or her government or superior does not relieve that peron of criminal responsiblility unless:
    1) the person was under a legal obligation to obey the orders in question; (meaning there must be a law in Singapore that actually say it is unlawful not to shoot an unarmed civilian)

    2) the person did not know that the order was unlawful; and

    3)the order was not manifesly unlawful

    An order is manifestly unlawful if it would appear to a person of ordinary sense and understanding to be clearly illegal. (I hope that killing an unarmed fellow Singaporean would appear to be manifestly unlawful to all who read this thread)

    The sources: Canadian case (R v Finta), Nuremberg Chart Art VIII, Control Council Law 10 Art 2(4)(b) ... just to name a few
