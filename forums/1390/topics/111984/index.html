    My brother signed with the RSAF after getting his NTU Honours degree, after serving as a QM when active. He's now a LTC. I believe he had to go through the equivalent of the Service-Wing & Professional Terms again (despite being comissioned), though in your case it should be only the lesser latter training, or some 23 weeks, with the School of Armour.

    As for gratuity, I think the Personnel Service Centre is a better source to brief you on with. But I'll say the SAF pay itself will be much better compared to the civil-service equivalent, with your headstart (as a commissioned officer) & a more rigid military hierarchy. The negative side for regulars is that you must perform enough to progressively climb in rank, or be labeled as "condemned" if you cannot get promoted so-&-so by a certain age.

    You'll probably make at least COL if you last that long into service-retireable age. Yes, there's some GIC-linked appointments that should be available but any viable job switch isn't 100% guaranteed (depending on circumstances & resume). You can always to choose strike out on your own, eg. heading NGOs rather than private sector.

    Once you attain MAJ, you'll probably be eligible to attend SCSC courses (& also do a Masters degree on your own) to prepare you for staff-level appointments at the BGE or DIV level. LHL wasn't always stuck in Artillery, neither was George Yeo in Signals - though albeit they're SAF scholars. Even if you're posted to Sungei Gedong, you'll probably be rich enough to afford a car as a signed-on CPT, lah.

    interesting. i was an infantry officer during ns too. the only difference is, i ord-ed, went to work for a year, and now, im back as an intelligence officer - at the age of 26. so u r not alone.

    the opportunity lost (and the one year of working experience which now count for nothing except for personal experiences) is something i learnt not to place too much emphasis on cos i re-enlisted because i believe that is what i am passionate in and will be happy to do. of course, its impractical to say that one can live on passion and happiness. but i thought i didnt lose much.

    so bottomline is, if you are sure the military is your cup of tea (and specifically armour) then age shouldnt be too much of a concern, even though it may mean you will have to ago through armour pro term as an officer. remember that doctors complete the rest of their NS liability (including MOCC and part os OCS) at this age too. of course it will be good if you can prepare yourself physically and mentally before going back.

    you will probably be rotated between armour units for your different tours, even going to OCS as instructor, or MINDEF for your staff tour.

    as for the SAVER scheme, i have yet to learn about the A to Z of it. it really isnt that high in my priority. but i can find out for you if you need the information. as for second careers, i believe no one owes us a living. so even now, i continue to learn and upgrade myself whenever i can. i believe in the end, our second careers will be very much up to us. taking into consideration our demographic trends and the inclination of our government to encourage the elderly to continue working, i dont see a major problem in this aspect.

    A word on future employment prospects for ex-regulars:

    The thing is once you survive into staff-officer training/appointment stage, your professional strength will be centered around planning & organization. Thus, any planned postgrad academic courses should bolster that, i.e. do Masters in Public Policy/Management/Admin (instead of in Marketing or Finance).

    That way when you leave the service, the NGOs & such headhunters will be receptive to your acquired skills. But you gotta decide early if this is your desired career orientation in the 1st place, cuz it'll be fooling yourself if you come out & plan to go into business or insurance or the ilk. The latter route would render you a complete novice to begin with, whatever your age.

    The biggest challenge to all regulars is the route they will take after they have ended their service with the SAF.Scholars have a much easier time as their ROA is usually catered for them to be fast-tracked into the upper echelons of GLCs or MINDEFs' Defence Groups.For the rest, it's worth noting that whatever military experience you possessed in the force, might not count for much when you seek employment in the private sector later in your eyes...So plan wisely, take up professional courses and be very sure of what you plan to do post-SAF when you are already a MAJ.Chances are, the COL ranks are the highest you might get and the timeframe would be at most 7 years therabouts.Look ahead, the iron bowl is not as huge as before.

    On the plus side, MEOs have it easier, since their work takes them to the fringes of the SAF and within the sphere of ST and DSTA, so the networking is much better there.
