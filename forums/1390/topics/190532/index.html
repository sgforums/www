    1. For every eat-snake medic that you see, remember that there are many other good medics who do their job as best as they can be it in the medical center or outfield.

    2. I can tell you that drawing of blood is not a skill which can be learnt in a textbook. It is not just about sticking a needle in and pulling a syringe. I can tell you how to do it right now over sgforums, but if I give you a needle and vacuum tube and ask you do try it on someone else, I'll bet dollars to donuts that you'll fail.

    Besides, different people have different physiological makeups. For some people their veins are closer to the surface, while for others it's further in. Some people have lots of valves in their veins. And not to mention hefty people who don't have very visible veins due to the fat.

    Besides, have you ever tried drawing blood samples from fifty guys within half a day? I have. It wasn't easy for me to keep my mind straight after that. You can't really blame the medic for missing the vein if he's tired.

    3. Once again, watch the tar and brush. Fair enough, there are medics who aren't committed to the cause and can't even remember basic first aid skills. I had one platoon medic who totally forgot EVERYTHING about POCM (Rockhound and other medics will know what's this) just prior to alert manning.

    I was forced to sacrifice one whole day of off in lieu just to come back to camp to give that bugger a crash course in casualty management.

    But for every medic like him who aren't conscientious, there are many others who do their job well.

    It would do you a whole lot of good to remember that.
