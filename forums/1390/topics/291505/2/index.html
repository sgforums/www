    I gather you are in the 17-18 year old range. Usually guys of that age group have got some pretty colourful ideas about being a fighter-pilot, with the A-level cohort particularly bent on being an officer for some strange reasons. Anyhow, let's take a closer look at these choices.

    First let's talk about being an officer. I do not know what you guys of the current generation are thinking of, but back then, you will hear guys on the basketball court bouncing their balls (basketball) while talking about wanting to shave their heads in preparation for enlistment..........

    Please, don't do that. That act says a lot about the fragile nature of their minds. Go ahead, dye your hair if you want to. When its time to shave it off, it will happen, no need to slowly wean yourself off all that crowning glory.

    The next topic will be OCS. They somehow think that having straight A's in A-levels is not good enough, they must headbutt their way into OCS by hook or by crook, or else their egos will take a beating. DO you know what being an officer means? It's not a permanent branding under your foot or something like that. It's just a role you need to play should you be selected for that period of time as an NSF, a small number of them do sign on and become captains eventually, but to carry on until that age of 45, that is really rare, and anything beyond that age, your chances of even having a contract offered to you is as good as being selected after several rounds of parading yourself on TV for star search. And this occurs in the Navy, Army and airforce. Talk about career decisions, unless you are born with blood on your palm like Genghis khan, I'd say its a high stakes gamble. After you finish your term as an officer during NSF.....what then? DOes it help in your career in the civilian world? Yes it does, to a certain extent depending on the nature of your choice of work, but don't look at it as a must. And if you do get your contract, by which time it means you may be ejected out at the age of 45, with no other work experience, balding, kids to feed, wife to please.......you wanna know what is happening to my friend who is a captain?

    Airforce fighterpilots......you know, the job's not all about flying, its about projects, projects, and more projects, lots of office politicking, none of that TOP GUN "oh I'm so tom cruise I'm going to pull a few G's and date my instructor" kind of stuff. Not even close. And if you do get close, be sure you clean up after being air-sick. See the exodus of flighter pilots to the airlines for a good measure of how much these guys actually love the job.

    I can go on and o, but my fingers are tired, and I need to catch a show. What you really need to do is speak to the people who are actually doing what you are doing, go for the older guys, and ask them what its really all about. If you need, I can intorduce a couple of them to you. WHo knows, you may actually have what it takes, and we all know what good connections mean.

    Happy enlistment. I enjoyed my NS, I hope you will too.

    the timeline for pilot training is about 2.5yrs

    after ur bmt, u cross over to airforce.

    give&take 1 or 2 months waiting period til end of airgrading.

    if u pass airgrading, u're up for the next available tri-service term & service term in ocs. so give about 1 month waiting time + 3 mths in ocs.

    thereafter u're posted back to airforce school, waiting period + time for ground school for s211 before u post over to pearce for your bwc is about 6-9mths.

    basic wing course is 10-11mths.

    fixed wing is about 10months as well.

    rotory & transport courses on average about 6 to 7 months.

    looking at which, by the time u finish and are successful, u'd be past your NS liability. bear in mind that 1 day in airforce as a pilot trainee = 1 day of NS liability. other vocations do not have such a favourable ratio.

    looking on the bad side, even if u fail ur basic wing course, u would have finished at least 1.5 yrs of your NS liabilty. left another 6 more months, u cant be expected to do much for ur posting anyway.

    its a good deal, just try it. doesn't matter if u fail. u still have to do ur NS anyway.

    for the down/dirty no holds barred answers. u gotta ask someone in the force to give u an honest view.

    anyway. airforce pilots will fly until they retire/ or medically grounded.
    but the amount u fly varies at certain stages of your career.

    example, when u're in the operational squadrons. u fly alot.
    then when u're doing your staff tour; u fly less, but u still fly to maintain proficiency.
    and throughout your career, u'll be having tours in the squadron for a few yrs, then to a staff job, then maybe back to the squadron n stuff.
    u're never really stagnant.
    n u'll prob keep flying til u cant fly anymore. so not to worry.
    its just a matter of how much u fly.

    as for projects n stuff and all. every job has it. just that in addition to flying, sometimes u have other things to do as well, but its part n parcel of the job.
    i.e; if ur groundwork has something to do with flying or operational stuff, who else better to do the paperwork for it than a pilot who knows the stuff well.

    just like in the army or wadever organisation, u'll need ppl to do the planning and organisation stuff higher up in the HQ, and the workers in the active units carrying out the plans.

    juz a matter of career progression. if i'm not wrong, even other airforces are also having this sorta thing where pilots not only fly but have other jobs as well.. not just ours.
