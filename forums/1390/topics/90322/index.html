    I see you've pored thru the "OCS FAQ" thread ;-)

    Don't over-interpret the info there. The key pre-call up criteria are educational qualifications & school ECA records, but they only serve as preliminary benchmarks. The more important scrutiny will occur during BMT, with regards to performance, aptitude, attitude &, needless to say, leadership.

    The appraisals are done by your commanders as well as peers (no blanket party is a good start ~lol~) throughout the course, & there'll also be predetermined simulation tests where your problem-solving skills are assessed. [For example, the instructors could use white tape to designate 2 riverbanks & provide some other props, then stand back & let the 8 of you work it out on how to get everyone across the "water."]

    Of course, you'll need to pass the various fitness & marksmanship tests too but they needn't be elite scores, but then they shouldn't be worse than the SISPEC batch's either.

    Don't overamplify your OCS ambitions unless you wanna get "marked". Reveal that eagerness only during your PC interview (he will conduct one with every recruit), but perform well at all other times to show that you deserve the recommendation. I don't mean wayang either; you need to show initiative as well as achieve tangible results with the tasks.

    If you want an inside edge, I'll suggest 2 reading materials. The 1st is the SAF Leadership Handbook. Not sure if the libraries have a copy but if you've got no lobang, I advise you get acquainted with the SAF Core Values &, in particular, the KAQ Leadership Framework ("Knowledge, Abilities, Qualities"). The latter is the SAFTI-developed philosophy for all local army officers to emulate & nurture.

    The 2nd book is Making The Corps by Thomas E Ricks, which tracked a platoon of US Marine recruits thru their BMT in the mid-90s. Never mind the American setting; one central tenet it reveals, & IMHO shares a lot with the Singapore version, is how enlistees can be separated into 3 groups:

    - there's a small group of Type-A, can-do people who forms the top tier, very self-motivated & correspondingly natural leaders.

    - at the other end are the condemned failures, the unresponsive, incompetent deadbeats who'll be misfit soldiers.

    - in between are the majority: the quiet-profile guys who sail thru training quite proficiently on the whole without standing out (the DI's often can't recall their names). They'll form the main body for operational units in various vocations/roles, some of them excelling enough to become NCOs or group i/cs.

    Having mentored over a mono-intake BMT before, I can testify that it's very much a universal truth. You'll likely meet the same types in your own basic training.
