    ColoursÂ’ refers to banners and flags. The history and traditions of military Colours date as far back as the Middles Ages. They served as a rally point and a powerful factor in maintaining morale.

    Their origin lies in the need for a mark of distinction between families, tribes and fighting formations throughout the history of warfare. Colours, therefore, served as conspicuous rallying points during battle.

    However, with the advancement of modern warfare, Colours are no longer carried into the battlefield for identification purposes. Nowadays, Colours are significant symbols of unit cohesion and esprit de corps, inspiring a sense of pride and unity in the servicemen.

    The history of Colours in the SAF dates back to 1954. There were then two Colours: the QueenÂ’s Colours and the Singapore Volunteer Corps Colours. The QueenÂ’s Colours and the Singapore Volunteer Corps Colours were presented to the Singapore Volunteer Corps in 1954 at the Padang by the then Governor of Singapore, Sir John Nicoll, to mark the centenary of the Volunteer Corps. With the re-structuring of the Singapore Volunteer Corps after the independence in 1965, these two Colours were retired.

    In the SAF, there are two classes of Colours: the State Colours and the Regimental Colours. These Colours are awarded in recognition of outstanding achievements in the field of combat, training, administrative efficiency and service to the community.

    Regimental Colours symbolise the pride and loyalty of the soldiers to the unit while State Colours symbolises the pride and loyalty of the units and their servicemen in service to the nation. Colours are always consecrated by religious leaders before they are presented by the President of Singapore. Every year, the SAF State Colours is presented to the Best Combat Unit. This is a much coveted prize which SAF units work hard to win.

    The SAFTI State Colours has the same design as the SAF State Colours, except that it has an added scroll beneath the SAF emblem. The scroll is inscribed with the words "SAFTI MI".



    The SAFTI State Colours is on the left, observe closely and you will be able to see the scroll in blue. It is only paraded during OCS Commissioning Parades (as far as I know...).

    In the past, many units had both the State and Regimental Colours (Ie. 1st CDO BN). After a revision of the Colours system, all unit State Colours were retired, but the SAFTI MI State Colours was left untouched. The unit State Colours look similar to the SAFTI MI State Colour, but the scroll would be inscribed with the name of the unit.
