    Hahaha. I'm unlicensed, but can contribute cuz informally "inducted" before.

    Most of all, better be prepared to drive with (two) joysticks & pedals/levers.

    Cannot slouch too cuz the feeling will always be like looking out of a "manhole", while sitting on an elevated stool with a tiny backrest. You'll get to wear a special helmet too (radio-connected to your commander's in the seat behind) except it's not very durable (can crack if it hits the armoured ring of the driver's hole, but then better that than your own head).

    I bet too that once you're more old-bird, you'll just sleep/nap sitting there in between deployments, in your helmet & it leaning on the ring. The better news shall be that, with more troops in your detachment than, say, the tanks & their smaller crew, they'll voluntarily exclude you from night watch/OP duty in order for you to obtain enough rest. (Better that than kena accident due to lack of sleep.)

    Tactically (with the driver hatch down), you'll learn to drive periscope-style too. Imagine doing it at night, downhill on a steep slope! Also got chance to drive the M113 into the sea to test its floatation, but don't worry, will be open-hatch & wearing life-jacket plus only after a preparation test at the camp's "swimming" pool.

    Sungei Gedong shall be your playground. All the SAR units, along with 21 SA (my old outfit) train there, though in my time the units were based in other camps elsewhere unlike now. The civilians living in the southwest used to call us "ang sua" troops, cuz our vehicles were always thickly caked in either red dust or red mud, massively dirtying the roads whenever we convoyed back. That's Sungei Gedong soil for ya; be ready to do a lot of maintainence even though the units are housed at its doorstep now. At least most everyone will help out in the cleanups, usually designated half a day on the training programmes.
