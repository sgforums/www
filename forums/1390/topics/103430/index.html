    1. You have no input, unless you sign on. It's controversial, but Malays are still more likely to be enlisted in the SCDF. The full-time NSF ratio between the services is something like 3:3:40.

    2. Before the current modular education system & extra-months BMT, the Tekong recruit training was informally split into "A Levels" (December, March) & "Poly" (June, September) intakes. Dunno about now. The PSLE/N levels/some O Levels Hokkien peng will go through the mono-intake BMT, at the combat unit self. Those who're retained on will form the main body of said outfits. The CDOs also has a mono-intake system, but with higher qualifications.

    3. No escaping the fact that the SAF has a military hierarchy, & those who're at the bottom tends to gripe all the time. The Hokkien peng are usually LCPs at most, but NS is more tolerant for them cuz everyone trains together as frontline soldiers. For service-side LCPs, you're usually part of a very-small team providing some sort of combat support, like storemen. No one's gonna boss you unless you screw up, but since it's a subordinate vocation, you're liable to be at the beck & call of others (eg. drawing stores at 5 am or returning them at 3 am).

    4. No, it's common enough - esp. with the mono-intakers last time who had problems understanding even English. It's said that Singlish was invented in the army, but its most-spoken language (even nowadays) remains Hokkien! Marching is easy to pick up, since you'll be drilled time & again on it. Familiarizing with the Malay commands is tougher if you lack previous familiarity/background but, not to worry, there'll be downtime oppotunities during BMTs for clarification or exchanging of notes, like during rifle-cleaning or bunk breaks at nights.

    Well, the guys from the uniformed groups should be able to help you with both the drills and their commands.

    With the commands, start with the basic words and put them together, e.g.:
    - kenan = right
    - kiri = left
    - puseng = turn
    - therefore kenan puseng = "turn right" and kiri puseng = "turn left"

    It's no different from any other language - break the phrase down into its component words and learn what each word means.

    As for the drills, you've got two factors: movements and timing. You can easily learn the movements from your friends in NCC or NPCC. As for the timing, you'll have to learn to coordinate your movements with all the other guys around you, so factor that in when your friends are teaching you and see if you can move in time with them.

    I'll also pick up on your question to Moxie. Units such as Commandos conduct their own BMT training - recruits enter the unit from day one and stay there for the duration of their national service, barring any downgrades. It's called a mono-intake because each company is comprised of men taken in at one shot, instead of having men join and leave as you go along.

    O Levels and below will also automatically get promotion? So where do all the Privates come from then if everyone is promoted to LCP and above?
    All the PTE's should climb to LCP's by ORD time - at worst by the 2nd year of reservist ICT (in-camp training). Otherwise, he's really either a condemned case or a troublemaker who's landed himself in the detention barracks or something.

    What's mono-intake BMT at the unit itself?
    Around 30% of the annual SAF enlistees (i.e. beyond the SPF or SCDF intakes) do not do their BMT at Tekong. Instead, these recruits are allocated to the SIR, GDS, SA, SAR, SCE or CDO units themselves, where they'll essentially be together for 2 years & then do reservist for 13 years more. Or, like I told my batch of 18-year-olds then: most of them would likely have to co-exist until age 35, & thus had better make an effort to get along early.

    The mono-intake training programme is roughly the same as at BMTC, consisting of weapon handling, individual fieldcraft (IFC), marksmanship, standard obstacle course (SOC), hand-grenade throwing, bayonet fighting, swimming, & drill. These basic soldiering skills are taught by a Training Cadre staff.

    Mono intakes don't really draw the public's respect, but servicemen know they form the main body of the combat units. Most Tekong-trained troops would wind up joining them sooner or later, following vocational training or leadership-plus-vocational courses. Thus it's the latter newcomers who'll have to assimilate into the unit culture, compared to the Hokkien peng already with the Formation for at least 6 months.

    Here's an extensive listing of Malay drill commands, compiled by a very enthusiastic NPCC team. Gosh, I don't even recognize a lot of them!

    http://www.chs.edu.sg/npcc/notes/drill.html

    A NSF should familiarize himself with only the following, as long as he's not tasked for form part of a NDP contingent ;-) -

    FOR STANCE (with or without rifle)
    "Sedia" Attention
    "Senang Diri" Stand at ease
    "Rehatkan Diri" Stand easy
    "Kekanan Lurus" Right Dress
    "Pandang Kehadapan Pandang" Eyes Front
    "Kekanan/Kekiri/Kebelakang Pusing" Right/Left/About Turn
    "Diam" Steady
    "Semula" As you were
    "Keluar Baris" Fall out

    FOR MARCHING
    "Dari Kiri/Kanan Cepat Jalan" By the Left/Right Quick March
    "Dari Kiri/Kanan Cepat Lari" By the Left/Right/Front Double
    "Hentak Kaki Cepat Hentak" Quick Mark Time
    "Kiri/Kanan Belok" Left/Right Wheel
    "Berhenti" Halt

    FOR RIFLES
    "Rusok Senja Ta" Shoulder Arm
    "Turun Senja Ta" Order Arm
    "Hormat Senja Ta" Present Arm
    "Julang Senja Ta" High Port
    "Tatang Senja Ta" For Inspection Port Arms
    "Lepaskan Sepering" Ease Springs

    Okay, make that total 20 ~lol~

    A few of the above commands can be elaboratedly prefaced, paticularly with turns, in which case I told my less-familiar recruits to listen out only for last part. For example:

    "Bergerak Kekanan, Bertiga Tiga, Kekanan Pusing" Move to the Right, In threes, Right turn
    "Menghadap Kebelakang/Kehadapan, Kebelakang Pusing" Retire/Advance, About turn

    One more full command, except it's usually done at the end of a rifle-range shoot & thus completely word-associable:

    "Untop Di-perrksa Tatang Senja Ta" For Inspection Port Arms

    Verbally, the SAF style is to slowly "slang" the drill command out into:
    (a) the introductory part (the whole stuff at the beginning),
    (b) the warning part (dragging out a word to signal the impending 3rd part),
    (c) the execution part (sharp burst at the end word/syllable of the command).

    So, "Hormat Senja Ta" becomes "Hormat..Senja-a-a-a-a-ah..[pause]..TAH!" Even a Hokkien peng could predict what to do here, since he would already be in shoulder-arms position - & could phonetically recognized the command for present-arms (plus having countlessly rote-practiced the latter drill beforehand).
