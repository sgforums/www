    The new uniform goes 'digital', with tiny pixels making soldiers harder to spot. The collar can be turned up to form a 'mandarin collar' so as to prevent abrasion from rifle slings. Its new fabric is also cooler and more comfortable. -- ST PHOTO: LIM WUI LIANG

    Photo by ChannelNewsAsia

    SPOT them if you can. Chances are, you can't.

    The Singapore Armed Forces' new 'digital camouflage' uniform has been designed to make it even more difficult for soldiers to be detected.

    The tiny pixels in the combat fatigues - commonly known as the No. 4 - have jagged boundary lines that produce a 'quivering effect' on the eye.

    The 'visual noise' they create means it will be harder for a soldier to be spotted at distances of less than 50m.

    The effect is akin to a person straining to make out a very low-resolution image on a computer screen.

    When the soldier moves farther away, the pixels merge into larger shapes, which means the new look works just as well as the traditional camouflage pattern from afar.

    This is the most radical revamp of the SAF's combat fatigues since the present uniform was introduced 25 years ago.

    Besides the pixel patterns, the uniform comes in new shades and with tweaks, like the creation of a 'mandarin collar' to prevent abrasion from rifle slings.

    It will be phased in over three years, with recruits, active servicemen and some national servicemen (NSmen) getting theirs from next year.

    By 2011, all NSmen will be wearing them. There are about 300,000 active and reservist personnel here.

    Soldiers in countries such as the United States, Canada and China have also adopted this camouflage pattern.

    The Ministry of Defence said that the changes will meet the varying demands of the areas where the third-generation SAF soldiers operate in.

    The new uniform will improve a soldier's ability to survive in the battlefield.

    While soldiers in the past relied on forests for cover as much as they could, the grounds where battles are fought have shifted to include urban areas fringed by foliage.

    With advances in equipment technology, soldiers today can be spotted more easily and shot at from closer range.

    A team from the army and the Defence Science & Technology Agency was in charge of coming up with the new uniform and its members studied the colours and patterns for over a year.

    Using computers, they mulled over hundreds of images in various light conditions.

    Field trials were held and it was found that it was harder to detect soldiers when they wore the new uniforms.

    Soldiers are trained to take aim and fire in four seconds once they spot their enemy. In their new greens, it took one or two seconds longer before they could be spotted.

    The new uniform works well at night too. The material has been treated so that it maintains the new camouflage effect even when soldiers are viewed through night-vision devices.

    The new fabric - woven from cotton and polyester - has also been designed to dry faster, transfer sweat to the outer surface more quickly and allow more air to circulate.

    All this means the soldier won't feel so warm and this will prevent heatstroke in hot weather. Among other changes, the new uniform comes with fewer buttons on the chest and none on the shoulders. This relieves the pressure on the soldier's body when he dons equipment like body armour.

    The epaulettes bearing officers' ranks have been moved from the shoulders to the chest area. For other servicemen, the ranks remain on the sleeves but are noticeably smaller.

    At least one soldier is happy with the new fatigues.

    Corporal Bryan Lee, 20, who has tried on the uniform, said: 'The new look will take some getting used to, but having tried it out in the field, I must say it is more effective and comfortable.'
