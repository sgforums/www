    Like others, I've seen first hand, the ability of privates and lance corporals leading sections during exercises. For combat units (non-technical vocations), it does not really matter if the person is O or A levels, or diploma holders. During fulltime NS, we were trained from privates to officers, on reaching objectives during a mission. And we knew the high possibilities of commanders being made casualties. The lance corporals and privates were taught to take over and complete the missions (this I observe, varies from unit to unit). And some have shown to have talent for it. This carries on into reservist training as well. NS is not just 2 years - its 2 years plus another 10 years in reserves. During that time, some of the lance corporals would have gained the experience to take over as section commanders. During ICTs, I've seen commanders' briefings, missions planning, etc, whereby lance corporals would turn up in place of the usual commanders for whatever reason (commander downgraded, excuse, defer, etc). From experience, they knew what to do for each mission.

    I think the SAF should promote these lance corporals to sergeants - some of them have stood in as section commanders for years because the regular commander could be on a long-term excuse. As to why sergeants and officers get promoted - there maybe a need for experienced reservists to carry on beyond the 10 year cycle. Sergeants move on to platoon sergeants or CSM, officers to OC, battalion staff officers and brigade level officers. I'm sure some lurking in this forum have the experience of being "persuaded" to carry on after their first completed cycle.

    Of course - promotion should be for those who have the ability, experience and would not mind taking up the appointment.

    The SAF is unique in the sense that it determines a person's ability by education level at the point of enlistment. Many have said we are based on the Israeli conscription system. However, from what I gathered, the IDF has a different system (based on ability) of selecting leaders. They have more time (3 years, fulltime), but with the coming reductions to their conscription service as well in the coming years, perhaps we should revisit and relearn from them again.

    I won't go into how some commanders have failed, but I have to agree that there should be a better selection method. Considering that NS is now 2 years fulltime for all ranks, I'm quite sure these things are being looked at and considered.

    Typing all of this reminds me of Thailand - even the drivers there were ranger and airborne trained, much, much older, seen counter-insurgency border action, and would give you a "I don't give a flying fcuk" stare about your rank look. The face says it all.
    At the end of the day - we are in the army, it does not matter to an enemy if you had straight As for your exam if he is determined to kill you.
