    Let's just say this:

    In NS, a positive mindset is one of the best assets you can ever have. Much better than having the ability to do 30 chin-ups and run 2.4km in 7 minutes... though I don't mind having those either.

    With a positive mindset, you can pretty much cope with anything that NS wants to throw at you. I went into NS wondering about whether I could survive all those sergeants screaming in my face (it's not just you who gets screamed at in the face, by the way), surviving all those tekan sessions and extreme PT (by my benchmark) and so on and so forth.

    I went in with a negative mindset, and I suffered as a result. My appetite suffered, and I got frequent chest pains. I would have difficulty falling asleep, and when I finally did, I would have terrible nightmares about things like getting charged by my superiors and wake up in cold sweat in the middle of the night.

    In the end, I realised that I was only hurting myself by thinking so negatively all the time. Most of the bad things which I thought were going to happen didn't actually happen. It took a while, but I made the effort to see the bright side of things. And things did actually seem better after a while.

    The bottom line is, you're in there for two years. Live with that fact, and make the most out of it. Look at your ORD date and tell yourself that no matter what, the SAF can't keep you in there any longer than that date. Two years may seem long right now, but believe me, the day will come when you'll look back and wonder how those two years passed so quickly.

    Good luck.

    My take on my bmt life. It was a torture going thru. Learning all those things that seemed sooo irrelevant. Whole day only hear the words "knock it down." At first I thought I didn't get along with my platoon mates and section mates either. I reported sick quite often too...

    Everybody thot i was some super kengster.. which i probably was. But heck, when u're sick, u're sick, don't hold back on reporting sick cos ppl would label u. Ur obligation as a soldier and as ur mother' son is to take care of urself. If u have chronic backpains, see ur Doc or MO. U may not be a combat fit soldier, but can still serve ur NS in non-combat roles.

    As for OCS and sispec, if u think u have the ability to lead, then go for it. But somehow I do no think so, since u are quite isolated from the rest of ur platoon. A leader has to be a team player. If u don't get to OCS or sispec, no worries, it does not make u any less a person.

    Really, get to know ur section mates well at least. I'm glad i did. Tho i was sick like half the time in BMT, i chipped in wherever i could to help my section mates. These are ur BROTHERS in BMT. People whom u should turn to for help. They may not give u the best advice all the time, but at least they know what u're thinking.

    Til today, I still go out with my section mates. We hang out, eat dinner, n play games together. (Aka Section Movement Outings) I'm glad to have been known them.
