    Jan 5, 2005
    PM Lee flies to disaster spots for first-hand look
    He visits Banda Aceh and Meulaboh, where a big Singapore relief operation is under way

    By Zuraidah Ibrahim
    POLITICAL EDITOR



    SUMATRA - PRIME Minister Lee Hsien Loong flew yesterday to two of the most devastated spots in Indonesia for a first-hand look at the scale of the tsunami disaster and the Singapore relief efforts rolling out on the ground.

    Mr Lee, who leaves for Jakarta this evening to attend tomorrow's summit to chart the international response, spent nearly 16 hours in Sumatra.

    'We have seen the images on TV, but it's not the same as being there, talking to the people, seeing first hand the devastation, the houses that have been knocked down, and the towns that are no longer there, and the people pulling themselves and getting back on their feet again,' he said.

    Accompanied by Defence Minister Teo Chee Hean, he also rallied Singapore's men and women in uniform, who had been catapulted from the comforts of home into nightmarish scenes of death and destruction.

    After landing in Medan, where they were received by North Sumatra Governor Tengku Rizal Nurdin, the Singapore leaders took a Chinook helicopter to Meulaboh, a focal point of relief efforts.

    There, just 50km from the epicentre of the Dec 26 earthquake, personnel from the RSS Endurance continued to clear the land to create a hub for relief supplies, working closely with the Indonesian military (TNI).

    'What additional resources do you need?' was the first question Mr Lee had for the SAF men.

    The response from the joint chairman of the joint humanitarian aid taskforce there, Colonel Tan Chuan-Jin: So far, things have been proceeding well, but let's assess each day as it comes.

    Mr Lee stressed the need to work closely with the TNI men and others on the ground in order to know the local needs. Col Tan replied that they were in constant touch.

    Singaporeans form the main foreign presence in this town of about 40,000, which has had about 10,000 of its residents decimated.

    'It's our responsibility,' Mr Lee said when asked by reporters about the extent of Singapore's help. 'We are an island, but in fact we depend on the world, and if we don't help out when other people have problems, we can't expect other people to help us when we have problems.'

    The SAF first established an air link and has now opened a sea link to ensure supplies to this otherwise marooned town that had lost its two bridges to Medan.

    'It's a turning point in our operations,' said Defence Minister Teo.

    Slowly, Meulaboh is picking up the pieces, as both Mr Lee and Mr Teo noted. Villagers are streaming to the field hospital set up by the TNI and SAF, with residents wading through their flattened homes and belongings, now shoved to the sides of streets and piled metres-high.

    The Indonesians who met the Singapore leaders had nothing but thanks for their neighbours. Coordinating Minister for Social Welfare Alwi Shihab was among those who accompanied them.

    Mr Lee, who had proposed tomorrow's summit, said it should look into galvanising sustained and coordinated aid for the long haul.

    At each of his three stops in Medan, Meulaboh and Banda Aceh, he chatted with the Singaporean men and women on the ground - men like full-time NSman Second Sergeant Yeo Boon Hock, 21.

    A week ago, he was in his barracks. Now he is in Meulaboh, clearing debris in the scorching sun.

    In Banda Aceh, Mr Lee visited the SAF medical unit. 'We are proud of you. You are flying our flag for us,' he told them.

    Jan 6, 2005
    LETTER FROM TEO CHEE HEAN
    Thank you, SAF
    He praises the troops for their role in relief missions in New Year letter

    By Goh Chin Lian



    DEFENCE Minister Teo Chee Hean praised Singapore's military and Ministry of Defence personnel for their relief efforts in various parts of the world in his New Year's message to the troops.

    The aid is on a scale never seen before here. Three of the Singapore Armed Forces' four helicopter landing ships are at sea, two off Sumatra and one in the Gulf.

    Many of its transport planes and choppers, including half of its fleet of Chinooks, are flying daily relief missions.

    More than 1,200 personnel are on overseas missions, with more than 1,000 in Indonesia, 50 in Thailand and 180 in the Gulf.

    At the same time, the military is keeping its anti-terror guard up at home and continuing with its routine training, including hosting a 10-day exercise between Singapore and Malaysia.

    The annual event started yesterday with six ships from both navies engaging in coordinated maritime surveillance in the Malacca Strait.

    'This is the largest number of overseas missions that we have undertaken simultaneously and the largest number of SAF personnel we have ever deployed on missions overseas at any one time,' Mr Teo said in a Jan 1 letter on www.miw.com.sg, a Web portal for national servicemen.

    SAF regulars, national servicemen, including full-timers, and volunteers are working 'shoulder to shoulder', and many others who did not go have also been hard at work preparing for the deployments, he said.

    'I know that there are many who were not deployed who have worked very hard, and continue to work very hard, to plan, prepare, support, sustain and monitor each mission so that it will be carried out successfully.'

    He praised the SAF for acting swiftly and effectively. Standby teams were ready to go within 24 hours, and teams assembled for the relief missions within 48 to 72 hours.

    'Our people showed a high level of professionalism and commitment, working around the clock to get the teams ready,' Mr Teo said.

    The Asia-Pacific editor of Jane's Defence Weekly, Mr Robert Karniol, described the deployment as commendable.

    He said it was not surprising, considering Singapore's military capabilities have become sophisticated over time, that it has been moving to show it is prepared to be more active in the international front and that the scale of the disaster requires such a response.

    Defence analyst Andrew Tan of the Institute of Defence and Strategic Studies does not believe the unprecedented scale of deployment is too big for the SAF to handle, as it has been sending its troops overseas for training over the years.

    Dr Tan, who also teaches at the Safti Military Institute, said that officers at the very top level have been expecting the SAF to be involved in peacekeeping and humanitarian missions, and subjects related to this are being taught to its personnel.

    He believes the SAF will be able to build up new non-combat skills, such as dealing with natural disasters and a traumatised population.

    A lot of people seem to be taking the cost of the SAF's relief effort to heart. However, I, for one, believe that participating in such relief operations provide a win-win situation for the SAF.

    Besides the fact that participating in such operations is a good deed in itself, the experience gained by our troops dispatched overseas would be well worth the cost.

    Imagine, every person dispatched would return not only as better human beings for helping those poor souls, but also as better soldiers. Here are some examples:

    1) Participating in establishing landing sites and clearing debris would be extremely beneficial to the combat engineers. Performing these tasks, they would be gaining actual experience out in the field solving REAL problems with effective solutions. No amount of exercises or simulated scenarios would give them this. At the end, they come back better at their jobs: better soldiers.

    2) Participating in the relief effort, combat medics and MOs would receive an immense amount of knowledge. Back in Singapore, at max, they would care for a few hundred soldiers a month? There they would receive a few thousand different patients each with their own problems. According to the press, they're even treating children! Coming back, they might well have more experience then every other medic or MO in Singapore.

    3) The mindef.gov.sg portal states that SAF communications specialists are assisting Telkomsel, the indon phone company, in re-establishing the GSM network. Signallers involved, would be helping to set up a GSM network from scratch! Where can you get that kind of experience if they had been left in Singapore?

    4) Also, a Mobile Air Traffic Control tower is being sent over there. CAAS and RSAF personnel will be helping to set up and will work with Indon officials to operate it. An RSAF WSO (C3) working there would be gaining experience about running a control centre on a bare air field without help from any of the other air traffic control facilities like in Singapore.

    The list goes on... RSAF pilots get flying time, landing in conditions not normally found at home, RSAF technicians get to work for extended periods on RSN ships. RSN sailors and RSAF airmen can fine tune interoperational issues.

    Moral of story: Every dollar spent would be worth it in what we gain in return. ie. An immense wealth of knowledge. In the end, no good deed goes unreturned.[/b]

    Aerozapper,
    Nice sentiments (which I concur wholehearted with) though I must disagree with some of your inferences.

    1stly, this whole HATF (that's what the MINDEF refers to the mission as in its website, compared to "Operation Lion Heart" in Phuket) isn't really a combat campaign, so there's a limited interpretation of what being better soldiers entail.

    2ndly, no dissession with the experience gained but, for military expertise itself, I'll have to contend that our troops involved have already acquired an excellent measure of it prior. That's what being operational-ready means: the constant repetition of drills so that proficiency becomes second nature, complemented by problem-solving skills during regular overseas training to internalize a can-do spirit. (Applicable for both RSAF flyboys & SAF ground troops.)

    So IMO the quip about "No amount of exercises or simulated scenarios would give them this" doesn't really hold. In fact, in many ways those on the ground over there are doing the same sai-kang work that servicemen complain about locally - but of course with a desperately-urgent purpose now.

    3rdly, I just made time to read through miw.com.sg's various reports closely & it appears that relatively few NSF soldiers went to Aceh (different from the DART teams, which use a lot of conscripts). For example, it's reported that some 350 of the 472 on the RSS Endurance are military personnel, & only 63 are said to be full-time NSF's. (TCH said it was 90 in his media briefing). In other words - roughly 20% of the total personnel; no breakdown yet of the 196 on the RSS Persistence that I could find.

    Also, the ST reported that "45 combat engineers" went ashore to clear to the debris & pack sandbags for the new landing point, so my guess are (a) it's possibly a standby SCE company or detachment that went over instead of the whole unit, & (b) 45 of 63 (or 90) suggests the bulk of the represented NSFs were from that Formation. Of course, none of these conclusions negates what a medic or a signal spec is contributing there - but if valid then it's generally more about what we can do for them (rather than gain back in reverse).

    4thly, I'm sure the extended multiple-week field deployment is nothing like what's experienced in normal SAF training but, in another sense, their daily grind ends earlier as well. For example, the medics finish work by 3:30 in order to commute back to camp; the chopper crew chews up incredible mileage but doesn't fly at night; ditto for our ground troops cuz things are streetlights aren't repaired yet. That said, there's still night briefings & repacking of stores to be done, so no attending to personal stuff (like phone calls back home) until 21:00-22:00.

    The biggest reward we're getting out of this is meaningful work, period. As far as professional knowledge gained, I say it's the SAF planners over here, & the various liaison teams over there, who've benefitted most. In other words, military bureaucrats being more than pencil-pushers - unlike what we're starting to hear about the inefficient Indonesian counterparts. It wasn't "customary" (as someone cavalierly suggested) to assemble 600+ different-skilled men within 48 hours, even though existing SAF components like standby personnel & Joint-Staff coordination helped greatly. But such institutional efficiency & preparation don't magically appear out of the air; they're rather the results of what generations of servicemen strove for during NS, no matter how minor their roles.

    By all means, give everyone a medal when they return safely home! ~lol~
