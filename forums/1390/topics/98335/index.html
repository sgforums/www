    its been some time for me, but i will try to remember some things here and there.

    1. yes the reclaimed land is somehow hotter and err.. stuffier. sometimes i wonder if this is a psychological effect.
    2. you should be learning individual field craft - things like camouflaging, up to group movement, some drills, etc. and if im not mistaken, the situational test has been collapsed together? that is simply a series of tests, which you and your team will go through, with different persons taking the lead at each mission, trying to solve everyone of them.
    3. tips:
    a. secure your belongings - ALL of them, using black tape, black rubber bands, etc. you won't want to lose anything out there.
    b. do not leave your rifle lying around. and secure them properly when you sleep lest they be "stolen"
    c. waterproof stuff in your field pack properly.
    d. have useful items handy in your sbo. think i mentioned this in one of the sticky threads.
    4. about proving your worth... well. it will be quite useless and hypocritical if someone has been uncooperative all along, only to become the super leader during field camp. the point is, consistency plays a part too. no point just shining out during this period. they look out for things like taking initiative, working as a team, being cooperative, helping each other, showing leadership, determination, discipline, etc.

    you may wish to approach some forumnites here who have recently been to bmt to find out in more detail.

    This is what I remember for my SIT Test......it was 4 years ago, so my memory's a bit faded:

    Boarded the tonner to this reclaimed area with lots of little shrubs and little cover....yes, it was hot. But nowhere as hot as Australia.

    Debussed, went to the training shed and got divided into groups. After that, they pick your job, whether you are a CT or a terrorist, whether u take AWP or AK47....just joking you will become a LAW guy, signaller, demo man, medic or something else, I can't remember.

    I was the signaller so I went to another training shed for some super-quick signal training and got my little handheld radio. I stuck it in the side pocket of my fullpack, and with the antenna sticking out, looked kinda cool.....

    Then went on to pitch basha. By now you should be very familiar with pitching bashas. Enjoy them, coz when u go to unit no more such luxury, just throw down your SBO and sleep.

    More talking and admin stuff, training to tie knots and boring stuff. THat was the end of the first day. Clean weapon and powder bath at night, and I slept pretty well. It was much better than in IFC where I slept in rainwater.

    Bright and early the next morning, we woke up for breakfast, 5BX, admin stuff again. Got a mission "briefing" before moving out in groups at 9am. Each group will rotate going to each station.

    The most irritating thing here was the walking. Although you may be near to the station, your spec will lead you on endless walks through the safari-like area, to admire the plethora of flora and fauna in the area. I don't think we should disclose the details of each "mission", you should try them for yourself. They were all pretty interesting and some took thinking. Some were plain stupid and we solved in less than 5 mins.

    Peppered through these leisurely walks are "arty-drills" to enhance the excitement, and somewhere in the festivities, someone will be declared a casualty. The remaining members of the group have to use a poncho to carry the fallen soldier and not walk, but RUN 2.4km to the next station. This is BLOODY TIRING. Apart from the fact that we had a Moby Dick in the poncho, the sun was glaring down on us and the satay pasta I had was sloshing in my stomach. One of the worst times I had in the whole course.

    The other thing that bothered me constantly was the lack of general hygiene leading to rashes breaking out on my back. I had to take off my shirt often because it was so hot and try and pour cold water on the rash to lessen the pain, much to the chagrin of the instructor. Apart from the fact that I fell asleep in the proning position while guarding a "bridge", consequently failing the entire mission, compounded by the crappy answers I gave in the interview on the last day led me to proudly become a MAN in combat engineers.....

    On the whole, not a bad experience although I was dreaming of MacDonalds fried chicken by the end of the field camp. (I don't live in Singapore anymore, do they still sell that stuff?) Nowhere near anything as bad as the things I would experience later on in unit days.

    But that's another story.

  • pkchukiss
    17 Oct 04, 18:32
    BMT field camp... I still cannot imagine I clocked 7 days of enduring continuous inhumane treatment out in the field.

    I still remembered how on the day we set out, we did around 3 sets of 20 push ups, because we were slow to pick up our field packs. We also did 2 sets at the harbouring site on day one, because some of us blur recruits couldn't get the basha up within 10 minutes, and had to take them down. 2 more for not falling in FBO within 10 minutes of dismantling basha. 3 more sets for not building it back up within 10 minutes, 1 more for not falling back in FBO within 10 minutes, 1 more set for not managing to display everything inside the field pack properly, 1 more for not shoving everything back in within 1 minute.

    And that was day one. But it was fun, especially when the instructors did a demonstration on field discipline. One of the officers was like puffing very hard on his cigarette, trying to show us how easily smoke is detected outfield. He emerged from the foilage rather red faced...

    Reclaimed land was one of the worse places, and the second last day of the camp. Dug a shellscrape and firetrench in one day, all with a naggy sergeant on my back, then endured countless rifle jumping jacks because we couldn't finish the shellscrape and firetrench. I took 10 hours to dig my shellscrape, and got "personal attention". Then I got too shacked and fell asleep after RO without hugging my rifle. Woke up at 3 am to find it missing, went to the training shack to find it, but did not have the guts to stun it right from under the duty sergeant's nose. Woke him up, got a huge dressing down for "sneaking up" on him (is that why they picked me to be a scout?), got rifle back, went to sleep. 2 hours later, got turned out with thunderflashes, had to put everything inside field pack, cover up shellscrape, then fall in within 15 minutes.

    Fell in, and whole company got pumped for being late. Then got pumped because shellscrape was not filled in properly. Given another 15 minutes to cover shellscrape properly. Got pumped again, because somebody buried his ET stick. 15 minutes for whole company to find ET stick. Got pumped again, instructor found combat rations half buried inside shellscrape. Then we settled down for breakfast at 7 am.

    The funny thing was, amidst all that tekanning, we had some welfare. Cold syrup drinks, cooked out rations, and all that. Totally awesome.

    Field camp is the time where you really learn how much mental stress your mind can take, and the true faces of your section commanders. You will also discover who your true friends are.

    Be prepared for tons of unreasonable requests and be punished for not meeting them. If you are good enough, sneak in some stuff in your underwear (at your own risk, but chances are they won't check your underwear), or ask for permission to go back to bunk to bring some stuff you "conveniently forgot" after field pack inspection. If your sergeant is a kind soul, he might carry some civilian comforts for you...

    everyone's mood hit a all time low. many just stand there in the rain staring at the ground. it's was that demoralizing.
