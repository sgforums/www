    May 18, 2007
    Nets fee hike angers retailers

    THE NETS cashcard network will be increasing its transaction fee for retailers, in stages from July 1 - by about three times.

    It had previously announced in January this year that it will raise the fee by between 0.75 and 2 per cent from April.

    But retailers told The Straits Times that this levy, currently pegged at around 0.35 to 0.55 per cent of transactions, will go up to between 1.5 and 1.8 per cent.

    This fee hike will be staggered between July and September. No other details were given.

    NETS, or the Network for Electronic Transfers, is owned by DBS, OCBC and UOB. Its card, used by 83 per cent of Singapore residents, is popular as an alternative to cash and credit cards.

    Retailers like it too as they get their money upfront.

    But many retailers have been upset since NETS first announced the hike.

    Some have advised consumers that, with the hike, they should either pay in cash or expect their purchases to cost more.

    Chairman of People's Park Traders' Association Poh Boon Pang, 60, who owns an organic food store, said to gear up for the Nets fee increase, he has started telling his customers to pay by cash. Otherwise, he will pass on the fee hike to them, he said.

    But one shopper, graphic designer Jackson Tan, 28, does not buy this argument.

    'If merchants can shoulder credit card transaction fees, why can they not take on the fees for Nets too?' he asked.

    Hike for merchants is Net loss

    'Monopolistic' move defeats aim of a cashless society Doesn't this translate into yet another price increase? Debit cards not so common Encourage Nets, not hinder it

    Thursday Â• June 7, 2007

    Letter from Cheng Shoong Tat
    Letter from Jayandran Sandra Alison
    Letter from Punitha Govindasamy
    Letter from Harry Chia

    I cannot agree more with the Consumers Association of Singapore that Nets' decision to raise its levy on businesses amounts to an abuse of its "monopolistic" position, and this should certainly be investigated by the Competition Commission Singapore.

    Nets is a supplier of a basic service and should be regulated as such. Nets claims it has to increase the levy because it faces competition from international debit cards that offer higher returns to card issuers. If that were true, would Nets do away with the proposed increase for retailers who agree to accept only Nets and no other international debit cards?

    The three local banks, as Nets' shareholders, should see Nets as their contribution to making Singapore a cashless society, rather than a purely business vehicle to milk more profit. After all, the three banks are not losing money from Nets as it serves to increase banking transactions and revenues.

    Where do the three banks stand on this issue? The public would like to hear from them, too.

    The announcement of the Nets fee hike is likely to have Singaporeans up in arms about yet another fee hike. Granted, the charges are being borne by the retailers, but many of us already feel that these increases will be passed on to consumers either directly or indirectly. Coupled with the Goods and Services Tax (GST) hike in July as well, the Nets fee increase could not have come at a worse time.

    We are constantly told that the economy is booming and the salaries of most employees have risen, and hence, we should bear with the price increases.

    The reality is that many mid-level income workers have yet to see a substantial salary increase but keep forking out more for basic necessities, utilities, healthcare and transportation.

    Although Case has called the Nets fee increase "a great disservice", in reality, Nets does not have to back down from its stance. Its explanation of rising competition from debit cards does not ring true to me. I believe most Singaporeans would rather use Nets for its convenience and the fact that they do not have to pay to use it.

    Knowing that the goods they buy now may cost more as a result of using this service, many consumers may choose to revert to cash. All along, I have been under the impression that the Nets concept was a way for Singapore to go cashless and to rely on the electronic transfer of money.

    The recent move by Nets to increase its fees for retailers, who in turn could pass this cost increase down to the consumer, is outrageous.

    Nets is a small convenience enjoyed by people who cannot afford credit cards. Debit cards are commonplace but more suited for people who travel. Also, debit card transactions take a few days to clear, and not many retailers, such as mini-marts and hair salons, have debit card facilities.

    Nets' chief executive officer Poh Mui Hoon is not entirely incorrect when she emphasised that the hike in fees for retailers is "not a consumer issue in any way and should not be made out in any way to be one".

    However, it cannot be denied that the consumer will ultimately suffer the collateral damage of that hike. Case, therefore, has a duty to look out for the consumer.

    In any retail price increase, retailers will almost always pass down the cost, either in price hikes or reduced quantities. Businessmen will be businessmen. If their costs increase, they will find ways and means to compensate for that increase; they are not exactly philanthropists when it comes to business.

    Nets is a secure way of payment in lieu of cash and should be encouraged; you need both the merchants and the consumers to make cashless payment a way of life. Any move by Nets to hinder this must be resisted. If the reasons for the hike are justifiable because of their higher operating costs that would lead to a loss, then increase the levy by all means. But it should not be an opportunity for higher dividends for shareholders.

    Case slams shoe store that charged Nets levy
    Parkway Parade shop made customers pay extra although practice is disallowed
    By Lim Wei Chean

    A PARKWAY Parade shoe seller has been found to be profiteering by charging a levy for Nets purchases ahead of a rise in the fee levied on retailers.
    Under their agreement with Nets, retailers cannot pass on this fee to consumers.

    The fee hike - to take place over three batches, will see merchants paying transaction fees of up to 1.9 per cent of purchases, up from the current 0.35 to 0.55 per cent - will start on July 1.

    Consumer watchdog Case and Nets have condemned the merchant's move.

    Since April 1, Dynasty Shoes in Parkway Parade has been charging customers who pay by Nets an extra 1.7 per cent on purchases.

    Yesterday, The Straits Times went there and paid $1.15 extra - via Nets - for a $69.90 pair of sandals.

    When asked about the extra charge, a shop assistant, who wanted to be known only as Nelly, said customers can opt to pay cash and avoid paying the fee.

    She then produced a letter from Nets dated last December stating that Nets will be levying a transaction fee of 1.7 per cent of purchases from the shop.

    The letter indicated the hike will start on April 1. It did not say that consumers will have to bear the increase.

    Nets later changed the implementation date to July 1.

    But Mr Patrick B.W., who runs Dynasty Shoes, claimed he did not receive a second letter from Nets that the implementation had been delayed. He also said he did not get a reply to a letter he sent to Nets informing it that he will pass on the charge to consumers if the hike is carried out.

    He said: 'We never agreed not to pass the fee to consumers.'

    Nets - owned by a consortium of local banks DBS, UOB and OCBC - is a popular cashless payment system with six million users.

    Some 13,000 retailers are on it. Many small-scale retailers, in fact, depend on Nets, as they cannot afford the fees levied by credit card issuers.

    Yesterday, Case's vice-president, Mr Lim Biow Chuan, referring to the Parkway Parade shop, said: 'Such business conduct is unacceptable.' Case also hit out at Nets earlier, saying its move to raise fees is a 'monopolistic practice'.

    Nets chief executive officer Poh Mui Hoon said consumers should call the Nets hotline on 6274-1212 to report any errant merchant.

    Yesterday, Ms Poh also responded to Case's criticism. She said: 'This is not a consumer issue since merchants are not allowed to pass on the charges to consumers.'

    She insisted that Nets has 'no choice' but to increase fees to ensure that banks will continue to issue Nets-linked cards in the face of competition from bank-issued debit cards, which number about 600,000 in Singapore.

    But Case filed a complaint with the Competition Commission of Singapore (CCS) against Nets yesterday.

    A CCS spokesman said a fine not exceeding 10 per cent of the business turnover may be imposed for up to three years if CCS 'deems an infringement was committed'.
