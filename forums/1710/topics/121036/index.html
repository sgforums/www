Declining interest in engineering may spell bad news for ind

  • sbst275

    Engineering departments at polytechnics are facing the problem of declining student numbers.

    Educators warn this might be a dangerous trend as Singapore might not be producing enough quality engineers for an economy where engineering continues to be a core pillar.

    Once one of the most coveted courses, engineering is now plagued by lack of interest.

    "Engineering is not as glamourous as the other business or info tech," said one student.

    "Government is promoting life sciences and not so much of engineering, that's why engineering is having a drop in enrollment," said another.

    Polytechnics say the number of students choosing engineering as their first choice has fallen by half over the last six years.

    Millions of dollars have already been invested in infrastructure used to teach engineering so a fall in enrollment numbers mean that these resources will be under-utilised.

    To address the problem, the 5 polytechnics have set up a joint committee to promote the engineering discipline.

    Over the next few months, they will be spending $300,000 on media campaigns, talks and exhibitions to attract more students.

    Professor Low Teck Seng, Chairman, Engineering Joint Committee, Principal, Republic Polytechnic, said: "If we don't draw these young people into our engineering courses, we will not be able to supply industry with the right quality people to support the growth of the high technology industries. There has been a lot of talk of growing new areas, but it should not be at the expense of an industry which continues to underpin the growth and development of Singapore, that is engineering."

    The Nanyang Technological University, long established for its engineering faculty, says it does not face the same problems as the polytechnics.

    This is because the faculty continually reinvents itself, incorporating life sciences, and even high-tech design elements into its curriculum.

    Dr Simon Yu, Vice Dean of Admissions, Nanyang Technological University, said: "We have broadened the curriculum significantly and in particular, we have this global immersion programme for them to participate and they can work up to one year in China or United States."

    In keeping with the multidisciplinary approach, NTU will be announcing the launch of a new School of Chemical and Biomedical Engineering this year. - CNA
