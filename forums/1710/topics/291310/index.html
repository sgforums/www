    Subsidised petrol 'not for foreigners'
    Straits Times, The (Singapore)
    Asia - Malaysia
    November 13, 2007

    KUALA LUMPUR - THE government has been urged to stop Singaporeans from filling up their vehicles with subsidised petrol and diesel in Malaysia.

    Mr Khalid Mohamad, Umno Youth chief for Johor Baru, said that the government should not allow foreigners to enjoy subsidised petrol any more.

    He suggested a system to ensure that foreigners pay unsubsidised rates, as well as a fee of RM20 (S$9) to be imposed on Singaporean and Thai vehicles entering the country.

    'When the government restructures the subsidy system, it should review the way in which foreigners have been enjoying subsidised petrol and diesel,' he said.

    'The government should set up a special method to identify foreign licence plates or a special terminal selling unsubsidised petrol and diesel in cities near neighbouring countries,' he was quoted as saying by the Malay-language Berita Harian.

    The newspaper reported that because of cheaper pump prices in Malaysia, thousands of vehicles from neighbouring Singapore and Thailand pour into the country.

    Even if petrol was sold at unsubsidised rates to Singaporeans, the price would still be low because of the currency exchange rate, which is favourable to the city-state, said Mr Khalid.

    The extra revenue could be channelled into other sectors such as health and infrastructure development, he said.

    The prices of unsubsidised petrol RON97, RON92 and diesel in Malaysia are S$1.08, S$1.04 and 87 cents per litre respectively, said Berita Harian.

    Malaysian Prime Minister Abdullah Badawi has said that the government will restructure

    fuel subsidies to ensure that the poor pay less for their petrol than the rich.

    Malaysia maintains petrol prices that are among the region's lowest, through hefty fuel subsidies.

    The subsidies are expected to cost the country at least RM35 billion a year if crude oil prices hit around US$100 (S$145) per barrel.
