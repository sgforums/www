    URA exhibition showcases the shaping of Singapore through urban planning

    SINGAPORE : Even though urban planning has been successful in transforming Singapore's landscape in the past 30 years, more challenges lie ahead, says Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong.

    He said Singapore may be clean, green and efficient, but that is not good enough to make it a truly vibrant, global city.

    And to give Singaporeans a glimpse of the future and a look at the past, he launched the Urban Redevelopment Authority exhibition "Shaping Singapore" and reopened the "City Gallery".

    How Singapore became what it is today is carefully documented in a first-ever comprehensive exhibition on urban planning and conservation.

    Exhibits in the newly-reopened city gallery make it easy to get a bird's eye view of the bustling modern metropolis that Singapore has become after 30 years of urban planning.

    But given our tight space constraints, what is on the horizon for Singapore's urban landscape in the next 30 years?

    Mr Lee said: "Every government, every city knows that in the era of globalisation, being clean, functional and efficient is not good enough if you aspire to be a global city.

    "Moving forward, what should distinguish our city is boldness, experimentation, setting new standards and pushing new frontiers.

    "Sometimes this could be at the expense of efficiency and like all bold measures, will involve taking some risks. But it is the only way to catch the attention of the world, and stand a chance of acquiring the x-factor."

    Mr Lee said he wanted vibrancy and optimism in the air - something that will bring pride to Singaporeans and excite visitors.

    Mr Lee said: "Ultimately what determines a great city is that extra x-factor. You cannot quite explain it, but you know it when you arrive in a city that has it. It is the quality that brings pride to the people living there and at the same time excites visitors.

    "The outlook is globalised and cosmopolitan. You immediately feel the vibrancy and excitement, the opportunity and optimism. You want to work and play all day and night, because there is too much to see and do and the city never sleeps."

    So the government's prepared to review and relax building rules, give bigger plots of land to private developers to tap on their creativity, and invest in bolder, ambitious concepts.

    The Shaping Singapore exhibition showcases some of these plans, like the expansion of the Central Business District at Marina Bay.

    The URA is also injecting $3 million to start a programme promoting architecture and urban design excellence with the private sector.

    It will set up a Architecture and Urban Design Promotion Programme to foster the development of design professionals and to generate public interest and awareness in architecture and urban design issues.

    All this to give Singapore that much needed x-factor.

    Mr Lee said: "Shaping Singapore is a long process. We will develop our city progressively and gradually.

    "It is like a garden which takes many years of patient planting, shaping and settling before it matures and displays its full richness and diversity.

    "If we guide our city to grow organically, it will bear the charm and cultural imprints of many generations of Singaporeans."

    The exhibition will run for four months at the URA building.
