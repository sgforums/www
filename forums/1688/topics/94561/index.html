    McDonald's loses trademark battle
    By Elena Chong

    A SINGAPORE food company has won a battle against United States fast-food giant McDonald's Corporation.

    The restaurant chain had wanted to stop listed Future Enterprises from distributing products named 'MacNoodles', 'MacTea' and 'MacChocolate'.

    However, the Court of Appeal, in a unanimous decision yesterday, did not find that the three trademarks were deceptively similar to the chain's distinctive 'Mac' or 'Mc' prefix.

    Chief Justice Yong Pung How, together with Judge of Appeal Chao Hick Tin and Justice Tan Lee Meng, dismissed the suit with costs.

    The three instant food and beverage products are packaged with an eagle logo and sold mainly at NTUC FairPrice and Mustafa supermarkets in Singapore. Their exports are mainly to Russia and Vietnam.

    'McDonald's has spent millions to create goodwill and instant public recognition for its 'Mc' series of marks.' - Senior Counsel Davinder Singh for McDonald's Corporation

    Said the company's lawyer, Senior Counsel Tan Tee Jim: 'There can be no likelihood of confusion or deception. The marks are different in appearance, sound and concept.

    'Our marks are visually and aurally different. Our colour scheme, font and typeface are different.'

    Also, the Singapore products are uncooked and unprepared food, whereas McDonald's sold prepared or cooked food that is consumed in its restaurants or as takeouts.

    The ruling is the third time that the US chain has lost in its bid to stop the Singapore company, which had applied to register the three trademarks in July 1995.

    It was allowed to do so by the Principal Assistant Registrar of Trademarks in April last year.

    In April this year, Justice Belinda Ang threw out McDonald's appeal in the High Court.

    Yesterday, Senior Counsel Davinder Singh, for McDonald's, argued that the proposed trademarks were calculated to deceive or to cause confusion.

    'McDonald's has spent millions to create goodwill and instant public recognition for its 'Mc' series of marks, for example, McChicken, McNuggets and McMuffin,' he said.

    He said Future Enterprises' managing director Tan Wang Cheow had copied McDonald's well-known 'Mc' marks, to leverage on its tremendous worldwide reputation and to ensure instant brand recognition and appeal for his new range of products.

    After the court's decision, Mr Tan said in a statement: 'We have invested substantial time and resources, over the years, to develop our own brands into global market leaders.'

    The company makes over 170 types of products which are sold in more than 40 countries, the statement added.

    Texas bulb clocks up 96 'light' years

    DALLAS - They sure don't make things like the Texas lightbulb anymore. It sold for a few cents and has burned for 96 straight years.

    The North Fort Worth Historical Society will have a birthday party today for its famous household fixture - a lightbulb that has burned continuously since Sept 21, 1908. It was first illuminated when a stagehand at a local opera house flicked a switch and posted a sign that the light over a stage entrance was not be turned off.

    Advertisement

    'We have no idea why it has lasted so long. That is the wonderful mystery of it,' said Ms Sarah Biles, the administrator of the museum where the bulb burns, complete with its own independent power supply.

    The Texas bulb is about 40 watts and made of thick glass that houses a sturdy carbon filament.

    Despite having lasted 96 years so far, the Texas bulb does not hold the record for the longest continuously burning bulb in the world.

    According to the Guinness Book of World Records, that honour goes to a 4-watt bulb that has been burning at a firehouse in Livermore, California, since it was turned on in 1901.

    Ms Biles said the keepers of the Texas bulb feel no luminescence envy with the California model because their bulb has had a much more celebrated history.

    The Texas bulb was touted - wrongly - in the 1930s as the longest-burning bulb in the world. The opera house where it burned became a cinema and actors would stop to admire the bulb's glow.

    'Our bulb has a unique past and can hold its own, even if it is No. 2,' Ms Biles said. -- Reuters

    Man mistakes wife for monkey and shoots her dead

    KUALA LUMPUR - A 68-year-old woman was shot dead by her husband after he mistook her for a monkey raiding their fruit trees, the New Straits Times reported yesterday.

    Madam Meah Hamid was plucking mangosteens from a tree behind their house in Pahang when her husband came by and saw the branches moving, the paper reported.

    Thinking it was a monkey, the 70-year-old man took a shotgun and fired at the tree only to hear his wife cry out in pain, it said.

    He rushed her to a clinic, but she died on the way.

    Police said shotgun pellets hit Madam Meah's face and chest. They have detained the man for causing death through recklessness.

    Initial investigations showed the man did not know his wife was in the tree, police said. He was allowed to attend his wife's funeral.

    The couple, who have been married for four decades, have 13 children, more than 100 grandchildren and eight great-grandchildren, news reports said. -- AFP

    Armed guards on ships 'not viable option'

    Maritime experts say shipowners can't afford hiring cost and suggest increased patrols

    By David Boey

    STATIONING armed guards on board merchant ships plying pirate-prone areas, such as the Malacca Strait, is not a viable option, especially for small ships, said international maritime watchdogs yesterday.

    The cost of hiring the guards would wipe out their daily earnings.

    A more viable option, suggested maritime analysts, is to intensify naval or police coast guard patrols and to encourage the merchant ships' crew members to be more vigilant against pirates.

    Last month, Deputy Prime Minister Tony Tan, who is also the Coordinating Minister for Security and Defence, said Singapore and Malaysia had floated the idea of having 'some security presence' on ships passing through vital straits in the region. But he did not say if the guards would be provided free.

    Four guards - the minimum needed to maintain a 24-hour watch on a merchant vessel - can cost US$2,000 (S$3,400) a day, said Captain Jayant Abhyankar, deputy director of the London-based International Maritime Bureau (IMB).

    According to his calculations, most shipowners can ill-afford such costs.

    'It's not economically viable. The sum is more than the freight earnings for a small freighter or can form a significant portion of a larger ship's earnings.

    'Armed guards should be used only if there is no other alternative to ensuring the safety and lives of the ship's crew,' said Capt Abhyankar.

    He is also not in favour of arming the crew to defend themselves, as some analysts have suggested.

    'We believe the crew are not trained to shoot to kill. They are not psychologically prepared so it may be risky to arm them,' he said.

    Capt Abhyankar was speaking to The Straits Times on the sidelines of a two-day workshop, titled Maritime Security, Maritime Terrorism And Piracy In Asia, organised by the Institute of South-east Asian Studies and held at the Orchard Hotel.

    Yesterday's talks were attended by about 120 maritime analysts, diplomats and people from the marine trade.

    Ms Carolin Liss, a postgraduate student from Murdoch University in Perth, Australia, noted that 'private military companies' are increasingly angling for contracts from shipping companies and shipowners.

    She noted that some cruise liner operators have hired former soldiers to protect their ships.

    Capt Abhyankar finds the growing number of companies offering armed services worrying because they are unregulated and their credentials can be suspect.

    He said the IMB is approached 'every two weeks or so' by people formerly in the military keen to start such services.

    'Obviously, the most effective way to meet the problem is to prevent the pirates boarding the ship in the first instance,' he added.

    He highlighted how increased vigilance by security forces in the 10 days following the Sept 11 terror attacks led to safe seas everywhere.

    'There were no piracy incidents all over the world. This had never happened before,' he said.

    'The pirates were probably as shocked as we all were, but it could also be due to heightened security worldwide after the attacks.'

    There must be respect for plurality

    Cultural and political diversity, built on the foundation of common values, is essential for human progress. The UN can be the platform to bring out the best in global plurality, said Foreign Minister George Yeo when he addressed the UN General Assembly on Friday

    THE human condition has not changed. There is in human beings a restless, competitive spirit that strives to get ahead, make discoveries and order or re-order the world according to our values and worldview. It is this spirit which drives us to achieve the three Gs of gold, glory and God, a compulsion which can lead to great good or great evil.

    'What we need... is an environment which encourages mutual learning and healthy competition. For this, we need to respect one another.' -- FOREIGN MINISTER GEORGE YEO

    The greatest evil is committed out of a sense of self-righteousness. This has been the case throughout history. Al-Qaeda carries out its actions in the name of Islam which is a gross perversion. Religious wars are the most cruel because human beings are maimed and killed in the name of the divine. Ethnic and ideological conflicts sometimes turn genocidal when one side believes right is completely on its side.

    Not a long time ago, the Khmer Rouge turned against fellow Cambodians with unbelievable ferocity in an absurd attempt to re-create human society. Ordinary people can be brutalised after long years of conflict, which was the case in Indochina. More bombs were dropped on Laos alone than on all of Europe during World War II.

    Untrammelled economic competition can also lead to grave injustice. Without rules, ruthless economic competition will return us to the jungle. Without the World Trade Organisation (WTO), globalisation can become a means through which the strong dominate the weak around the world. In an earlier period, this resulted in entire continents being carved up by imperial powers. Global organisations like the United Nations (UN) and the WTO give us hope that this century can be better than previous ones. We need rules which put limits on our competitiveness in the political and economic arenas.

    Rules to spur positive achievement

    LIKE the Olympic Games, clear rules and rigorous policing of the rules enable individuals to compete fiercely and triumph within a framework of sportsmanship and fairness. The rules are not there to dampen our competitive spirit, which is in the nature of man, but to channel it towards positive achievement.

    The rules have to be determined by common consensus. Their legitimacy is derived from shared values which bind participants together. The rules have evolved over the years along with our collective sense of what is fair and proper. They cannot be imposed. If they are imposed, what we have will not be the Olympics but the gladiatorial pits of the Roman Colosseum.

    At the most fundamental level, discussions and debates in the UN and the WTO are really about the values which bind us together as human beings.

    As the world grows smaller, our sense of interdependence grows. As we interact more, we discover we are more similar than we think. So many problems like global warming, epidemics and terrorism can be overcome only if we work together. So many opportunities made possible by new scientific discoveries can be fully exploited only if we combine our efforts.

    Yes, we have become closer. We celebrated together when for the first time the entire human genome was mapped a few years ago. We grieved as one when Sept 11 happened. The slaughter of children at Beslan outraged us all. But will we ever become the same? That is not possible. It is neither in our biological nature nor in the nature of the historical process for human societies to converge and become identical. Even the same society changes over time in response to changing conditions.

    There is in each and everyone of us a deep desire to be free, to experiment and to do better than others around us. Like all forms of energy, human energy must be channelled so it is constructive and not destructive. This is the challenge of governance at all levels, from the village to global institutions.

    With the world becoming a village because of ease of travel and instant communication, the design of global institutions is very important. They help us solve problems which each of us cannot solve by himself and set limits on unacceptable behaviour.

    The problem of Palestine, for example, cannot be solved without the participation of the larger global community. All of us are aware of the rights and wrongs, and sometimes particular issues are right or wrong depending on the perspective we take.

    However, we should never lose hope. We should always look for new and creative ways to break old deadlocks. After all, it was only a few years ago that the prospects for peace seemed so much brighter.

    I remember as Singapore's Trade Minister meeting the Israeli Trade Minister at Davos, Switzerland, in 2000. He said he would like to visit me in Singapore with his Palestinian counterpart and, together with me, take a boat to the Singapore industrial park on a nearby Indonesian island where he hoped we could all be received by the Indonesian Trade Minister. I worked on this project of understanding and goodwill with my Indonesian counterpart immediately. Unfortunately, within half a year, the intifada (Arabic for uprising) started and the cycle of violence got steadily worse, with each side blaming the other.

    However, we must not give up. With goodwill and statesmanship on both sides, and the support of the international community, it is possible to re-establish trust and start again.

    The international community should also not allow the deteriorating relationship across the Taiwan Strait to get out of control. The push towards independence by certain groups in Taiwan is most dangerous because it will lead to war with China and drag in other countries.

    At stake is the stability of the entire Asia-Pacific region.

    The relationship between China and Taiwan was much better in the not-too-distant past. In 1991, Apec (Asia-Pacific Economic Cooperation), which is a grouping of economies around the Pacific Ocean, admitted China, Taiwan and Hong Kong based on certain agreed principles. In 1992, I remember chatting with the trade ministers from China, Taiwan and Hong Kong over an informal lunch in Bangkok. Later in 1993, representatives from China and Taiwan met in Singapore for informal talks with both sides acknowledging 'one China, to each its own interpretation'. All problems seemed soluble then.

    However, in 1994, Taiwan's then president Lee Teng-hui gave a shocking interview to a Japanese magazine, describing himself as Moses leading his people out of Egypt. From then on, cross-strait relations went from bad to worse as pro-independence forces in Taiwan became increasingly adventurous.

    Moral authority of the United Nations

    LIKE in the Middle East, the international community has a strong vested interest in supporting a peaceful resolution of cross-strait conflict, based on the 'one China' position adopted and settled by the UN in 1971.

    Even when the UN has no legal authority to enforce its wishes, its views carry moral weight. This legitimacy of the UN is derived from its wide membership, its transparent processes and the active participation of member states. It is important that the Security Council, which has the power to pass resolutions binding on all UN member states, be reformed and enlarged to reflect the reality of the current international environment, not that of 60 years ago.

    We need rules which enable us to make decisions and express our collective judgment of right and wrong. These rules must evolve according to our collective moral sense. If any party believes it has a monopoly of truth or wisdom, that is the beginning of evil.

    While there is much we share in common as human beings wherever we live, there will never be a unanimity of views on all matters. Human society is changing all the time. New scientific discoveries throw up new challenges to which different societies must respond differently.

    On matters of religious belief, for example, no one should expect convergence. Our disagreements over issues like the death penalty, abortion, the nature of democracy, gay rights, animal rights and therapeutic cloning are in a sense inevitable and necessary.

    What we need profoundly is a respect for plurality in the world, one that is built on a common substrate which defines us as civilised human beings in the 21st century. In the 19th century, slavery was abolished. In the 20th century, gender equality became the norm. In this century, we must add more layers to what we share in common.

    Upon this shared substrate, however, we must not only accept diversity, we should encourage it. Indeed, like biological diversity, it is essential for human progress that there should be cultural and political diversity in the world. Without diversity, our ability to respond to new challenges will be weakened.

    Respecting plurality does not mean we stop recognising strengths and weaknesses. That would be hypocrisy. Some cultural values are more suited to modern times than others. Some economic systems are more productive. Some political systems are better able to mobilise the creative energies of their people. We must be honest enough to recognise that others can be superior to us in this or that area, and that it is therefore in our interest to learn from them.

    But no one should force his views on others. We should never impose a particular political or economic system on societies with different histories and traditions. What we need instead is an environment which encourages mutual learning and healthy competition. For this, we need to respect one another.

    Over four centuries ago, a wise man from the West visited the East. The Italian Jesuit, Matteo Ricci, went to China with the goal to convert China to Catholicism. He had a deep respect for the civilisation which he wanted to Christianise. Realising the only way to impress the Chinese was to interact with them on their own terms, he proceeded to study the Chinese language, master the Chinese classics and employ Chinese philosophical concepts in his discourses on Christianity.

    Instead of talking at them, he talked to them in conceptual terms they could relate to. The Lord's Prayer, for example, was rendered as if Jesus was preaching in a Chinese environment. Although Father Ricci did not succeed in converting the Chinese, he left behind a lasting legacy. When he died, the Chinese emperor consented to his burial in China and his tomb, inscribed with his Chinese name of Li Ma Dou, is to be found today in the compound of the Party School of the Beijing Municipal Committee.

    History is unending. We need wise men like Father Ricci from the West and the East, and from the South and the North, to help us prevent the clash of civilisations through debate and dialogue.

    Respecting diversity should never reduce us to cultural relativism as if all points of view are equally valid. We should never stop trying to influence one another, and here in the UN we are always trying to, but we must always be prepared to see the same issues from the perspective of others. Here in the UN, we have an institution which can help us create this better world.

    Open university doors to more students from polytechnics

    I AM about to graduate from a polytechnic and I would like to further my studies in a local university. However, the chances of my doing so are rather slim.

    I understand that the National University of Singapore, Nanyang Technological University and Singapore Management University take in only the top 5 to 10 per cent of polytechnic students.

    This means that only the top few students in each of the polytechnic courses will have the chance to enter one of the local universities.

    Unfortunately, my grades are not in the top 5 per cent and I do not have much of a chance of making it. That led me to question whether the education system could be changed for the better.

    Now, the general consensus is that better students go to junior colleges and weaker ones, to polytechnics. Thus, the difference in the priority given to JC students when it comes to admission to the local universities.

    A look at the syllabus studied by college students and polytechnic students is illuminating. The former focus on subjects that are more academic while the latter learn skills that are relevant to the workplace.

    It is evident that students in polytechnics are better equipped to study in a university as they have acquired skills earlier, compared to college students. If so, why the lack of opportunity for polytechnic students when it comes to admission to university?

    Is it because once polytechnic students graduate with a diploma, they can go out to work, with the skills they have acquired? Is the current system to provide assurance to junior-college students that they have a relatively higher chance of entering local universities?

    If the system is to provide an automatic route to universities for JC students because they do not have the necessary technical skills, I would say that it would not be very fair to students studying in schools other than JCs. A friend of mine who graduated from a JC told me that polytechnic students can step into the working world once they graduate but JC students cannot achieve much with an A-level certificate.

    If so, why waste two years in a college? Would it not be better to learn relevant skills in a polytechnic and, after getting a diploma, continue to upgrade oneself by furthering studies in a university?

    This brings me to my point. If more priority is given to polytechnic students, would more people choose a polytechnic education? Would there be a need for college education anymore? Would one go to a JC or a polytechnic if equal priority is given to both?

    Islamophobia creates the very monster it fears
    By Mafoot Simon

    THE clock was ticking close to 11pm but American scholar John L. Esposito was still fielding questions at a public forum in Singapore - and this after a long day of talks and meetings elsewhere. The founder of the Centre for Muslim-Christian Understanding in Georgetown University, Washington, is seen by many as a champion of Islam. But a member of the audience accused him of being an Orientalist - used as a derogatory term for a Western scholar who studies and distorts Islam.

    Most Muslims in the west are like other people - they want prosperity, freedom, a place to raise their family. So why see their demographic increase as a threat, asks Prof Esposito. -- BERITA HARIAN
    This was swiftly debunked by someone else in the audience, who commended him for what he had done for the Muslim world. Professor Esposito's voice cracked when he thanked his backer, and ignored his accuser. That was in 1997.

    Four years later, in 2001, Prof Esposito was the butt of criticism in a book by another American academic, Dr Martin Kramer. The book, Ivory Towers On Sand: The Failure Of Middle Eastern Studies In America, asserted that Prof Esposito gave wrong advice to the Clinton administration by underplaying the danger of Osama bin Laden and Islamic fundamentalism.

    When the Brooklyn-born Roman Catholic was in Singapore last week, we asked him about what he has described as Islamophobia in Europe.

    Talk about the Islamisation of Europe - that Europe will become part of the Arab West or the Maghreb - is growing. Historians Francis Fukuyama and Bernard Lewis are among the most prominent doomsayers. What's your view on this development?

    These doomsayers are wrong in their approach. They regrettably wind up contributing to this post-9/11 hysteria about Muslims.

    We ought to be concerned about extremists, but we also need to distinguish extremism from mainstream Islam. If we're not careful, we in Europe and America will end up seeing the significant presence of Muslims in our countries as somehow a threat or a potential threat.

    In America, we have elements of the Christian right, what I call the militant Christian right. In some ways, they are like the right-wing nationalists in Europe in terms of their attitude towards others, and particularly Muslims.

    The radical nationalists in many ways are 'anti-foreigners', and Muslims get put in that category, even those Muslims who have lived there for a long time. This is something that many of us need to speak out about and counter.

    Their worry is that the Muslim population in Europe is increasing faster than the non-Muslim population. One estimate is that there are already more than 23 million Muslims in Europe.

    Why should that be an issue? If you're talking about the growth of extremists, whoever they are, that's one thing. But why should the growth of a religion like Islam, with a growth of Muslim population, become an issue?

    The majority of Muslims in America and in Europe are like other people in terms of what they want - economic prosperity, freedom, a place to raise their family. Muslims make significant contributions as professionals and workers, the same as other ethnic and religious groups do. So why see their demographic increase as necessarily a threat?

    The fear is that it'll turn Europe into a Muslim ghetto.This argument is coming from people who are right-wing - who are not just anti-foreign, but in many ways anti-Muslim. It's Islamophobia. If - this is an important point - you were to write about the Jewish, for example, and substitute the word Judaism for Islam and Jews for Muslims, and you were to write the same kinds of pieces that these people write, use all the rest of the words, you would be accused of anti-Semitism. You couldn't do it. So why is it okay to write about Muslims that way?

    There's also a fear that anti-Semitism in Europe may be increasing.

    Some people try to equate anti-Semitism with the demographic increase of Muslims. But anti-Semitism in Europe existed long before Muslims came to Europe. Think of World War II and Hitler in Germany. In America, anti-Semitism has existed for a long time.

    At the end of the day, people who make these arguments have their own agenda. They are concerned that if you have a large Muslim population, it might become an influential political lobby.

    Isn't that a reality?

    Well, why shouldn't that be the case? In the United States, if you have a significant Italian or Greek or Turkish or Indian population, they then obviously influence politics. It's normal. The American Jewish community is also a significant influence on American elections and a major influence on America's policy towards Israel.

    So what's wrong with Muslims having influence? There is nothing wrong in principle.

    If we espouse in America and Europe things like citizenship rights and the duties and freedoms that come with it - such as freedom of speech, self-determination and human rights - then Muslims should have the same dignity, the same status and respect.

    When people deny these rights to Muslims, they are in fact feeding the notion that Islam equates with extremism.

    If they are concerned about the growth of extremism, they have to address the causes of extremism - and the causes are both foreign policy issues as well as domestic issues.

    That's what Muslim European scholar Tariq Ramadan tried to do, and he got into a problem in Europe. Now the US has denied him a visa to teach there.

    That's the irony. American and European authorities want to hear the moderate Muslims, but they also fear the moderate Muslim voice.

    Look at the problems that Professor Ramadan got into in France. He criticised some of the intellectuals saying that their support for America's invasion of Iraq was not based on universal values, but because they were of Jewish background. It was their Israeli sympathies, he said, that led them to see the invasion of Iraq as good for Israel.

    Now, one can disagree with Prof Ramadan on that, but he had a right to say what he believed. If a non-Muslim had said it, would he have been accused of being anti-Semitic? Probably not.

    It's not just Muslims who come under fire when they criticise the Iraq invasion. In the US, for example, members of Congress, members in academia, intellectuals like myself - we will all be accused of being unpatriotic or even anti-Semitic.

    Among Muslims, it's not just Prof Ramadan bearing the brunt. Look at what just happened to Mr Yusuf Islam (the former singer Cat Stevens who was deported to Britain by the US last week). What does that deportation say? What message is it sending to the Muslim world? Muslims must be asking themselves: Is there any Muslim who's 'acceptable'?

    They said he's on a watch-list and that he gives money to 'groups that might potentially support terrorists'. They weren't even saying that he gives money to groups that support terrorists.

    That means that anybody who gives money to a Muslim charity is going to worry. 'Well, wait a minute, if I give money to the Muslim charity and they're not supporting terrorists right now, will somebody in the American government say, 'Yes, but potentially they might support terrorists in the future'?' I think that this raises issues.

    You are saying that they are sidelining the very moderate Muslims they want to work with?

    Exactly. The danger is that they play right into the hands of radicals because what radicals will say in this situation is, 'See, they say one thing with their mouth, but they don't really mean it.' And so what radicals will say to moderates is: 'It doesn't matter whether you are moderate or not, these people are really anti-Islam. Well, this isn't a war against global terrorism, it's a war against Islam and the Muslim world.'

    That lends credence to the talk about the clash of civilisations.

    Absolutely right. Regrettably, it can contribute to a clash of civilisations.

    It gives opportunity to the extremists to say to the moderates: 'Look, this is the way they really are. They say they don't believe in a clash of civilisations, they say they distinguish between Islam and extremists, but don't just look at what they say, look at what they do: who they arrest and why, who they refuse visas to and why. Doesn't it look like they're equating extremism with simply being critical of American foreign policy?'

    If you're critical of American foreign policy, does that make you anti-American?

    Jimmy Carter celebrates 80th birthday

    Former president Jimmy Carter celebrates his 80th birthday Friday, basking in newfound respect both abroad and from his own Democratic party, which shunned him for many years.

    Carter is to celebrate at a low-key ceremony in the museum and bookshop of the Carter Center in Atlanta, Georgia, spokesman Jay Hakes said.

    "The event is going to be thrown up into the public, no ticket charge, and we expect a large crowd of people who want to come and enjoy the day; no effort to send out a lot of invitations, it is not orchestrated that way around big names, no attempt to urge people to come in from outside," Hakes said.

    Although he has long been known for his work supervising foreign elections, Carter has re-emerged recently as a player in US politics, with a month to go before the November 2 presidential elections.

    In an opinion piece Monday in the Washington Post, Carter said that "basic international requirements" for a fair vote are missing in Florida, scene of a bitter dispute over votes in 2000.

    Carter, a Democrat who was president from 1977-1981, led a bipartisan commission charged with recommending changes to US electoral processes following the 2000 vote dispute. The dispute ended when the US Supreme Court halted recounts of contested ballots in Florida after a weeks-long draw.

    He also was given a feature role at the Democratic party convention in July, where he delivered a stern attack on Republican President George W. Bush.

    For years, however, Carter was shunned by Democrats, who remembered him for a presidency marked by an energy crisis, the humiliation of US diplomats being held hostage in Iran and his defeat by Republican Ronald Reagan in the 1980 election, ushering in 12 years of Republican control of the White House.

    "That's someting they rather forget," said Eric Davis, a political science professor at Middlebury College in Vermont.

    Carter prefers to publicly evoke the Camp David agreement between Egypt and Israel, the normalization of relations with China, and his administration's promotion of human rights. Since leaving office, Carter has been an advocate for a number of causes, written 19 books and won the Nobel Peace Prize in 2002.

    "In the Democratic party there is a respect for the way he handled his life after the presidency, that instead of playing golf or charging a lot of money for making speeches in front of business groups, he tried to use his personal visibility and his skills to promote socially beneficial programs," said Linda Fawlert, a professor at Darmouth College in New Hampshire.

    "He was given a feature role in the convention this year in part because he has become an elder stateman, there was enough time and distance between his presidency and the present, there was also offense that the party should be ignoring him which is what they have tried to do for a long time, this was a change, there is a sense he is old enough now, he has done enough of his work in the international arena that they should reclaim him."

    Added Douglas Brinkley, author of "The Unfinished Presidency," a book about Carter's post-presidency years: "The Democratic party spent the last 25 years fleeing the legacy of Jimmy Carter, so it is significant that the party is embracing him in a new way. They are finally recognizing that he gives them something they sorely need. He represents the integrity of the Democratic party."

    Counsel idiosyncratic teachers too

    THE Ministry of Education has plans for more full-time counsellors to help students with various issues.     When more may be bad

    Chua Mui Hoong

    Competition, usually good for consumers, can result in perverse outcomes in the media sector. If unregulated, it spurs a race to the bottom and provokes biased coverage, writes CHUA MUI HOONG

    FOUR years ago, when news of the 'media liberalisation' first broke in The Straits Times, a colleague said: 'I give them two or three years, and we'll be back to square one.'

    I disagreed. I argued that the trend of injecting competition into former monopolistic industries (like telecommunications and power generation) would not be reversed. In a few years' time, I ventured rashly, there would be more players, possibly a foreign company as a junior joint-venture partner.

    Well, my colleague was right and I was wrong.

    If I had known then what I know now about the economics of the media sector, I would have ventured a different opinion.

    I'm an interested party in this issue, as an employee of print giant Singapore Press Holdings (SPH). But emotion and partisan interest aside, it's hard to gainsay the basic laws of economics. And what economics predicts about media competition, I would argue (with the benefit of hindsight), is more or less what transpired.

    Competition in a duopoly

    FIRST, economics suggests there is market failure in the media sector. That is, that economic laws of demand and supply do not work and the sector needs mediation, or even regulation.

    Much has been said already about the media sector's tendency towards a natural monopoly. In fact, it can be argued that the media sector has more of the features of oligopolistic competition (just a few big players) than a natural monopoly.

    The requirement for broadcast or printing facilities keeps fixed costs high and creates high barriers to entry, which is a classic condition of a natural monopoly.

    But unlike telecommunications or power, media products - newspapers and TV programmes - are not identical. A natural monopoly occurs when products are good substitutes for each other: it doesn't matter who the provider of gas is, so long as the gas is piped to your home when needed.

    But the media market is differentiated: that is, each company comes up with newspapers and TV programming that is different from the other.

    This gives the media market room for some competitors. But the high fixed costs involved rule out the easy entry of competitors. Hence, a market with a few big, dominant players may work best for the media. Indeed, many cities have just one or two, or at the most a handful, of media players.

    In Singapore, the media market until 2000 had a monopolistic structure: SPH was a print monopoly and MediaCorp a broadcast monopoly. The so-called liberalisation that year introduced duopolistic competition: each was allowed to cross into the other's territory.

    Several things can happen when you turn a monopoly into a duopoly. As predicted by Bertrand's Law, the two companies can compete on either price or product differentiation.

    To differentiate itself, the newcomer will analyse the market positioning of its rival and try to capture a market segment not catered to by the incumbent.

    It's no secret that SPH newspapers tend to be pro-establishment. Using a spatial model of product differentiation, where 'anti-establishment' is on the extreme left of a horizontal axis and 'pro-establishment' on the extreme right, SPH papers congregate towards the right. A smart newcomer would position itself towards the left, as being more edgy, in order to capture market share.

    That's precisely what the new MediaCorp newspaper, Today, did. It has continued to position itself as an 'alternative' to SPH's establishment paper, The Straits Times. (Whether its editorial content merits its claim to be an 'alternative' is another question).

    In so doing, Today is trying to differentiate itself from SPH newspapers to capture the market segment to the left of the spatial spectrum. This explains why Today may want to be seen as an 'alternative' even when its parent company MediaCorp is 100 per cent owned by state investment agency Temasek Holdings.

    Despite those initial attempts, media analysts have suggested that the two media companies were not able to differentiate themselves sufficiently.

    The Bertrand theorem predicts that if duopolies can't differentiate their products properly, they'll compete on price and undercut each other. There'll be a price war, which stops only when price goes down to marginal cost - when profits are zero. In the case of a media industry with high fixed costs, zero profits mean not being able to recoup the massive costs sunk into setting up the infrastructure.

    This is what happened with SPH and MediaCorp, which engaged in bidding wars to acquire TV programmes and price wars to reduce advertising rates to get customers.

    The result: both companies bled red ink from day one of competition. Both were forced into talks to merge their mass market TV and free newspaper operations, to curb the losses. The deal was announced two weeks ago.

    Advertising distorts consumer demand

    APART from the market failure caused by high barriers to entry, advertising also distorts the media market.

    In most markets, consumers pay a price for the goods they want. If you want a chair, a car, or a handbag, you decide if you're willing to pay the asking price for it, and you fork out the money.

    Producers who make the merchandise get direct feedback on their wares and prices. Consumers' purchases act as powerful signals of what they want and the prices they are willing to pay.

    The media market is different because it's mediated by advertisers. Readers don't signal how much they would be willing to pay to read an article; nor do viewers say how much they would pay to watch a TV programme (except for pay-per-view ones).

    Instead, media companies depend on advertisers for much of their revenue. Advertisers want to reach as many people as possible, so prefer programmes or newspapers that have a mass reach. The result: media outlets churn out newspapers or TV programming to draw the mass audiences or readers advertisers want.

    Niche preferences for, say, opera performances, or long commentaries like this one, do not draw large audiences or readership and are usually shunned by advertisers.

    In advertising-driven media, it's the law of numbers that counts.

    This would not be the case if consumers (readers and viewers) could signal the intensity of their demand: that is, how much they are prepared to pay for a TV programme or newspaper article.

    For example, one million people may be willing to pay 50 cents each to watch a wrestling show, which would give the TV station $500,000 for showing wrestling.

    But 100,000 people may be willing to pay $10 each to watch an opera performance, giving the TV station $1 million.

    If viewers could aggregate what they were willing to pay to watch, then the TV station would broadcast the opera programme, instead of wrestling. But the typical media market doesn't allow that. Instead, advertisers will pay more to reach the one million viewers.

    Result: a tendency for media programming (and newspaper content) to cater to the masses, which often translates into catering to the lowest common denominator. (It need not always be that way, of course, since some advertisers want to reach well-heeled niche audiences.)

    But most of the time, media companies face very real pressure to put out products that draw in the crowds - and a very real temptation to go downmarket to bring in the numbers.

    Some decision-makers have alleged that mainstream newspapers in Singapore - especially the ST - went 'downmarket' after 2000, in response to competitive pressures.

    What does going 'downmarket' mean? Usually, when people use it as a derogatory term, they are talking about the use of scandal, sex or soap (human drama) to sell newspapers.

    But the truth is that scandal, sex and soap may not be what readers want.

    Some empirical studies in the United States, such as surveys by the Pew Research Centre for the People and the Press, suggest that audiences favour entertainment programming over news. Within news programmes, audiences like crime and human interest stories, as well as disaster stories. Health stories also attract high ratings.

    'Hard news' stories about politics or government, or policies, draw smaller audiences.

    If viewer preferences are similar in Singapore, then going 'downmarket' to attract larger audiences would require TV and newspaper executives to include a large dose of entertainment, and 'soft' news programmes, and decrease the coverage of politics and government.

    In the ST, however, the number of pages devoted to political commentaries and news analyses has gone up and will increase further when the newspaper is revamped on Oct 19. (This is just one of many possible indicators of whether a paper is going 'downmarket'.)

    In the absence of empirical data, the question of whether Singapore newspapers and TV channels have gone 'downmarket' in response to competition remains just that: a question, on which there are different points of view.

    Without empirical data, we rely on economic theories to predict what may happen. In this case, economics tells us that the temptation to get large audiences is pretty much inherent in the advertising-driven media market. So, whatever moves to inject more competition into the media sector must take that dynamic into account. If too much mass content is seen as socially undesirable, then the regulator has to take steps to influence the outcome, in a way that does not censor content.

    Less competition, less choice? Not always

    AS THE above analysis shows, competition in the media market can result in a race to the bottom.

    Bilingual policy to stay, but will be adjusted

    Changes are needed to help students engage new economies, says MM Lee

    THE bilingual education policy, despite its weaknesses, has benefited Singapore and will remain.

    The challenge is to look at how to adjust it so students are given language skills to engage with the new world economies, said Minister Mentor Lee Kuan Yew.

    Without the bilingual policy, Singapore's ethnic communities will not be able to interact with each other and build understanding.

    Nor would Singapore have been able to develop a core group of people who can pass on vernacular languages and cultures to future generations, for example as teachers, writers and journalists, he said.

    Also, if Singaporeans spoke only English, or Chinese, or Malay, or Tamil, Singapore's usefulness to the world would diminish, he noted.

    Mr Lee launched into this defence of Singapore's 40-year-old bilingual policy during a meeting with clan association and trade union members, teachers and media editors.

    He called the meeting to hear their views on proposals to make Chinese teaching more enjoyable, made by a committee set up to review Chinese language teaching.

    He stressed several times that the focus on learning Chinese did not spell an end to the bilingual policy.

    English remained the dominant, working language in Singapore, he assured his audience of more than 70, who included non-Chinese editors.

    It remained government policy to equip students with English, as well as their mother tongue, whether Chinese, Malay or Tamil.

    Singapore needs a group conversant in these languages, as well as Arabic, to engage the emerging economies in the region, he said.

    'I assure you we are not going back to Chinese. This is only for specific purposes and we'll do whatever we're doing for the Chinese, for the Malays, the Indians... We must have the ability to reach out to China, to India and others... and the region around us, Indonesia.'

    Mr Lee acknowledged that making students learn two languages means many Singaporeans cannot master both.

    Citing his own experience, he said his children became fluent in both languages, after attending Chinese-stream schools for 10 years, and speaking English at home.

    But now all three work in an English-speaking environment, English has become their dominant language.

    He joked that friends such as former Cabinet minister Lim Kim San told him if his children had not become bilingual, he might have learnt earlier that most people cannot master both languages, and the problem would not have taken so long to resolve.

    Learning two languages and not being able to master both is a trade-off that must be made.

    For example, if Singapore became totally English-speaking, it would lose its value to the world. Its values would also be different.

    Even if Singaporeans learnt only English in school, they could not be as fluent as native English speakers.

    Similarly, no amount of language instruction in school can turn out Singaporeans who are as eloquent in Mandarin as those from China.

    'What we're trying to do today is to find a way to maximise the trade-off for our children.'

    A modular teaching approach allows schools to tailor teaching to students' needs, and gives parents options in choosing the kind of bilingual education they want for their children, said Mr Lee.
