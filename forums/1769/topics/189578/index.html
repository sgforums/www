    In a lot of societies, mental illnesses are still considered an 'affliction' and very much a taboo subject.

    Be it depression, disorders of thought, speech, mood and behaviour, most people (and families) would rather live in self-denial and keep it under wraps for fear of the ostracization of a cruel society.

    I find that it's such a sad situation - when we all realize that mental health equals to a mental state of well being, and very treatable when diagnosed and helped by medication, anti-depressants and pyschotherapy sessions.

    People who have problems relating to their mentality often have problems going about their daily activities, organisation, and responsibilities. When they are left alone to wallow in their 'depressed state, more often than not it comes to a point when their disorders and problems take over their already fragile stability. They can have a specific problem, or many different types all at one time.

    Imagine those times when you feel frustrated and don't know what to do. When you feel like you have reached a dead end and your existence cease to matter to yourself, and to those around you. Imagine hearing constant voices in your head - telling you what to do, how to feel, and how to react. Imagine having many different voices with conflicting opinions. Imagine being chased by taunting shadows every corner you turn.

    These people need help, and a form of understanding from society and their loved ones that they are not alone in their volatile state, but are we ready to do so?

    Can all of us be open minded enough to not speak in hushed whispers about 'the flapjack next door'? Are we able to cast aside all notions that 'mad' people are pretty much the same as you and me - apart from the fact that they have in a way, succumbed to being slaves of their own thoughts?

    Often,s the stigma sometimes attached to mental illness means that some people delay seeking help. Perhaps if we are more aware of the causes and effects of mental illnesses - and realize that it can happen to [/b]anyone;[/b] even ourselves, more people would seek help as and when they feel 'disturbed' by the silent forces of the mind.

    I think the society's attitude and mindset toward these individuals has to change. Then, and only then... perhaps more would work toward improving their ability to function, and thus, enhancing their way of life.

    As long as one has been officially diagnosed to have some psychological / mental / emotional disorder, he/she will then be put on a Suicide Precautions alert, which means that even if he/she insists on dying and refuses help, we STILL have to administer treatment, because to refuse to do so would render us professionally liable for the outcome. People with diagnosed disorders like these are considered to not be in a fit state of mind and like it or not, worthwhile or not, the onus of their safety lies on the health professionals who have been put in charge of looking after the case.

    BDD is a very real psychological disorder, listed under the official DSM-IV list of mental / psychological / mental disorders. There's nothing unreal about it. Just like Depression, it's very real. It's something you might think is constantly used as a convenient excuse by irresponsible people out to abuse the system, but to people suffering from Depression, it is very real. THey may see themselves plundering into the depths of depression, they might even try to rationalise themselves out of it in the beginning, but it is not a battle they can win alone, without the loving support of close friends and family, as well as trained professionals, and sometimes, medication too.
