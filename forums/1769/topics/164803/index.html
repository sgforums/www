    Maid prostitute case shows basic rights have to be enshrined in law

    I suspect that readers who saw the report on the conviction of an employer for prostituting her domestic worker ('Fined $15,000 for pimping her maid', ST Nov 22) would have shaken their heads and thought 'What next?'

    The case once again highlights the vulnerability of domestic workers who have the misfortune to work for unscrupulous employers.

    The defendant lawyer's claim that the worker was 'a willing party ready to provide sexual services so that she could make extra income to repay her debt to her agent' should not go unchallenged.

    Very few women who engage in prostitution do so by free choice. Many are physically coerced while others are driven by economic or social circumstances.

    In this instance, it was reported that the worker in question was not physically coerced into prostitution but she may have felt that she had no choice but to cooperate with her employer.

    The burden of a debt that typically takes three to five months for a domestic worker to pay off before she can start sending money home to her family weighs heavily on her mind.

    She feels under pressure not only to send money as soon as she can but also to avoid any problems with her employer that might result in her being sent back home or returned to her agency. This would have extended her indebtedness.

    Many workers in this situation are unwilling to express their true opinions if they want to go against their employers or reject their proposals. The choices they make are not free.

    These include the choices they make when they seek work and are told that they will not be employed unless they agree to having no days off or doing almost anything required by an employer.

    A second issue that arises from this case is the role of the employment agency that provided the worker to the employer. It was reported that the police acted after it lodged a complaint about what was going on.

    It did the right thing and that is commendable. It kept the agency on the right side of the law.

    Section 23(d) of the Employment Agencies Act states that an agency licensed under the Act that 'knowingly sends, directs or takes any girl or woman to any place for immoral purposes or to a place where she is likely to be morally corrupted, shall be guilty of an offence.'

    Section 24 - (2) stipulates the penalties to which an offending agency can be subject: 'A licensee or any person who is guilty of an offence under section 23 (d) shall be liable on conviction to a fine not exceeding $5,000 or to imprisonment for a term not exceeding two years or to both.'

    In this instance, the agency could legitimately argue that it did not knowingly send a woman into prostitution, but it must certainly be the case that once it became aware of her position, it could have jeopardised its own status if it had not taken the responsibility to inform the police.

    This underlines a point that TWC2 (Transcient Workers Count Too) has been arguing for some time: basic rights and protection for domestic workers need to be ensured under the law.

    Among these, we have consistently urged for their right to have a day off each week and a limit on the length of the working day. An avenue must also be provided for a domestic worker who feels wronged to seek help and advice.

    We have heard the arguments that this is best left to market forces and that many protective measures for domestic workers are not practical because the abuses take place in the privacy of people's homes.

    In this case, the legal provisions existed to prevent a form of abuse and they may well have played a key part in stopping it even though it occurred within the employer's home.

    It may be hard to enforce some provisions but their very existence help to dispose those who have responsibilities under them to see that they are implemented.
