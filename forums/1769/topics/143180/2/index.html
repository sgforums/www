    Wow! It looks like panhandlers in Singapore are taking a cue from their counterparts in the US.

    It's no secret that the overwhelming majority of street panhandlers here are nothing more than common criminals and street thugs. They'll usually approach you with some sob story about their predicament, and hit you up for cash.

    The more common opening lines they'll throw at you include:

    "My car broke down and I need cab fare to go get help"

    "My mother is sick and I need cab fare to go visit her."

    "I haven't eaten in several days."

    "I need money for medicine."

    And when you politely refuse or just ignore them, they'll invariably cuss up a storm and then slither away to find their next victim.

    Sometimes, a confrontation with a "panhandler" can actually be a precursor to a robbery attempt, as a colleague and I found out a couple of winters ago. We had emerged from a cineplex after a late night showing and were walking back to his vehicle in an adjacent parking ramp, when a couple of brutish-looking negroes emerged from the shadows and approached us. One of them asked brusquely if we could spare them some cash as their "ride" had apparently broken down and they needed cab fare to get home. My colleague politely declined and we walked past them, but the two thugs started following us while hurling insults and obscenities. By the time we got to the vehicle, the obscenities had turned into demands for cash, and the two thugs were getting menacingly close to us.

    I should mention that both my colleague and I have concealed handgun permits, and we were both carrying that night. When the two thugs ignored our demand to not get any closer, my colleague unholstered and drew his handgun, and I began to reach for mine. I'll give the two negroes credit for knowing when to back down from a volatile situation, as they immediately turned tail and ran off.

    It took a while for the adrenaline to wear off and I hope never to have to go through something like this again. This incident has caused me to take a very skeptical view of panhandling.

    MG

    There used to be a guy going around Margaret Drive, asking folks for money. I never gave him anything because I didn't have good vibes about it. One of my friends gave him $2 one day and I chided her for it but I needn't have to do it because the very next instant, this man went up to order kopi for himself, sat down right in front of us, and sipped his kopi happily, watching soccer on tv at the same time! A stallholder then came up to tell us that this man is a regular cheat who preys on soft-hearted folks. You should have seen the look of shock on my friend's face.

    I've been approached before, for bus fare and what-not. I always tell them, 'Hey, approach the police, they'll help and they might even give you a ride home!'. Usually, they will be for a while, but then they'll try to convince me again so, I'll just repeat that same sentence, and if need be, repeat it again and again like some robot, until they realise that I'm not going to be their next victim, then they'll move off.

    I'm so relieved that when I was in the US, I wasn't approached by any of the 'street beggars'. I've heard accounts from my American friends of how they were robbed after being approached by one of them. My favourite 'defence' move is, to always look like I'm mighty pissed or royally angry if I'm moving around alone. I always make sure to NEVER look vulnerable / lost / timid when I was there!! I might have been shivering in my shoes when I was out and about alone in the States but I never ever showed it in my face. I try to fit in, and put on this 'been there, done that' look to blend in with the crowd. I was lost on a few occasions but if I had to backtrack, I always put on this bored look and confident swagger to show whoever was observing that hey! I'm not lost... I meant to walk this way!

    MG, your encounter was really scary! I'm glad though, that you and your friend are safe!
