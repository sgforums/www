Email forwards: Myth or Fact?

  • SydneyLibrarian

    I receive quite a number of forwarded emails, with stories (and sometimes pictures) attached, followed by "Don't take this!" or "Don't go there!"; "Beware of this, that, and the other!"

    While some of them may be true, some are just hoaxes (like emails giving info on false computer viruses)

    Let's start a thread on the type of emails we've received, and point each other to the right direction on whether it's a hoax, or is what we received really factual! 

    I'll start the ball rolling; got this in an email today:

    -----

    Hi Everyone

    I am passing this email forwarded to me this morning. It's about a medication that is familiar to many of us. I received this information from a friend whose mother recently passed away. Apparently, this was caused by a medication that is deadly. Here are the details and I suggest you pass it on to your loved ones and others. 

          Subject: Phenylpropanolamine (PPA)

           I would like to thank those of you who expressed condolences on the recent passing of my mother. She suffered a hemorrhagic stroke while she was driving home from my house on 7/30 and passed away on 8/3. My mother's stroke and passing was an enormous shock to my family because she did

    not have any symptoms or risk factors for a stroke. Just the week before she had gone to her doctor for a check up and received a clean bill of health. She did, however, develop a cold not while she was visiting me and had taken Alka Seltzer Cold Plus for 3 days. Since her passing, we have learned that Alka Seltzer is one of the many cold medicines that contains Phenylpropanolamine (PPA) which can cause hemorrhagic stokes or cerebral bleeding even with the first use. I am forwarding a list of other medications that currently use PPA. These medicines are supposedly being recalled but my mother just purchased this medication less than two weeks ago. Pharmaceutical companies have known about this danger for years, we unfortunately, did not.
