    Originally posted by Nelstar:
    I detest the red packets scheme. A shame that guest turning up had to give them.

    Thing is.. I think a lot of people put too much emphasis on angpows - for good or for bad. People think too much about how much they should put in, how much they want people to put in, how much is appropriate, how much give then don't lose face and all. That's probably cos we are chinese are obsessed with money and obsessed with how money 'looks like' either negatively or positively.

    A red packet on its own is harmless. It's just a gift. You didn't force the person to give an amount. And whatever amount a person gave you (small or large), appreciate it for what its worth - a gift. Some of my guests didn't give me a angpow at all, they brought gifts such as bibles, photo albums etc, which are a culture in their countries. That's ok - again - its the thought and blessing that counts.

    Some of my friends couldnt come to my wedding but also sent a cash gift. I appreciate that thought, and would treat them to a home cooked meal to show my appreciation.
    I would prefer to give cash to newly weds if possible because I know they have a lot of expenses and I rather give them something they can use, UNLESS I know the couple very well and know exactly what they want. I am sure you have given gifts to your friends and feel joy that your gift have made them happy. In return, I think the ability to accept gifts graciously and don't feel that you owe people is important too.

    And that's really it. It doesn't have to be crass or materialistic. It's just money. It's people's obsession with it that corrupts the gift.

    As long as you take it in the right spirit, an angpow is a gift given with best intentions by people who care about you.

    We've become enthused a couple of times over the years about getting married, but something has always come along that's a tad more urgent. Like a mortgage. The mortgage is retreating at a glacial pace as it should. My partner's ever patient mother is waiting for me to make an honest man of her son although she is far too tactful to say so. Of course, we are not planning to get hitched for her sakes.

    We are adamant to keep the thing relatively intimate. So we set out to draft a list which proved to be unexpectedly, hideously tricky. Well meaning friends advised us to invite only the people we really wanted to share our day with. Great advice, if you don't mind sparking off 25 family feuds. Because nobody wants to spend an afternoon with their scary great aunt but your mother will disown you if you let her off the list. And so it goes on. You can't invite some cousins and not others. You can't throw ex-partners plus their respective new loves into the same confined space.

    Then there is the budget. Our parents, generous souls keep offering to pay for the whole bleedy shebang. Which is very nice and all, but sort of unconscionable given that my partner and I are grown adults with dogs, mortgages and station wagons. We are not blushing youths taking that first tentatve step on the path to connubial bliss.

    Meanwhile my bloke and I will flounder about marital possibilities for a bit longer. It's bound to put us off for another couple of years.
