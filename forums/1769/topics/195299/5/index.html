    That's a bit of an exaggeration.

    A fully laden 747 is perfectly capable of taking off and climbing out (albeit at reduced performance) with an engine out. This is an FAA requirement--all multii-engined commercial aircraft have to demonstrate adequate take-off performance with an engine inoperative, to be certified for operation in US airspace.

    If the engine failure occurs after "decision speed" (known as V1 in aviation parlance), the pilot continues with the take-off and retrims the aircraft for a three-engine climbout. At this point, most airline operational procedures call for fuel to be dumped overboard to bring the aircraft's weight down below the maximum landing weight, and an expedited (not emergency) landing to be performed.

    If the engine failure occurs before V1 (as it most likely did in your scenario), standard procedure is to abort the take-off. When the flight crew calculate the performance numbers prior to the flight, runway length requirements are determined based on an aborted takeoff at V1, so there's no question as to having adequate runway length to safely stop the aircraft.

    Fire containment and suppression systems on modern turbofan powerplants have advanced to the point where uncontained and sustained engine fires are almost unheard of. I'm willing to bet that what you experienced was a relatively rare phenomenon called compressor stall, wherein airflow into the engine is disturbed or interrupted, causing the turbine to essentialy backfire and throw some flame out the jetpipe. A compressor stall prior to V1 would have resulted in an engine shutdown and aborted take-off.

    So, to sum it up, in your scenario, if the "fire" had occurred 30 seconds after take-off, it's hightly doubtful you would have "crashed and burst into flame." Also, if the passengers didn't deplane for a full hour after the 747 rolled to a stop, the "emergency" couldn't have been that serious.

    Question: Are engine failures common during take-off? Kindly explain what the captain does when an engine fails while taking off. Can the captain continue down the runway and take off on only one engine or do they have to remain on the ground and hope they have enough runway to stop?
    Â— M.J. Harris, Danville, Va.

    Answer: Engine failures during takeoff seem to be a major cause of concern for passengers as I've been asked this question several times. I can give you several reasons to put this fear to rest.

    Firstly, engine failure on takeoff is something that doesn't happen very often. In over 32 years of flying, I've not only never experienced one, but I don't even know any pilots who've mentioned having had one. I'm not saying they're impossible or that they never happen Â— just that they happen very rarely. Engine reliability is quite remarkable.

    The second reason is that though rare, it's one of the most highly practiced maneuvers pilots learn in simulator training.

    So if it happens rarely, why do pilots train for it so much? Because if it does happen, it's extremely important to execute the maneuver correctly.

    Pilots practice it every time they go in for recurrent training, and it is a mandatory maneuver for check-rides during which pilots demonstrate on a regular basis that they can deal with various emergencies and execute normal procedures.

    If an engine were to fail during takeoff, a pilot would keep the airplane going straight down the runway by adding rudder to compensate for the loss of thrust on one side. The airplane I fly Â— the B-777 Â— is so smart that it will put in its own rudder depending on exactly how and where the engine failed. The pilot backs this up with rudder input (which is done with our feet on the rudder pedals) if necessary. Once airborne, the autopilot may be turned on after the pilots ensure the airplane is stabilized and gaining altitude. The autopilot helps fly the airplane while the pilots run the appropriate checklists.

    On every takeoff there is a certain speed, known as V1 (where V is "velocity") that is calculated ahead of time based on factors like aircraft weight, wind, flap settings, runway conditions, air temperature, etc. Calculations are made by the pilots using performance charts or, on some aircraft, through the aircraft computer.

    If an engine fails prior to reaching V1, pilots will abort the takeoff. After V1, it's safer to continue. For these reasons V1 is sometimes referred to as the "go, no-go" speed. Under all but the most extraordinary of circumstances, if the aircraft speed is V1 or greater, the pilot will continue the takeoff.

    Airplanes are perfectly capable of continuing a takeoff with a failed engine, and it's a far safer situation to become airborne with an engine out than to abort a takeoff at a high speed and run out of runway. Once airborne, the pilots have the time they need to deal with the situation in an orderly manner and prepare to land again with a full length of runway at their disposal. When the weather is bad, there is a designated alternate for each takeoff. This is where we'd go to land if landing at the airport of departure isn't possible.

    Both emergency and normal procedures are briefed by each crew prior to every flight. Each airline has its own procedures, but the bottom line is that pilots are trained to expect the worst. So, even though it happens rarely, pilots are always prepared for engine failure Â— just in case.
