    The Characteristic Of Sound

    After extensive tweaking, room tuning and vibrational control recently , I have formulated my own principles and come to several conclusions.

    1. Good sound can be had easily and cheaply if you put in a little effort.

    All materials have their own sonic characteristics and we can use them to good effect.
    I used bitumen sheet and aluminium foil to dampen the vibrational effect of my audio rack with excellent results. You just need to determine how much and placement.

    By placing some aluminium foil (those used for insulating air-con ducts and heat control) on the platform of my audio rack, I am shocked to discover that my sound had come alive, even though my system had already sounded lively in the first place.

    So I used more of these aluminium foils, and eventually discontinued the cellulose sponge tweak.

    Initially, I secured the foil by taping them onto the platform rack and there was an immediate loss of air and richness to the sound fabric. I removed the masking tape immediately.

    Air matrix will lend dimension to your sound fabric
    - my sound consultant had already understood its importance even before I do and she is only Sec. 1.

    So this means if you diy your cables and interconnects, having air matrix to work for you is a good thing.

    Everything has its own sound characteristics.
    Those that feels good to the touch, is very likely to perform well sonically - egs. include cellulose sponge. But I think natural sea sponge is better. They are harder to find now. Sold in pharmacies.

    Aluminium is very good - it will liven and enrich your sound and give it a natural balance.

    Ebony is good. In fact any kind of wood is good. It will help diffuse the sound and give it a more organic feel. I simply think hardwood perform better.
    The guitar and piano makers use spruce and rosewood to good effect.

    Carbon is good - it adds more transparency and clarity.

    Good quality cables - high purity copper or annealed / high grade silver is a must.
    And stratospheric cables are for the rich and famous.
    It is clever geometry and exotic materials like aluminium and gold that give these cables a leading edge.
    It is also possible to achieve the same leading edge effect by cleverly implementing tweaks like the aluminium foil, carbon and ebonys.

    If I had to choose between marble and granite, I will chose neither.
    There is another material, they call basalt stone or lava stone - I strongly believe it will better the above two.
    I had the good fortune to walk bare footed on basalt stones. It feels very, very good and have health benefits. My relative uses alot of it. His system sound was very much enhanced with these lava-stone.

    Crystals - rutilated quartz, rose quartz, etc. they easily add to the sound performance, no doubts about that. Just need to explore placement position for optimum effect.
    My friend uses rose quartz embedded onto wooden footers under his cdp.

    Foam - high quality foam in speakers will eliminate some of the bass boom found in china made cotton stuff speakers.

    Disc Demagnetisers - they enrich the sound. The more transparent your system, the more they will show up. I demagnetise my discs everytime before I played it. It offers a richer sound.

    Speakers cabinet - solid wood not only look good but will perform head and shoulders over dressed up veneer plywood, imho.

    An accidental discovery
    Recently, I redo my ebony placement in 2 corners of my room. I have discovered that placing the ebonys 1" - 2" away from the corners would offer a better resonating effect - translates into richer ambience.

    When everything was going nicely, I decided to realign the 2 ambient light lamps - now sitting on a spare dac and a dictionary. Then I discovered the sound had deteriorated.
    I had to exert and spent a fair bit of time to fine tune the lamps and the dictionary placement to get back to the original sound with my sound consultant in tow.

    To create a equal height for the lamp platform, I had placed a 4" width maple strip somewhat diagonally under the dictionary.
    By adding two vibrapods under the lamp side of the dictionary, I discovered the vibralto was back in play.
    I had lost some of the vibralto with all these new equations.
    These lamp shades are not connected to any equipment, yet they influence the soundscape.

    Simply put, just about anything that you placed in your room that are near to your source or speakers can be tweaked because they do influence the sound more.

    I have added 3 new aluminium foil in the rear wall carpeted space between my 2 amplifiers. It is concealed by my cotton towel drapes but they sure make the sound come alive like the sound of music.

    These last 3 aluminium pieces, I improvised by wrapping kitchen aluminium foil around art drawing paper - this is as cheap as it gets.
    Good tweaks can perform sonically well and cost very little and will certainly raised your system performance to reference level for next to nothing.

    Even my Joni Mitchell "Hits" disc - which I had put away for awhile because of poor recording, sounded shrill and sharp to my ears - is now not only listenable but enjoyable.

    Bell tweak does significantly enrich the sound. Proper implementation is essential. I think getting a good brass bell will resonate better than my ice-cream bell. Given the opportunity will explore further with other bell materials. Since I already started by tweaking the lamp and the dictionary that the lamp sits on, I might as well stretch myself further, but only with the help of my consultant, who is not only decisive, but razor sharp and surefooted. Otherwise, it is tough.

By tilting this projected plane, more up or more down, the tonal balance of Katie Melua's voice changes accordingly. When I got to Katie's tone, as I used to hear it, I secured the position with the barest of double sided masking tape.
