Throw off the shackles of misplaced shame

    The Business Times
    Published June 11, 2005
    By SIOW LI SEN

    BAD luck is just bad luck; nothing to be ashamed of. And being different from other people should not be seen as a matter of shame, either.

    But too often these days in middle-class Singapore where we all seem so preoccupied with status, shame - or the fear of it - rules.

    One recent example of misplaced shame was a newspaper picture I saw illustrating an article about single mothers and their uphill struggle against discriminatory policies and a society that views their situation as shameful.

    The young woman in question was pictured from behind, so as to be unrecognisable. It could be, of course, that it was a posed picture, with the mother and baby not actually part of a single-parent family.

    But, in any case, the editors who selected the photograph were well aware of the potential for shame; they could after all have used a real photograph of a recognisable young woman unashamed of her status, if they could have found one. It is so sad that the woman in the picture could not proudly show off her baby.

    Discrimination against single mothers - which adds to the humiliation for them and their babies - can take the form of denying them public housing.

    In practice, applications from such women are sometimes granted - but after much pleading and months of effort that may include intervention from a Member of Parliament.

    To force these women into having to plead for housing, by handling their applications 'case by case', seems wasteful of official time, as well as adding to the young mothers' feelings of disgrace. It is all so unnecessary.

    If we are not careful, Singapore can end up a nation where too many people fear shame - a feeling not confined to underprivileged people or the outcasts from society.

    I know of a family here where the father is a millionaire, and a daughter with poor qualifications found it impossible to get the job she thought she should have.

    It seemed her inaction was out of shame that she might not succeed because of her very successful father and other siblings.

    So she languishes at home, perhaps seeing it as less dishonourable to be unemployed than to work in a low status job.

    Things can be so different, when people are not ruled by this misplaced sense of honour. In Hong Kong recently, I was reminded of the sort of attitude to adversity that can make a city vibrant.

    My aunt's driver is also a factory owner in China. The Asian financial crisis meant that the man's factory in neighbouring Dongguan shrank from employing several hundred workers to one with about 30 workers. Mr Chen was, before the 1997 crisis, considered a minor tycoon; two years ago, he decided to augment his uncertain income by working as a chauffeur.

    Talking to Mr Chen was refreshing, with his attitude so different from what we are used to in Singapore. At no time did I sense that he felt any shame at holding a lowly job as a driver in brand-conscious Hong Kong.

    Another positive example: The wife of my husband's nephew has taken a job as cleaning lady in the software company her husband works for - a company which his father co-founded.

    The wife, at home minding the two young children during the day, could only work in the evenings after her husband gets home. The cleaning job came up, she wanted the extra money, so she decided to go for it.

    They just get on with their lives and shame does not enter the picture. She hopes that when the children are older, she will have time to go for training and then get better-paid work.

    In Singapore, we have become too judgmental. Alternative lifestyles (a phrase which was once used only to describe gay people but now apparently includes single mothers and any non-standard household) have to be either condemned or pitied, before they are accepted. At least that seems to be the public perception.

    In making Singapore a safe city for families, along the way we have become a little obsessed with Kodak perfect compositions. The concept of what's ideal, the 'right' jobs, or that a family unit has to be made up of a man, wife and children, is so out of touch.

    Single mothers have always been around, as indeed have gays. Hard work, including a willingness to get our hands dirty, jobs and social mobility are what built Singapore. Not shame.

Sometimes, the circumstances under which a couple got together wasn't 'solid' and the foundation was shaky in the first place so if you don't have strong pillars, it's just a matter of time before the roof collapses in on you and then, it'll be a matter of putting an early end to a bad mistake.     Remember Maya, that Singapore Idol Finalist? She was 'different'. She was a single mom at what... 21 years old? She proudly claimed that it was a 'planned pregnancy'. She works hard to support herself and her toddler now.

    Did you hear the comments people have made about her? Some called her a 'loose woman', 'so hiao at such a young age', and other non-flattering comments. We go around imposing our own value systems and moral values on others at times and forget that each of us have the freedom of choice and as long as one takes responsibility for the consequences of one's actions and decisions, it's really not our place to judge harshly. True, we might never live our lives the way Maya chooses to, we might even not dress the way she does, behave the way she does, etc. but that's her life and it's her choices and she's bravely supporting herself and her son without begging for public pity nor govt handouts, which in itself, is admirable.

    So, whilst I might not agree with her lifestyle and choices, I have to respect her for her dignity.

    We need to live and let live. Just because someone doesn't fall into our mould and expectations, it doesn't mean that they're any lesser.

    Think of it this way, wouldn't it be better to extend a hand to help in whatever way we could, to those who are reaching out for help, than to slam the door in their faces, and force them to make drastic decisions like ... God forbid! suicide?

    The Government does need to stop trying to compartmentalise us into neat little boxes, but this change in mindset has to start with you and me.
