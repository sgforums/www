Only trying matters...or does it?

  • Kuali Baba
    29 Nov 07, 03:38
    An interesting topic cropped up on the other forum I visit. One member claims that the standard of sport is falling in places where the politically-correct brigade have a stronghold. Kids lose their fighting spirit when they're told that taking part is the most important thing, and not winning. In the hopes of keeping their interests and participation up they are advised to 'enjoy' it, rather than sulk or fight to develop their skills further if they lose.

    This can also be applied to education, where the clowns try to keep people from failing not by making them work harder, but by making it so obscure through 'competency learning'/streaming, or ditching the usual alphabetical grades for 'satisfactory/unsatisfactory'. It becomes difficult to distinguish who has real mastery of the subject.

    What's all this for, to massage some egos? Sometimes I wonder whether we've gone too far with 'compassion'. People should be put in their place when they clearly lack any aptitude in a particular area, and not patronised. In our local contest it may be called 'spoiling the market'. It may kill off a kid's interest in the sport or subject, but they should learn to deal with it, train or try something else.

    So the point is: people should be made to face up to their weaknesses; anything that happens as a result (like choosing a subject that is easier to score in) is just a reflection of their character. What do you guys think? You can relate this to our experiences in school in P.E. or art class, for instance.
