    in other news....there will be Geminid Meteor appearing tonight....

    Geminid Meteor Shower 2008
    It seems that we just can't catch a break for a meteor shower this year. Most of the major meteor showers of 2008 have been diminished by a full or mostly full Moon. Unfortunately, the Geminid meteor shower of December 2008 will also be under a full Moon. Ironically, the Moon will also be in Gemini, were the meteor shower's radiant is located. The radiant is the imaginary point from which the meteors appear to come from. The illustration at right shows the shower's radiant position and where to look for meteors. Even with the bright full Moon, I would still check this meteor shower out if you've got clear skies. The Geminids have never disappointed me, and even through the moonlight, you should be able to see the brighter Geminid meteors.

    When?
    The Geminid meteor shower has a very broad maximum peak. The night of December 13th will most likely be the best night to observe the meteor shower. The best time to view a meteor shower is in the late night to early morning hours. This is because as the Earth rotates, we are moving away from the meteors during dusk and moving toward them during dawn. Therefore, a viewing time of 2 AM local time can often be the best time to view meteor showers. If this is inconvenient, however (we all have to sleep sometime), the meteors will be visible starting at dusk.

    Where?
    So to see these meteors, just go outside after 10 PM local time or so, and sit in a reclining lawn chair or other comfortable seat. Bring nice, warm clothes and dress in layers, because its going to be cold. Face your chair to the East and look about half-way to overhead to see the most meteors. If you can see enough meteors, try and find the shower's radiant (trace each meteor backwards to find the radiant). The Geminids are also one of the few meteor showers that regularly display colored meteors, also, with colors ranging from green to blue, and sometimes even yellowish.

    Clear skies!

    With an average of 75 meteors per hour radiating from near the bright star Castor, this shower is usually one of the year’s best. But the presence of the Moon, a mere two days past full, will spoil the show though bright meteors will pierce the moonlight
