    Malaysia may build nuclear power plant
    KUALA LUMPUR - MALAYSIAN utility company Tenaga may construct the country's first nuclear power plant at a cost of US$3.1 billion (S$4.2 billion) but is braced for objections from the public.

    The head of Tenaga's nuclear energy taskforce, Dr Mohamad Zam Zam Jaafar, said Malaysia going nuclear post-2020 was looking more and more like a necessity rather than a probability in view of the high oil prices.

    'We are looking at about 10 billion ringgit (S$4.18 billion) for a 1,000MW plant,' Dr Mohamad Zam Zam was quoted as saying by The Edge financial daily newspaper.

    'The government has asked Tenaga to look at nuclear power.'

    Deputy Prime Minister Najib Razak had said last month that Malaysia might consider nuclear power to meet its long-term energy needs amid surging global oil prices.

    Currently, half of Malaysia's power plants run on gas. Other sources include coal and hydropower.

    With surging oil prices, nuclear energy looks attractive, as electricity from nuclear power plants in comparison is much cheaper.

    In fact, nuclear energy is expected to play a large part in the country's National Energy Blueprint, which is due to be launched next month, reported The Edge.

    Dr Mohamad Zam Zam said the taskforce was discussing the plant's location and how to source uranium, adding that Tenaga anticipated the public would object to the plant.

    'A lot has to be done to change the public mindset when it comes to nuclear,' he said.

    Tenaga would likely enter into a joint venture with an 'experienced party to build its very first plant'.

    He said nuclear power is not a new issue with Tenaga.

    The national power provider had set up a division to look at nuclear energy as early as the 1970s.

    However the easy availability of gas, along with the controversies surrounding nuclear power, meant that any plans were shifted to the back burner for the time being.

    Last year, the government said it would build South-east Asia's first nuclear monitoring laboratory to allow scientists to check the safety of atomic energy programmes in the region.
