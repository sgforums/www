    Yesterday, in The New Paper, I read a touching account of a lady and her li'l son who waited for the people who cleared rubbish in her neighbourhood to turn up so they could give them packets of cold Ribena drinks just to show appreciation for the work that they do.

    I don't think I can reproduce the article written by Leong Ching in its entirety, though I really wish I could cause it is such a meaningful article, but here is an excerpt :

    ""Remember, when the truck comes by later, the uncles will come and pick up our rubbish. It will be smelly but please don't shout that it's smelly," I told him.

    ""Why?" he asked.

    ""Otherwise, they will be sad," I told him.

    ""Why?" he asked.

    ""Because they are doing something miportant to us, and they get thirsty, So we are helping by giving them a drink. We don't want them to feel that they are doing something that is smelly," I explained desperately.

    Wow! You see a lot of people walking past the rubbish trucks / trolleys with utter disdain on their faces. We seldom hear of, nor see a fellow Singaporean teaching his/her children to respect the domestic help, much less a 'waste management worker'. I really applaud this lady for teaching her son to respect all humans, regardless of status, race, and occupation. Too many parents pass teach their children, either consciously or unwittingly, to segregate between the have's and have-not's, the 'successful' and the 'failures' in society. We definitely need more enlightened parents like this lady around to ensure that our society remains gracious!

    This is definitely something to think about as we go about our busy lives.

    The point is, they are humans too, but they usually get disrespected by the majority so the lady is just being considerate and teaching her son likewise.

    These days, there's too much emphasis on "What do I get out of this?" I guess that's the crux of your question. True, by distributing drinks to them, you do not gain anything in return, but if life has been reduced to a very utilitarian "I-Give-So-That-I-Can-Get" kind of mentality, then that's truly something to bemoan and be a tad ashamed about.

    It's always good to stop now and then to say "Thanks!" to those who work hard alongside us, but who are in a worse situation than we are in. In moving forward ourselves at a fast, blinding pace, we should not turn a cold, blind eye to those who are falling by the wayside.

    It doesn't take a lot of time nor money, but I'm sure that kind gesture sure means a lot to the workers that day!
