    Singapore “Just Like Home”: North Korea
    Posted on Monday, July 21, 2008
    Topic: International News
    by Sam Gai Tang

    Political observers were surprised that Singapore was chosen as the venue for high level nuclear disarmament talks between North Korea and the US.

    But North Korean diplomats have said that Singapore is the “perfect place” because it’s “just like home.”

    When pressed for reasons, North Korean Embassy spokesman Kim Dam Ill provided an entire list:

    1. Singapore and North Korea are both one party states.

    2. The people of Singapore and North Korea both revere their Great Leader – or else.

    3. In both Singapore and North Korea, the Great Leader’s son is now the Dear Leader.

    4. Both governments hold the same views on secrecy: the Government Investment Crap of Singapore refuses to reveal details about the assets it manages, while the North Korean government refuses to reveal details about its uranium enrichment programme.

    5. In both Singapore and North Korea, there is a great gap between the lives of the average citizen and the political and social elite.

    6. Leaders of both countries believe there is a conspiracy to do them in.

    7. The people of both countries are always hungry.

    “I am sure I can come up with more,” said Mr. Kim. “But I have to go for buffet at Seoul Garden. Once I go back to Pyongyang, I don’t know when I can eat again.”

    In the meantime, the US Embassy was asked why it agreed to Singapore as the venue, considering its divergent stance on freedom of expression.

    “Well, we have our differences, but we also have a lot in common,” said US Embassy spokesman Dubb L. Standdards. “I can give you a list…”

    1. In both Singapore and the US (and come to think of it, North Korea too), the current leader is the son of a former leader.

    2. In both Singapore and the US, no matter what we say, not everyone gets a fair trial.

    3. Despite both being former British colonies, the people In Singapore and the US speak English funny.

    4. Both Singapore and the US have jailed so-called terrorists off-shore: Singapore on Sentosa, and the US on Guantanamo.

    5. Neighbouring countries always say they hate the US and Singapore, but their people always try to come and work there.

    6. The people of both countries are very into food.

    7. Come the end of the year, both Singapore and the US will have brown-skinned Presidents…

    LTA to Introduce Means Testing for ERP
    Posted on Sunday, July 20, 2008
    Topic: Local News
    by Chee Ken Wing

    In response to complaints that the ERP system unfairly penalizes motorists who have no choice but to commute to the CBD daily, the Lame Transport Authority (LTA) has announced that selected ERP gantries will adopt means testing instead.

    From 1 August, 20 ERP gantries will be reconfigured to detect the annual income of motorists and charge fees that are commensurate with their income bracket.

    For example, a Minister earning $1.9 million annually will pay a per-entry fee of $190 during peak hours, while a taxi driver earning $30,000 a year will pay just $3.

    "We want to make sure that CEOs and political leaders bear a cost that is roughly equivalent to that of the typical man-on-the-street," BG (NS) Yum Mar Nee, Chief Executive, LTA, said to reporters.

    When queried about the sudden change in policy, especially since the revised ERP rates would now hurt the rich, secretary to the Minister of Transport Mr. Alexander Hampshire said, "Our Ministry and the LTA are situated comfortably outside the CBD area, and we want to encourage other government agencies to move out of prime land and into the heartlands."

    University undergraduate Ms. Whyte-Elle Fan, 18, was overjoyed that means testing would soon determine ERP rates. "Since I have an annual income of nothing, I can drive my Ferrari into the Bras Basah area at no cost," she squealed in joy.

    However, things are not as rosy for Ms. Fan's father. He is a permanent secretary at a government ministry, and has to pass through 2 ERP gantries to get to work. That's because there's a gantry outside their family condo in Bukit Timah and another outside his office in the CBD. Mr. Fan's one-way trip would now cost $80, or about $40 per gantry, if he commutes by car.

    Mrs. Kee Toh Loh, a housewife who is happy waiting 25-minutes for a feeder bus, had no sympathy for the highly-paid. She explained, "If these CEOs and rich uncles can't afford to pay the ERP, they should just take public transport."

    Indeed, SBS Transit and SMRT are in the final stages of planning a super premium bus service that would ferry highly-paid individuals from their plush bungalows in Bukit Timah to downtown areas such as Shenton Way.

    "You can't expect us multi-millionaires to stand side-by-side with an old auntie carrying empty aluminium cans on the bus, can you?" asked Ms. Lily Atas, a lawyer earning a six-figure salary.
