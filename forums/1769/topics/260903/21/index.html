    In any case, as the authors of the paper Fertility & The Real Exchange Rate see it, the link between the two 'seems intuitive' enough. Child-rearing means greater spending and reduced savings, and children in themselves are consumers rather than producers, so having fewer babies means higher national savings.

    If savings rise and investment also falls - due to a smaller population - the current account improves, and the exchange rate typically weakens in response.

    This much seems straightforward - and the links between fertility and savings, investment and the exchange rate, have been well explored in economic literature, the authors (a Berkeley professor and a MAS economist) note. What's different about their paper is - their findings, based on data from 87 countries between 1975 and 2005 - stem from empirical evidence, rather than theory.

    And, even after taking into account the usual reasons why exchange rates move - such as the effects of government policy, trade reforms - they still find that a one-point decline in the fertility rate spells a 0.15 per cent fall in the real exchange rate. The result is plausible and also robust, the authors assert. Clearly, the study is meant to add to the understanding and debate about the profound social and economic impact of what appears to be inevitable, and perhaps irreversible, demographic change in many economies.
