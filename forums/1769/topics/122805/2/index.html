    I agree with Rhonda that we are no longer cavemen/cavewomen.

    Our level of needs and wants have evolved and we should no longer think like neanderthals. If survival or money becomes the reasons a couple got married, then it's no wonder if one of them bails when these reasons are threatened. There are many ways to survive in this modern world. Ever noticed single working men/women are often living a 'better' life? They don't have children to worry about and this itself is a huge money saver. Then wouldn't you say that they are surviving better?

    The decision to be together for life should not be lowered to a level of 'Need'. It should be a 'Want' -

    I want to commit my life to making that man/woman happy,
    I want to build a life with him/her and be a part of his/her life,
    I want to set my eyes on none other,
    I want to declare to the world that in sickness or in health, for richer or for poorer, i choose to love, hold and cherish this man/woman till death do us part.

    Marriage is a covenant that is not to be broken. No how much the world has perverted the sanctity of such a beautiful declaration of love, I believe the there are still people out there who would still hold true the real reason for marriage. Now all is left is to find the one who also shares your views and cherishes them like you do.

    Don, I totally agree with your belief on WANT vs NEED when it comes to marriage. Sadly, a lot of folks pay lip-service to these ideals - they fall deeply in love, make these promises, but then, they don't follow up with the commitment and tenacity required to make marriages strong when tough times come a-knocking on the door.

    That is one of the reasons why I find it very hard to trust any one person till today. The vast majority of people think that they know themselves but they actually haven't delved deep enough. They THINK they love you, they THINK they want to live the rest of their lives with you, they commit to you because they THINK they are ready for it... but as the years go by, more is discovered about the self and the other and then issues start to surface and differences start to be glaring.

    There's actually an 'acid test' that I apply to people I like that usually shows in a short space of time, whether they are true gentlemen or otherwise. It's what I call, a 'reverse closeness' strategy. It can end up hurting like hell because you make yourself vulnerable in the process, but it helps me decide in a relatively short space of time, whether to start building the walls or to continue to trust that person.

    I feel self-awareness is very important. How can you contribute fully in a relationship unless you understand yourself well enough? Only when you start to understand yourself can you then be a step closer to perceiving who would be an ideal partner for you. Otherwise, you'd be bringing false hopes and various ills into a relationship. Sad, but you see a lot of marriage doomed to failure before they even sign on that piece of paper...

    Too many folks enter into a marriage thinking it would solve problems, but they forget that marriage in itself presents a new set of challenges that requires tough work, sheer commitment and steadfastness, and deep love to pull through. It is definitely not all lovey-dovey all of the time.
