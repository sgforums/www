    The subprime crisis claimed a victim beyond the market for low-quality home loans when American Home Mortgage Investment said it could not pay its debts.

    American Home Mortgage is a real estate investment trust that makes mortgages and bundles them into securities. Although it does not deal directly in subprime mortgages, the value of its better-quality assets suffered in the recent market rerating of risks that began with defaults on home loans to uncreditworthy borrowers made during the housing boom at abruptly ended early this year.

    The reduced value of American Home's assets led to margin calls this week from its lenders, which it could not meet. The company said it was unable to tap credit lines to make the payments, although it did not explain why this was so. The credit lines were generally with major banks.

    After its announcement, American Home's stock lost 87.1% of its value, falling to $1.35 just after 2 p.m. down from $10.47 when it last traded on Friday. The New York Stock Exchange suspended the stock prior to trading on Monday pending an announcement. The market took a nosedive at about the same time with the Dow Jones industrial average losing about 52 points and falling to 13,339.61 a few minutes later, though its loss for the day was just 18.70 points.

    Other mortgage lenders also suffered on Tuesday. Impac Mortgage shares plummeted 14.2%, or 42 cents, to $2.53, in Tuesday afternoon trading, Novastar Financial tumbled 15.1%, or $1.95, to $10.98.

    One firm that is holding its own is IndyMac Bancorp, the second-largest mortgage lender in the United States. Despite a 60% drop in its second-quarter earnings, shares of the company shot up 4.8%, or $1.04, to $22.73, in Tuesday afternoon trading, after the company announced it still planned to pay its 50 cent-per-share quarterly dividend.
