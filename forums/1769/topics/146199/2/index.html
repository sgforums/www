    Originally posted by alexkusu:
    U seem to know a lot. Are u a journalist? So what are your feelings on this matter?
    Nah, I'm a psychologist. My interest in this started when I was in Sec 3 and read Stephane Groueff's "The Manhattan Project". Initially, I was horrified, and over the years it's nagged at me about how Truman could have made such a decision, especially since he refused to share the burden of the decision with the other Allied leaders. That kind of a decision is something I wouldn't wish on my worst enemy.

    Like I said, dropping the bomb isn't an ideal solution by any stretch of the imagination. Getting the Japanese to lay down their arms without bloodshed would have been the best outcome - after all, Sun Tzu did say that the best war fought is one that is won without a single shot being fired - but the Japanese were never going to be succeptible to a "hearts and minds" campaign.

    It came down to two things: invasion or dropping the bomb. Based on the invasions of the outer Japanese islands, we may (very conservatively) estimate Japanese causalties to be twice that of Allied casualties, and on the lowest estimates presented to Truman, that would have been 210,000 over 120 days of fighting, with 105,000 Allied losses. Mind you, those are conservative figures. Let me explain.

    The invasion of Japan was never going to be easy - an ambhibious assault on Tokyo could only take place on the beaches of Kyushu and Kanto, and the Japanese knew this and had both heavily covered. This would have made Normandy look like a minor skirmish. Mind you, the Battle of Normandy resulted in 209,000 Allied casualties, and that was against an enemy that had been faked out into defending the wrong spot, a mistake which the Japanese were not going to commit.

    In light of this, I would personally figure the Allied casualty figures at 300-400,000, and Japanese somewhere between 600-800,000. The total casualty figure of Hiroshima and Nagasaki combined was still less than half of the lower end of this estimate.

    So it comes down to this: the need for the decision was created by the Japanese, not the Allies, when Suzuki rejected the Potsdam Declaration. The decision taken resulted in half the number of Japanese being killed, and no needless expenditure of Allied lives. So the problem really lies only in the weapon of choice, but for that one point, would you have 3 times the number of deaths on your conscience? I wouldn't.

    The nature of history is such that for every decision you make today, there's going to be a whole bunch of people second-guessing you tomorrow with the benefit of 20/20 hindsight. Rather than making that mistake, I'd leave it at saying that the decision to use the bombs was, quite simply, the lesser of two evils based upon the figures above.
