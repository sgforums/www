    ChocoB, SL, I love my eggs done the same way both of you mentioned, a bit soft and runny, with dark soya sauce and pepper. Also, a childhood legacy.

    I remember my childhood spent in my grandma's 3-room HDB flat very well. It had these silver-painted metal gates that would creak a certain way when you pushed it aside. Many an idyllic day was spent sitting just behind the gate, sticking my legs out into the corridor for a stretch, and then quickly sticking my legs back in before grandma, who was a neat-freak, got wise to my antics.

    The metal rails outside the common corridor were sooooo high to me then! I had to look up to see the top of the rails. Now, they reach my waist. I've become a giant!!

    During the Mooncake Festival, the kids would congregate along the corridor and proudly parade their lanterns. We had 'factions' back then and I remember one year, we were envious of the larger and nicer cellophane lanterns the kids in the other 'faction' had... and how we giggled madly when they bumped into each other and their lanterns got burnt to a wicked black crisp!

    In fact, I laughed so hard that day, I started to turn round and round, throwing my head up, guffawing... and... next thing I knew, I was down on the floor, with grandma yelling some Hokkien expletives and rubbing my forehead with the bottom of her slipper!! Apparently, in my giddy glee, I had walked right smack into the silver gate and sustained a 'bua-doo-ku' (contusion) on my forehead, and it was old wives' tale that if you immediately rubbed a bua-doo-ku with the bottom of your shoe, all would be well.

    I remember yowling out in pain, not so much from the bua-doo-ku but from the dirt on my grandma's slipper being rubbed with great friction against my forehead!!! Oddly enough, back in those days, kids don't succumb to illnesses and infections as easily as they seem to do nowadays. I certainly didn't get some funny infection from having dust and dirt and maybe even, soil (!!!) rubbed into the wound on my forehead! Come to think of it, my guardian angel must have been watching over me, otherwise, I could've sustained some nasty infection like meningitis or tetanus, and died!

    Grandma and Grandpa still live in the same old apartment. A few of their elderly neighbours are still there too. Going back to visit them each time just brings back a flood of childhood memories. Ah... the days when I was young and playful, with not a care in the world!

    The '70's was a period when neighbours interacted with each other and enjoyed a sense of comaraderie and helped to watch out for each other.

    If grandma ran out of sugar, she just had to walk over to any neighbour living on the same floor and 'borrow' a cup of it, then 'return' the sugar when she bought it.

    Groceries were bought by calling 'Ah Teck' and ordering via the phone and a few hours later, 'Ah Teck' would come with the groceries in a box being wheeled on his squeaky thingy. (eh... pai seh... suddenly just cannot recall what those things are called!)

    Hearing grandma call 'Ah Teck' on the phone always made my day because sometimes, she would include a pack or two of 'Koka' crispy snacks or Twisties for me! Or if I was really really good that day, there'll be a lollipop!

    Soft drinks were sold in glass bottles then. After drinking, the glass bottles would be left in the carton by a corner of the kitchen for 'Ah Teck' to come and collect for recycling. Sometimes, I'd wake up and find a cockroach inside the bottle, fatally attracted by the sweet syrupy smells and then had fallen inside the smooth bottle in its attempt to get some of the sweet nectar!

    Grandpa would occasionally bring home a huge bag of cockles and then the special yellow metal basin would be brought out where they made a huge din when being emptied into it. I would continue the symphony by grabbing a spoon and shovelling them around, causing grandma to yell at me to stop the noise! Well, I'd then sit there and stop... for a short while of course, before the lure of the orchestra got overwhelmed me again! hehehe!

    In those days, if you wanted to eat chicken, you had to bring home a live one! On special occasions, grandma would return from the market with a live chicken tucked under her armpit and it would then be tied in the bathroom where it'll be my reluctant playmate for a while as I sat there trying to 'make friends' with it then run yelling when it made sudden movements at me. Yeah, I got 'hen-pecked' a lot then!

    Thank God Grandpa would always bring me into the bedroom to play when grandma went into the bathroom to slaughter the chicken. I never got to see the awful process. PHEW! I also never got to associate the missing chicken with the plate of nice steamed 'bai zhan ji' grandma would place on the altar table as offering to the gods and descendants before taking it to the dinner table. Sweet innocence of youth!
